## Introduction

The Most Valuable Asset in Your Business Is You I remember exactly where I was standing when everything changed.

It was an internet marketing seminar, the kind where a few hundred people pile into a hotel ballroom and sit through back-to-back presentations, each one hoping to be the thing that finally transforms their business. Most presentations at events like that never are. You take a lot of notes, eat bad catered food, and fly home wondering if the trip was worth the time away from your desk.

At the time, I was just a kid from Boise who was genuinely terrified of talking to strangers. I'm an introvert, and starting a conversation with someone I don't know is, for me, legitimately uncomfortable. I had built a small online business selling information products, but I was always hiding behind my computer screen, behind my ads, behind my sales letters. I was putting marketing out into the world, but I wasn't really there in any of it. Not as a person, anyway.

The emcee stepped up to the microphone and made an announcement I wasn't expecting. “Our next speaker has a successful business online making millions of dollars a year teaching men how to get women to approach them.”

A few people chuckled, and I felt my posture change. I leaned forward in my chair and put my pen to my notepad, because something about that sentence told me I needed to pay attention.

“Let’s put our hands together for John Alanis!” John walked onstage and opened with a line I still think about today.

“Notice that I didn’t say ‘how to pick up women,’” he said, pausing long enough for the distinction to land. “I show men how to get women to come to them. Big difference. Now, how many of you here would like to have customers come to you?”



<!-- Page 7 -->



My hand shot up before I even finished processing the question, and when I looked around the room, nearly every hand was in the air.

“When I start working with a guy, the first thing I teach him is something I call the ‘Attractive Character.’ If you want to attract someone, you have to be attractive. And I’m not talking about your looks. I’m talking about your personality.”

I filled the next two pages of my notepad trying to keep up with everything he said. The Moment Everything Changed John spent the next hour teaching a room full of internet marketers something that none of us had ever framed quite that way before. He wasn’t talking about better ads. He wasn’t talking about conversion rate optimization or split-testing headlines. He was talking about who you are, and how you present yourself to the world, and the magnetic pull that a well-crafted personality can create when it’s deployed consistently in your marketing.

Here’s what hit me hardest: John didn’t develop this framework for business people originally. He built it to help men who were completely lost when it came to meeting women. Guys who were awkward, unsure of themselves, or who had simply never figured out how to make themselves genuinely interesting to someone else. His entire system was built on one foundational insight: you don’t chase people. You become the kind of person they want to come to.

The moment he explained how that dynamic played out in dating, I could see with complete clarity exactly how it worked in business.

When I got home from that event, I started experimenting. At the time, I was still a student-athlete at Boise State University, wrestling on the college team, and when I spoke at small marketing events I started working wrestling stories into my presentations.

Stories about my coach. Stories about the mat. Stories about what I had learned from years of competition about discipline, losing, and finding a way to win anyway.

The results were immediate and fascinating. When I made my offer at the end of the presentation and people came to the back of the room to buy, I noticed that they all had something in common. They were male athletes. Guys who had played football in college, or wrestled, or competed in some other sport. They would walk up and say things like, “Hey, man, I ran track in college,” or “I was a wrestler, too. That story you told about your coach, I had a coach exactly like that.” They felt like they knew me, and because they



<!-- Page 8 -->



felt like they knew me, they trusted me enough to buy. I knew something was working. I just didn’t fully understand yet what it was.

The Story I Almost Didn’t Tell A few years later, my wife Collette and I were trying to start a family. It wasn’t happening the way we’d hoped. We spent months working through a fertility process that was emotionally draining for both of us, and there were long stretches where we genuinely didn’t know how it was going to turn out. Eventually, after working with a fertility doctor, Collette got pregnant. Not just pregnant, as it turned out. Pregnant with twins.

Some time after that, I was at a marketing seminar, standing onstage giving a presentation I had given many times before. And then something happened that I didn’t anticipate: I felt a strong pull to tell that fertility story to the audience. I pushed back against it immediately. That’s private, I thought. That’s personal. I’m up here talking about business and marketing and funnels, and there is no reason to bring up something that vulnerable in front of a room full of people I barely know.

But the feeling didn’t go away. So I told the story. I got emotional in the middle of it. The room got very quiet. And when I finished and made my offer, I looked toward the back of the room and something was genuinely different from every other time I had sold from that stage. The athletes were still there, just like always. But now there was an entirely new group of people walking up to buy: wives, mothers, couples, people who had never purchased anything from me before and who had no particular interest in marketing or business strategy.

I stood there for a moment trying to make sense of what I was seeing. And then it clicked. Different parts of you attract different people. The wrestling stories had been calling in the male athletes. The fertility story called in the parents and families. Neither group was my “target audience” in any predetermined sense. They were just people who found a part of my story that resonated with something in their own experience, and once they felt that connection, they trusted me enough to take the next step.

That was when I realized that what John Alanis had been teaching us wasn’t a marketing tactic. It was something much more fundamental than that. It was the whole game.

## Secret #1

Why the Attractive Character Works I want to start this chapter with a short quiz, and I need you to actually try it before you keep reading. It’s only two questions, and it only takes a few seconds, but those few seconds will probably do more to explain why this book exists than anything else I could say.

Ready? Here we go. First, take ten seconds right now and try to name three things you learned in college.

Specific things. Facts, formulas, concepts, anything from those four years of lectures, textbooks, and tuition payments you made.

Okay, how many did you get? If you’re like most people, you either struggled to come up with three or you’re not entirely sure the things you named are actually from college or if you picked them up somewhere else.

Now try the second part. Name three characters from Seinfeld. That one took you about half a second, didn’t it? Jerry. Kramer. George. Elaine. Newman.

You probably rattled off four or five names before I even finished the sentence, and you could probably describe each of those characters in detail: how they talked, what they cared about, the specific quirks that made them memorable and impossible to forget… Here’s the thing that makes this quiz fascinating: you likely paid somewhere around a hundred thousand dollars for that college education. And you watched Seinfeld for free.

Frank Kern, one of the best copywriters and direct response marketers alive, used that quiz in a course I was studying years ago, and the point he was making stopped me cold.

We don’t remember content. We remember characters. Our brains are wired to hold onto personalities, stories, and the people who made us feel something, and to let go of almost everything else. The information fades. The character stays. And that insight, as simple as it sounds, is the entire foundation of what you’re going to learn in this book.

The Undercurrent of Fear



<!-- Page 14 -->



Every business transaction in the world involves some version of the same problem: the person considering buying from you doesn’t fully trust you yet. They want what you’re selling. They’ve read the sales page, they’ve watched the video, they’ve heard the pitch.

But somewhere underneath all of that, there’s a quiet voice running a series of questions they haven’t said out loud. What if I’m making the wrong decision? What if this person is just going to take my money and disappear? What if my friends find out I bought this and they think I’m an idiot?

Frank Kern described this as the “undercurrent of fear,” and it’s one of the most accurate descriptions of what’s happening in a prospect’s mind that I’ve ever come across. That undercurrent is always there, and it acts like friction in your sales process. It’s invisible, but it’s real, and it slows everything down.

But here’s what Frank also said: when someone feels like they know you, like they’ve been following you for a while and they understand who you are and what you stand for, that undercurrent disappears. The fear dissolves. And when the fear is gone, buying becomes a no-brainer. Not because you made a better argument or lowered your price or ran a bigger discount, but simply because the relationship changed the entire emotional context of the transaction.

That’s what the Attractive Character does, at its core. It builds perceived trust at scale. It creates the feeling of a real relationship with people who have never met you in person, and it does that through the stories you tell, the personality you show up with, and the consistency with which you present yourself over time. The people on your list, following your content, watching your videos, and reading your emails start to feel like they know you. And once they feel that, everything changes.

The Never-Ending Novel Dan Kennedy, who has been one of the most important mentors in my business career, put it this way when I was interviewing him on a call about story and personality in marketing: the information business is a never-ending novel built on plot, characters, adventures, and ideas.

Read that again, because it’s more important than it might look on the surface. When someone enters your world, whether it’s through a free book, a lead magnet, a YouTube video, or an ad that caught their eye, they’re not just opting into information.

They’re stepping into a story. Your story. And from that point forward, every email you



<!-- Page 15 -->



send, every piece of content you publish, every product you launch, and every offer you make is another chapter in that story. The people who stay aren’t staying because they need more information. They’re staying because they want to know what happens next.

They’re invested in the character. Dan made another point on that same call that I think every entrepreneur needs to write on a sticky note and put somewhere they’ll see it every single day. He said: customers who are involved in your story have more tolerance to be sold to frequently, because of their relationship with you, not because of what you’re selling.

Think about what that means in practical terms. When your audience has a genuine connection with you as the Attractive Character, they actually want to hear your offers.

They’re not tuning out when you sell. They’re leaning in, because buying from you is another way to participate in the story they’re already invested in. The relationship doesn’t just make people more likely to buy once. It makes them more likely to buy again, and again, and again, and to do it without the kind of sales resistance that forces most businesses to keep running ever-bigger discounts to close the same customers they’ve already won.

The National Enquirer Secret On that same call, Dan told me a story about the editor of the National Enquirer, who had spoken at one of their info summits years earlier. What this editor shared was something that Dan found genuinely fascinating, and when he described it to me, I understood immediately why it mattered for everyone building an online business.

At its peak, the National Enquirer was the most-read newspaper on the planet. More readers than any other publication, anywhere. And when this editor explained how they kept people coming back week after week, he drew a very clear distinction between two separate jobs that the magazine did.

The cover was about hooks. The 300-pound baby. The alien sighting. The celebrity scandal. Those outrageous, impossible-to-ignore headlines were designed to do one thing only, which was to get someone to pick up the magazine off the rack. That’s new customer acquisition. You stop the scroll, you grab the attention, you get them in the door.

But the reason people came back every single week, the reason they subscribed and kept paying and built a habit around reading that publication, had nothing to do with the



<!-- Page 16 -->



covers. It had everything to do with the characters inside. The recurring personalities. The ongoing stories. The familiar voices that people looked forward to finding each time they opened a new issue. The hooks got them in. The characters kept them there.

This is exactly how your business works, or how it should work, once you understand it. Your ads, your hooks, your lead magnets, and your free offers are the cover of the National Enquirer. They exist to get someone to raise their hand and step into your world.

But what turns that new subscriber into a customer, and that customer into a loyal follower who keeps buying from you for years, is the Attractive Character they meet once they’re inside.

Content Gets Forgotten. Character Gets Remembered. Let me bring this back to Frank Kern’s quiz for a moment, because there’s one more layer to it that I want you to think about.

After a while, Frank said, there’s only so much content your audience is going to stay interested in. The information gets stale. The tactics they already know. They’ve heard the concepts before. But if you’ve done this right, what happens instead of them leaving is that they stop caring about the content and start caring about you. They become more intrigued by the character who’s writing to them than by the information that character is sharing. The bottom line, as Frank put it, is that we remember and respond more to entertainment than we do to useful knowledge. Character beats content every single time.

What this means practically is that your Attractive Character is not just a marketing tool you layer on top of an otherwise functional business. It’s the thing that makes your business last. Products change, tactics evolve, platforms come and go, and what worked three years ago might not work the same way today. But the relationship your audience has with you as a person, as a character, as someone whose story they’ve followed and whose voice they’ve come to trust, that compounds over time. It grows stronger the longer you show up consistently as yourself.

Dan Kennedy put this principle into one of the simplest and most useful sentences I’ve ever heard in my years of studying marketing: we are all in a relationship business, not a product business. People come in because of a product. They stay because of a relationship. And every chapter of this book is built on that single foundational truth.

## Secret #2

The Psychology of Attraction Think about the last time you ran into someone you genuinely admire, someone you’ve followed online for years, and had a brief conversation with them. Maybe it was at an event, or in a line somewhere, or a chance encounter at an airport. And think about how that conversation felt on your side of it. You probably already knew what state they lived in. You knew something about their family. You knew about the challenges they’d been through, the beliefs they hold, the way they think about their industry. You felt, in some meaningful sense, like you already knew this person, even though you’d never met.

Now think about how that encounter felt from their side. To them, you were a stranger who walked up out of nowhere and started a conversation. They had no idea who you were, what you did, or anything about your life. The relationship was completely one-sided, and both of you knew it.

John Alanis, the man who first taught me this framework, explained that this experience has a name in psychology. It’s called a parasocial relationship. And understanding why it happens, and specifically how it happens, is the key to understanding why the Attractive Character is one of the most powerful forces in business today.

The One-Sided Relationship That Feels Real John traced this psychology back to research that had been done in the 1950s by two sociologists named Horton and Wohl, who noticed something unusual happening between television audiences and the personalities they watched on screen. Viewers weren’t just passively absorbing entertainment. They were developing what felt, from the inside, like genuine personal relationships with people they had never met and would never meet. They worried about TV hosts when they seemed tired. They felt happy for them when good things happened. They talked about them to friends and family as if they were discussing a mutual acquaintance. The emotional architecture of those one-sided relationships was, in many measurable ways, identical to the emotional architecture of real friendships.

What Horton and Wohl identified in the 1950s with television has only become more intense in the decades since, and particularly with the rise of the internet. When someone



<!-- Page 19 -->



subscribes to your email list and reads your messages every day, when they watch your YouTube videos and hear you talk about your kids and your failures and the lessons you’ve learned, when they follow your social media account and see you showing up consistently week after week with a recognizable voice and point of view, their brain starts building a mental model of you. It catalogs your beliefs, your quirks, your stories, your values. It builds what feels, neurologically, like a real relationship.

And here’s the thing that makes this so important for your business: the trust that forms inside a parasocial relationship is real. It’s not simulated or imagined or somehow less valid than the trust in a face-to-face friendship. The emotions are genuine. The loyalty is genuine. And the willingness to buy from, recommend, and defend someone you trust is every bit as genuine as it would be if you’d spent ten years going to dinner together every week.

The Environment You’re Selling Into Before we get into how to build the kind of character that creates these relationships at scale, I want to spend a moment on the environment we’re actually operating in, because I think a lot of entrepreneurs dramatically underestimate how difficult the problem is that the Attractive Character solves.

John opened his presentation with something that stopped me cold the first time I heard it. He said that trust is at an all-time low. Not just low. At an all-time low. And when he laid out the evidence for that, it was hard to argue with. People have watched institutions they once trusted collapse or disappoint them, one after another. Corporate leaders, financial advisors, government agencies, and yes, internet marketers, have given people plenty of reasons to be skeptical of anyone who shows up with a pitch.

The result is an audience that comes to you already defensive, already guarded, and already carrying a mental checklist of every reason they might be about to get taken advantage of. They want what you’re selling. They know they have a problem that needs solving. But that quiet undercurrent of fear that Frank Kern describes, the one running questions they haven’t said out loud about whether you’re trustworthy, whether they’ll regret this, whether their friends will think they’re an idiot for buying it, that undercurrent is running constantly. And it is friction in every transaction you try to complete.

Layer on top of that the sheer volume of distraction that people are managing every hour of every day. Notifications. Social media. News. Streaming. Emails from a hundred different businesses all competing for the same inbox. The average person’s attention is



<!-- Page 20 -->



being pulled in more directions simultaneously than at any other point in human history, and every one of those competing pulls is another reason they might not see your message, not open your email, not finish watching your video, not follow through on the purchase they were three clicks away from making.

This is the environment. And the question that John was answering when he first taught this material is exactly the right question to be asking: how do you sell effectively, build a real business, and create loyal customers who come back again and again, inside of an environment defined by distrust and distraction?

His answer was three words: captivate, mesmerize, and fascinate. Why Howard Stern and Rush Limbaugh Outlasted Everything John made a point during that presentation that I want you to sit with, because it’s one of the most useful frames I’ve ever encountered for understanding what you’re actually trying to build with your Attractive Character.

He asked the room to think about Howard Stern and Rush Limbaugh. Two radio personalities from completely different ends of the cultural and political spectrum, with virtually nothing in common except one thing: both of them built audiences that actively sought them out, day after day, year after year, through every wave of new technology and new distraction that came along. Satellite radio. The internet. Podcasts. Streaming.

Social media. All of it arrived and disrupted the media landscape, and yet both of those personalities kept their audiences through all of it, decade after decade.

Now think about the mechanics of that loyalty for a moment. Howard Stern wasn’t showing up in his listeners’ inboxes begging them to tune in. Rush Limbaugh wasn’t running retargeting ads asking his audience to come back. The audience was doing something entirely different: they were rearranging their schedules to make sure they didn’t miss the show. The Pursuer and the Pursued Every relationship dynamic has a direction to it. Someone is doing the pursuing and someone is being pursued. And here is something that is universally true about attraction: the person being pursued has all the power.

Think about why this is. When you pursue someone, whether in dating, business, or any other context, you are communicating something about your own position. You are saying, in effect, that you need this more than they do. That you are more invested in this relationship happening than they are. And the moment that perception takes hold, you’ve handed over your leverage, because leverage always sits with the person who cares less, or at least appears to.

Most businesses are built entirely on the pursuer model. They run ads to find prospects. They send cold outreach to get attention. They beg for reviews, chase referrals, and constantly work to drag reluctant audiences toward offers that haven’t earned enough trust to sell themselves. The Leapfrog Theory One of the most useful frameworks John ever shared for thinking about authority and positioning came from a book he mentioned in his original presentation: Winning Through Intimidation by Robert Ringer. Most people have never read it, and the title puts some people off, but the core concept inside it is something every entrepreneur needs to understand.

Ringer observed that there are two ways to reach the top of any profession. The first is the ladder: you work your way up incrementally, earning each rung through demonstrated competence, and you wait for the people above you to acknowledge your progress and formally elevate you. The second is what he called the leapfrog: you simply declare yourself at the top and operate from there, letting your actual results fill in the proof over time.

Most people take the ladder by default, not because it’s the better strategy but because it’s the safer-feeling one. The leapfrog requires a kind of internal commitment that makes a lot of people deeply uncomfortable: it requires you to position yourself as an authority before you have external permission to do so. Before someone credentialed you. Before the industry recognized you. Before your numbers were big enough to feel undeniable.

John made an important caveat to the leapfrog theory that I want to make sure you don’t miss, because people misapply this concept all the time. You declare yourself at

the top when you are ready to be at the top, not one moment before. There are plenty of people who skip straight to the declaration without the substance behind it, and they get exposed quickly. The market is not stupid. The audience forgives a lot, but they don’t forgive being misled about the fundamental competence of the person they trusted to guide them.

When John started his business, he declared himself the king of let-them-come-to-you attraction. He was ready for that claim when he made it. That’s where you start.

The Architecture of Attraction: The Five Elements and Four Character Types The first three secrets of this book built the foundation: why the Attractive Character works, the psychology behind it, and the specific dynamic it creates between you and your audience. Now we get into the actual construction. And the first thing I want you to understand about building your character is that the people who do it badly almost always make the same mistake. They build from the outside in. They pick a type, write an origin story, choose a voice, and then spend the next year trying to keep the costume on.

By the time you finish it, you should be able to see exactly where your current character is working from genuine material and where it’s working from design. And that distinction changes everything.

The Five Elements: What Most People Get Wrong Element #1 - Backstory: It’s not an achievement story. It’s an empathy story.

Here’s the mistake almost everyone makes with their backstory. They tell it as a journey from struggle to success, which sounds right, because that’s the arc. But the emphasis ends up on the destination rather than the starting point, and that’s backwards. Your audience is not reading your backstory hoping to celebrate where you ended up. They’re reading it hoping to find themselves somewhere inside it. The moment they see their current situation reflected in your past, something unlocks. The wall between prospect and follower comes down, often permanently.

The test for whether your backstory is doing its job is this: does it describe the specific, uncomfortable reality of where you started (not the polished retrospective version of it) at a level of detail that makes your dream customer think, That’s exactly what my life



<!-- Page 35 -->



looks like right now? Not approximately. Exactly. The specific fear. The specific failure. The specific moment when you knew the way you were doing things wasn’t going to work.

When I started, I was a kid in Boise hiding behind my computer screen, genuinely scared to talk to strangers, watching everyone else seem to figure out this internet marketing thing while I kept producing marketing that had no person in it. That specific description, introverted, hiding, marketing that was technically competent but had no soul, is what lands for a specific kind of person. Not for everyone. For that person. Your backstory doesn’t need to resonate with everyone. It needs to resonate completely with the person you’re actually trying to reach, because that person will feel seen in a way they never felt before.

One more thing about backstory that most people miss: it’s not a single story you tell once and archive. It’s a bank of moments you draw from repeatedly, pairing different fragments of your past with different situations your audience is facing right now. Kaelin Poulin built LadyBoss to over $200 million in revenue partly because she understood that her backstory wasn’t just one story. It was hundreds of them. And different women needed different pieces of it at different times. Her struggle with identity after having kids.

The moment she stopped recognizing herself in the mirror. The decision to rebuild rather than retreat. Each of those moments is a separate entry point into her world, each one calling out a specific person who is living through that exact same thing today.

Element #2 - Character Flaws: The flaw has to cost you something. Most people, when they decide to “share their flaws,” share what are actually disguised strengths. “I work too hard. I care too much. I’m a perfectionist who can’t let things go until they’re right…” These are not flaws. They are virtues wearing a flaw costume, and your audience can feel the difference instantly. Sharing a fake flaw doesn’t make you relatable. It makes you seem like someone who is trying to appear relatable, which is the opposite of the effect you’re going for.

A real flaw is something that actually cost you something. Something that hurt people you cared about. A pattern of behavior you repeated even after you knew better. A belief you held that was wrong and that you acted on in ways you’re not proud of. The story of your genuine failure, not the kind that leads cleanly to triumph in the next paragraph, but the kind that left a mark, is what actually builds the bridge to your audience. Because your audience has real failures too. They’ve made decisions that cost them things. When you admit yours, they don’t think less of you. They think: Finally, someone who’s willing to be honest!



<!-- Page 36 -->



John Alanis drilled this into me and I haven’t forgotten it. He said you want your audience to believe you were worse off than they are right now. And not as some strategy. As the actual truth. So if your starting point was genuinely harder than where your audience is today, say that. Specifically. Don’t summarize it. Don’t clean it up. Tell them the specific thing that happened, the embarrassing part, the part that cost you more money than you want to admit, the part that made you feel like an idiot.

Because here’s what I’ve found: The polished version of your story doesn’t do anything. People hear it and they move on. But that one specific detail, the humiliating one, the one you almost cut because you didn’t want people to know, that’s the one they remember forever. That’s the one that makes them feel like they actually know you. Like they’re connected to you in a way they can’t quite explain. That detail is the whole thing. Don’t leave it out.

Element #3 - Parables: You’re building a library, not a lecture. A parable is not an illustration of a point. It’s a story that is the point. The version of the point that bypasses the audience’s analytical brain and hits the emotional brain directly, where beliefs actually live. When I need an audience to understand the principle of investment, that you have to make a real commitment for a result to mean something, I don’t explain the principle. I tell them about the night my wrestling coach Mark Schultz knocked on my dorm room door, asked for my wallet, took all the money out of it, handed the empty wallet back, and left me holding a videotape I’d just paid for without agreeing to. Then he told me: “If I’d given you that tape for free, you’d never watch it.”

I have told that story hundreds of times across twenty years of presentations and emails and webinars. It never gets old to me because every time I tell it, I can see the audience land on the principle in real time. Not hearing it, but feeling it. That’s what a great parable does. And the reason I can tell it so consistently is that I’ve attached it to one specific principle, and I know exactly when to reach for it.

The work here is to build a stable of parables like that. Stories from your genuine life, each one attached to a specific principle you teach repeatedly. You probably already have more of these than you realize. The experiences that changed how you see something, the conversations that shifted your thinking, the failures that taught you the thing you now teach most confidently, those are all parable candidates. For now, the key insight is that parables are not decoration. They are the primary delivery mechanism for every important idea in your character’s teaching. If you don’t have a parable for it, the



<!-- Page 37 -->



principle won’t stick. Element #4 - Character Voice: The thing you keep editing out is probably the thing.

There’s a version of every piece of content you write before you clean it up for publication, and then there’s the published version. Most people’s instinct is to close the gap between those two versions, to make the published version more polished, more professional, more careful. That instinct is costing them their character.

The specific things about how you naturally express yourself, the phrase you return to without thinking, the analogy that only makes sense in your specific cultural context, the joke that lands for your audience and nobody else, the direct statement of opinion that your internal editor tells you is too strong, those things are your voice. They are what makes your content sound like you rather than like a competent professional who could be interchangeable with five other competent professionals in your market. The moment you edit them out, you become the competent professional. And competent professionals don’t build true fans.

John Alanis called himself “The King of Let Them Come to You.” He said it everywhere, constantly, with zero embarrassment. That specific phrase, that exact level of confidence, is what made him instantly stand out in a crowded market. It would have been easy to dial it back, to let the title be implicit rather than stated. Instead he planted his flag in eight words and built his entire character around them. Your voice works the same way.

The specific, slightly odd, entirely yours expression of your worldview is not a liability to be softened. It is the asset your audience will remember long after they’ve forgotten the content.

Element #5 - Polarity: You don’t need to be controversial. You need to be specific. The word “polarity” makes a lot of people think about political controversy or inflammatory takes designed to provoke a reaction. That’s not what this is. You don’t need to say anything incendiary. You need to say something specific, which in most markets is already more than enough to create real division, because most markets are dominated by people saying carefully vague things that nobody can disagree with and nobody can actually follow.

The polarity your character needs comes from having a genuine opinion about the conventional wisdom in your market and being willing to say it clearly. The thing everyone in your space believes that you think is wrong, or at best incomplete. The approach everyone recommends that you’ve seen fail the same way too many times.



<!-- Page 38 -->



The promise your competitors are making that you know doesn’t hold up at the level of actual execution. When you say those things directly, not as provocation, but as honest assessment, you split the room. Some people think you’re wrong and they leave. Some people think you’ve just said the truest thing they’ve ever heard about their situation and they never leave. The second group is your real audience, and they could never have found you while you were still trying to hold the room together with the safe version.

As John Alanis showed us in Chapter 2, Howard Stern and Rush Limbaugh both built audiences that outlasted every format change in media for decades. Neither of them held their audience through the power of their information. They held it through the power of their conviction. People tuned in not because they always agreed, but because they always knew exactly where these characters stood. That clarity is magnetic.

Vagueness is not. The more specifically you define what you believe and what you’re willing to say clearly, the more powerfully you attract the people who needed to hear someone say exactly that.

The Four Character Types: Finding Your Natural Register The character types are not boxes to climb into. They’re a tool for understanding the natural register in which you are most compelling, the type your audience finds you most credible and most followable in. Most people have a dominant type, but the types are fluid depending on context. Knowing all four means you can shift deliberately rather than defaulting to whatever feels safe in the moment.

The Expert Leader has been where the audience wants to go and is leading them there. The authority is credential-based: you’ve done the thing, you know the path, you’re ahead on the journey and reaching back to guide people through the parts that nearly broke you. The Expert Leader’s content is directional: here’s where we are, here’s where we’re going, here’s what to do at this specific point in the journey.

The failure mode of this type is losing the backstory. Expert Leaders often drift into presenting their current success without regularly returning to the beginning, to the version of themselves that looked exactly like their audience looks now. When that happens, the authority remains but the pull disappears. The audience respects you but can no longer see themselves in you, which means they stop believing they can follow the same path. The fix is deliberate, regular, specific, uncomfortable returns to the starting point, told with enough detail that the person who is there right now can feel themselves in it.

The Adventurer/Crusader is on a mission and bringing the audience along for it. The authority is not expertise. It’s commitment and curiosity. The Adventurer goes somewhere the audience hasn’t been, finds something they couldn’t find themselves, and brings it back. The content is framed as discovery: I went looking for this. Here’s what I found.

Here’s why it changes everything. This type is remarkably powerful when entering a new market or a new area within an existing market, because it doesn’t depend on how much experience or how many credentials you have. Your authority is the depth of the search, not a prior claim to expertise in the territory. The Reporter/Evangelist curates, interviews, and advocates. Their superpower is synthesis. The ability to go wide across a subject, find the best of what’s there, and bring it back organized and contextualized in a way that serves their audience better than the audience could do for itself. The Reporter doesn’t need to be the original source of the ideas. They need to be the most trustworthy filter.

This type works best when the material or person being reported on is genuinely more compelling than the reporter’s own story. The Reporter builds authority through association and curation over time. Done consistently, it creates an audience that trusts you to find the right things, which means they’ll follow you into any new territory you explore because you’ve already demonstrated that you won’t waste their attention.

The Reluctant Hero doesn’t want to be up there in the spotlight. But the stakes are too high to stay quiet. They have something that could genuinely help people, but they would prefer not to be in front of the room. And the combination of those two things, the valuable thing and the authentic discomfort with holding it, is what makes people lean in.

The audience can feel the difference between someone who loves the spotlight and someone who shows up in it because they care too much about the outcome to stay away.

The Reluctant Hero is the type most often performed badly and most powerful when it’s genuine. If you’re actually an Expert Leader who loves the stage and you’re pretending to be reluctant because you think it reads as humble, your audience will feel the gap and the whole thing will undermine your credibility rather than build it. But if the discomfort is real, if you genuinely find the spotlight uncomfortable and you’re showing up anyway

Building Your Identity: Backstory, Flaws, Voice, and Polarity The last chapter gave you the diagnostic. This one gives you the construction tools. Your Backstory Is a Bank, Not a Biography The first mistake people make with their backstory is treating it as a single story, a fixed narrative that runs from struggle to success and gets told the same way every time.

Once you’ve told that story a dozen times, your audience has heard it. They know the ending. And telling it again in exactly the same arc doesn’t deepen the relationship. It just reminds them they’ve been with you long enough to know your origin story by heart.

The way Kaelin solved this, and the reason she was able to build a community of 700,000 customers without the content ever feeling stale, is that she treated her backstory as a bank. Not just one story, but an inventory of moments across her entire lifetime, each one a potential entry point for a different person at a different place in their journey. Before she was married. Before she had kids. When she was in college and barely paying rent.

When she looked at herself in the mirror after having her second child and felt like a stranger in her own body. When she had a food addiction she’d never told anyone about.

Each of those moments is a separate drawer in the bank, and a different woman opens that drawer when she hears it.

The mechanic that makes this work is what Kaelin calls uppers and downers. A downer is a struggle, a failure, an embarrassing truth, a moment when things were hard or you were wrong. An upper is the result, the lesson, the win, the transformation. You never tell a



<!-- Page 42 -->



downer without pairing it with an upper. You never lead with just the win without grounding it in the downer that preceded it. Every story is a pair.

The reason the pairing matters is precision. Telling just the downer without the upper slides into something that feels like asking for sympathy rather than building a bridge.

Telling just the upper without the downer looks a lot like bragging. Either one alone creates distance with your audience. But as a pair, they create the specific emotional combination that makes someone feel: She was where I am right now, and she found the way through. Which means the way through exists.

Here’s how to build the bank: Take a blank piece of paper and map out a timeline of your life in four sections: your past life (before the transformation your business is built around), your first phase of change (when you started figuring it out), your current life (what’s possible now), and your ongoing struggles (the things you’re still working on, which are gold, and which most people skip). Under each section, list specific moments, not themes, not summaries, but specific moments with a time, a place, and details you remember. The couch. The three glasses of wine. The number on the scale. The conversation you had in the car. The bank gets more useful the more granular your stories get.

Once you have the moments listed, pair them. Every downer in your past life section gets matched with an upper, either a result you eventually achieved, a lesson you finally learned, or a transformation that came from that specific struggle. Those pairs become your deployable story units. When you sit down to write an email or script a video, you’re not writing from scratch. You’re choosing which pair to reach for based on what you’re selling and who in your audience you’re trying to reach that day. Different pairs will resonate with different people. The same woman who doesn’t relate to the post-pregnancy identity struggle might completely identify with the college dorm room broke-and-scared story. Your bank makes sure you can reach her too.

Not in a disqualifying way. Not something that makes you untrustworthy or incompetent. But something that makes you genuinely human in a way that reveals a limitation, a mistake, a pattern you’ve repeated that you’re not proud of. If the honest answer is no, if sharing it only makes you look appealingly imperfect without revealing anything that actually cost you something, it’s not a real flaw. It’s brand-safe vulnerability, and your audience can



<!-- Page 43 -->



feel the difference. John Alanis was precise about this. He said you want your audience to believe you were worse off than they are. The implication isn’t that you manufacture a worse starting point than you actually had. It’s that if your genuine starting point was genuinely hard, if you were more lost, more wrong, more broke, more scared than your current status suggests, then say that specifically. The people who are currently in that hard place need to hear the true version, not the cleaned-up retrospective that makes your early mistakes sound like charming detours on an otherwise steady path to success.

The other thing most people get wrong about flaws is that they treat them as a one-time disclosure. You share the flaw origin story once in your backstory, and then you never mention it again because you’ve “addressed” it. What actually works is using your flaws as a recurring lens, a way of interpreting new situations and new lessons that reminds your audience continuously that you are a person in process, not a finished product. Developing Your Voice With Purpose Most people treat voice as something that either emerges naturally or doesn’t, a quality you either have or you don’t. John Alanis taught the opposite. He spent years studying colorful language the way other people study copywriting or sales technique. He tracked which phrases landed and which ones didn’t. He pointed to the Marx Brothers as the model: they road-tested every comedy bit in front of live audiences for months before anything went into a film, which is why their movies are still funny ninety years later. Nothing got kept that hadn’t already been proven to work.

The principle he identified is this: colorful language catches people off guard. It’s different and specific in a market where almost everyone sounds the same. And when it gets

someone to laugh at something they didn’t expect to laugh at, to respond despite themselves, that involuntary response is the feeling of attraction. You can’t force that reaction. Voice is made of several specific components, and you can work on each one deliberately. The first is characteristic phrases, the expressions you return to consistently enough that your audience begins to associate them with you specifically. John Alanis called himself “The King of Let Them Come to You” so often and so unself-consciously that the phrase became a marker. When you heard it, you knew who said it. Funnel Hackers. Your character needs its own version of these, and you don’t discover them by sitting down to invent them. The language carried the philosophy. Your insider language works the same way. The third component, and the most important, is permission to sound like yourself. The specific analogy that only makes sense in your cultural context. The reference that reveals exactly who you are.

The direct statement of opinion that your internal editor says is too blunt. These are the things that make up your own distinct voice. The editorial impulse that softens them into something more broadly acceptable is the impulse that turns a distinctive character into a competent professional. Competent professionals are easy to find. Characters are not.

Your Story Toolkit: Origin Story, Story Inventory, Miracles, and Six Core Story Arcs During a presentation I gave a few years ago, a friend of mine sat in the audience and counted the number of stories I told in the first sixty minutes. I guessed it would add up to maybe ten. The actual number was over fifty. Almost one per minute. But to hit the bullseye of the specific mental obstacle standing between my audience and the action I needed them to take.

That’s the thing most people miss about storytelling in marketing. They treat it as a relationship-building exercise, as a way to charm their audience before doing the “real work”: selling. But stories are the real work. Our analytical brain can hear a logical argument and produce thirty reasons why it doesn’t apply to our specific situation. The emotional brain hears a story and makes the connection before the analytical brain has time to object. The story lands. The belief shifts. The objection dissolves. That sequence is the entire mechanism. And people who master it aren’t just better storytellers. They’re playing a totally different ball game than those who don’t.

•How the Six Core Story Arcs are structures for your stories that connect with audiences and tap deep into the human subconscious Your Origin Story: How to Deliver It with Maximum Impact The Origin Story is not your biography. This is the first and most important thing to understand. Because the minute you think of it as your biography, as just a factual account of how you got to where you are now, you start telling it the wrong way. You front-load the credentials and the accomplishments, you summarize the struggle in a paragraph and spend the rest of the time on the result, and you end up with a story that positions you accurately but connects with nobody.

The Origin Story has one job: to create the emotional condition in which your audience believes the journey you took is available to them. Not just that it’s possible in theory, but that it’s actually available to someone who is currently in the exact position they are in right now. That belief only forms when two things happen in sequence.

First, they have to see themselves at your starting point, and I mean really see themselves, not just vaguely relate to the general themes. The specific detail matters: the exact thing that was wrong, the exact way you were thinking about it, the exact reason the approaches you tried before didn’t work.

Second, they have to feel the moment the thing changed. They don’t need to be told about it. They need to feel it, exactly the way you felt it. Both of those things require the story to follow a very specific structure.

That structure is the Epiphany Bridge, a five-phase framework that moves the listener from your backstory through to your moment of transformation in a way that lets them experience the journey alongside you rather than watching it from the outside.

Phase #1 - The Backstory The first phase is about where you were before the change, what desires you had, what things you tried that didn’t work, and what specific wall you kept hitting. This phase needs to land at the same point where your audience is right now. The closer you can get, the more specifically your starting point mirrors their current situation, the more powerfully the rest of the story works.

Phase #2 - The Journey



<!-- Page 49 -->



The second phase is about the call that finally made you try again despite all the previous failures. The villain you encountered (which in most business stories is a false belief system, not a person). And what was at stake if you didn’t find the answer this time.

The villain element is worth drilling deeper on. The villain is not a competitor. It’s the wrong idea that was running in the background of your market, shaping how everyone thought about the problem, and making sure that every solution tried inside that wrong frame was going to fail. Naming that villain precisely is what makes the journey feel like it was in service of something larger than personal success.

Phase #3 - The New Opportunity Phase three is about the guide who gave you the epiphany, the specific aha moment, and the new possibility that opened up once you had it. This is the hinge of the whole story. The guide can be a person, a book, a conversation, or a moment of clarity that hit you out of nowhere. It doesn’t matter what shape it took. What matters is that it needs to be specific, named, and real. And the epiphany itself needs to be described from the inside: not what you realized, but what it felt like to realize it, in that specific moment, before you had any evidence it would work. That vulnerability, sharing the moment before it was validated, is what makes the epiphany feel genuine rather than manufactured.

Phase #4 - The Framework The next phase is about the framework, the strategy you built after having the epiphany, the results you got from testing it, and the results others got when they applied it. This is where you establish that the thing works, not just for you but for others too. Not just once, but repeatably.

Phase #5 - Transformation The last phase is not just about what you accomplished externally. It’s not about the numbers you hit. It’s about who you became through the process. Your audience needs to hear about both the external result and the internal transformation you went through.

The external result shows them what’s possible. And the internal transformation shows them the person they could become when they get there, which is actually the real result they want to invest in, deep down.

Once you’ve built the full five-phase version, you can compress it down to a thirty-second story, or you can expand it to thirty minutes, depending on the context. Journey in a sentence, the New Opportunity in a sentence, the Framework in a sentence, your Transformation in your final sentence. That thirty-second version becomes the hook you drop into emails, ads, and social posts to pull people into the full version when they’re ready for it. The full version deepens their relationship to you. The compressed version triggers the click that gets them there.

Your Story Inventory: The Treasure Trove That Keeps on Giving Early in my career, I was with Dan Kennedy at a small event. At some point, I pulled him aside to ask for his advice. Here’s why this matters: When you’re in front of your audience, you never want to be reaching for a story. You want to have easy access to a deep well you’ve already built.

Your stories are the hooks that make your teaching stick. They’re the reason people trust you before they’ve ever even bought from you. They’re the evidence that you’ve actually been where your audience is right now.

Your Origin Story is a major foundation of your inventory, don’t get me wrong. But a character who only has one story they tell the same way every time isn’t going to be attractive for long. Once your audience has heard your origin story enough times to know it by heart, retelling it stops building the relationship and only maintains it at best. The people whose audiences keep growing year after year without the relationship ever going stale don’t get that by telling the same story over and over again. They get that level of engagement by constantly adding to a vast treasure trove of stories they can draw from. Each story reaches a slightly different person at a slightly different moment.

Your Story Inventory should be a living and breathing resource. Not just a list of memories you come up with one day and archive forever. The Six Core Story Arcs: Framing Stories for Effective Delivery By this point, if you’ve done the work, your Story Inventory has probably expanded quite a bit. You might have noticed that several of these stories follow certain patterns.

People are often afraid that these patterns are clichés. But I have good news for you: people respond best to patterns they’re familiar with. Good vs. evil. Redemption. Victory against all odds. Those aren’t clichés. They are the foundational plots that humans have been responding to for thousands of years. You don’t need to reinvent the wheel. Just lean into the six core story arcs that connect with people on a deep, subconscious level.

Arc #1 - Loss and Redemption The loss-and-redemption arc begins with success. Something you had or achieved, and how you lost it completely before finding your way back to something better on the other side. The specific power of this arc is that it reaches the person who is currently in loss.

Not someone who has never succeeded. Someone who had something and watched it disappear. Someone who is struggling with the despair of knowing what it felt like to have the thing and no longer having it. That person is unreachable by success stories. They’ve already had success. They know it’s possible. What they need is evidence that the loss itself isn’t the end. That there’s a path that runs through the bottom rather than around it.

Rocky is a great example. Not because he wins. In the first film, he doesn’t. But because the journey through loss transforms him into someone new. The external loss leads to an internal gain. That structure makes loss-and-redemption stories feel honest rather than falsely optimistic. Your version of this arc doesn’t need a triumphant ending. It needs an honest accounting of what the bottom actually looked like and a genuine account of what you found there that you couldn’t have found without that loss.

Arc #2 - Us vs. Them This arc is really effective at building tribal identity. It rallies your audience around a sense of shared purpose. It turns casual followers into raving fans. It strengthens the polarity element we talked about in Secrets #4 and #5. It sets you up to build a mass movement, not just a group of people you sell to.



<!-- Page 55 -->



The “us” is not just your customers, but also people who haven’t found their way to you just yet. It’s a new way of thinking, a set of values, a solution to a problem. The “them” is not your competitors. It’s the wrong belief system. The conventional wisdom. The lazy approach. The thing everyone does because “that’s how it’s always been done.” The thing your audience has already tried that didn’t work.

The mistake people make with us-vs.-them is making it about specific people or specific companies rather than about ideas. Attacking a competitor by name is small and comes off as insecure. The enemy is the bad idea, not the people who sell it. That framing is more durable, more honest, and far more effective at creating the sense of shared identity that turns an audience into a community.

Arc #3 - Before and After This arc is both the simplest one of all six and the one that requires the most skill to deliver well. A before-and-after story is nothing more than contrast, but the quality of the contrast makes or breaks how it lands.

Many before-and-after stories fail when these two elements aren’t given equal weight. The “before” is too vague or too sanitized. It gestures broadly at something instead of getting into the specifics that create emotional connection. Meanwhile all the attention goes on the “after,” the result, the lifestyle, the transformation. The fix is to balance the two. The more clearly your audience sees themselves in the before, the more powerfully the after lands. Great stories are built on contrast. Light vs. dark, before vs. after, the life you have vs. the life you want. Your audience is investing their time and money to bridge that gap.

Arc #4 - Amazing Discovery People are wired to pay attention to discoveries. Our brains see new information as important to our survival. It works wonders in marketing as much as it did when our distant ancestors discovered how to control fire. The amazing-discovery arc follows a simple structure: you went looking for something and discovered something you didn’t expect. What you found changes the way you, and by extension your audience, look at a specific problem.

Framing that discovery as “I found this for you” comes off as honest and generous. It’s a great tool for eliminating resistance when closing a presentation with your offer without coming off as salesy or pushy. You’re not selling. You’re reporting. This is the natural arc for the Adventurer/Crusader and Reluctant Hero character types, but any character can



<!-- Page 56 -->



use it because the underlying psychology is universal. Arc #5 - Revealing a Secret This is one of my favorite story arcs. I wrote the Secrets trilogy. Every chapter in those books is called a “Secret.” Same with the book you’re reading now. Being told a secret satisfies people’s curiosity. And being given access to exclusive knowledge gives people a sense of increased status. They discovered something known by few, not widely understood, running underneath the surface of what everyone can see. Knowing the thing puts you in a different category from people who don’t know it.

But this arc isn’t always done right. “The secret the gurus don’t want you to know about” often turns out to be something every guru talks about freely. Not necessarily confidential.

Not necessarily suppressed. But it has to run against what most people are operating on in your market. It has to be somewhat counter-intuitive, and yet make total sense when you present it. When done right, the effects are lasting.

Arc #6 - Third-Party Testimonials Some stories are great when told by the protagonist, straight from the horse’s mouth. But sometimes they’re more effective when delivered as third-person narratives. Appearing more neutral can be a great way to build trust.

Here’s what she tried. Here’s the moment things shifted. Here’s where she is now.” Most people make the mistake of only using testimonials as social proof. The thing is, not everyone is going to relate to your experience. They may hear your story and think, that’s not quite me. You multiply the chances that your audience will emotionally relate to someone who found success with your teachings. Other people’s stories might have different starting points and different sets of circumstances than your own. But they still got the end result all the same.

The Epiphany Bridge: Your Belief System and New Opportunity Most people who’ve studied the Epiphany Bridge have learned it as a story structure. Five phases. Fourteen questions. Backstory, journey, new opportunity, framework, achievement. They know the arc. They can label the stages. What most of them haven’t done is understand what the structure is actually for. And until you understand that, the five phases are just a sophisticated way to tell a story that may or may not do the work you need it to do.

The Epiphany Bridge is not a storytelling tool. It’s a belief-change tool. The story is the delivery mechanism. The actual function is to take a specific false belief your audience is carrying and replace it with a new belief that makes taking action feel not just possible but obvious. Do that successfully and the objection dissolves before you’ve even addressed it directly, because you’ve changed the belief the objection was built on. Fail to do it, tell a technically perfect five-phase story aimed at the wrong belief, or the right belief but in the wrong sequence, and you can tell the most compelling story of your life to an audience that stays exactly where they were when you started.

This chapter is about the belief architecture that sits underneath the Epiphany Bridge, the map you need to draw before you write a single story, because the map tells you which belief to target, in what order, with what kind of story. It also covers the New Opportunity framework, which is the belief-level reason why some characters build movements and others build audiences that plateau. These two systems, the belief map and the new opportunity, are the hidden infrastructure of every great Attractive Character at scale. The character is the face. These are the bones.

The Belief Map: Why Stories Fail When the Target Is Wrong When I was backstage at Grant Cardone’s 10X event, preparing to present to nine thousand people, I went in with a clear story I’d told hundreds of times. What I didn’t have was a complete map of all the beliefs that audience was carrying about my subject, not just the big central belief I was trying to change, but the full landscape of every objection,



<!-- Page 60 -->



every resistance point, every prior experience that had shaped how they thought about the opportunity I was about to present.

What I didn’t know until afterward, when my friend drove me home and I watched from the back seat as he fired off Epiphany Bridge story after story for ninety straight minutes, busting fifty objections I hadn’t touched, tripling our sales in the time it took me to double them, was that the central story isn’t enough. You can knock down the biggest belief obstacle your audience faces and then watch them construct three new ones out of the rubble. Unless you’ve mapped the whole landscape and prepared for all of it, you’re always one objection away from losing the person you just spent an hour winning.

The belief map has a specific structure. Your audience’s false beliefs cluster into three categories, and they almost always arrive in this sequence: first comes the belief about the vehicle, whether the thing you’re offering is actually the right approach to the result they want. Second comes the belief about themselves, whether they specifically, with their specific limitations, circumstances, and history of failures, are capable of executing on the vehicle even if they accept it’s the right one. Third comes the belief about external forces, whether something outside their control will prevent them from succeeding even if they believe in the vehicle and in themselves. All three categories need an Epiphany Bridge story, and they need to arrive in that order, because each one only becomes the dominant objection after the previous one has been resolved.

The vehicle false belief is the one most characters prepare for. The internal and external beliefs are the ones that silently kill conversions after people have already said yes in principle. Here’s what those two categories actually look like in practice.

Internal false beliefs sound like: I believe this works for people who are more technical than me. I believe this works for people who already have an audience. I believe this works for people who don’t have a full-time job and three kids and no extra money to invest in ads. Notice what all of these have in common: they accept the vehicle completely and locate the problem entirely inside the person. The audience member has already been sold on the concept. What they don’t believe is that the concept applies to their specific version of life. This is where most sales are actually lost, not in the pitch, but in the private conversation the prospect has with themselves about whether the result you achieved is available to someone like them specifically. The Epiphany Bridge story that dissolves this belief has one job: to take the person further back in your own timeline to a version of yourself who was in an even worse position than they’re describing and show them exactly how the same journey played out from that starting point.



<!-- Page 61 -->



External false beliefs sound like: I believe this works and I believe I’m capable of it, but my market is too saturated. My niche is too small. The algorithm changed. The economy is wrong for this right now. My geographic location makes it harder. These are the beliefs that operate furthest from direct logic, because they’re about forces beyond anyone’s control, which means they’re the hardest to dislodge with argument. The Epiphany Bridge story for an external false belief works by finding a person who faced the exact external obstacle your audience is describing and succeeded anyway, not despite the obstacle, but by finding a path that rendered the obstacle irrelevant. The goal isn’t to argue that the external obstacle isn’t real. It’s to show that the right framework navigates around it in a way that makes worrying about it unnecessary.

The practical work here: for your primary offer, list every false belief your audience carries about the vehicle, about their own ability, and about external forces. Don’t cap the list.

Write until you’ve genuinely exhausted the category. Then rank each list by how commonly you encounter that belief and how much it actually stops people from moving forward. The top belief in each category needs its own Epiphany Bridge story. The rest of the list needs at least a thirty-second version. Because you will encounter all of them eventually, and the person asking the question you haven’t prepared for is the one who walks away.

The Big Domino: The One Belief That Unlocks Everything Else Belief maps can get complicated fast. You can end up with thirty distinct false beliefs spread across the three categories, each one technically requiring its own story, and the whole thing starts to feel like an infinite regression of objections with no clear starting point. The way out of that complexity is the Big Domino, the single core belief that, once established, makes every other objection either self-resolving or trivially easy to address.

The Big Domino statement follows a specific template: If I can make people believe that [new opportunity] is the key to [result they desire most] and is only attainable through [my specific vehicle/framework], then all other objections and concerns become irrelevant and they have no choice but to move forward. The reason this works is that the logical structure is genuinely true: if someone fully believes the premise, the conclusion follows automatically. You don’t have to argue for the conclusion. The belief in the premise produces it.

When I built ClickFunnels, the Big Domino was: if I can make people believe that sales funnels are the key to growing any business online and that ClickFunnels is the only



<!-- Page 62 -->



practical way to build them, then someone who holds that belief has no real alternative but to use ClickFunnels. Every story I told in every presentation was aimed at that domino.

Once it fell, once someone genuinely believed that funnels were the mechanism and that ClickFunnels was the vehicle, the internal and external objections shrank to manageable size, because they were no longer objections to the concept. They were just logistics. And logistics are solvable.

The mistake people make when building their Big Domino statement is creating one that’s actually an improvement claim rather than a new opportunity. The statement if I can make people believe that reducing calorie intake and increasing exercise is the key to sustainable weight loss and that my program is the best way to do it fails as a Big Domino because it doesn’t fall. No matter how hard you hit it, the audience has heard that statement in seventeen other forms from seventeen other experts, and every new version of it confirms what they already suspect: that this is the same thing repackaged.

The domino that falls is the one that offers genuinely different territory. And that brings us to the most important structural concept in this chapter.

New Opportunity vs. Improvement Offer: The Belief-Level Distinction There’s a belief-level reason why new opportunities outsell improvement offers that has nothing to do with the quality of the pitch or the caliber of the storytelling, and most people who understand the surface distinction between these two categories have never thought about it this clearly.

When you present an improvement offer, a better version of something your audience has already tried, the first thing that happens in their mind is an accounting of their previous attempts. Every diet they tried. Every marketing course they bought. Every relationship advice book they read. The improvement offer forces them to engage that history, because accepting your offer requires accepting that the previous things they tried were wrong and that this time the improvement will actually work. That accounting is painful. It requires admitting failure. It requires the prospect to believe they can distinguish between an improvement that will work and the improvements that didn’t, when they don’t have a reliable track record of making that distinction correctly. The status cost of being wrong again, after all the previous times, is enormous. And so the improvement offer, even when it’s genuinely better, is swimming upstream against the prospect’s entire history of disappointment with similar things.



<!-- Page 63 -->



A new opportunity bypasses that history completely. The prospect doesn’t have to admit the previous things were wrong. They were right for what they were. They just weren’t this.

There’s no pain of disconnect, no admission of failure, no renegotiation with a history of poor choices. Instead there’s discovery, the psychological state of encountering something genuinely new, which carries its own immediate status reward. Sharing a new discovery increases perceived status. Being among the first to understand something increases perceived status. The new opportunity doesn’t ask the prospect to tear down their existing self-image. It offers them an upgraded one. That’s a fundamentally different emotional experience, and it’s why new opportunities generate the levels of enthusiasm that improvement offers almost never can.

The question is how you create one. The mechanism is the opportunity switch, a reframing of the result your audience wants in terms of a new vehicle rather than a better version of the current one. Your audience knows the result they want. What they’ve been using to get there isn’t working. Rather than offering them a more effective version of what isn’t working, you introduce an entirely new vehicle for achieving the same result, one they don’t have a history of failure with, one they can approach with genuine fresh hope rather than cautious skepticism.

When I was positioning ClickFunnels, the vehicle my audience had been using was websites. The improvement offer would have been a better website builder, faster, more customizable, better templates. The opportunity switch was to tell them that websites were the wrong vehicle entirely. That the website-centric approach to online business was built on a flawed premise about how people make buying decisions online. That funnels, a completely different architecture for guiding someone from first contact to purchase, were not a better website but an entirely different thing with different mechanics and different results. The person who accepted that switch didn’t have to admit their website was bad. They could accept that websites are fine for what they are and that funnels are a new category serving a different purpose. No history of failure to overcome. New territory to explore.

Identifying your opportunity switch requires starting from the result your audience wants and working backward to all the vehicles they’ve already tried. List them. Then ask: is there a genuinely different mechanism for achieving that result, one that doesn’t share the failure modes of the vehicles on the list? If there is, that mechanism is your new opportunity. Your job is to name it, build a frame around why the previous vehicles were structurally limited rather than simply less good, and establish your vehicle as the one that solves the problem the previous vehicles couldn’t solve by design.

The Attractive Character as the New Opportunity’s Guide Here’s the connection between everything in this chapter and the Attractive Character specifically, the piece that explains why belief architecture is in a book about character rather than in a book about sales scripts.

Your Dogma: The Belief System That Becomes a Movement Every movement that has ever lasted longer than a trend has had one thing underneath it that made it more than a collection of interested people: a doctrine. A set of beliefs so specific, so clearly articulated, and so deeply owned by the people inside the movement that new members could learn them, recite them, and filter their decisions through them without needing the leader present. The LadyBosses do not just follow Kaelin Poulin’s workouts. They have adopted a specific identity and a specific way of thinking about what a strong woman does and does not do. The people inside Dan Kennedy’s world do not just read his newsletters. They have absorbed a worldview, about how to market, how to think about customers, how to treat your time, that makes them recognizable to each other even when they have never met.

That worldview, that doctrine, that specific set of beliefs that defines who is inside the movement and what they believe, that is what the Attractive Character calls their dogma. And building it is one of the most important and most overlooked parts of constructing a character that creates a genuine movement rather than just an audience.

An audience watches. A movement acts. The difference between the two is almost always dogma. Dogma is not a list of your opinions. It is not your brand voice guide or your content pillars or your unique selling proposition. It is the deep structure of belief that underlies everything you say and everything you teach, the specific worldview that your Attractive Character carries and transmits, story by story and email by email and video by video, until your followers have absorbed it so completely that they begin to see the world through your framework rather than their old one. When that happens, they do not just buy from you. They belong to something. And belonging is the most durable form of loyalty that exists.

The One Belief That Changes Everything At the center of every piece of dogma is a single foundational belief, the one that, once a person accepts it, makes everything else in your worldview follow logically. I learned this principle from studying the structure of great sales presentations, and I gave it a name: the Big Domino. The idea is simple and devastating in its clarity. Every objection your potential followers or buyers might have is really just a smaller domino standing upright.

And all those smaller dominos stay upright as long as the big one is still standing. But if you can knock down the one central belief, the Big Domino, every other objection, concern, and hesitation falls on its own, because none of them can stand once the core belief has been broken and replaced.

The ClickFunnels Big Domino we covered in the previous chapter is the cleanest illustration of this: once someone genuinely believed that funnels were the key mechanism for online business success, every competitor argument and every price objection became irrelevant logistics. The central belief produced the conclusion automatically, without requiring the smaller dominoes to be addressed individually.

The formula for finding your own Big Domino is this: If I can make people believe that [my new opportunity] is the key to [the result they desire most] and is only attainable through [my specific vehicle or framework], then all other objections and concerns become irrelevant and they have no choice but to come with me. That structure is not a sales script. It is a test of whether your dogma is built on a genuine new opportunity or just a marginal improvement on something that already exists. If your Big Domino statement does not hold up under scrutiny, if someone could get the same result through a dozen other available options, then you do not yet have a new opportunity, and your dogma will not stick the way it needs to.

The reason the formula requires a new opportunity rather than an improvement is one of the deepest insights in the entire framework. Improvement offers are psychologically treacherous, because they require your potential follower to admit that what they have been doing was insufficient. That admission carries status risk. If I tell you that my program will help you lose weight better than the diet you have been on, I am implying that your diet was inadequate, and by extension, so were you for choosing it. That implication creates resistance, even when the person consciously wants what you are offering. A new opportunity sidesteps that trap entirely. You are not saying their old approach was wrong. You are saying that something new exists that they could not have known about, and that this new thing opens possibilities the old approach was never designed to reach. The status dynamics flip completely. Accepting a new opportunity



<!-- Page 68 -->



makes someone feel forward-looking and smart. Accepting an improvement offer requires them to feel behind.

What You Stand For - and What You Stand Against The Big Domino tells you what your dogma believes in. But dogma without opposition is inert. Every movement that has created genuine loyalty, religious, political, commercial, has been defined as much by what it stands against as by what it stands for. The Attractive Character who refuses to draw a line in the sand, who tries to be agreeable to everyone and inoffensive to all, does not create a movement. They create content. People can appreciate content without following it. They follow movements because movements demand a choice, and choosing activates identity in a way that passive consumption never can.

John Alanis described this mechanism as standing in advocacy, taking a position on the things that matter within your domain and refusing to move from it regardless of who objects. His own position was built around self-reliance: the belief that personal responsibility is the foundation of success in every area of life, and that the cultural trend toward external blame was not just wrong but actively harmful to the people he was trying to serve. When the media ran stories about bullying, he did not ignore them or treat them as irrelevant to his topic. He connected them to his central doctrine. A kid who learns to handle a bully at twelve is building the same muscle he will need when the IRS sends a letter at forty. Every cultural moment became material for reinforcing the worldview, and the worldview was consistent enough that his audience always knew exactly where he stood.

I made the same mechanism visible through the language of throwing rocks at the enemy. The enemy is not a person. It is the old way, the old vehicle, the old belief system, the old structure that your movement is offering people an escape from. For ClickFunnels, the enemy was the venture-capital-backed competitors who answered to their board of directors instead of their customers, who made decisions based on investor returns rather than entrepreneur outcomes. Throwing rocks at that enemy was not petty or unprofessional. It was a clarifying act that gave the Funnel Hackers a specific thing to stand against, which sharpened and deepened what they stood for. The enemy does not need to be a company. It can be an idea, the idea that you need a technical team to build a website, the idea that weight loss requires suffering, the idea that marketing is manipulation. What matters is that the enemy is real, that your people recognize it as the thing that has been standing between them and the result they want, and that your dogma offers a clear alternative.

The practical exercise is to sit with two questions until the answers are specific enough to matter. What do you believe so strongly about your domain that you are willing to say it out loud and lose some people over it? And what do you believe has been the lie, the false promise, the broken vehicle, the wrong approach, that your movement exists to replace? The answers to those two questions are the spine of your dogma. Everything else hangs on them.

Parables: The Stories That Carry the Doctrine Dogma transmitted as abstract principle does not travel. Dogma transmitted as story travels everywhere, lasts for generations, and creates the kind of emotional understanding that makes a belief feel personally discovered rather than externally imposed. Every religious tradition worth studying understood this intuitively, which is why virtually every durable belief system in human history has been carried primarily by stories, not by propositions. The parables do not illustrate the doctrine after the fact. The parables are the doctrine, in the form that the human mind was designed to receive and retain.

I learned this from watching Dan Kennedy operate. In any conversation, on any topic, Kennedy could reach into his story inventory and pull out a parable that made the point more precisely and memorably than any direct instruction could. Ask him about pricing, he has a story. Ask him about client selection, he has a story. Ask him about the psychology of deadlines, he has a story. Not because Kennedy is an unusually gifted storyteller by nature, but because he had done the work of explicitly mapping his doctrine to his stories and building a library deep enough to cover every situation he was likely to encounter.

The practical mechanism I recommend is the same two-column Story Inventory document we built in Secret #6. On the left side: the story itself, the time you did a thing, the moment something happened, the specific scene with a specific detail that makes it real and retrievable. On the right side: the goal of the story, the false belief it breaks, the principle it illustrates, the emotional truth it delivers. The left column is the vehicle. The right column is the destination. Together they create something you can pull from on demand, whether you are writing an email, delivering a presentation, answering a question on a live video, or talking to a reporter. The story that does not get written down is a story that may never get told, because the human mind does not reliably surface the right material under pressure the way a well-organized library does. Write them down.

Map them. Build the inventory, and keep adding to it every time life generates new material worth preserving.

Dan Kennedy’s most famous parables are often mundane, a conversation in a parking lot, a decision about a horse farm, a client who refused good advice and paid for it. You teach the what through a framework. You teach the why through a parable. And the why, once it lands, is the thing the person actually changes their mind about.

Insider Language: The Vocabulary of Belonging The final layer of dogma is the one that most people building an Attractive Character underestimate until they see it working: insider language. Every subculture, every community, every movement that has real cohesion has its own vocabulary, words and phrases that mean something specific to insiders and signal membership to anyone who uses them correctly. The language is not just shorthand. It is identity infrastructure. When someone uses the right word, they are not just communicating information efficiently.

They are announcing which side of the line they are standing on, and they are signaling to everyone else on that side that they belong.

In the Funnel Hacker world, the language is thick and specific. People do not have websites, they have funnels. They do not have email lists, they have traffic they own. They do not have upsells, they have one-time offers. They do not test their marketing, they funnel hack. A new person encountering that vocabulary for the first time feels the gap between inside and outside acutely. The language does the sorting automatically, without anyone having to explicitly draw the boundary.

Dan Kennedy used the same mechanism deliberately and memorably. He was a horse person, and when he talked about building an audience he did not call it list building. He called it herd building. The metaphor carried the worldview: the herd is yours, you manage it, you feed it, you know every animal in it, and you do not let strangers scatter it.

That one word choice encoded an entire philosophy of audience management into a term so vivid that anyone who heard it once remembered it and used it themselves. That is the goal with insider language, not just to create jargon, but to create vocabulary that carries your doctrine inside it, so that every time someone uses your terms they are also thinking inside your framework.

Creating your insider language is not a branding exercise you do in an afternoon and then file away. It is something that develops organically as your character finds the words that are most natural to your way of seeing your domain, and then commits to using those words consistently enough that your audience learns them and begins using them too. Watch for the moments when you coin a phrase that people repeat back to you. Watch for the metaphors that generate recognition rather than puzzlement. Those are the seeds of your vocabulary, and every time someone outside your movement encounters them and asks what they mean, you have a teaching moment that also serves as a recruiting moment.

Putting the Doctrine Together Dogma is not a document you write once and then refer to. It is a living system that you demonstrate through your stories, reinforce through your language, deepen through your parables, and clarify through your willingness to take a stand when taking a stand costs you something. The Attractive Character who has fully articulated their dogma knows exactly what they believe and why, knows exactly what they are replacing and what they are offering instead, has a library of stories that can carry every dimension of that belief to any audience in any context, and speaks in a vocabulary so consistent and specific that their people recognize them instantly across every medium.

The exercise is to get the four elements on paper explicitly, before you need them. Start with the Big Domino: what is the one belief that, if your audience truly accepts it, makes everything else you offer feel not just desirable but necessary? Then articulate what you stand for and what you stand against, not vaguely, but specifically enough to lose some people over. Then begin mapping your parables: what are the ten or twenty stories already in your life that can carry your doctrine across the full range of situations you will face? And finally, start paying attention to the language your movement uses and the language it should use, the words and metaphors that, once adopted by your audience, will mark them as insiders and carry your worldview inside them everywhere they go.

When those four elements are in place and working together, the Attractive Character stops being a person your audience follows and becomes a belief system your audience has adopted. And a belief system travels in a way that no individual ever can, through conversations, through the words people use to describe themselves, through the way they explain your work to their friends before you have ever spoken to those friends directly. That is what dogma builds. That is why it sits at the foundation of every movement that has ever lasted.

The Influencer can have enormous reach and engagement. What they often lack is the specific professional credibility that makes someone think: this person can help me get what they have. Lots of followers, low conversion. The gap is knowledge, it’s simply not present in sufficient quantity at the base of what the audience sees.

The Professor leads with knowledge, but only knowledge, with methodology pushed to the periphery and real life absent entirely. The audience understands that this person is



<!-- Page 83 -->



genuinely expert. What they can’t see is how to use what they’re being taught, because the Professor almost never shows it working in a real context. The audience loves the content and doesn’t buy, because buying would require believing they could execute on what they’re learning, and the Professor has given them no model of execution to believe in.

The Debater leads with industry beliefs. Their audience watches with something between fascination and caution, they tune in because the takes are interesting and the rocks are entertaining, but they wouldn’t trust the Debater with their business because someone who throws that many rocks publicly might throw rocks at them next. The Debater earns attention and loses trust. Engagement stays high. Relationships stay shallow.

The Customer Magnet, the configuration that actually builds a business, is the character who leads with knowledge, backs it with grounded beliefs, demonstrates it through methodology, shows all of it living in a real life, and lets personal beliefs emerge naturally as context. That character is what the audience reads as a thought leader: someone who knows the domain, has a reasoned point of view on it, can show you how to do the thing, and is actually doing it themselves. When you encounter that person, you trust them.

When you trust them, you follow them. When you follow them long enough, you buy from them, not because they sold you, but because trusting them and not buying from them would be leaving something on the table.

The Soap Opera Sequence: Building the Bridge for New Subscribers Every person who joins your list for the first time is a stranger who signed up because something caught their attention, an ad, a piece of content, a referral from someone they trust. They don’t know you yet. They don’t know your story, don’t know your voice, haven’t made the connection between the interest that got them to subscribe and the character they’re going to be in a relationship with. The Soap Opera Sequence is the mechanism that closes that gap, the sequence of communications that transforms a new subscriber into someone who knows your Attractive Character and has made an initial judgment about whether they want to keep listening.

The name comes from what the sequence is trying to accomplish structurally: just like a soap opera uses open loops, unresolved storylines that pull viewers from episode to episode and season to season, the sequence uses each email to open a loop that can only be closed by reading the next one. The goal is not to deliver information that earns a subscription. The goal is to build enough character rapport that the person wants to keep reading you specifically, not just content in your category.



<!-- Page 84 -->



Here’s where most people get it wrong: they treat the Soap Opera Sequence as a content delivery mechanism and build five emails around the information value they want to establish. What actually needs to happen is emotional work. The first email sets the stage, it tells the subscriber what’s coming and hooks them with an open storyline that creates a reason to check tomorrow’s email. The second email opens with high drama, because the deepest character connection forms fastest when the audience enters your story at a moment of genuine tension rather than at the polished beginning. Your failures, your worst moment, the day everything went wrong, that’s where most people’s real emotional connection to your character forms, not at your success story.

The third email introduces the epiphany, the moment things changed for you, told in the same Epiphany Bridge structure from Secret #7. The fourth email reveals hidden benefits: not the benefits your audience already knows about, but the unexpected ways this has played out in your life and others’ that they wouldn’t have predicted going in. The fifth email adds urgency, a real, non-manufactured reason to take the next step now rather than later.

The sequence moves through a specific emotional arc: emotion first, then logic, then urgency. This sequence isn’t arbitrary. People buy emotionally before they buy rationally, and they need to feel the emotional case before they’re receptive to the logical one. An email that leads with logic before the emotional connection is established is the right argument at the wrong time, technically sound, functionally wasted. Get the emotional close first. Follow it with the logical case. Then activate urgency for the people who are persuaded but haven’t yet committed to action. Different buyers respond to different closes, and the sequence is designed to reach all three groups in the order they’re most likely to engage.

Daily Seinfeld Emails: The Infinite Content Engine Ben Settle has emailed his list every day for over a decade. One product. One core offer. A ninety-seven-dollar print newsletter that hasn’t changed in price or format in all that time. And every single day, a new email with a new hook and a new story that points back to that same product. What Ben figured out, and what I internalized when my content-packed emails started underperforming relative to emails about nothing, is that your audience is not waiting for new information from you. They already trust you. What they’re waiting for is their next installment of you, the next story, the next angle on the world, the next thing that makes them think: that’s exactly how I see it, or, I never thought of it that way, but now I can’t unthink it.



<!-- Page 85 -->



The Hook, Story, Offer structure is the engine that makes this work at scale. The subject line is the hook, the one thing that makes this email impossible to leave unread. Not a subject line that describes the content (‘Five Tips for Better Marketing’) but one that opens a loop the mind can’t close without opening the email (‘I almost got fired for this’ or ‘The most embarrassing thing I’ve ever mailed’). The hook is the only real variable in the equation every day, and it’s a high-leverage variable because a great hook on an ordinary story gets opened, and a great story with a weak hook dies in the inbox unread.

The body story is where the Attractive Character lives. The story doesn’t need to be dramatic or unusual. It needs to be specific, told from a genuine point of view, and connected to the offer in a way that feels natural rather than forced. Stories pull from three zones: episode style (something that happened today or recently in your life, told with the Attractive Character’s voice and worldview), epiphany style (an insight or realization that shifts how you see something, offered as a genuine discovery rather than a lesson), and educational style (a specific how-to or answer to a question your audience is asking, deployed in story format so it lands as lived experience rather than instruction). All three styles work. The key is that each one uses the story to do its work rather than making the information carry the weight alone.

The offer at the end is not a pitch. It’s a natural extension of where the story went, a door the reader can walk through if the story landed. The goal of the offer is not to convert every reader on every email. It’s to be present and available so that the reader who is ready on a given day finds the door open. Ben Settle sending the same offer every day for a decade isn’t laziness. It’s an understanding that his list turns over, that readers arrive at readiness at different times, and that a consistent offer seen by a ready reader is worth more than an elaborate launch seen by a reader who isn’t there yet.

The Personality Pyramid as a Content Calendar The practical question every character faces after understanding the pyramid is: how do I actually translate a ratio framework into a production system? The answer is to build the pyramid explicitly into the calendar. Not as a vague aspiration, as actual slot allocation.

Start with your total content output for a given month, however many emails, videos, posts you’re committing to across all channels. Then apportion those slots in pyramid ratio. The majority go to industry knowledge: demonstrations of specific expertise, breakdowns of how something works, answers to questions your audience is asking that require genuine domain knowledge to answer well. A smaller number go to industry



<!-- Page 86 -->



beliefs: your positions on what’s right and wrong in your space, the things you’d tell someone to stop doing, the conventional wisdom you’d dismantle if you could. A smaller number still go to methodology: your frameworks in action, the specific processes you teach, before-and-after demonstrations of the approach being applied. Real-life slots show your work in progress, the thing you’re building right now, the outcome from this week, the experiment and its result. Personal belief content is the smallest allocation and mostly lives in the informal layer: the aside in a video, the aside in an email, the Instagram story where you’re clearly you and not performing expertise.

The pain and dream bank from Secret #9 feeds the knowledge tier: every pain your audience is carrying is a knowledge topic waiting to be addressed, and every dream they have is a proof-of-concept topic waiting to be demonstrated. The personality pyramid gives you the ratio. The pain and dream bank gives you the specific subjects. Together they produce a content calendar that is self-generating, not because you’re grinding out content from obligation, but because the system tells you what your audience needs and how much of each type they need, and you simply fill the slots from the material you’ve already excavated.

The multiplication layer is what turns a good content calendar into an almost inexhaustible one. When a piece of content performs, when an email gets an unusually high open rate, when a video generates an unusually high share count, when a post sparks a conversation that runs deeper than anything you expected, that content is not done. That content is a proof of concept. It tells you that a specific hook, a specific angle, a specific story is resonating at a level worth pursuing further. The multiplication step takes that top performer and asks: what other formats can carry this? What other angles does this open? What adjacent topics does the engagement signal that my audience would receive equally well? A single high-performing piece of content, properly mined, can generate five to ten additional pieces that each have a higher prior probability of performing because they’re all downstream of something that already proved the audience wanted it.

The Daily Practice: Turning Your Life Into Character Material Everything described in this chapter, the pyramid ratio, the email structures, the content calendar, the multiplication system, is a mechanical system that requires fuel. The fuel is the character’s life, continuously converted into stories. And the discipline that keeps the fuel flowing is the daily practice: the habit of moving through the world with the part of your brain that asks is there a story in this? switched on.



<!-- Page 87 -->



Instagram Stories became, for me, the best tool for building this habit, not because of the platform specifically, but because of the format’s demands. Fifteen-second clips that disappear in twenty-four hours. No editing, no production value required, no pressure to produce something worth archiving. Just documentation of the day as it’s happening: the thought you had in the car, the observation that struck you during the workout, the thing that went wrong in the meeting and what you did about it, the book you’re reading and the sentence that stopped you cold. Every one of those is potential character material. Not all of them will become full emails or videos or polished posts. Most of them are just evidence that you’re paying attention, and an audience that watches you pay attention to your life eventually starts paying attention to it too.

The daily practice is also where voice gets refined. The more you produce, particularly in the informal, low-stakes format of stories and quick posts, the more your genuine voice asserts itself over the edited, acceptable version. You start to notice which phrases you reach for consistently. You start to hear the rhythm of your own expression. You start to develop the shorthand expressions and recurring references that become the insider language of your community. None of that happens through planning. All of it happens through volume, and volume only accumulates through daily practice.

The characters who built lasting audiences, the ones whose content hasn’t gotten old after five or ten years in the market, almost universally share one trait: they’re genuinely interested in their own lives as source material. Not narcissistic about it, not performing it, but actually curious about the stories their experiences contain and committed to the daily work of surfacing them. That curiosity is not a personality type you either have or don’t. It’s a practice you build. And the moment you stop treating content production as an obligation to fulfill and start treating it as documentation of a life that’s genuinely interesting, even when the individual moments are small and ordinary, is the moment the machine stops feeling like work and starts running on something that can’t be exhausted.

## Secret #11

Business Integration: Advertising, Funnels, Offer Alignment, and Your Character Arc Most people who understand the Attractive Character think of it as a content and relationship strategy, the warm, human layer that sits alongside their business and makes people like them. Most people who understand funnels think of them as a sales architecture, the sequence of pages and offers that converts attention into revenue. And most people who run paid ads think of them as a traffic acquisition system, the mechanism for getting strangers in front of their funnel. These three things are treated as separate disciplines with separate teams, separate budgets, and separate success metrics. That separation is the most expensive mistake in the playbook.

The Attractive Character is not a content strategy. It is the core business asset that the content, the advertising, the funnels, and the offers all depend on to work. Every conversion that happens in any of those systems is happening because a person made a trust decision, and trust decisions are made about characters, not products. The funnel doesn’t close them. The character closes them. The ad doesn’t warm them up. The character warms them up. The offer doesn’t make them stay on the value ladder. The relationship with the character makes them stay. When you understand that sequence clearly, the integration question answers itself: the character doesn’t live alongside the business. The character is the business, and everything else, ads, funnels, offers, emails, is the infrastructure that delivers the character to the right person at the right moment in the relationship.

This chapter is about building that integration deliberately. It covers four specific pressure points where the character either strengthens or breaks the business result: ads, funnels, offer alignment, and the character arc across the value ladder. Each one has a way it usually gets done and a way it should get done, and the gap between those two approaches is almost always the gap between a business that grows predictably and one that grows in spurts and plateaus.



<!-- Page 89 -->



Advertising: The Character Showing Up for Strangers Cold traffic is the hardest test the Attractive Character faces. When someone in your email list gets a Daily Seinfeld Email from you, they already know you, they have a context for your voice, a sense of your story, a prior judgment that you’re worth reading. When a stranger scrolls past your ad on a platform where they didn’t come to buy anything and owe you nothing, you have approximately three seconds to create a context that didn’t previously exist and a reason for them to give you any attention at all. The Hook, Story, Offer framework is the architecture for doing that, but what most people miss is that the hook and story layers are not primarily about the product. They are primarily about the character.

A hook is the thing that stops the scroll. It can be a phrase, an image, an unexpected juxtaposition, a question that opens a loop the mind can’t close without watching another ten seconds. What makes a hook work is not cleverness. It’s specificity. The hook that speaks precisely to the interior monologue of one specific kind of person outperforms the hook that tries to speak to everyone. And the character who has done the excavation work from Parts One through Three already has the raw material for specific hooks, because they know their audience’s specific pains and specific dreams and specific language well enough to write the hook that sounds like it was written from inside that person’s head rather than pitched at them from the outside.

The story layer of the ad is where the character’s real work begins. The goal is not to explain the product. The goal is to create enough of a character connection in a brief window that the stranger makes a preliminary trust decision before they ever encounter an offer. In a sixty-second ad, that might mean thirty seconds of genuine story, the specific starting point, the specific failure, the specific moment of change, with the offer appearing only after the character has been established. The ad that leads with the offer and follows with the story has it backwards. The offer can only do its work once the character has earned the right to make it. Reversed, the offer lands on someone who has no context for why they should trust the person making it.

Here’s the insight that changes how you think about ad creative: your best ad is almost always a story your audience has already responded to in another context. The email that got an unusually high click rate. The Instagram Story that generated more DMs than anything you’d posted that month. The webinar moment where you could feel the room shift. Those moments are character-audience connections that already proved themselves at low cost, and the job of ad creative, at its most efficient, is to find those proven connection points and put them in front of the right cold audience. The character



<!-- Page 90 -->



who treats their organic content as a testing ground and their paid ads as a distribution channel for proven material is doing fundamentally smarter creative work than the character who tries to manufacture winning ad creative from scratch.

The retargeting layer is where the character deepens the relationship with people who have already shown interest. Someone who watched seventy-five percent of your video ad and then left without converting is not a failure. They’re a warm lead who needs more of the character before they’re ready for the offer. The retargeting sequence for that person should move them further down the same relationship arc the original ad started: more story, more specificity, more of the character’s genuine perspective, and then a more direct offer once the relationship has advanced far enough to support it. The cold-to-warm-to-hot progression in advertising mirrors the same progression in any relationship, and the character is the constant that makes the progression feel coherent rather than like a series of disconnected sales pitches.

Funnels: The Character Makes Every Promise the Funnel Keeps A funnel is a sequence of promises. The opt-in page promises that the thing on the other side of the form is worth the email address. The thank-you page promises that the next logical step is worth the attention. The sales page promises that the offer is worth the money. The upsell page promises that the additional investment will compound what’s already been bought. At every step, someone is deciding whether the character who made the previous promise delivered, and whether they trust that character enough to follow the next promise. This is why funnels that work for one character don’t work cleanly when grafted onto another, the promises in the copy were calibrated to a specific character relationship, and when the character changes, the promises no longer land the same way.

The most common funnel-character misalignment is the one that happens when the character in the ad is not the same character who shows up in the funnel. The ad does its job, the hook stops the scroll, the story builds a connection, the person clicks through, and then the funnel page presents a polished, corporate-sounding sales message that could have been written by anyone. The character who earned the click has evaporated, replaced by a generic sales environment that feels entirely disconnected from the thing the person clicked on. Trust, which was building, suddenly has nothing to attach to.

Conversion drops at the seam between the ad and the funnel, which is exactly where it shouldn’t.



<!-- Page 91 -->



The fix is character continuity across every surface. The language in the funnel copy should sound like the character who made the ad. The stories on the sales page should come from the same story bank as the stories in the emails and the content. The tone, the voice markers, the specific phrases that have become part of the character’s insider language, all of it should persist from the first ad impression through the final purchase confirmation, so the person moving through the funnel feels like they are deepening a single coherent relationship rather than being handed off between a charming marketer and an impersonal sales process.

The seven phases of a funnel, from traffic temperature assessment through qualifying subscribers and buyers, identifying hyperactive buyers, aging and ascending the relationship, and eventually changing the selling environment for high-ticket offers, are all character-relationship phases with commercial mechanics attached. Each phase asks the same underlying question: how much relationship depth does this person have with the character, and what level of commitment is appropriate to ask for at this depth?

A stranger who just clicked an ad can be asked for an email. A subscriber who has been through a Soap Opera Sequence can be asked for a small purchase. A buyer who has received value from the first offer can be asked for a larger one. The value ladder is not a pricing architecture. It is a relationship depth architecture, and the character is what creates the depth at every level.

Offer Alignment: When the Character and the Offer Tell the Same Story Here is the test for whether your offers are aligned with your character: does each offer feel like a natural extension of what your character is, a logical next step that someone who genuinely understands and trusts your character would be glad to find available? Or does each offer feel like a product that was built and then positioned around a character after the fact? The difference is felt instantly by any audience that has spent real time with your character, even if they can’t articulate why.

Offer alignment means that the promise of the offer is an exact extension of the promise the character has already been making through their content, their stories, and their polarity position. When I built ClickFunnels, the character I’d been building for years made one consistent promise: that there was a more effective architecture for selling online than the one most businesses were using, and that I had figured out what it was.

ClickFunnels as an offer was not a product I built and then attached my character to. It was the tangible version of the exact thing the character had been demonstrating was possible. The audience who’d been following the character didn’t need to be persuaded



<!-- Page 92 -->



that the product was relevant. They’d been waiting for it. The inverse of this is the offer that requires the character to step outside their established lane to make. The expert in the online marketing space who suddenly launches a physical supplement. The relationship coach who pivots to a financial planning course.

These moves can work, but they require rebuilding character trust in a new domain from scratch, the trust that was built in the original space doesn’t automatically transfer. The audience who followed the character into the new space will be the same ones who trusted the character enough to follow them anywhere. That’s a smaller group than the audience who trusted the character specifically in their established domain. The offer that lives cleanly inside what the character has already established as their territory closes that gap, no trust rebuild required, because the offer was always the logical destination of the journey the character was already on.

The value ladder, examined through this lens, is a sequence of progressively deeper versions of the same core character promise. The free content demonstrates the promise exists. The entry-level offer proves it delivers at a low-stakes commitment. The mid-tier offer delivers it at a level that requires real engagement. The high-ticket offer delivers it at the level where the character’s full expertise and relationship are brought to bear on a specific person’s specific situation. At each rung, the offer is asking for more investment, and getting it, because the relationship is deeper and the proof that the promise is real is more thoroughly established. Characters who understand this build value ladders as a single coherent promise at increasing depth, rather than as a collection of separate products that happen to share a brand.

The Character Arc Across the Value Ladder Here is the thing most people building long-term businesses never think about: your customer changes as they move up your value ladder. The person who bought your first book and the person who two years later is in your mastermind are not the same person, because your character’s guidance changed them. They know more. They’ve applied what you taught. They’ve gotten results, or they’ve gotten stuck in specific new ways that only emerge after the early results are in. The version of your character that was right for them at the beginning is not the version they need at the top of the ladder. And if your character doesn’t evolve with them, the relationship reaches a ceiling that’s lower than it needs to be.



<!-- Page 93 -->



The character arc across the value ladder has two dimensions. The first is depth of self-disclosure. At the entry level, your character is establishing initial credibility, enough backstory to create the empathy bridge, enough voice to be recognizable, enough polarity to attract the right people and repel the wrong ones. As the relationship deepens, the character can disclose more: the specific struggles that are still ongoing, the failures that didn’t make it into the origin story, the parts of the transformation that were messier and more ambiguous than the clean version suggests. Customers who have been with you for years don’t need the origin story again. They need to see who you are now and what you’re still figuring out. That ongoing disclosure is what keeps the relationship from calcifying into something transactional.

The second dimension is teaching depth. The character who has been sharing industry knowledge for three years with the same audience has, through that process, fundamentally changed what that audience knows. The knowledge tier of the Personality Pyramid that was establishing credentials at the beginning of the relationship is now the shared foundation. The audience’s false beliefs have largely been addressed. Their questions are more sophisticated. Their capacity for application is higher. The character who keeps teaching at the same level they established at the beginning is not growing the relationship, they’re maintaining it. And maintained relationships don’t ascend. The character arc means that the teaching deepens as the audience deepens, not just the intimacy.

Dan Kennedy said it clearly: we are all in a relationship business, not a product business. People may enter your value ladder because of a product, but they stay because of their relationship with you. What that means structurally is that the character arc is not a nice-to-have layer on top of the business architecture. It is the business architecture. The value ladder is just the commercial expression of the relationship arc, each rung represents a deeper level of commitment and trust between the character and the customer, and the investment at each level is the customer’s way of saying: I believe in what you’re building, I believe you can guide me further, and I want to go deeper with you.

The character who understands this doesn’t think about their business in terms of conversion rates and average cart value. They think about it in terms of relationship depth, and they build accordingly.

The Integration Audit: Finding Where the Character Is Leaking



<!-- Page 94 -->



Most businesses that have been running for more than a year have character leaks, places where the continuity breaks and the person moving through the funnel or the value ladder encounters a version of the character that doesn’t match the one they came for. These leaks are almost always invisible to the person running the business because they built everything themselves and can fill in the gaps with what they know about the character. The audience can’t fill in those gaps. They just feel the disconnection and often can’t explain why they stopped engaging.

The integration audit asks a simple question at every touchpoint: if someone encountered only this, only this ad, only this funnel page, only this email, only this offer page, would they have a clear, accurate sense of the character? Does the voice here sound like the same person who appears everywhere else? Does the story on this page come from the same story bank as the stories in the content? Does the offer feel like it was designed by someone who genuinely believes in the promise they’ve been making, or does it feel like a product that was priced and packaged and attached to a character as an afterthought?

Run that audit starting at the coldest touchpoint, the ad that reaches people who have never heard of you, and work your way to the warmest, the high-ticket offer page that’s seen only by people who have already invested significant time and money in the relationship. At each touchpoint, ask whether the character is fully present or partially present or absent entirely. The places where the character is absent or muffled are the places where conversion is leaking. And the fix is almost always simpler than it appears: bring the real voice back in, replace the generic copy with story, let the character’s genuine perspective on the offer be visible. Not a redesign of the funnel architecture. Just the character being present in the places they’ve been edited out of.

## Secret #12

Building a Movement, Not Just an Audience There is a version of everything we have covered in this book that is technically correct and completely insufficient. You can have a perfectly structured Attractive Character with a compelling backstory and well-developed flaws, a story inventory full of precisely built Epiphany Bridge stories, a future-based cause with a campaign slogan and milestone awards and a manifesto. You can have all of it, built to spec, and still produce something that the people inside it eventually leave because it never gave them the feeling they were actually looking for when they signed up. The feeling is not that they learned something valuable. It is not even that they belong to something meaningful. The feeling they are looking for is that they are part of something that matters, something worth the cost of the journey, including the cost of the hard parts.

That feeling cannot be manufactured from the outside in. It cannot be engineered through better positioning or more sophisticated content architecture. It comes from one source and one source only: the Attractive Character at the center of the movement actually being on a crusade. Not building a business that resembles a crusade. Not running campaigns that carry the aesthetic of a mission. An actual, personal, irreversible commitment to something that extends beyond any single product, any revenue milestone, any follower count, something they would be doing even if it were not making them money, because it is the thing they were put here to do.

Winston Churchill said something that I quote more than almost any other line when I talk to my Inner Circle about what it means to build a movement rather than just a business: there comes in every lifetime a special moment when a person is figuratively tapped on the shoulder and offered the chance to do a very special thing, unique to them and fitted to their talents. What a tragedy if that moment finds them unprepared or unqualified for what could have been their finest hour. The tap on the shoulder is not a metaphor for a career opportunity. It is a description of the calling that either gets answered or does not.

Most people do not answer it, not because the opportunity never comes, but because the voice of inadequacy that almost everyone carries is louder in that moment than the voice of the calling itself.



<!-- Page 96 -->



Your crusade is the answer to your tap on the shoulder. It is the reason your movement exists beyond the obvious transactional explanation. It is the articulation of what you are actually doing in the world through this work, stated in terms that have nothing to do with revenue or reach and everything to do with the specific transformation you have been called to create in the specific people you have been called to serve.

The Shift That Creates the Crusade The arc that every genuine expert travels on the way to building a movement with staying power is not from success to more success. It is from growth to contribution. The first phase is entirely about personal development, getting obsessed with a topic, consuming every book and podcast and interview available, running experiments, studying the best practitioners, failing a lot, and gradually building a body of knowledge earned through direct experience rather than borrowed from secondary sources. This phase can go on for years and it is necessary and it should not be rushed. You cannot lead people down a road you have not walked. The experience you accumulate in this phase is the raw material of everything else.

But at some point, the only way to keep growing is no longer to learn more. It is to start helping others become like you. This is the shift that the calling requires, and it is counterintuitive enough that most people resist it. They continue acquiring knowledge instead of deploying it, because acquiring knowledge feels safe and deploying it feels exposed. The problem is that contribution is not just strategically useful. It is the mechanism by which the expert continues to grow at all. Teaching forces a depth of articulation that mere knowing never demands. Watching someone else struggle with the same wall you once hit reveals aspects of the problem that you had forgotten were invisible from their vantage point. The guide grows more than the student in almost every transaction, and the expert who understands this is not giving something away when they contribute. They are investing in their own continued development while simultaneously serving the people they were called to serve.

When that shift happens fully, when an expert stops asking what they can learn and starts asking who they can serve, something changes in the character their audience experiences. The content does not change immediately. The frameworks are the same.

The stories are the same. But the energy behind everything is different, and audiences feel that difference viscerally even if they cannot name it. They feel the difference between someone who is performing expertise and someone who is operating from a genuine obligation to give it away. Jay Abraham articulated the obligation directly: if you



<!-- Page 97 -->



truly believe that what you have is useful and valuable to your clients, then you have a moral obligation to try to serve them in every way possible. The moral obligation framing is important, because it reframes aggression in marketing from arrogance to responsibility. The expert who holds back, who is timid about selling, who hesitates to push hard for the message, is, in Jay Abraham’s framing, failing the people they could be serving. The crusade makes selling feel like a duty rather than an imposition.

Crusading For Something and Against Something The Adventurer/Crusader character identity type, which we covered in Part Two, shows up most directly in the shape of the crusade, but the crusade is not limited to that archetype. Every genuinely effective Attractive Character regardless of their primary identity type has a crusade at the center of their movement, and that crusade always operates in two directions simultaneously: toward something and against something.

The two directions are not separable. You cannot effectively crusade for a new world without implicitly crusading against the forces that are keeping your audience from it, and you cannot effectively crusade against an enemy without pointing toward the alternative that justifies the fight.

The crusade toward something is the vision, the specific future you are pulling your audience toward, the world that exists on the other side of the transformation you are helping them make. But the vision alone is not the crusade. The crusade is the personal conviction that getting people to that future matters more than any individual transaction, that it is worth the sacrifice of comfort and approval and safety that being a genuine leader requires.

The crusade against something is the enemy, and this is where many experts hesitate in ways that cost them the depth of connection their movement needs to survive. The enemy in the crusade is never your audience. It is always the third party, the false vehicle they have been using, the lie they have been told, the system that has been set up against them, the culture that profits from keeping them stuck. When you name the enemy clearly and aim your energy at it rather than at the people you are trying to help, two things happen that cannot be achieved any other way. First, your audience’s past failures immediately get a different explanation. They were not failing because they were weak or undisciplined or insufficiently motivated. They were failing because they were fighting against something designed to beat them. The relief of that reframe is enormous and it is the foundation of the trust that makes a crusade possible. Second, you give your tribe something to rally against together, and that shared opposition creates the internal

cohesion that no amount of positive messaging can manufacture on its own. The Funnel Hackers do not just share enthusiasm for funnels. They share, in a much more fundamental sense, a rejection of the old way of trying to grow a company online, the website, the cold traffic, the hope-based marketing that was the standard before we named it broken.

The Two Journeys and Why Contribution Becomes the Crusade Every person inside your movement is on two journeys at the same time, whether they are aware of it or not. The first is the journey of achievement, the external result they came for, the specific outcome they are trying to reach, the colored ribbon they are chasing. The second is the journey of transformation, who they are becoming in the process of pursuing the first journey. The person they were when they started and the person they will be when they arrive are not the same person, and the transformation is often more durable and more life-changing than the achievement itself.

If the only thing you deliver to your tribe is the achievement journey, you will eventually lose them. Even if they hit the milestone. Even if they reach the result you promised. Once the result is reached, the reason to stay goes away, unless the transformation journey has created a different and more permanent kind of connection. The transformation journey, who they are becoming, what they now value, how they see themselves and the world differently because of the work they have done inside your movement, is the thing that creates lifelong membership. It is also the thing that a crusade uniquely provides, because a crusade is not just a path to a result. It is a path to a different kind of person, and people who are in the middle of becoming a different kind of person do not stop mid-journey and start looking for somewhere else to go.

When ClickFunnels launched, I wanted to weave giving into the DNA of the company in a way that would make the transformation journey explicit and real for every person inside the tribe. Every time a new funnel went live, ClickFunnels donated a dollar to Village Impact, the organization that builds schools in Kenya. At every Funnel Hacking LIVE event, a check was presented to Stu and Amy McLaren, representing how many funnels the community had built together. The giving was not a marketing tactic, though it functioned as one. It was the embodiment of the crusade’s transformation layer: the message being communicated was that building funnels was not just a vehicle to personal income, but a vehicle to changing the world, and that every member of the tribe was participating in that change with every funnel they built. The journey of achievement and the journey of transformation were the same journey, told from two different vantage points.

Later, when Tim Ballard and Operation Underground Railroad came to my attention, the organization that rescues children from sex slavery, working against a global crisis affecting an estimated two million children, the response was the same instinct operating at a larger scale. That is the deepest version of the transformation journey, and it is only accessible through a crusade that goes all the way to the bottom of what you actually believe you are here to do.

Finding Your Crusade The question that surfaces the crusade is not what do you want to sell. It is not even what do you want to build. It is: what is the specific transformation you were made to help create in the specific people you were made to serve, and why does it matter in a way that extends beyond what any single transaction can capture? That question tends to produce either immediate clarity or immediate deflection, and the deflection is almost always some version of not feeling qualified to claim a crusade, feeling too small, too new, too uncertain, too much like someone who has not earned the right to organize their work around something as grand as a calling.

The inadequacy voice that almost every expert carries is loudest precisely at this question, and it is worth understanding why. The voice is loudest here not because the crusade is wrong but because it is right, and claiming it means taking on the full weight of leading people toward a transformation rather than just teaching them tactics. That weight is real. It does require something from you that tactics do not. But the weight is also the source of the only thing that gives your movement its staying power, the genuine conviction that the work matters beyond the transaction, and that you would be doing it whether or not the money followed.

They market from a position of obligation, and audiences feel the difference in everything.

Your crusade does not have to be as large as saving children or building schools. The scale of the contribution does not determine the depth of the calling. The woman teaching other women to reclaim their identity and their health after years of putting everyone else first is on a crusade. The man teaching entrepreneurs to build businesses that protect their marriages rather than consume them is on a crusade. The depth of the conviction is the point, the certainty that the specific transformation you are helping create in the specific people you are called to serve matters enough to build your entire professional life around it, even on the days when it is hard and the days when no one is watching and the days when the results are not coming as fast as you need them to come.

Audience vs. Movement: A Structural Difference There is a word that gets used loosely in almost every conversation about building an online business, and the looseness of it costs people something they cannot easily get back. The word is community. People say they want to build a community the way they say they want to build an audience, as if the two things are on the same spectrum, just different points on a scale of size and engagement. They are not on the same spectrum.

They are different things entirely, built through different mechanisms, held together by different forces, and capable of producing radically different outcomes for the person at the center of them.

An audience is a group of people who have opted into a relationship with you as the source. They follow your content, they consume what you produce, they may buy from you regularly, and their connection runs primarily in one direction, from you outward to them. You are the reason they showed up, and you are the reason they stay. If you stop producing, the audience drifts. If you disappear for long enough, most of them find someone else to follow. The audience is held together by the quality and consistency of what you put out into the world, and the moment that quality or consistency drops, the thread starts to fray.



<!-- Page 101 -->



A movement is something structurally different. It is a group of people who are connected not just to you but to each other, to a shared identity, to a set of beliefs about what is true and what is possible, and to a future they are collectively moving toward. The movement has internal gravity that an audience never develops, because in a movement the connections between members create a web of belonging that does not depend entirely on your continued output to hold it together. You are the guide at the center of the movement, not the content machine at the top of a distribution channel.

The insight that unlocks the architectural difference between an audience and a movement is something I observed firsthand in the years after ClickFunnels launched: people come into a community for the expert, but they stay for the community. They show up for you. They stay because of each other. And once you understand that, you understand why building a movement is not just a larger version of building an audience.

It is a fundamentally different design project, one that requires you to deliberately engineer the internal connections, the shared identity, the visible evidence of what is possible, and the rituals of belonging that keep people inside the web even after they no longer need you as much as they did when they arrived.

The Architecture of Belonging Every durable movement, political, religious, cultural, commercial, has an architecture of belonging that serves the same structural function regardless of what the movement is about. The architecture answers three questions that every person inside the movement is subconsciously asking from the moment they arrive: Who am I now? Who are we together? Where are we going? Movements that cannot answer all three questions fluently and repeatedly do not develop the internal gravity that keeps members inside when the initial enthusiasm wears off. Movements that answer them well develop something that almost no amount of marketing budget can replicate: the kind of loyalty that is not a decision but an identity.

The first question, who am I now, is answered by the identity shift. This is the mechanism at the foundation of tribe-building, and Kaelin Poulin discovered its power in a way that quantified it precisely. When she and her husband Brandon were building their fitness business under the name Tuell Time Trainer, the results were real but the retention was limited. People were buying once, getting results, and drifting away. The product was delivering. The identity was not. What the business was missing was the answer to the question every new customer was subconsciously asking when they landed: am I now a different kind of person, or did I just buy a fitness product? But the more significant change was not in the churn number. It was in what Kaelin started seeing in the behavior of her customers after the rebrand. They were talking differently. They were introducing themselves as LadyBosses. They were buying the swag not just to wear but because putting it on did something to how they felt about themselves. It is not branding in the conventional sense of the word. The tribe name is the first tool that delivers that offer, but it only works if it is constructed to answer the “I am” statement directly. Not “I bought a LadyBoss product.” Not “I follow LadyBoss.” “I am a LadyBoss.” Those three words are doing the entire job, and the job is converting a buyer into a member, someone whose connection to the movement is now rooted in who they are, not just in what they purchased.

The Manifesto: Your Title of Liberty Once the identity shift is engineered through the tribe name and the “I am” statement, the movement needs a document that holds the identity in place across time and circumstances. This is what the manifesto does, and its function is older than marketing.

In the ancient Americas, as recorded in Alma 46 in the Book of Mormon, Captain Moroni led an army into a war they were not winning. Some of his soldiers had lost faith. Some had defected. The army needed more than a new strategy. It needed a visible articulation of what they were fighting for that was concrete enough to rally around and public enough to hold everyone in. Moroni took his coat, tore it into a rough banner, and wrote on it: in memory of our God, our religion, and freedom, and our peace, our wives, and our children. Then he put it on a pole and called it the Title of Liberty. When people saw it, they came. They renewed their commitment. They went on to win.

The manifesto is the Title of Liberty of your movement. It is not a mission statement and it is not a brand guide. It is a declaration of who your tribe is, what they believe, what they are fighting for, and who they are in the process of becoming. Kaelin wrote hers first, and the process she used was as unpolished and direct as Captain Moroni’s banner: she sat down with Brandon, he pressed record on his phone, and she talked. She talked about what it meant to be a LadyBoss. When people carry your manifesto with them on the device they touch a hundred times a day, the identity shift is not a one-time event at the moment of purchase. It is a daily renewal. And nothing answers it more powerfully than visible evidence that the people inside the movement are actually achieving the result it promises. The Practical Architecture: What You Need to Build The movement architecture this chapter describes is not a set of marketing campaigns that you launch and then complete. It is a structural layer that you build once, maintain consistently, and allow to compound over time. The building blocks are simpler than they might appear when viewed as a complete system, because each one follows directly from the elements that came before it in this book.

The tribe name comes first, because without it nothing else can assemble around it. The test for a strong tribe name is whether a member can put “I am a ___” in front of it and feel something shift. Not “I buy from ___” or “I follow ___,” but “I am a ___.” Funnel Hacker. LadyBoss. Warrior. The name points toward the new identity the movement offers, and the identity should connect directly to the result the movement delivers and the new opportunity the Attractive Character is guiding people toward. If the result is financial freedom through online business, the identity is someone who has broken the matrix of trading time for money. If the result is physical transformation after years of failed dieting, the identity is someone who has discovered they were never broken, the vehicle was wrong. The name is the most compressed possible expression of who the member gets to become by joining.

The manifesto comes second, and its purpose is to hold the identity in place between milestones. The process Kaelin used is as effective as any: sit down with someone who can record you, talk about what it means to be a member of your tribe, and let the document find itself in what you say rather than crafting it from the outside in. What do you believe about these people? What are they capable of that they do not yet know?

What do they stand for? What are they fighting against? What does winning look like for someone who has fully claimed this identity? The answers to those questions, spoken honestly and transcribed directly, will produce a manifesto that lands differently than anything assembled through a drafting process, because the audience can hear the difference between something written and something said.

The milestone system comes third, and its design starts with a single question: what is the four-minute mile for your movement? What is the result that your dream customers believe is possible for other people but probably not for themselves, and that if they saw enough of their peers achieve it, they would finally believe was possible for them too?

That result, properly named and given a physical artifact that members can display and photograph and take onstage to receive, is the engine of the movement’s word-of-mouth growth. You do not need a tiered system like the Two Comma Club in the beginning. You need one milestone. One colored ribbon. One moment that a member can point to and say: I did the thing. And every person in my network who is watching will know exactly what that means.

The physical artifacts that carry the identity across all three layers, the T-shirts, the plaques, the phone backgrounds, the stickers, the prints that hang on walls, are not merchandise. They are identity technology. Every time a member wears or displays or is reminded of an artifact connected to the movement, they are renewing the identity shift that the movement offered them when they arrived. The renewal keeps the commitment alive between the high-engagement moments, the events, the big wins, the milestones, and it is the quiet accumulation of those renewals across thousands of daily moments that gives a movement its durability. The audience drifts when the content stops. The movement holds because the identity has been woven into too many ordinary moments of daily life to be easily abandoned.

Build the name. Write the manifesto. Design the four-minute mile. That is the moment the audience becomes a movement, and once you have felt it happen, you will understand why it was worth building everything in this book to get here.

The Authenticity-Strategy Tension: Resolving It Once and For All There is a version of the authenticity argument that gets used to justify not having a strategy at all. It sounds like: I’m just going to show up and be real, and the right people will find me. This is romantically appealing and practically useless. The characters who built lasting audiences while being genuinely themselves were almost universally people who also made deliberate choices about what to share, how to frame it, when to deploy it, and what they wanted it to do in their audience’s belief system. The choice to be authentic is not the same as the choice to be unstrategic. You can be one without the other, and the characters who last are almost always both.

The tension people feel between authenticity and strategy comes from a misunderstanding of what strategy actually means in this context. Strategy in the Attractive Character framework does not mean manufacturing a persona that isn’t you.

It means excavating who you actually are, your real backstory, your real flaws, your real voice, your genuine polarity position, and then making intentional choices about how to organize and deploy that genuine material for maximum resonance with the audience you’re trying to reach. That is not inauthenticity. That is craftsmanship. Every storyteller, every artist, every communicator who has ever moved an audience did it through the disciplined shaping of genuine material, not through raw unfiltered self-expression with no craft applied to it.

The test for whether what you’re doing is authentic strategy or performed persona is simple: does the content feel like you, or does it feel like the version of you that you think will work? When you watch back a video you made last year, does the person on screen still feel like the same person you are today, or does it feel slightly foreign, like watching someone else play a character based on you? When a reader meets you in person after following your content for a year, do they feel like they already know you, or do they feel like they met someone different from who they expected? The character that passes those tests is genuine. The character that fails them is a costume that’s been worn too long, and costumes eventually come off.

The construction work in Parts One and Two of this book was designed specifically to make this distinction tractable. When your backstory is built from real, specific, uncomfortable moments rather than polished retrospective summaries, when your flaws are real ones that cost you something rather than charming imperfections that make you look good, when your voice is the one that comes out before the editorial pass rather than after it, the character that emerges from that material is not a performance. It is genuinely you, organized and expressed with skill. And a character built on genuine

Your Moral Code: The Anchor That Keeps the Character From Drifting John Alanis taught something he called the position of advocacy, a distilled statement of the moral code that the character stands for, from which every piece of content and every polarity position flows. His was precise enough to fit in a sentence: be a self-reliant real man, because everything is your fault. Whether or not that specific position resonates with you, the function it serves is critical to understand. It is the fixed point that the character can always return to when the pressure to drift is highest. When the algorithm rewards something that doesn’t feel quite like you, the position of advocacy is the thing you hold against it. When the criticism comes in, it’s the standard against which you evaluate whether the criticism contains information you should use or noise you should ignore. When the audience’s preferences shift and you’re not sure how to shift with them, it’s the thing that tells you which shifts are authentic evolution and which are you chasing trends at the cost of who you actually are.

A well-built position of advocacy has three qualities. It is specific enough to generate disagreement, a vague commitment to quality, excellence, or positive impact is not a position of advocacy, it’s a press release. It names a specific worldview, a specific way of seeing the problem, a specific stand on how the thing you teach should be done and why the alternatives fall short. It is genuine enough to be sustainable, not something you’ve reasoned yourself into believing is the right position for your market, but something that actually reflects how you see the world, something you’d hold even if it cost you audience members, something that doesn’t require you to remember to believe it because you already do. And it is connected to the practical work your audience is doing, not a philosophical abstraction floating above the daily business of your market, but something with direct implications for how your audience should approach the specific challenges they’re facing right now.

The Prolific Zone: Where Authenticity Lives in the Market There’s a positioning dimension to the authenticity question that most people don’t think about consciously even though it’s always in play: how far from the mainstream does your genuine position on things actually sit? Because the characters who build the most loyal, most durable audiences are almost never the ones at the mainstream center of their market, the ones saying the things everyone already knows in the ways everyone already says them, and they’re almost never the ones at the radical fringe either, where the ideas are so far outside consensus that the barrier to believing them is simply too high for most people to clear. The durable characters live in what I’ve called the Prolific Zone, close enough to the mainstream that the ideas are recognizable and actionable, different enough that encountering them is a genuinely novel experience.

Dave Asprey and Bulletproof Coffee is the right example here. Putting butter and coconut oil in your coffee to lose weight sits in the Prolific Zone not because Asprey designed it to land there, but because it’s his genuine belief about what the research supports, and that genuine belief happens to be unusual enough to cause polarity, the mainstream nutritionist thinks it’s wrong, the person who tries it and feels the difference thinks Asprey is the first person who’s told them something true, without being so far outside consensus that it requires a wholesale rejection of everything people have been taught. That’s the sweet spot. And it’s a sweet spot that, crucially, cannot be manufactured. The character who tries to position their ideas in the Prolific Zone without genuinely believing what they’re saying will hold the position fine until the first serious challenge, and then the position will collapse because it wasn’t built on anything real. The character whose genuine position happens to land in the Prolific Zone holds it effortlessly, because holding it is just being themselves.

The market research version of you is usually a more cautious, more hedged, more committee-approved version of the actual you. The actual you, honestly expressed, is usually the more interesting and more differentiated character.

Backlash: What It Is, What It Isn’t, and How to Hold Your Ground When Kaelin first started getting real criticism, not just the gentle “this wasn’t my favorite” from people who already liked her, but the vocal, public, sometimes mean-spirited criticism that comes when content reaches people who didn’t choose to find you, her initial instinct was the same instinct almost everyone has: figure out what they objected to and adjust. The logic feels sound. If people are unhappy with something I’m doing, I should understand the feedback and incorporate it.

The problem with that logic is that it treats all criticism as signal when most of it is noise. The person who opts into your content because the algorithm surfaced it and then immediately announces that your voice is annoying has not given you actionable feedback. They have given you evidence that your content is reaching people outside your core audience, which is a sign that your reach is growing, not a sign that your content needs to change. The criticism is collateral contact. The appropriate response is not to become less yourself in hopes of keeping someone who wasn’t your customer anyway. The appropriate response is, as Kaelin said with refreshing directness, to laugh about it.

There’s a counterintuitive but accurate observation underneath this: if you are not being criticized, your content is not reaching enough people. Criticism at scale is an almost inevitable consequence of reach at scale, because scale means your content is reaching the outer rings of your audience, people with no prior relationship to your character, no context for your voice, no reason to extend the benefit of the doubt. Some percentage of those people will react negatively and say so publicly. That percentage doesn’t change much as your reach grows. What changes is the absolute number, which means the criticism feels louder even when the proportion is the same or better. Characters who understand this can hold steady through it. Characters who don’t understand it read the growing volume of criticism as evidence that something has gone wrong and start making adjustments that erode the character that earned the reach in the first place.

There is, however, a category of criticism that contains genuine signal, and the ability to distinguish it from noise is what separates characters who learn and improve from characters who either ignore all feedback or adjust to all feedback indiscriminately. The criticism that contains signal is the criticism that comes from inside your intended audience, from people who actually understand what you’re trying to do and have a reasoned objection to a specific thing you did or said. It usually sounds different from noise: more specific, less emotional, coming from someone whose engagement history



<!-- Page 112 -->



suggests genuine investment in the relationship. When that person raises an objection, it’s worth examining seriously. Not necessarily agreeing with, but genuinely considering against the standard of your position of advocacy. If the criticism is pointing to a gap between what you claim to stand for and how you actually showed up on a given day, that’s information. If the criticism is pointing to the fact that you hold a position they disagree with, that’s your polarity working correctly.

The community itself becomes a defense mechanism over time for characters who build it deliberately. When Kaelin’s community encountered critics in the comments, they didn’t need her to respond, the community responded on her behalf, not because she’d trained them to, but because the shared values she’d established through consistent character presence meant that her audience already knew what the right response to that kind of criticism was. The community of a character with a genuine, well-expressed position of advocacy will defend that position because they share it, and they share it because the character built it into the culture from the beginning. That’s the deepest form of brand protection available, and no PR strategy or crisis communications firm can manufacture it. It only comes from years of showing up as yourself, consistently, until the people who stayed because of who you are have internalized who you are as a shared identity.

The Drift Diagnostic: Checking Whether You’re Still You The drift that hollows out a character doesn’t usually announce itself. It accumulates in small adjustments that each seem defensible in isolation: softening a position because the last time you stated it directly someone complained loudly about it. Starting to talk more about your wins and less about your struggles because the win posts get better engagement. Spending more time on the topics the algorithm rewards and less time on the topics your audience actually needs but doesn’t click on as readily. Adopting the language and framing of content creators you admire because it seems to work for them. None of these individual adjustments feels like a betrayal of the character.

Accumulated over months, they can produce a character that’s recognizably less real than the one you started with.

The drift diagnostic is a periodic check against the original material. Pull out your story bank and read back through it, does the content you’ve been producing recently draw on this material, or has it been floating free of it? Read your polarity position, is it still present in your content at the same intensity as when you wrote it, or has it been gradually softened into something easier to hold? Listen to a piece of your older content and a



<!-- Page 113 -->



piece of your recent content back to back, does the same voice come through in both, or has the recent version started to sound more polished, more careful, more like someone who’s learned what plays well? The gap between those two versions, if there is one, is the drift to correct.

Correcting drift doesn’t require a dramatic public recalibration or a performative return to your roots. It requires going back to the source material, the genuine backstory, the real flaws, the voice that exists before editing, the polarity position you actually hold, and producing content from that place again. Usually the first piece of content that comes out of that return to source lands better than almost anything produced during the drift period, because the audience can feel the difference even when they can’t articulate it.

They just know: that’s the person I came here for. That’s why I subscribed. That’s who I trust. Social media love is fickle. Kaelin said this too, and it’s the kind of thing that sounds obvious until you’re inside a platform watching your engagement numbers move daily and starting to make decisions based on them. One day everyone loves you, the next day the wave has moved somewhere else and the numbers reflect it. Characters who anchor their sense of what they’re doing in the metrics are riding a wave that has nothing to do with whether they’re building something real. Characters who anchor it in the genuine material, in who they actually are, in the audience that chose to stay through the fluctuations, in the relationships that have deepened past the point where a dip in reach matters, are building something that the metrics can’t destroy. That’s the only version of the Attractive Character that lasts.

The AC for Life: Different Business Models and Character Evolution Here’s a question that doesn’t get asked enough: what does an Attractive Character look like when it’s fully grown? Not at the launch, not in the first year when everything is fresh and the backstory is new and the audience is still being built, but a decade in, across multiple products, multiple platforms, multiple seasons of your life, with a customer base that has seen you go through the full arc of what you were building when they first showed up? What does the character look like then? How has it changed? What has it kept? And what was it actually building toward all along?

Most of the teaching around the Attractive Character focuses on the beginning, how to construct it, how to establish it, how to get it working inside a funnel system. That’s where the leverage is for someone who’s starting out or trying to systematize what they’ve been doing intuitively. But everyone reading this book is already past that stage. You have an audience. You have a character that’s been developing, whether you’ve been thinking about it deliberately or not. What you may not have is a clear picture of where it goes from here, how it continues developing, how it adapts to the business it’s actually inside of rather than some theoretical ideal business, and what the long arc of building it looks like over the years ahead.

This chapter covers both. The first half is practical: how the Attractive Character framework applies across different business model types, because the mechanics shift meaningfully depending on whether you’re running an information business, a physical product company, a software company, a service business, or something that combines several of these. The second half is architectural: how the character evolves over time, how the four character identities function not as permanent categories but as situational tools, and what the fully developed version of the Attractive Character is ultimately building toward, which turns out not to be a business at all, but something larger that the business is an expression of.

The Two Types of Business: Information and Everything Else

Every business that deploys an Attractive Character falls into one of two categories, and understanding which category you’re in determines where the character does its primary work. The first category is the information business: courses, coaching, consulting, books, masterminds, memberships where the primary thing being sold is knowledge, frameworks, and guidance. In these businesses, the character is the product in a more direct sense than anywhere else, people aren’t just buying access to information, they’re buying a relationship with the specific person who holds and teaches it. Characters in information businesses who understand this don’t think about the Attractive Character as a promotional layer on top of what they sell. They understand that their character depth is their primary competitive moat.

The second category, which includes physical products, software, services, local businesses, e-commerce, and essentially everything else, uses the Attractive Character differently. Here the character functions as what I’ve called the fuel for the business: the mechanism that builds trust, creates desire, generates loyalty, and ultimately reduces the friction of every sale. A dentist’s character doesn’t replace the dental work, but it absolutely determines whether a patient refers three friends or zero. A physical product company’s founder character doesn’t replace the product, but it’s often what turns a one-time purchase into a customer relationship that lasts years. A software company’s character doesn’t replace the software’s functionality, but it’s frequently the reason users stay through rough patches and become advocates instead of churning out when a competitor offers a marginally better feature set.

The tactical difference between these two deployments is where the information product sits in the business architecture. In the information business, the information product is the business. In everything else, information products are the most powerful front-end asset available for building character relationships at scale before anyone has spent money on the actual core offer. The dentist who runs a free guide to avoiding root canals and follows it with a five-part email sequence about oral health isn’t selling dentistry, they’re building a character relationship that makes the eventual dentistry conversation something other than a cold transaction. ClickFunnels used exactly this model: frameworks taught through information products created desire for software that implemented the frameworks. The information and the software weren’t competing products. One was the fuel that made the other something people wanted before they even saw the pricing page.

The practical question for anyone in the non-information category is: what is the framework your ideal customer needs to understand in order to fully appreciate the value of what you actually sell? Whatever that framework is, teaching it, through content, emails, a lead magnet, a book, a free course, is the front-end character-building work that makes everything downstream easier. The character who teaches the framework earns the trust that makes the core product sale feel like the obvious next step rather than a persuasion challenge.

The Four Character Types Are Situational, Not Permanent One of the most useful shifts in understanding the four character identities, the Expert Leader, the Adventurer/Crusader, the Reporter/Evangelist, and the Reluctant Hero, is recognizing that they’re not categories you’re assigned to for life. They’re modes you move through depending on what you’re doing, who you’re talking to, and what the situation requires. The character who spent the first decade of their business leaning into the Reluctant Hero, genuinely awkward, genuinely uncertain, sharing discoveries as someone who found the path rather than someone who built it, doesn’t have to abandon that identity when they’ve become an Expert Leader by any objective measure. But they do need to understand that the primary identity will shift based on context, and that holding onto an identity that no longer fits the situation creates a disconnect the audience will feel even if they can’t articulate it.

The example that makes this clearest is the one from the Dan Kennedy acquisition. When I bought Dan’s company and stepped into his world, I couldn’t come in as the Expert Leader, because Dan is the Expert Leader in that world, and his existing audience would have sensed immediately if I’d tried to take that position. The natural role was Reporter/Evangelist: I’m the biggest fan in the room, I’m here because I’ve been studying this person’s work for years, I’m bringing it to you as someone who is genuinely awed by what I’m finding in the archives. That’s not a diminishment of my Expert Leader identity.

It’s a situationally accurate identity for that specific context. The Reporter/Evangelist stance in that situation built trust faster with Kennedy’s audience than any display of my own expertise could have, because it correctly positioned me relative to the authority structure already in place.

The same flexibility applies across a business as it grows. When presenting to a room of Inner Circle members, experienced, sophisticated operators who are themselves experts in their domains, the Expert Leader identity is the right register: high conviction, high energy, leading from the front. When entering a new market, territory, or audience

segment where the character has no established credibility yet, the Adventurer/Crusader or Reporter/Evangelist identity is usually more honest and more effective, because it accurately represents the character’s actual position relative to that audience. When the stakes of what you’re teaching are genuinely high enough that the discomfort of the spotlight is less important than the urgency of sharing what you’ve found, the Reluctant Hero mode creates the most authentic expression of that specific situation. The character with full command of all four identities has a dramatically wider range of situations they can serve effectively, and a much harder-to-displace position in any market they choose to enter.

Evolving the Backstory Without Losing the Origin One of the quieter challenges of a long-running Attractive Character is what happens to the backstory over time. The origin story that launched the character, specific, uncomfortable, emotionally resonant, meeting the audience exactly where they are, was accurate when it was written. But the character keeps living, and the distance between that starting point and the current moment keeps growing. The struggle that felt immediate in year one can start to feel performatively distant in year eight, especially to a long-term audience who has watched the character’s success compound past the point where the origin story’s stakes feel present.

The solution is what John Alanis modeled across decades of building his character: the backstory is never just one story. It’s a bank of stories at different depths of the same underlying character. The specific phrases, the characteristic ways of seeing things, the recurring cultural references, these stay consistent and actually deepen as the audience encounters them more times and associates them more thoroughly with the character.

What evolves is the current chapter of the story. The character who is still figuring something out, still working through a real challenge, still carrying the specific combination of wins and active struggles that makes their life genuinely interesting to watch, that character never runs out of material, because the story never resolves into a static success state. John added whiskey collecting, a personal trainer he argues with seventy percent of the time, the specific strains of being forty-two and still trying to go hard in the gym. None of that changes who he is. All of it deepens the character by showing someone who is still living in the story rather than summarizing it from a comfortable distance.

The backstory bank needs active maintenance for exactly this reason. When you deposit new moments into it, the coaching call that went sideways this week, the experiment that failed publicly, the book that changed how you’re thinking about the next phase of the

business, you’re not replacing the origin story. You’re extending it. You’re showing an audience that has been with you for years that the character who attracted them is still the same person, still doing the thing, still honest about the gaps between where they are and where they’re trying to go. That ongoing extension of the story is what keeps a long-term audience relationship from calcifying into a transactional one.

The Thousand True Fans and What They’re Actually Building Kevin Kelly’s observation that a creator needs only a thousand true fans, people who will purchase everything the character produces, follow them anywhere, advocate for them to everyone they know, to make a sustainable living cuts against most of the metrics people use to evaluate whether their Attractive Character is working. Follower count doesn’t capture it. Reach doesn’t capture it. Even revenue in a given month doesn’t capture it, because revenue can be generated through one-time transactions with people who have no relationship with the character at all. What captures it is depth of relationship with the specific people who have been with the character long enough and trusted them fully enough to become true fans, people whose buying behavior is driven not by the quality of any individual offer but by their relationship with the character behind all of them.

This framing reorients the whole project of building an Attractive Character in a way that the funnel metrics and the conversion rates never quite do. The goal was never to maximize the number of people who know about you. It was always to maximize the depth of relationship with the people who are genuinely right for what you’re building. A thousand people who trust you completely and would buy anything you recommend represent a more durable, more valuable, more meaningful business asset than a hundred thousand people who vaguely recognize your name and occasionally click your ads. The character work, the backstory, the flaws, the voice, the polarity, the stories, the daily practice, is not a traffic strategy. It is a trust-building strategy. And trust, unlike traffic, compounds in ways that can sustain a business for decades without requiring constant reinvention of the acquisition mechanism.

The question every character should be able to answer at any point in the development of their business is: how many true fans do I currently have, and what is the depth of relationship I have with them? Not with my total list size, not with my total follower count, but with the specific group of people who have been present through multiple seasons of the character’s development and have consistently chosen to deepen the relationship at each opportunity to do so. That group is the business. Everything else, the ads, the

From Business to Movement: The Future-Based Cause The fully developed Attractive Character doesn’t just run a business. It leads a movement. This is not a metaphor and it’s not a marketing positioning choice. It is a description of what naturally emerges when the character work is done well and sustained over time. The three elements that every mass movement in history has shared are a charismatic leader, a new opportunity, and a future-based cause. The charismatic leader is the Attractive Character, fully expressed. The new opportunity is the specific vehicle the character has built their business around. The future-based cause is the answer to the question every character eventually has to be able to answer clearly: why does this work matter beyond the revenue it generates?

Not as a mission statement to post on a website, but as a genuine orientation toward the future that shapes every decision about what content to produce, what offers to build, what partnerships to pursue, and what to say no to. The character whose work is in service of a clear future-based cause makes better decisions at every level of the business because the cause functions as a filter: does this move us toward the future we’re building, or away from it? Characters without a cause filter on tactics, trends, and whatever is currently working for someone else. Characters with a cause filter on vision, and the business that emerges from that is coherent in a way that a tactics-first business almost never is.
