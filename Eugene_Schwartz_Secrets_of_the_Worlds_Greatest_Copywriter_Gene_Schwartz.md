## Agent Reference Summary

This document explains Gene Schwartz's direct-response copywriting principles, especially the strategic thinking behind *Breakthrough Advertising*. The core lesson is that successful copy does not create desire; it channels existing mass desire into a product by matching the message to the prospect's current state of awareness and the market's level of sophistication.

### Core Operating Principles

1. **Do not create desire. Channel it.**  
   The market already contains hopes, fears, dreams, frustrations, and desires. Copy focuses those existing forces onto a product.

2. **Research is the source of great copy.**  
   Study the product, the prospect, and the market until you can identify unique "fascinations," golden nuggets, mechanisms, proof points, and emotional language.

3. **The headline is a bridge.**  
   The headline connects the prospect's existing awareness and desire to the product. Its job is not to sell everything; its job is to make the prospect read the next sentence.

4. **Use benefits to sell the promise; use features to prove it.**  
   The physical product does not sell by itself. The product's performance - what it does for the customer - is the true selling material. Features support, document, justify, and make the promised performance believable.

5. **Match the headline to awareness.**  
   A headline that works for one state of awareness may fail in another. Always diagnose what the prospect already knows before writing.

6. **Match the mechanism to market sophistication.**  
   As markets mature, claims become less believable. The more sophisticated the market, the more important it becomes to introduce or improve a mechanism, or eventually identify with the prospect's state of mind.

## Gene Schwartz's Four Pillars of Successful Advertising

### 1. Establish a Success-Based Work Ethic

Gene used timed work blocks, famously 33 minutes and 33 seconds, followed by breaks. The lesson is focused, disciplined, repeatable work rather than endless unfocused effort.

**Agent use:** When advising a copywriter, recommend focused research/writing sprints of roughly 30-40 minutes, followed by short breaks.

### 2. Study the Product Better Than Anyone Else

Schwartz read source material repeatedly, took intense notes, built a vocabulary file, and looked for fascinations - surprising details that make copy sparkle.

**Agent use:** Before writing copy, extract:

- Unique mechanisms
- Specific proof points
- Surprising facts
- Numbers and time frames
- Before/after contrasts
- Usable customer-facing language
- Hidden benefits and product performances

### 3. Know the Prospect Like a Good Friend

The copywriter must understand the prospect narrowly and broadly: specialized media, mainstream media, conversations, fears, aspirations, frustrations, language, and daily life.

**Agent use:** Build customer insight from:

- Data cards and list information
- Surveys
- Testimonials
- Complaints
- Forums and discussion groups
- Popular media consumed by the prospect
- Product users and non-users
- Real-world conversations

### 4. Analyze the Market

The market is shaped by forces outside the copywriter's control. Schwartz identified three major factors:

- Mass desire
- State of awareness
- Market sophistication

**Agent use:** Diagnose the market before writing headlines, leads, bullets, subheads, or offers.

## Strategy of Persuasion

### Factor 1: Mass Desire

Mass desire is the public spread of a private want. It is not created by the copywriter. It already exists and must be channeled.

#### Three Dimensions of Mass Desire

1. **Urgency / intensity:** How strong is the need now?
2. **Staying power / repetition:** Does the desire repeat or remain unsatisfied?
3. **Scope:** How many people share the desire?

#### How to Channel Mass Desire

1. Choose the strongest desire that can be applied to the product.
2. Acknowledge and reinforce that desire in the headline.
3. Show how the product's performances inevitably satisfy that desire.

---

## State of Awareness Framework

### State 1: Most Aware

The prospect knows the product, knows what it does, and wants it. They need a reason to act now.

**Headline strategy:** Product + offer + reason to buy.

### State 2: Knows Product But Doesn't Want It Yet

The prospect knows the product but is not fully convinced.

**Headline strategies:**

- Reinforce desire
- Sharpen the image of satisfaction
- Extend where/when the product satisfies desire
- Add proof or documentation
- Announce a new mechanism

### State 3: Wants the Result But Does Not Know the Product Exists

The prospect recognizes the desire but does not know your product can fulfill it.

**Headline strategy:** Name the desire or solution, prove it can be done, and reveal the product mechanism.

### State 4: Has a Need But Not Yet a Desire

The prospect recognizes a problem but does not connect it to your product.

**Headline strategy:** Name and dramatize the problem/need until the prospect recognizes the need for a solution.

### State 5: Unaware or Market-Fatigued Identification

The prospect is unaware, resistant, or so saturated that product claims and mechanisms no longer work.

**Headline strategy:** Use identification. Echo the prospect's emotion, attitude, dissatisfaction, or private state of mind.

---

## Market Sophistication Framework

### Stage 1: First in the Market

Use a simple, direct claim. Name the need or result. Prove the product works.

### Stage 2: Second in the Market

The direct claim still works, but competition has appeared. Enlarge the claim and push it further.

### Stage 3: Prospects Have Heard It All

The claim is overused. Introduce a **new mechanism** that makes the old promise fresh and believable again.

### Stage 4: Mechanisms Compete

Competitors copy the mechanism. Elaborate, improve, intensify, or upgrade the mechanism. Make it faster, easier, more complete, or more believable.

### Stage 5: Market Fatigue

Prospects are tired of claims and mechanisms. Return to the prospect's identity and state of mind. Write an identification headline.

---

## Creativity Principle: Connectivity

Schwartz disliked the idea of "creating" from nothing. He defined creativity as connecting two previously separate ideas. New does not mean never existed before; it means never joined before.

**Agent use:** Generate ideas by connecting:

- Product mechanism + prospect emotion
- Current trend + old desire
- Hidden proof + dominant frustration
- Familiar problem + surprising solution
- Market fatigue + identification language

---

## Practical Agent Workflow

When writing copy using this document, follow this sequence:

1. Identify the product and its functional performances.
2. Identify the dominant mass desire.
3. Rank the desire by urgency, staying power, and scope.
4. Diagnose the prospect's state of awareness.
5. Diagnose the market's sophistication stage.
6. Select the appropriate headline strategy.
7. Extract product fascinations and proof points.
8. Build the promotion from the prospect's current state of mind toward the product.
9. Use benefits to sell and features to prove.
10. If the market is tired, stop leading with claims and start with identification.

Answer this question: Who creates the desire to buy your product? If you answered that you (as the copywriter) do — Gene says you’d be wrong. And he has good reason for saying this.

In Gene’s words:

Let’s get right down to the heart of the matter. The power, the force, the overwhelming urge to own that makes advertising work, comes from the market itself, and not from the copy. How Mass Desire Is Created

What is mass desire? Gene Schwartz defines it simply as the “public spread of a private want.”

In Gene’s words:

Advertising is a business of statistics. We address our ads to individuals, and yet the success of our advertising depends on thousands, or even millions, of these individuals sharing the same response to these ads. Before these individuals can share this buying response, they must first share the desire upon which our ad is based. Privately, each of them wants the same thing. Publicly, there are enough of them to repay us the cost of advertising,

He would stand at the billiard table, and he would have a single white billiard ball. He would have a pen and an inkwell, and he would have the score. And he would take the white billiard ball in his left hand while he had the pen in the right hand and he would throw the white billiard ball out against the three cushions. And it would bounce off the three cushions. It’s random, how it comes back, to a certain extent. It never comes back exactly at the same place, so he had to focus on the trajectory of the billiard ball until it came back. When it came back here or here or here, he had to focus on that hand being at the exact right place. Meanwhile, while his conscious mind was over here, his unconscious mind slipped the note back to him and then he had the next note. Every note was a billiard ball traveling. Every note was a distraction. Every note was an addition. You’ve got to break out of that conscious prison to be unconsciously creative, which means to connect unconsciously things that haven’t been consciously connected before.
