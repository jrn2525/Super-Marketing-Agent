## Document Context

This PDF is a newsletter-style companion guide to Gene Schwartz's *Breakthrough Advertising*. It does **not** replace the book; instead, it explains some of the most difficult concepts in a practical, simplified way.

The guide focuses on several core ideas:

- Copy does **not create desire**; it channels existing desire.
- The headline must meet the prospect at their current **state of awareness**.
- A market's **level of sophistication** determines what kind of claim, mechanism, or emotional lead will work.
- As markets mature, direct promises lose believability, and the copywriter must shift from simple claims to mechanisms, then to deeper emotional identification.
- Great headlines often come from accurately verbalizing what the prospect already feels but may not be able to say clearly.

The PDF repeatedly emphasizes that these concepts have generated enormous direct-response results over time because they are based on enduring human motivations.

---

## Core Thesis: Desire Is Not Created, It Is Channeled

The central idea from *Breakthrough Advertising* is that advertising does not create desire from nothing. The market already contains hopes, dreams, fears, frustrations, and desires. The copywriter's job is to focus those existing forces onto a product or offer.

### Key Principle

> Copy cannot create desire. It can only channel existing desire.

### What This Means for Marketing

A marketing message should not try to convince someone to want something they do not already care about. Instead, it should:

1. Identify a desire, fear, frustration, or aspiration that already exists in the market.
2. Intensify or bring that desire into conscious focus.
3. Show that the product is a credible way to satisfy that desire.
4. Make the product feel like the best path to fulfillment.

### Practical Example from the PDF

The PDF uses a hypothetical fat-flushing water product.

A poor framing would be: "Let us educate you about why you need this new water."

A stronger framing starts with an existing desire: people already want to lose weight, especially without dieting or exercise. The product should be positioned as a new way to fulfill that desire.

Possible headline direction:

> Drink Your Fat Away: Flush Fat From Your Body While Watching TV - Without Exercise or Dieting

The important insight is that the product does not create the desire to lose weight. It only offers a new route to satisfy it.

## Why This Is More Than Semantics

The PDF stresses that the distinction between creating desire and channeling desire is not just wordplay. It changes how a marketer thinks.

### Weak Marketing Mindset

- "We need to educate the market until they want this."
- "If we explain enough features, people will become interested."
- "Our product is good, so demand will follow."

### Strong Marketing Mindset

- "What does this market already urgently want?"
- "What pain, fear, pride, greed, hope, or frustration already lives in the prospect?"
- "How does this product satisfy that existing emotion better than the alternatives?"
- "How do we get in front of the parade instead of trying to create a new one?"

### Core Human Desires Mentioned

The PDF notes that human nature is relatively constant. People continue to be motivated by:

- Greed
- Lust
- Pride
- Love
- Approval
- Fear
- Anger
- Hope
- Desire for status
- Desire for relief
- Desire for freedom

A marketing agent should treat these as recurring motivational categories when analyzing markets.

## Case Example: Health Newsletter Positioning

The PDF describes a health newsletter campaign that failed when positioned as conventional medical education. Prospects did not want more advice from mainstream doctors; they had already received that for years and felt sicker, heavier, and more frustrated.

The winning repositioning tapped into a different pre-existing desire:

- Anger at the conventional medical establishment
- Desire for natural alternatives
- Desire for practical health improvement
- Feeling that mainstream medicine was not solving the real problem

When the approach shifted toward natural healing and anti-establishment positioning through *Health and Healing* by Dr. Julian Whitaker, it became a major direct-response success.

### Agent Lesson

When a campaign fails, do not only rewrite the copy. Re-examine the underlying desire being targeted.

Ask:

- Are we selling what the market truly wants?
- Are we speaking to the dominant resident emotion?
- Are we trying to educate people into caring instead of entering the desire they already have?

## The Real Purpose of a Headline

The headline is the bridge between the prospect and the product.

Its job is to:

1. Acknowledge the prospect's desire.
2. Reinforce or intensify that desire.
3. Offer or imply a means to satisfy that desire.
4. Meet the prospect at their current point of awareness.
5. Get the prospect to read the next sentence.

### Important Distinction

A headline does **not** need to:

- Explain every benefit
- Present the full USP
- Name the product
- Close the sale
- Tell the whole story

Its immediate job is simple:

> Get the prospect to read the next sentence.

## The Three Foundation Questions for an Advertisement

The PDF explains that the content of an ad, especially the headline, depends on answering three questions.

### 1. What is the mass desire that creates this market?

This is the dominant emotional force already present in the prospect.

Examples:

- Lose weight
- Save money
- Make more income
- Avoid illness
- Protect retirement
- Regain youth
- Improve status
- Escape embarrassment
- Beat competitors

### 2. How much does the prospect know about how your product satisfies that desire?

This is the prospect's **state of awareness**.

They may know:

- The product itself
- The product category
- The problem
- The desire
- Nothing consciously at all

### 3. How many other products have been presented to them before yours?

This is the market's **state of sophistication**.

It determines how skeptical they are and what kind of claim will still feel believable.

## State of Awareness

State of awareness describes how aware the prospect is of:

- Their desire
- Their problem
- Possible solutions
- Your product category
- Your specific product
- The relationship between your product and their desire

The PDF breaks awareness into five levels.

---

### Awareness Level 1: Most Aware

The prospect already knows:

- The product
- What it does
- That they want it
- Why it matters

They simply have not bought yet.

#### Recommended Headline Approach

Use a direct offer, name the product, and provide a bargain or reason to act.

#### Examples

- Hamburger for 79 cents
- Big-screen TV for $500
- Known product with discount
- Known product with bonus

#### Agent Guidance

Use this level when the prospect already wants the exact thing being sold. Focus on:

- Price
- Deal
- Scarcity
- Bonus
- Deadline
- Convenience

---

### Awareness Level 2: Product-Aware But Not Yet Convinced

The prospect knows the product or brand, but:

- Does not fully understand what it does
- Does not know how well it works
- Does not believe it is superior
- Does not yet feel urgency to buy

#### Recommended Headline Approach

Communicate why this product is better, stronger, faster, easier, newer, safer, or more valuable than alternatives.

#### Agent Guidance

Use this level when selling in a familiar category but the prospect needs differentiation.

Possible angles:

- Better performance
- Improved version
- Faster result
- Stronger proof
- Lower risk
- Better guarantee
- More complete solution

---

### Awareness Level 3: Desire-Aware / Solution-Unaware

The prospect knows they want the result, but does not know your product exists or that a product like yours can help.

#### PDF Example

People want to save money on gas, but they may not know a satellite-based tool could locate cheaper gas nearby.

A possible headline:

> Satellite Breakthrough Saves You $50 on Gas Each Month - Without Reducing Your Driving a Single Mile

#### Recommended Headline Approach

1. Pinpoint the desire.
2. Make it vivid and recognizable.
3. Introduce a believable mechanism that connects the product to the desire.

#### Agent Guidance

This level requires more creativity than Levels 1 and 2. A simple benefit claim may not be believed because the prospect does not yet know the solution category exists.

Use:

- New mechanism
- Breakthrough framing
- Discovery language
- "Without" clauses
- Specific benefit plus credible reason

---

### Awareness Level 4: Problem-Aware / Product-Unaware

The prospect recognizes a need or problem but does not connect the solution to your product.

This is the "problem-solving" ad.

#### Recommended Headline Approach

Lead with the problem or dramatize the problem until the prospect feels they cannot ignore it.

#### Examples

- "Plaque in Your Arteries?"
- "If You Have No Clue What to Say to an Attractive Woman..."

#### Agent Guidance

Use when the market knows it has pain but does not yet know the solution. The headline should make the problem feel personal, urgent, and solvable.

Good formulas:

- "Do You Struggle With [Problem]?"
- "Why [Painful Problem] Keeps Happening - And What to Do About It"
- "The Hidden Reason [Problem] Won't Go Away"
- "If [Problem] Has Ever Made You Feel [Emotion], Read This"

---

### Awareness Level 5: Completely Unaware

The prospect is not aware of the desire, need, product, or solution - or they will not admit it consciously.

This is the hardest market to reach.

At this level, the PDF says price, product name, product function, and benefit claims usually do not work.

#### What Is Left?

The prospect's current state of mind.

#### Recommended Headline Approach

Use an identification headline.

The goal is to echo an emotion, attitude, frustration, dissatisfaction, fear, or worldview that binds the audience together.

#### Examples Mentioned

- "Shameless Two-Faced S.O.B.'s!"
- "Had ENOUGH?"

These headlines do not make a direct product promise. They call out the prospect's emotional state.

#### Agent Guidance

Use this approach when:

- The market is highly skeptical
- Product claims are ignored
- The prospect has heard everything before
- The product category is crowded
- Prospects feel anger, fear, betrayal, embarrassment, or frustration
- The dominant emotion is stronger than the product benefit

## Advertorial Progression for Unaware or Highly Skeptical Markets

For Level 5 awareness or Stage 5 sophistication, the PDF describes an advertorial-style progression:

1. Start with an identification headline that echoes the prospect's emotion.
2. Build awareness of the problem or desire.
3. Intensify the emotional significance of the problem.
4. Reveal that a solution exists.
5. Focus the solution onto the product.
6. Prove the product is the best solution.
7. Make the offer and ask for action.

This is useful when a direct claim would trigger skepticism.

## Market Sophistication

Market sophistication describes how many similar claims, products, or mechanisms the prospect has already seen.

As a market becomes more sophisticated:

- Simple claims stop working.
- Larger claims may temporarily work.
- Mechanisms become necessary.
- Mechanisms themselves become crowded.
- Eventually, the marketer must return to deep emotional identification.

The PDF presents five stages.

---

### Sophistication Stage 1: First in the Market

The market has never received information about this kind of product before. Prospects are less skeptical because the claim is new.

#### Recommended Approach

Be simple. Be direct. Do not get fancy.

Name the need or the claim in the headline. Dramatize the claim in the copy. Introduce the product and prove it works.

#### Example

Classic self-improvement headline:

> How to Win Friends and Influence People

#### Agent Guidance

When first in a market, clarity beats cleverness.

Use:

- Direct benefit
- Simple promise
- Clear proof
- Strong explanation

---

### Sophistication Stage 2: Second in the Market

Competition has entered, but the original promise still works.

#### Recommended Approach

Copy the successful claim but enlarge it. Drive it to the limit. Outbid the competition.

#### Example Pattern

Original claim:

> Make six figures as a copywriter.

Expanded claim:

> Make six figures in your first year as a copywriter.

Further expanded:

> Make $300,000 to $400,000 in your first year as a copywriter.

#### Agent Guidance

Use bigger, faster, easier, more dramatic versions of the same core promise - as long as the market still believes the promise.

---

### Sophistication Stage 3: The Market Has Heard It All Before

Prospects have seen the same promises repeatedly. Skepticism increases.

The underlying desire still exists, but the old claims lose power.

#### Recommended Approach

Reveal a new mechanism.

A mechanism is the specific reason the benefit can now be achieved in a new, fresh, believable way.

#### Examples from the PDF

- Lose weight by cutting carbs - Atkins
- Protect arteries and reverse heart disease with Vitamin D - Instant Sunshine
- Lose weight by reducing the stress hormone cortisol - Cortislim
- Grow rich in real estate with nothing down - Robert Allen
- Get fit at home using a new Bowflex machine - Bowflex

#### Hair Loss Example

Early market:

> The Cure for Baldness

Expanded claim:

> How to Re-grow Your Hair Back in 30 Days or Less

Mechanism-driven claim:

> New Ionic Comb Slows Hair Loss and Re-grows Hair by Up to 50%

Another mechanism-driven claim:

> Scientists Discover Herbal DHT Blocker That Stops Hair Loss at the Source

#### Agent Guidance

When prospects have heard the benefit before, lead with the new reason the benefit is now believable.

Mechanism types:

- New ingredient
- New method
- New technology
- New discovery
- New timing
- New process
- New category
- New cause of the problem
- Newly revealed hidden factor
- Recent event that changes the opportunity

---

### Sophistication Stage 4: Mechanisms Compete

Competitors see a mechanism working and copy it. Soon everyone claims to have a unique mechanism.

#### Recommended Approach

Two options:

1. Find a completely new mechanism.
2. Enlarge and expand the existing mechanism.

#### Example

If everyone has an aphrodisiac herb, claim the superior version:

- More potent
- More bioavailable
- Faster acting
- Clinically tested
- Concentrated extract
- Stronger dosage
- Better delivery system

#### Agent Guidance

At this stage, the mechanism itself must be differentiated.

Ask:

- What makes our mechanism different?
- What makes it stronger or more credible?
- What proof supports this version?
- Can we introduce a new sub-mechanism?
- Can we reframe the mechanism around a new enemy or hidden cause?

---

### Sophistication Stage 5: Nothing Else Works

The market disbelieves almost everything. Prospects know the benefits, claims, and mechanisms. They are heavily advertised to and highly skeptical.

#### Recommended Approach

Return to the prospect.

Do not lead with:

- Product name
- Mechanism
- Benefit
- Price
- Direct promise

Instead, call out the prospect's deepest emotional state.

#### Headline Goal

The prospect should see the headline and feel:

> "That's exactly how I feel."

#### Agent Guidance

This is where dominant emotion headlines are most important. The copy should begin by articulating the prospect's inner world better than they can articulate it themselves.

Use when:

- The category is saturated
- Prospects are jaded
- Claims feel unbelievable
- Mechanisms feel overused
- Emotional identification is the only remaining door into attention

## Stage 5 / Dominant Emotion Headline Guidance

The PDF gives practical advice for writing headlines for highly aware and sophisticated prospects.

### 1. Know the Prospect Deeply

You must know:

- Their hopes
- Their fears
- Their frustrations
- Their embarrassments
- Their anger
- Their disappointments
- Their secret desires
- Their past failed attempts
- Their current skepticism

Best-case scenario: become an actual prospect in the market yourself.

If selling heart-health supplements, become passionate about heart health. Read what the prospect reads. Try products. Talk to people in the market. Understand their lived experience.

### 2. Put Their Strongest Feelings Into Words

Ask:

- What enrages them?
- What embarrasses them?
- What terrifies them?
- What feels unfair?
- What do they secretly suspect?
- What do they wish they could say out loud?
- What betrayal do they feel?
- What outcome are they afraid is inevitable?

Do not censor too early. The PDF encourages boldness, energy, risk, and emotional honesty.

### 3. Study Winning Headlines

The mind needs inputs. Study previous successful headlines from great copywriters and proven campaigns.

Look especially for headlines that address:

- Highly skeptical prospects
- Highly aware prospects
- Mature markets
- Emotional identification
- Advertorial-style openings
- Dominant emotion leads

### 4. Practice Constantly

Practice by putting people's feelings into dramatic single statements.

Example exercise:

1. Talk to a person about a frustration.
2. Ask them how it feels.
3. Write several statements that express the emotion.
4. Show the statements back to them.
5. Notice which statements make them react with recognition.
6. Refine until the line feels like a direct hit.

This is a skill, not a random gift.

## Practical Process for Applying These Ideas

Use this workflow when creating a campaign, headline, landing page, email, advertorial, or sales letter.

### Step 1: Identify the Existing Desire

Ask:

- What does this market already want?
- What pain are they already trying to escape?
- What outcome are they already seeking?
- What emotional charge already exists?

Do not begin by listing product features. Begin with desire.

### Step 2: Identify the Dominant Resident Emotion

Ask:

- Are they angry?
- Afraid?
- Embarrassed?
- Hopeful?
- Skeptical?
- Confused?
- Betrayed?
- Exhausted?
- Greedy?
- Proud?
- Desperate?

The dominant resident emotion is often the best entry point.

### Step 3: Determine State of Awareness

Classify the prospect:

1. Most aware
2. Product-aware but not convinced
3. Desire-aware / solution-unaware
4. Problem-aware / product-unaware
5. Completely unaware

### Step 4: Determine Market Sophistication

Classify the market:

1. First in market
2. Second / early competition
3. Heard claims before - need mechanism
4. Mechanisms competing - need stronger or new mechanism
5. Saturated - need dominant emotion / identification

### Step 5: Choose the Headline Type

Match headline style to the awareness and sophistication level.

| Situation | Best Headline Direction |
|---|---|
| Prospect knows product and wants it | Deal / price / urgency |
| Prospect knows product but needs convincing | Superiority / proof / differentiation |
| Prospect wants result but does not know solution | New mechanism / breakthrough |
| Prospect knows problem but not product | Problem dramatization |
| Prospect is unaware or skeptical | Identification / dominant emotion |
| Market is new | Simple direct claim |
| Market has competitors | Enlarged claim |
| Market has heard all claims | New mechanism |
| Mechanisms are crowded | Stronger mechanism or new mechanism |
| Market believes nothing | Emotional identification / advertorial |

### Step 6: Build the Ad Progression

For mature markets, use this structure:

1. Identification headline
2. Emotional or situational opening
3. Problem crystallization
4. Consequences of the problem
5. Discovery of solution
6. Mechanism explanation
7. Product as best embodiment of solution
8. Proof
9. Offer
10. Call to action

## Agent-Ready Decision Framework

When analyzing a product or market, the marketing agent should answer:

### Market Desire

- What is the existing mass desire?
- How urgent is it?
- Is it conscious or buried?
- Is it emotional, practical, social, financial, sexual, health-related, status-related, or survival-related?

### Prospect Awareness

- Does the prospect know the product?
- Does the prospect know the category?
- Does the prospect know the problem?
- Does the prospect know the desire?
- Is the prospect unaware or unwilling to admit the desire?

### Market Sophistication

- How many similar products has the prospect seen?
- How many claims have they heard?
- Are they skeptical of the main promise?
- Are mechanisms already common?
- Has the market reached emotional saturation?

### Headline Strategy

- Should the headline lead with price?
- Should it lead with a stronger claim?
- Should it lead with a new mechanism?
- Should it lead with the problem?
- Should it lead with dominant emotion?

### Mechanism Strategy

- What is the mechanism?
- Is it new enough?
- Is it credible?
- Is it specific?
- Can it make an old promise feel fresh?
- Can it be expanded or dramatized?

### Emotional Strategy

- What does the prospect secretly feel?
- What would make them say, "Yes, that's me"?
- What are they tired of hearing?
- What do they no longer believe?
- What emotional doorway remains open?

## Quick Reference Cheat Sheet

### Core Rule

Do not create desire. Channel existing desire.

### Headline Rule

A headline's job is to meet the prospect at their current level of awareness and get them to read the next sentence.

### Three Questions

1. What mass desire creates this market?
2. How aware is the prospect of the way your product satisfies that desire?
3. How many similar products or claims has the prospect seen before?

### Five Levels of Awareness

1. **Most aware:** Name product + deal.
2. **Product-aware:** Show superiority.
3. **Desire-aware:** Introduce solution/mechanism.
4. **Problem-aware:** Dramatize the problem.
5. **Unaware:** Echo the prospect's emotional state.

### Five Stages of Sophistication

1. **First in market:** Simple direct claim.
2. **Second in market:** Enlarge the claim.
3. **Heard it before:** Reveal a new mechanism.
4. **Mechanisms competing:** New or stronger mechanism.
5. **Nothing works:** Return to prospect identity and emotion.

### Mechanism Definition

A mechanism is the specific reason the promise can now be believed.

Examples:

- Cutting carbs
- Vitamin D
- Stress hormone cortisol
- Nothing-down real estate
- Bowflex machine
- DHT blocker
- Satellite-based price finder

### Dominant Emotion Headline Definition

A headline that does not lead with product or benefit, but with the prospect's intense emotional state.

Examples:

- "Had ENOUGH?"
- "Shameless Two-Faced S.O.B.'s!"

### Mature Market Advertorial Flow

1. Identify with emotion.
2. Build problem awareness.
3. Intensify the dissatisfaction.
4. Reveal the solution exists.
5. Explain the mechanism.
6. Position the product as the best solution.
7. Prove it.
8. Make the offer.

## Application Prompt for a Marketing Agent

Use the following prompt internally when applying this framework:

```text
Analyze this product and market using Breakthrough Advertising principles.

1. Identify the existing mass desire.
2. Identify the prospect's dominant resident emotion.
3. Determine the prospect's state of awareness.
4. Determine the market's state of sophistication.
5. Decide whether the headline should lead with:
   - price/deal,
   - product superiority,
   - new mechanism,
   - problem dramatization,
   - or dominant emotion.
6. If the market is sophisticated, define the mechanism that makes the promise believable.
7. If the market is saturated, write identification headlines that verbalize the prospect's emotional state.
8. Build the campaign progression from emotion/problem to solution/mechanism to product/proof/offer.
```

## Final Takeaway

The PDF's primary strategic lesson is that copywriting is not primarily about clever writing. It is about accurately diagnosing the market's existing desire, awareness, sophistication, skepticism, and emotional state - then choosing the headline and sales argument that fits that exact moment in the market.

A great marketing agent should therefore think like a market diagnostician before it thinks like a writer.
