## What This Reference Is For

This is a knowledge file for a marketing AI agent. It captures the frameworks, decision criteria, and craft principles from Nancy Duarte's *Slidedocs* in a structured form optimized for retrieval and application. Use it whenever the task involves: producing visual business documents, deciding between a presentation and a document, structuring sales collateral, building modular reusable content, or critiquing/improving slide-based deliverables.

## Part 0 — Core Concept: What a Slidedoc Is

A **slidedoc** is a visual document built in presentation software (PowerPoint, Keynote, Google Slides) that is intended to be **read and referenced — not projected and presented**. It sits between two extremes:

- **Dense documents** — exhaustive, prose-heavy, hard to scan, easy to ignore
- **Sparse presentation slides** — minimalist, cinematic, meaningless without a presenter

A slidedoc combines the best of both: scannable like a slide, substantive like a document. One clear idea per page, words and visuals integrated on the same page, designed for self-guided reading.

### The Three Format Spectrum

| Attribute | Document | Slidedoc | Presentation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Exhaustive reference | Explanatory | Cinematic story |
| Structure | Topical | Modular | Dramatic |
| Process | Analytical | Visual thinking | Creative |
| Density | Visually dense | Tight visual-to-prose ratio | Visually sparse |
| Mode | Read | Read quickly | Heard |
| Guide | Self-guided | Self-guided | Presenter-guided |
| Words/page | 500+ | ~100 (up to 250 dense) | 1–10 |

### Why Slidedocs Work
- The slide format forces a single idea per page, encouraging clear articulation.
- Editable nature makes them living, collaborative documents.
- Outline/sorter views let you see the whole structure at once.
- Modular pages spread easily — great pages get reused across the organization.

## Part 1 — The Case for Slidedocs (When to Use One)

### The Communication Shift
Modern business operates in compressed time. People prefer short, visual chunks (tweets, blog posts, texts). Long prose documents go unread. Meanwhile, defaulting to PowerPoint for everything has produced terrible projected read-along slides that are too dense to present and too sparse to stand alone.

### Presentations vs. Conversations: The Decision

| Use a **Presentation** when you... | Use a **Conversation** (with a slidedoc) when you... |
|---|---|
| Need to communicate an already-formed idea | Need to develop, build on, or reach consensus on an idea |
| Already understand the audience's needs | Need more information about the group's needs |
| Need to inform, persuade, or entertain | Need to build a personal relationship |
| Don't need real-time feedback | Need the group's input to move forward |
| Want a single event to drive an objective | Need ongoing engagement |

### Two Diagnostic Questions
1. **Do people need to hear the message directly from you?** → Presentation
2. **Does the subject require detailed context to understand?** → Slidedoc

### Use Cases by Communication Type

**Lean toward Slidedoc:**
- Proposals, research findings, earnings reports, client strategy docs
- Sales sheets, RFIs, case studies, white papers, datasheets
- Internal: reports, briefs/memos, corporate strategy, execution plans, appendices, status updates
- Pre-reads, follow-up material, modular sales collateral, reference handouts

**Lean toward Presentation:**
- Press briefings, investor presentations, executive briefings, VC pitches
- Keynote talks, product launches, analyst presentations, sales presentations
- Internal: lectures/training, internal launches, board meetings, vision/all-hands meetings

### The Four Roles a Slidedoc Plays
1. **Pre-read** — Distributed before a meeting so the meeting itself can focus on consensus-building rather than information transfer.
2. **Emissary** — Stands in for you when an executive says "send me your slides" before booking a meeting.
3. **Reference material** — Available during a discussion so people can refer to specific pages without losing their place.
4. **Follow-up material** — Answers the post-presentation question: *"How do I embrace your idea?"* (Great as modular sales collateral.)

### Why This Benefits the Audience
- **Forced concision** — space limits make the writer boil content to its essence
- **Visualized ideas** — readers "see" what you're saying, reaching consensus faster
- **Time savings** — reading is faster than listening to a presentation
- **Consensus building** — discussion is richer when everyone has the same prepared context
- **Shorter time to understanding** — chunked, structured, visualized content is grasped quickly

### Three Ways to Produce Effective Slidedocs
1. **Convert documents to slidedocs** — Rechunk dense prose, turn words into pictures.
2. **Convert teleprompter text to slidedocs** — When slides are awkwardly mid-length (neither cinematic slide nor full document), either shorten for projection or expand for stand-alone use.
3. **Convert sparse slides to slidedocs** — After a talk, embed your simple slides as graphics in a slidedoc with longer prose context.

## Part 2 — Writing a Slidedoc

### Foundation Principles

**Know your audience first.** People aren't motivated by what's important to *you*; they're motivated by *"What's in it for me?"* Make the benefit to the reader explicit.

**State your Big Idea.** Every slidedoc needs one. A Big Idea has three required components, written as a complete sentence:
1. Your unique point of view
2. What's at stake for those who do or don't adopt that point of view
3. Expressed as one complete sentence (not a topic phrase)

*Example Big Idea:* "Slidedocs spread your message through modular content that empowers people to quickly understand and easily share your ideas."

**One idea per page.** This eliminates distraction and keeps the writer honest. If a page is getting crowded, you've strayed off topic — either edit down or split into a new page.

### Recommended Length by Use Case

| Use | Distribution | Length |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-read (before meeting) | Sent ahead | ≤ 20 min reading time |
| Pre-read (start of meeting) | Read in room | ≤ 10 pages (10 min) |
| Emissary (sent to executive) | Sent | ~5 slides — exec will fill in gaps |
| Reference (during a talk) | Distributed | ≤ 10 pages (or they'll read instead of listen) |
| Reference (deep detail) | Sent before/after | Up to 50 pages |
| Follow-up collateral | Sent after | ~10 pages |
| Follow-up book/comprehensive | Sent after | Up to 200 pages |
| Sent to potential client | Sent | ≤ 10 pages — answer all buying questions |

### The Editorial Process: Ideate → Create → Refine

**Step 1: Ideate.** Open presentation software. Outline the main sections, then drop in pages with topic titles under each. Treat slides like 3×5 index cards — easy to rearrange. Walk the team through the wall of pages and make sure the flow works.

**Step 2: Create.** Spill all your thoughts onto the pages. Don't filter. Aim for **quantity, not quality**. Use each page as a bucket for ideas on that topic. This bypasses mental blocks.

**Step 3: Refine.** Now craft. Make every word and sentence serve the page's main idea and the slidedoc's Big Idea. Bring the team back in for critique. Repost the wall, audit the flow, ensure information is properly chunked.

### Writing Scannable Copy

Use these elements (and design them consistently):
- **Headlines** — 6–10 word titles around the page's main topic
- **Subheads** — the thesis or summary of the page's main point
- **Paragraphs** — sentences clustered into complete thoughts
- **Bullets** — full sentences in parallel structure (not fragments)
- **Pull-quotes** — for emphasis; either inline or boxed

### The Word-Count Discipline
- Target **~100 words per page** for "concise" slidedocs.
- Up to **250 words per page** counts as "dense."
- More than 250 words → make a document instead.
- Constraint forces clarity, prevents reader fatigue, and leaves room for visuals.

### Develop Titles With Meaning
Weak title: *"Network Router Options"*
Strong title: *"Fast Network Routers Speed Time to Market"*

The strong title introduces a unique point of view (it's *fast*) and a benefit to the audience (faster time-to-market). After writing the body, audit each title:
1. Does the body support the title?
2. Does the title support the slidedoc's overall message?
3. Is it concise (under two lines)?

### Topical Structure Beats Categorical Outlines
A traditional outline reads like a list of categories ("I. Introduction"). A topical outline reads like a story arc, where each title is itself a thesis statement. When you read the outline view of your slidedoc, the page titles in sequence should form a logical, persuasive arc — not a glossary.

### Good Writing Practices
- **Use active voice.** Hunt for forms of "to be" — they often signal passive voice. Active voice: subject does action, at the start of the sentence.
  - Passive: "The new version was developed in 9 months."
  - Active: "We developed the new version in 9 months."
- **Use full sentences**, not fragmented bullets. Fragments require a presenter; slidedocs don't have one.
- **Reference:** William Zinsser, *On Writing Well*.

### Compelling Copy Techniques
- **Emotional appeal** — Decisions are made from the gut and then rationalized. Use emotive visuals, surprising statistics, and stories.
- **Make benefits explicit** — Show the reader what they get by aligning with you.
- **Use analogies** — Comparing similar/different ideas aids understanding and recall.
- **Cite examples** — Case studies and proof points help readers move through the decision process.

### Edit Ruthlessly: Amplify the Essential
Cut anything that isn't a main point you want the reader to remember. The more content you leave in, the more reader attention scatters; less content means more control over what readers take away. Strunk & White: *"When a sentence becomes stronger, it usually becomes shorter. Thus, brevity is a by-product of vigor."*

### Chunking
Structure material in small, uniform bits of discourse that build toward a larger point. This makes information look less intimidating and lets readers pick up where they left off after interruptions.

### Critique for Excellence
Don't wait for a polished draft. Get peer review early. Group editing works especially well with slidedocs because pages can be laid out in a room and discussed individually. Have reviewers:
- Suggest cuts
- Look for reasoning/execution flaws
- Strengthen titles
- Validate structure and flow
- Improve examples, phrases, or data

### Introduction and Summary
Slidedocs (unlike presentations) often have an introduction and/or summary. Write the intro after the slidedoc is done — it should derive from your page titles. If you can't write a coherent summary from your titles, your structure is flawed.

## Part 3 — Architecture of a Slidedoc (Borrowing From Books)

A great slidedoc borrows information architecture from books: cover, table of contents, chapter indicators, page numbers, headers/footers, end matter.

### The Cover
Like a book cover. Include:
- A **conceptual visual** that grabs attention
- A **snappy, intuitive title** (not "Q4 Strategy" — try "Land Grab: Competitive Shake-up in Q4")
- A subtitle
- Author's name
- Company logo treated as a *publisher* — small and discrete (think book spine), not a giant corporate stamp

### Table of Contents (TOC)
- Section heads, section names, page numbers
- TOC items can be hyperlinked to jump within the document
- A reader should "get" what the slidedoc is about just from reading the TOC
- **Write the TOC last** — it's a derivative of the final outline view
- Don't add page numbers until they're locked in

### Headers and Footers
- Use the footer for **confidentiality/copyright** information — this travels with the page when individual slides spread.
- Always include **page numbers** so readers can jump to specific pages during discussion (presentations don't show page numbers; slidedocs require them).
- The "header" setting in PowerPoint actually appears on the notes page.

### Section Heads
- Like book chapter dividers — make them visually distinct from content pages.
- Use color floods, bold graphics, or memorable type.
- Contrast cues the reader that they've entered a new section.

### Section Signifiers (Visual Devices for Navigation)
- **Tabs** — mimic physical tabbed dividers
- **Color coding** — assign each section its own color; use color bars or full-page color floods
- **Distinct layout** — the section page itself looks different from content pages
- **Graphical device** — a recurring diagram acts as a navigational map, with the current section highlighted

### Text Emphasis Techniques
- Color or effect (bold, italic) on text
- Pull-out boxes with framed text
- Increase text size to break the grid
- Set text inside a graphic that adds visual metaphor

### Strong Figure-to-Text Relationship
**Critical advantage of slidedocs:** the words and graphic that explain each other live on the same page. No "see Figure 4 (page 73)" book problem. One idea + supporting visual + supporting text, all visible at once.

### Interactive Navigation
Digital slidedocs benefit from:
- Direct-to-section clicks
- Cookie trail / breadcrumbs
- Hyperlinks (internal and external)
- Buttons
- Tabs

### End Matter Options
- **Summary** — recap the main points
- **Activities** — quizzes/worksheets that test learning
- **Discussion topics** — provocative questions for follow-up
- **Glossary** — define your distinct vocabulary or acronyms
- **Appendix** — supporting research and statistics for deep readers
- **Additional info / reference pages** — links to forums, papers, further reading
- **Index** — alphabetical key words tied to page numbers

## Part 4 — Data and Diagrams

Data and diagrams are content. They're not decoration — they create meaning.

### Data Principles
- Don't just "stick a chart on a page." Determine the **finding** the data supports and write prose around that finding.
- Strip chart decoration — gradients, 3D effects, unnecessary borders, tick marks. Decorated data looks manipulated and erodes credibility with analytical audiences.
- **Avoid 3D charts** — perspective skews the actual numbers and makes them hard to read.

### The Three-Layer Data Chart Model
Build every chart in three layers:
1. **Background** — tick marks, scales, legends, grid lines. Use **neutral colors** for context.
2. **Data** — plot all data in a neutral color first.
3. **Emphasis** — highlight the specific data points that support your conclusion in a contrasting bright color.

The result keeps all data visible while focusing the reader's eye on the takeaway.

### Diagram Taxonomy: Five Diagram Types
Putting words in shapes and connecting them creates meaning. Choose the diagram type whose visual logic matches the relationship you're describing.

| Type | What it shows | Connection method | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Flow** | Direction or movement | Arrows | Process, cycle, timeline, gears, sequence |
| **Network** | Hierarchy or relationship | Lines | Tree, mind-map, cluster, ecosystem, radial, web |
| **Segment** | Parts of a whole | Divisions | Pie, donut, circle, core |
| **Stack** | Ascending/descending order | Stacked shapes | Bar chart, concentric circles |
| **Join** | Interconnection / shared values | Touching/interlocking shapes | Puzzle, Venn, link |

### Diagram Sub-Patterns

**Flow** — *Loop* (closed cycle), *Parallel* (no shapes intersect), *Linear* (sequential, may bend), *Merge & Divide* (shapes split or combine).

**Network** — *Hub and Spokes* (center hub, radial expansion), *Flare* (vertical/horizontal burst), *Spokes* (radiate without a hub), *Ring* (closed-loop exterior connections).

**Segment** — *Pie* (slices to a center point), *Donut* (slices around a hole/hub).

**Stack** — *Horizontal* or *Vertical*.

**Join** — *Hook* (interlocking with hook-and-eye shapes), *Overlap* (Venn-style shared space).

## Part 5 — Designing a Slidedoc

### Visual Systems Unify
Establish a consistent visual language so readers associate the look with you/your brand. Bake it into a template so employees produce on-brand slidedocs by default.

A consistent visual language has:
- **A constrained color palette** — 3–5 colors, plus a neutral and a highlight color (always include at least 2 neutrals)
- **A photography library** that looks like one photographer shot it all
- **An illustration library** that's relevant to your industry and stylistically consistent (avoid clip art)
- **A defined typesetting hierarchy** (titles, subtitles, body, captions all distinctly styled)

### Make Slidedocs and Presentations Visually Distinct
Train your organization to recognize at a glance whether a deck is meant to be read or projected. Differentiation options:

| Choice | Slidedoc | Presentation |
|---|---|---|
| **Aspect ratio** | 4:3 (prints well, fits letter paper) | 16:9 (wide projector format) |
| **Orientation** | Portrait (mimics document) | Landscape |
| **Animation** | Static (often printed) | Animates |
| **Word density** | Higher (up to 175+ words) | Sparse (sometimes one word) |
| **Background color** | Light/white (print- and read-friendly) | Dark (formal projection) |
| **Document features** | TOC, page numbers, section heads | Cinematic, emotive visuals only |

### When to Bring in a Professional Designer
- High-stakes situations (the deal, the vision adoption)
- When your team's time is too valuable to spend laying out slides
- When templates need an upgrade (don't force-fit; commission new ones)
- When slidedocs have too much content and you can't cut more
- When slides are highly complex (heavy typography + detailed diagrams)

Rule of thumb: if the importance of the message exceeds the cost of the design firm, hire a pro.

### Grids: The Skeleton
Grids organize content. They give images a "spine" to align to and determine column widths for text. Slidedocs need grids more than presentations do because slidedocs are content-dense.

- **Default classic slidedoc grid: 4×3** (4 columns wide × 3 rows tall = 12 cells).
- The 4×3 grid has 892 unique partition options (Dubberly Design has a poster of all of them).
- Long text blocks should always run in **columns** — never the full slide width.

### White Space: Room to Breathe
- White space (also "open space" or "clear space") makes content feel premium.
- Surround key points with white space to draw the eye.
- Luxury brands rely on white space — same principle applies to slidedocs.
- Historical reference: Alexey Brodovitch revolutionized magazine design at *Harper's Bazaar* (from 1934) using white space and dramatic image cropping.

### Typesetting

**Why it matters:** Reading slows and retention drops when line lengths exceed the eye's natural visual field at normal reading distance.

**Column width rule:** Each line of text should be **50–70 characters** (or 12–15 words). Don't use the full slide width — break into 2 or 3 columns.

**Two failure modes:**
- *Too wide* — eyes lose their place returning to the left margin
- *Too narrow* — eyes ricochet back and forth, fatiguing the reader

### Text Hierarchy

Establish hierarchy rules **before** you start laying out copy. Edit easily by predefined rules rather than ad-hoc. Define how each text role looks:

- **Title** — the first thing people read; make it dominant (bold, large, all caps, distinctive position)
- **Subtitle** — directly below title; expands the title; smaller than title but larger than body
- **Subhead** — labels sections within a page; smaller than subtitle; differentiated by weight or color
- **Body copy** — the workhorse; classic clean fonts; large enough and dark enough not to strain
- **Callouts** — point out items in graphs/diagrams; same size as body but italicized or different color

**On color discipline:** Don't make a "text rainbow." Color is one tool among many — weight, CASE, size, italics also differentiate. Use them.

### Typesetting Refinements (Detail Polish)
- **Paragraph spacing** — gives readers a break and signals topic change; makes large blocks less intimidating
- **Columns** — split big chunks into 2+ columns for readability
- **Line spacing** — enough room between lines to keep place comfortably
- **Alignment** — left-aligned usually wins; justified text creates ugly word-gaps; align callouts toward the graphic they describe so they feel connected
- **Final cleanup** — fix jagged right edges, eliminate orphan words, use soft returns (Shift+Return) for the last polish

### Small Screen Readability
Don't assume desktop reading. Recommended font sizes:

| Element | Tablet (Retina) | Phone (Retina) | 13" Laptop | Print |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Slide Title | 40 pt | 50 pt | 22 pt | 21 pt |
| Subtitle | 28 pt | 38 pt | 18 pt | 16 pt |
| Body Copy | 24 pt | 34 pt | 11 pt | 10 pt |
| Graphic Callouts | 30 pt | 40 pt | 10 pt | 8 pt |

### Slidedoc Self-Audit Checklist
Before shipping, walk through each page and ask:
- [ ] Too many words on this slide?
- [ ] Is text big enough — no straining?
- [ ] Does my eye know what to read first?
- [ ] Are main messages easy to pick out?
- [ ] Is the text scannable (slidedoc) or dense (document)?
- [ ] Does my eye have a clear path to follow?
- [ ] Too many fonts or font colors?

Also: review the whole deck in **slide sorter / lightbox view**. Pages should not all be text-heavy. There should be visible white space and breathing room throughout.

## Part 6 — Delivering and Distributing Slidedocs

### Print Formats
- **Handout** — read at meeting start; reference during a talk; appendix support
- **Book** — spiral-bound or perfect-bound; feels substantial and formal; printable at services like blurb.com or bookemon.com
- **Placemat** — single page printed on 11×17 paper for collaborative annotation; one placemat can spark a great conversation
- **Poster** — oversized pages posted around a room; readers walk and read (common at scientific conferences); blank posters between can capture notes

### When It's OK to Project a Slidedoc
Generally, **don't project slidedocs — print them**. Acceptable exceptions:
- **Collaboration** — projecting a single page to discuss and refine in detail
- **Review meeting** — collaborative editing with a small group
- **Visual reference** — during a formal presentation, briefly project the page you've asked the audience to read along with
- **Overlay sketches** — projecting onto a whiteboard so people can sketch over graphics

If projection is happening, the room should typically be **≤ 10 internal people**. For 6+ people externally, reconsider whether to use a presentation instead.

### The Four Bad Habits to Break

**Don't verbally present slidedocs.** The four reasons people fall into this trap:

1. **Slides as teleprompter** — Authors load every word onto slides so they don't forget. The audience suffers a read-along. Fix: build a real visual aid for projection, or distribute as a document.
2. **No time to prepare a real presentation** — Slidedocs are the dump for all thinking; the next step is distilling into a true visual aid. If there's no time for that step, just hand the slidedoc out.
3. **Dense slides look smart** — Analytical roles often equate density with rigor. But projecting layered charts is a fundamentally different experience than letting people read and interpret charts in a slidedoc.
4. **Desire for accreditation** — Presenting feels like ownership. But this is pride of authorship, not communication efficiency.

### Online Distribution

**SlideShare (LinkedIn) capabilities:**
- **Embed** anywhere a YouTube video can go
- **Email** a link instead of a fat attachment that clogs inboxes
- **Share** broadly and publicly
- **Analytics** — track who saw the file, capture leads

### Webinars and Remote Meetings
Most presentations now happen remotely. Attendees compete with their inboxes. Use a slidedoc to keep them engaged:
- Have attendees read one page, then discuss
- Each new page forces multitaskers to refocus
- Don't read aloud — let them read it
- Use the slidedoc as a topic guide and progress meter
- Send ahead of time as a tech-failure failsafe

### Mobile / Tablet Distribution
The 4:3 aspect ratio is perfect for tablets. Tools mentioned:
- **SlideShark** — converts PowerPoint to interactive tablet presentations with analytics
- **Keynote for iPad** — view and edit Keynote/PowerPoint files

### The Sparse-Slide Problem (and the Notes-View Fix)

Beautifully sparse cinematic slides go vague when they leave the room without the speaker. Recipients fill in blanks incorrectly. Misinterpretation spreads.

**Fix: PowerPoint Notes Page view.** Modify the Notes master to combine your slide image with your script and supporting content. The notes-page layout is fully customizable:
- Scale and reposition the slide image
- Add text, charts, quotes, additional images
- Convert Notes view to PDF for distribution
- Available in **vertical (portrait) or horizontal (landscape)** layouts

This lets cinematic slides travel as effective standalone documents.

## Part 7 — Spreading Slidedocs: The Marketing Multiplier

### Why Slidedocs Are Marketing-Powerful

The **modular nature** is the key marketing advantage. Pages are atomic, organized, and reusable. This enables:
- **Audience-specific assemblies** — mix and match pages for different prospects, verticals, or stakeholders
- **Agile sales enablement** — reps assemble the right collateral without learning new software
- **Idea spread within an organization** — great pages get adopted, copied, and re-expressed across departments
- **Marketing standardization** — marketing departments use slidedocs to seed key messaging into all employee communications

### The Risk of Frankendecks
If your slides aren't visually unified or content-rich, recipients can tell when pages are stitched together from different decks. A consistent visual language and a single-idea-per-page discipline keep the modular advantage intact.

### When the Slidedoc Itself Is High-Stakes
Slidedocs are usually pragmatic; presentations are usually inspirational. But when the slidedoc itself must influence a make-or-break decision, it needs the inspirational, persuasive structure usually reserved for in-person presentations: good content, a clear storyline, and a structure that compels action.

### Pairing Slidedocs With Presentations
When you need to *present* a slidedoc:
1. **Don't present the research — present the findings.** Identify the key insights from the slidedoc.
2. **Use a different creative process for the presentation.** Inspirational, story-led, audience-transforming. Not analytical.
3. **Brainstorm metaphors, stories, and visuals** that make the data come alive verbally.
4. The slidedoc remains the comprehensive reference; the presentation is the persuasive derivative.

(Historical analogy: old printed annual reports — a glossy, story-led front section followed by the dense financial document.)

## Part 8 — Quick Reference for Marketing Agent Decisions

### Deliverable Decision Tree
```
Is the audience reading on their own (no presenter)?
├── YES → Slidedoc
│   ├── Pre-meeting context? → ≤20 min read
│   ├── In-meeting reference? → ≤10 pages
│   ├── Sales collateral? → ≤10 pages, modular
│   ├── Executive emissary? → ~5 slides
│   ├── Comprehensive follow-up? → up to 200 pages
│   └── Detailed appendix? → up to 50 pages
└── NO (live audience, presenter present) → Presentation
    ├── Cinematic, sparse, emotive
    ├── 16:9, animations OK, dark backgrounds OK
    └── Consider distributing a separate slidedoc for follow-up
```

### Format Specs At-a-Glance

**Slidedoc defaults:**
- Aspect ratio: 4:3 (or portrait)
- Background: light/white
- Word count target: ~100/page (up to 250 dense)
- Grid: 4×3
- Line length: 50–70 chars / 12–15 words per line
- Columns: 2–3 (never full width)
- Animation: none
- Page numbers: required
- Footer: confidentiality/copyright on every page
- Logo treatment: small, like a publisher mark

**Presentation defaults:**
- Aspect ratio: 16:9
- Background: often dark
- Word count: 1–10 per slide
- Animations OK
- Cinematic visuals
- No page numbers

### Big Idea Template
> [Subject] [unique action verb] [what's at stake/the benefit] for/through [audience or mechanism].

### Title Quality Test
A strong page title:
1. Is supported by the page body
2. Supports the slidedoc's overall Big Idea
3. States a *point*, not a category
4. Is 6–10 words, max 2 lines
5. When read with adjacent titles, contributes to a logical/persuasive arc

### Copy Compelling Checklist
- [ ] Active voice (avoid "to be" forms)
- [ ] Full sentences, not fragmented bullets
- [ ] Emotional appeal somewhere (visual, statistic, or story)
- [ ] Benefit to reader is explicit
- [ ] At least one analogy, example, or proof point
- [ ] Murdered the darlings — every kept word earns its place

### Data Display Rules
- 3-layer model: neutral background + neutral data + bright emphasis
- No 3D charts
- No gradients / borders / unnecessary tick marks
- Match diagram type to relationship (Flow / Network / Segment / Stack / Join)

### Typography Rules
- 50–70 chars per line
- 2–3 columns
- Left-aligned (not justified)
- Hierarchy defined before layout starts
- Color used sparingly; lean on weight, CASE, size, italics
- Final pass for orphan words / jagged edges / soft returns

### Cross-Channel Distribution Options
1. PDF email attachment
2. SlideShare (with analytics + lead capture)
3. Embedded in HTML pages
4. Tablet-loaded (4:3 fits perfectly)
5. Printed: handout, book, placemat (11×17), poster
6. Notes-view PDF (when based on cinematic slides)
