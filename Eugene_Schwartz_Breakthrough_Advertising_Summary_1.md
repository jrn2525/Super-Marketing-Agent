# Breakthrough Advertising by Eugene Schwartz — Comprehensive Summary

## Introduction & Core Principles

**Central Premise:** The power that makes advertising work comes from the market itself, not from the copy. Copy cannot create desire for a product; it can only channel and direct desires that already exist in the prospect's mind. As Schwartz explains:

> "Let's get right down to the heart of the matter. The power, the force, the overwhelming urge to own that makes advertising work, comes from the market itself, and not from the copy."

This is the foundation of Schwartz's approach — advertising is not about creating desire out of nothing, but harnessing existing market forces. Any attempt to run against these forces is doomed to failure, as demonstrated by numerous automotive marketing failures Schwartz cites, including the Edsel's demise despite enormous marketing expenditure.

### The Copy Writer's Three Core Tasks

1. **Choose the most powerful desire that can be applied to your product** — This requires identifying which of several desires associated with your product has the greatest urgency, staying power, and scope. Only one desire can predominate in your headline.
2. **Acknowledge that desire and offer the means to satisfy it in your headline** — Your headline forms the bridge between your prospect and your product, touching the prospect at their current stage of awareness.
3. **Show how your product's performance will satisfy that desire** — Take the functional attributes of your product and demonstrate how they inevitably fulfill the dominant desire that brought the prospect to your ad.

### Two Aspects of Every Product

- **The physical product** — The actual material goods the manufacturer has shaped (steel, glass, paper, etc.) of which they are proud
- **The functional product** — The product in action — the benefits it delivers to consumers, which is what they're actually buying

Schwartz emphasizes:

> "The physical product does not sell. People do not buy the steel in a car, the glass in a vase, the tobacco in a cigarette, or the paper in a book."

The physical product matters only to the extent that it enables the functional benefits. Physical product attributes serve several important purposes:

- Justifying the price
- Documenting performance quality
- Assuring the prospect of continued performance
- Sharpening the mental picture of performance
- Providing a fresh basis for believability (the point of difference)

### The Amplification Effect of Successful Advertising

When advertising exploits already-existing desires, it commands an economic force hundreds of times more powerful than the mere dollars spent on it. This "Amplification Effect" means that $1 spent on advertising can create $50 or even $100 in sales — but only when tapping into existing desire. When trying to create desire from scratch, you can produce at best only one dollar in sales for every dollar spent on advertising.

# Part 1: The Basic Strategy of Persuasion

## Chapter 1: Mass Desire — The Force That Makes Advertising Work

**Mass Desire Definition:** The public spread of a private want. When a desire is shared by a statistically significant number of people willing to pay to satisfy it, a market is born.

> "It is the moment when a private desire is shared by a statistically significant number of people, large enough to profitably repay selling these people, that a market is born."

### Critical Dimensions of Mass Desire

1. **Urgency** — The intensity or degree of demand for satisfaction. For example, constant arthritic pain creates more urgency than a minor headache.
2. **Staying power** — The degree of repetition and inability to become satiated. Raw hunger has more staying power than a craving for gourmet food.
3. **Scope** — The sheer number of people who share this desire. For instance, more people are willing to pay for automotive accessories that save gas than those that merely prevent future repair bills.

### Nation-wide Forces Creating Mass Desire

**1. Permanent Forces:**

- **Mass Instinct** — Desires that never fade, such as women wanting to be attractive, men wanting to be virile, or everyone wanting to maintain health. The copywriter's challenge here is distinguishing their product from others by creating fresh appeals, stronger believability, or shifting desire from competitors to their product.
- **Mass technological problems** — Ongoing issues like bad TV reception, corroding mufflers, or slow-acting pain relief. Until these problems are fully solved, customers will continue buying and trying solutions. The copywriter must offer the same claim of relief as competitors but in a new, more compelling way.

**2. Forces of Change:**

- **Trends** — The beginning, fulfillment, and reversal of cultural shifts. Examples include the shift from showing status through car size to showing it through swimming pools, or the horsepower appeal of the 1950s giving way to gas economy. The copywriter must have sensitivity and foresight to catch rising tides when they're almost imperceptible.
- **Mass Education** — The combined influence of school education, media, society figures, group pressure, and even the overall effect of all advertising multiplying people's dreams and desires. The copywriter must recognize when shifts become statistically significant enough to act upon.

### Channeling Mass Desire to Your Product

The copywriter performs their work in three stages:

1. **Choose the most powerful applicable desire** — Every product appeals to multiple mass desires, but only one can predominate in your headline. This choice is the most important step you'll take in writing your ad.
2. **Acknowledge and offer satisfaction for that desire in your headline** — Your headline must touch your prospect at their current point of awareness, starting either with your product (if they know it can satisfy their desire) or with the desire itself (if they don't yet connect your product to their desire).
3. **Demonstrate how your product's performance satisfies that desire** — Show the inevitable path from your product's features to the fulfillment of the prospect's dominant desire.

## Chapter 2: Your Prospect's State of Awareness

**Your Headline's Real Job:** Contrary to common belief, your headline doesn't need to sell your product, mention your product, or even state your main appeal. Its only job is to stop your prospect and compel them to read the second sentence of your ad.

> "In exactly the same way, your second sentence has only one job — to force him to read the third sentence of your ad."

This chain continues through your entire advertisement. Attempting to accomplish the full selling job in a headline alone (except in very specific circumstances) is essentially shooting craps with your client's money.

### The Five States of Awareness

#### 1. Most Aware

The prospect knows your product, knows what it does, and wants it. They just haven't gotten around to buying it yet.

- **Headline Strategy:** Simply state the product name and a bargain price.
- **Example:** "Revere Zomar Lens, Electric Eye Camera — Formerly $149.50 — Now Only $119.95."
- **Content Needed:** Brief summary of desirable selling points, then store name or coupon.
- **Schwartz's Take:** "This is the typical department store, discount store, mail-order-bargain-catalog type of advertising. It takes advantage of the full weight of all the advertising that has been done on the same product before it."

#### 2. Product-Aware

The prospect knows your product exists but doesn't yet want it — either because they aren't fully convinced of its benefits or haven't been told how much better it does its job now.

- **Headline Strategy:** Display the product name prominently and use the headline to point out its superiority.

**Seven Tasks for Headlines at This Stage:**

- Reinforce desire: "Steinway — The Instrument of the Immortals"
- Sharpen product image: "At 60 miles an hour, the loudest noise in a Rolls Royce is the electric clock"
- Extend usage occasions: "Thirst knows no season — Coca Cola"
- Introduce new proof: "9 out of 10 screen stars use Lux Toilet Soap"
- Announce new mechanism: "Hoover's new invention washes floors and vacuums up the scrub water"
- Announce mechanism that eliminates limitations: "You breathe no dusty odors when you do it with Lewyt"
- Change product image to remove it from competition: "It's What's Up Front That Counts!" (Winston)

**Content Needed:** Elaboration of superiority, including visualization, documentation, and mechanization.

#### 3. Solution-Aware

The prospect recognizes they want what the product does but doesn't know your product will do it for them.

- **Headline Strategy:** Name the desire and/or its solution in your headline.
- **Examples:** "How to win friends and influence people" or "Light a Lucky, and you'll never miss sweets that make you fat"
- **Approach:** Three steps — name the desire/solution in headline, prove it can be accomplished, show your product contains the mechanism for that accomplishment.
- **Skills Required:** Analysis of receptive markets, intuition to sense trends, and verbal creativity to name undefined desires.
- **Content Needed:** Clear communication that your product delivers the solution they seek.

#### 4. Problem-Aware

The prospect has a need (not yet a desire) and recognizes it immediately, but doesn't realize your product can fulfill that need.

- **Headline Strategy:** Name the problem and/or its solution in your headline.

**Examples:**

- Simply naming the problem: "Corns?"
- Stating problem and solution: "Stops maddening itch"
- Promising solution without limitations: "Shrinks hemorrhoids without surgery"
- Offering substitute for unpleasant tasks: "Now! A ring and piston job in a tube!"
- Prevention headlines: "Look, Mom! No cavities!"
- Adding emotional impact: "Do YOU make these mistakes in English?"
- Using verbal twists for novelty: "How a bald-headed barber helped save my hair"

**Content Needed:** Dramatization of the need followed by presentation of your product as the inevitable solution.

#### 5. Completely Unaware

The prospect either doesn't recognize their desire/need, won't admit it, or the need is too amorphous to fit in a headline.

- **Headline Strategy:** Use an indirect approach that connects with the prospect's life and pulls them into the ad.

**What Not To Do:**

- Don't mention price (means nothing to someone who doesn't want your product)
- Don't feature product name (may actually damage your ad if it has negative associations)
- Don't directly state what your product does (either hasn't reached that stage or has passed beyond it)

**What To Do Instead:**

- Write an identification headline that echoes an emotion or attitude
- Define your prospects for themselves
- Sell nothing but reading the remainder of the ad

**Examples:**

- "The University of the Night" (for home correspondence courses)
- "Why Men Crack..." (for coffee substitute)
- "Within the Curve of a Woman's Arm" (for deodorant)
- "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano — But When I Started to Play" (for music lessons)
- "Often a Bridesmaid but Never a Bride" (for mouthwash)

**Content Needed:** Build a progression from identification with the headline, to awareness of the problem, to realization that a solution exists, to focusing on your specific product.

> Schwartz emphasizes that each of these stages is separated from the others by a psychological wall. A headline that works brilliantly for one stage will fail completely when addressed to a market at a different stage.

## Chapter 3: The Sophistication of Your Market

**Market Sophistication:** How many similar products has your market been told about before? This determines how you must position your product and what kinds of claims will be effective.

### Five Stages of Market Sophistication

#### Stage 1: First in the Market

You are first in your market with your particular product.

- **Strategy:** Be simple, direct, and straightforward. Name either the need or the claim in your headline — nothing more.
- **Example:** "NOW! LOSE UGLY FAT!"
- **Approach:** Dramatize your claim in the copy, make it as powerful as possible, and then introduce your product and prove it works.
- **Schwartz's Take:** "Nothing more — because nothing more is needed."

#### Stage 2: Direct Claim Still Working

You're second in the market, and the direct claim is still working.

- **Strategy:** Copy the successful claim but enlarge it. Drive it to the absolute limit. Outbid your competition.

**Examples:**

- "LOSE UP TO 47 POUNDS IN 4 WEEKS — OR RECEIVE $40 BACK!"
- "I AM 61 POUNDS LIGHTER...NEVER A HUNGRY MINUTE."
- The evolution from "PICK 25–50–100 ROSES FROM THIS ONE MAGNIFICENT PLANT!" to "SIX HUNDRED MUMS FROM A SINGLE BUSH!" to "WHO EVER HEARD OF 17,000 BLOOMS FROM A SINGLE PLANT?"

**Caution:** This process eventually reaches the limits of believability. Headlines double and triple in size, words lose meaning, prospects become confused and skeptical, claims are automatically discounted, and sales curves turn downward.

#### Stage 3: Market Has Heard All Claims

Your market has heard all the claims in all their extremes.

- **Strategy:** Provide a new mechanism — a new way to make all the old claims believable again.

**Examples:**

- "FLOATS FAT RIGHT OUT OF YOUR BODY!"
- "FIRST WONDER DRUG FOR REDUCING!"

**Key Shift:** The headline now features the mechanism rather than the claim itself. This shifts emphasis from what the product does to HOW it works.

**Structure Change:** In Second-Stage ads, the entire headline stated the main claim, with mechanism below in smaller type. In Third-Stage ads, this arrangement reverses — the mechanism dominates the headline, with the claim stated below.

#### Stage 4: Competitor Introduced New Mechanism

Your competitor has just introduced a new mechanism.

- **Strategy:** Elaborate or enlarge upon this successful mechanism. Make it easier, quicker, surer; allow it to solve more of the problem; overcome limitations; promise extra benefits.
- **Example:** "FIRST NO-DIET REDUCING WONDER DRUG!"
- **Evolution Pattern:** This stage follows the same pattern of embellishment as the Second Stage, eventually pushing beyond believability.

#### Stage 5: Market Exhausted

The market is exhausted; competitors dropping out.

- **Strategy:** Use identification advertising (as in the Fifth Stage of Awareness). Focus on the prospect rather than the product.
- **Example:** "WHY MEN CRACK..." (Postum ad)
- **Approach:** This stage corresponds to the Fifth Stage of Awareness in Chapter 2. The problems and strategies are the same.

### Case Study: The Cigarette Industry

Schwartz provides a detailed analysis of how the cigarette industry navigated all five stages of sophistication:

1. **First Stage:** Simple pleasure claims
   - "I'D WALK A MILE FOR A CAMEL!"
   - "CHESTERFIELD — THEY SATISFY!"
2. **Second Stage:** Elaborated claims
   - "LIGHT UP A LUCKY, AND YOU WON'T MISS THE SWEETS THAT MAKE YOU FAT!"
3. **Third Stage:** New mechanisms
   - "LUCKIES — THEY'RE TOASTED!"
   - "PALL MALL'S GREATER LENGTH FILTERS THE SMOKE FURTHER!"
   - "CAMELS — PROTECT YOUR T-ZONE!"
4. **Fourth Stage:** Elaboration of mechanisms
   - "PHILIP MORRIS — ALL THE HARSHNESS BAKED OUT!"
   - "CHESTERFIELD — REGULAR AND KING-SIZED TOO!"
   - "NINE OUT OF TEN DOCTORS PREFER LUCKIES!"
5. **Fifth Stage:** Visual identifications (no claims)
   - Marlboro "Virile Men" ads
   - Similar approaches in Chesterfield and Camel ads

Additionally, Schwartz discusses two critical side problems the industry faced:

1. Opening the women's market ("BLOW SOME MY WAY")
2. Addressing the cancer scare with filter cigarettes, which then went through their own sophistication cycle

## Chapter 4: 38 Ways to Strengthen Your Headline

**Verbalization:** The art of increasing a headline's impact through how it's stated. Verbalization can:

1. Strengthen the claim
2. Make the claim fresh again
3. Help pull the prospect into the body of the ad

### The 38 Verbalization Techniques

**1. Measure the size of the claim:**
- "20,000 FILTER TRAPS IN VICEROY!"
- "I AM 61 POUNDS LIGHTER..."
- "WHO EVER HEARD OF 17,000 BLOOMS FROM A SINGLE PLANT?"

**2. Measure the speed of the claim:**
- "FEEL BETTER FAST!"
- "IN TWO SECONDS, BAYER ASPIRIN BEGINS TO DISSOLVE IN YOUR GLASS!"

**3. Compare the claim:**
- "SIX TIMES WHITER WASHES!"
- "COSTS UP TO $300 LESS THAN MANY MODELS OF THE LOW-PRICED THREE!"

**4. Metaphorize the claim:**
- "BANISHES CORNS!"
- "MELTS AWAY UGLY FAT!"

**5. Sensitize the claim** by making the prospect feel, smell, touch, see or hear it:
- "TASTES LIKE YOU JUST PICKED IT!"
- "THE SKIN YOU LOVE TO TOUCH!"

**6. Demonstrate the claim** by showing a prime example:
- "JAKE LAMOTTA, 160 POUND FIGHTER, FAILS TO FLATTEN MONO PAPER CUP!"
- "AT 60 MILES AN HOUR, THE LOUDEST NOISE IN THIS ROLLS ROYCE IS THE ELECTRIC CLOCK!"

**7. Dramatize the claim, or its result:**
- "HERE'S AN EXTRA $50, GRACE — I'M MAKING BIG MONEY NOW!"
- "THEY LAUGHED WHEN I SAT DOWN AT THE PIANO — BUT WHEN I STARTED TO PLAY..."

**8. State the claim as a paradox:**
- "HOW A BALD-HEADED BARBER HELPED SAVE MY HAIR!"
- "BEAT THE RACES BY PICKING LOSERS!"

**9. Remove limitations from the claim:**
- "SHRINKS HEMORRHOIDS WITHOUT SURGERY!"
- "YOU BREATHE NO DUSTY ODORS WHEN YOU DO IT WITH LEWYT!"

**10. Associate the claim with values or people** with whom the prospect wishes to be identified:
- "MICKEY MANTLE SAYS: CAMELS NEVER BOTHER MY THROAT!"
- "9 OUT OF 10 DECORATORS USE WUNDAWEAVE CARPETS FOR LONG LIFE AT LOW COST!"

**11. Show how much work, in detail, the claim does:**
- "NOW! RELIEF FROM ALL 5 ACID-CAUSED STOMACH TROUBLES — IN SECONDS!"
- "RELIEVES CONGESTION IN ALL 7 NASAL PASSAGES INSTANTLY!"

**12. State the claim as a question:**
- "WHO ELSE WANTS A WHITER WASH — WITH NO HARD WORK?"
- "COULD YOU USE $25 A WEEK EXTRA INCOME?"

**13. Offer information about how to accomplish the claim:**
- "HOW TO WIN FRIENDS AND INFLUENCE PEOPLE!"
- "HERE'S WHAT TO DO TO GET RID OF PIMPLES FAST!"

**14. Tie authority into the claim:**
- "BOSS MECHANIC SHOWS HOW TO AVOID ENGINE REPAIR BILLS!"
- "HERE'S WHAT DOCTORS DO WHEN THEY FEEL ROTTEN!"

**15. Before-and-after the claim:**
- "BEFORE COLDENE A CHILD GOT OVER A COLD AFTER 5 DAYS OF ACHING, SNEEZING, WHEEZING, DRIPPING, SUFFERING, COUGHING, CRYING, GAGGING, SPITTING."
- "WITH COLDENE A CHILD GETS OVER A COLD IN FIVE DAYS!"

**16. Stress the newness of the claim:**
- "ANNOUNCING! GUIDED MISSILE SPARK PLUGS!"
- "NOW! CHROME PLATE WITHOUT HEAT, ELECTRICITY, MACHINERY!"

**17. Stress the exclusivity of the claim:**
- "OURS ALONE! PERSIAN LAMB ORIGINALS — $389.40!"
- "ONLY GLEEM HAS GL-70 TO KEEP TEETH CLEAN ALL DAY LONG WITH ONE BRUSHING!"

**18. Turn the claim into a challenge for the reader:**
- "WHICH TWIN HAS THE TONI? AND WHICH HAS THE $15 PERMANENT?"
- "DOES SHE OR DOESN'T SHE? HAIR COLORING SO NATURAL ONLY HER HAIRDRESSER KNOWS FOR SURE!"

**19. State the claim as a case-history quotation:**
- "LOOK, MOM — NO CAVITIES!"
- "WOULD YOU BELIEVE IT — I HAVE A COLD!"

**20. Condense the claim** — interchange your product and the product it replaces:
- "NOW! A RING AND PISTON JOB IN A TUBE!"
- "POUR YOURSELF A NEW ENGINE!"

**21. Symbolize the claim** — replace the direct statement or measurement of the claim with a parallel reality:
- "STARTING NEXT TUESDAY, THE ATLANTIC OCEAN BECOMES ONLY ONE-FIFTH AS LONG!"

**22. Connect the mechanism to the claim in the headline:**
- "FLOATS FAT RIGHT OUT OF YOUR BODY!"
- "FEEDS WASTE GAS FUMES BACK INTO YOUR ENGINE!"

**23. Startle the reader** by contradicting the way he thinks the mechanism should work:
- "HIT HELL OUT OF THE BALL WITH YOUR RIGHT HAND,' SAYS TOMMY ARMOUR!"

**24. Connect the need and the claim in the headline:**
- "THERE IS ONLY ONE SOLUTION TO AN ADVERTISING PROBLEM: FIND THE MAN!"

**25. Offer information in the ad itself:**
- "WHY MEN CRACK..."
- "WHAT EVERYBODY OUGHT TO KNOW ABOUT THIS STOCK AND BOND BUSINESS!"

**26. Turn the claim or the need into a case history:**
- "AUNT MEG, WHO NEVER MARRIED..."
- "AGAIN SHE ORDERS — A CHICKEN SALAD, PLEASE!"

**27. Give a name to the problem or need:**
- "WHEN YOU'RE WEARY WITH DAY-TIME FATIGUE, TAKE ALKA-SELTZER."

**28. Warn the reader about possible pitfalls** if he doesn't use the product:
- "DON'T INVEST ONE CENT OF YOUR HARD-EARNED MONEY UNTIL YOU CHECK THIS GUIDE!"

**29. Emphasize the claim by its phraseology** — by breaking it into two sentences, or repeating it, or a part of it:
- "A MAN YOU CAN LEAN ON! THAT'S KLOPERMAN!"
- "NOBODY BUT NOBODY UNDERSELLS GIMBEL'S!"

**30. Show how easy the claim is to accomplish** by imposing a universally-overcome limitation:
- "IF YOU CAN COUNT TO ELEVEN, YOU CAN INCREASE YOUR SPEED AND SKILL AT NUMBERS!"

**31. State the difference in the headline:**
- "THE DIFFERENCE IN PREMIUM GASOLINES IS RIGHT IN THE ADDITIVES!"

**32. Surprise your reader** into realizing that former limitations have now been overcome:
- "SEE WHAT HAPPENS WHEN YOU CRUSH A HARTMAN DC-8? NOTHING!"

**33. Address the people who can't buy your product:**
- "IF YOU'VE ALREADY TAKEN YOUR VACATION, DON'T READ THIS. IT'LL BREAK YOUR HEART."

**34. Address your prospect directly:**
- "TO THE MAN WHO WILL SETTLE FOR NOTHING LESS THAN THE PRESIDENCY OF HIS FIRM."

**35. Dramatize how hard it was to produce the claim:**
- "WHEN JENS FINISHED DESIGNING THIS CANDLEHOLDER WE HAD TO INVENT A WHOLE NEW KIND OF CANDLE."

**36. Accuse the claim of being too good:**
- "IS IT IMMORAL TO MAKE MONEY THIS EASILY?"

**37. Challenge the prospect's present limiting beliefs:**
- "YOU ARE TWICE AS SMART AS YOU THINK."

**38. Turn the claim into a question and answer:**
- "YOU DON'T KNOW WHAT'S UNDER THE HOOD AND YOU COULDN'T CARE LESS AS LONG AS YOUR CAR RUNS SMOOTHLY. WHO SHOULD YOU SEE IF IT DOESN'T?...SOMEONE WHO CARES — UNITED DELCO."

## Chapter 5: The Art of Creative Planning

### Three Levels of Creativity

**1. Word-Substitute Technique** (shallowest and most ineffective)

- The copywriter consults successful headlines and substitutes their own product name
- Example: "I'D WALK A MILE FOR A CUP OF [BLANK] COFFEE!"
- **Schwartz's Take:** "These are Echo Ads — ads that remind people of some other product. They pay no attention to unique product-market-timing relationship that exists at the moment."
- Most effective only when copied from similar products in the same market at the same time

**2. Formula Approach** (deeper but still limited)

- Copywriter applies memorized rules or principles to their headline
- This approach works for strengthening verbalization but struggles with core ideas
- **Schwartz's Take:** "But the idea for an ad or a headline demands its own shape. It cannot be fitted into someone else's solution."

**3. Analytical Approach** (most effective)

- Instead of fixed answers, this approach provides guidepost questions
- Allows the copywriter to cut through surface issues to what makes a problem tick
- **Schwartz's Take:** "Analysis is the art of asking the right questions and letting the problem dictate the right answers. It is the technique of the break-through."

### On Motivation Research (MR) and the Copywriter

MR serves two valuable functions:

1. Providing information about powerful needs and desires
2. Testing the copywriter's hunches and theories

However, Schwartz cautions that MR findings are not headlines or themes by themselves:

> "Like any other fact, it is a direction. First it tells you where not to go, to avoid wasting your time. And then it indicates the general area of your solution."

> "The transformation of those facts into an idea, and the expression of that idea in the strongest possible form, still requires as much creative talent as any other starting point."

### On Product Personality in Headlines

- Products have distinct personalities in consumers' minds
- One trait is usually most effective at expressing this personality
- This "summary trait" should be featured in headlines/illustrations
- Other traits should strengthen the dominant trait, not compete with it
- Example: Cadillac's primary personality trait is quality, even though it also represents prestige, performance, comfort, etc.

### On Prevention Headlines

- Most prevention headlines fail because prospects can't identify with problems that haven't affected them yet
- The solution: Focus the prevention not on the prospect, but on their loved ones

**Examples:**

- Decay-preventing toothpaste sells when ads focus decay on children, not the parent
- Life insurance sells by picturing consequences for family, not the prospect's death

**Schwartz's Rule:** "A man will not visualize future disasters occurring to himself, but he is perfectly capable of visualizing, and buying preventatives from, the image of such future problems affecting others around him."

### On Splinter Markets

- Rather than addressing entire markets, smaller companies can focus on specific segments
- This can be based on sociology rather than geography
- Example: A reducing aid abandoning the appearance segment to focus specifically on the health segment
- This affects every aspect of the campaign from headline to media selection to distribution channels
- Success in a niche can later be used as a point of difference when expanding to larger markets

### Summary of the Creative Process

1. Start with thorough market analysis
2. Study the product itself in detail
3. Combine these to develop the theme: the desire your market demands and its satisfaction
4. Explore market maturity to find your headline's entry point
5. Write the headline — this may be only 5–10 words but represents about 90% of your ad's value

**Schwartz's Cardinal Rule:** What you're looking for is the one element that makes your product and market unique. The solution lies inside that product-market-timing relationship, nowhere else. No outside formula will give it to you.

# Part 2: The Seven Basic Techniques of Breakthrough Advertising

## Chapter 6: Inside Your Prospect's Mind

### Three Dimensions of Your Prospect's Mind

**1. Desires**

- The wants, needs, cravings, and hungers driving your prospect
- They are physical, material, and sensual
- Examples: desire to be thin, to possess money, to enjoy a cold beer
- Your primary task is to make your prospect want — to sharpen desire
- You need to build vivid mental pictures of satisfaction through your product

**2. Identifications**

- The roles prospects want to play in life and personality traits they want to project
- These longings for identification complement and intensify physical desires
- Examples: A woman buys low-calorie food not just to become thin but to project youth and attractiveness
- Your task is to make the prospect feel the prestige of using your product
- You need to show them the specific type of people who currently use your product

**3. Beliefs**

- Opinions, attitudes, prejudices, knowledge, and conceptions of reality
- These form the filter through which your product information must pass
- Your task is to build from these existing beliefs using the prospect's logic
- You must show that your product satisfies desires, that it works, and that others rely on it

### The Length of Your Ad

The length of your ad depends on:

1. How much copy you need to build desire to maximum strength
2. How much additional copy you need to create comfortable identification
3. How much additional copy you need to overcome objections and build belief

Your copy needs to be as long as necessary to accomplish these three tasks, no more, no less.

---

## Chapter 7: Intensification — The First Technique

**Definition:** Intensification is the process of expanding and magnifying desire by presenting concrete images of satisfaction through your product.

**Core Concept:** Desires exist in the prospect's mind as vague, indistinct emotions without direction. Your job is to fill these out with concrete images and show every possible way they can be fulfilled.

**The Power of Intensification:**

> "The sharper you can draw your pictures — and the greater the number of them that you can legitimately present — the more your prospect will demand your product, and the less important will seem your price."

### Application in Different Media

- Small-space and broadcast advertising requires compression and campaigns
- Large-space and direct mail allows full development of multiple perspectives
- The challenge is to present variety without repetitiveness

### Thirteen Ways to Strengthen Desire

**1. Direct Presentation of Your Claims**
- Detailed, thorough description of product appearance or results
- Example from rose ad: "Can you imagine the fantastic beauty of a rosebush that can easily give you MORE THAN 4,000 ROSES from June to September — and then keeps on blooming heavily right up into frost..."
- Makes the satisfaction real and concrete through vivid imagery

**2. Put the Claims in Action**
- Show not just how the product looks and what benefits it gives, but exactly how it does this
- Example from model airplane ad: "As soon as it picks up power from the motion of your hand, it will lift up its nose, its wings will begin to cut through the air, it will flash upward and streak ahead of you!"
- Transforms static description into dynamic demonstration

**3. Bring in the Reader**
- Put the prospect directly into the story and show what will happen to them
- Example from power booster ad: "We ask you to pull up to another car at the stop light, of approximately the same year and make as your own! Wait until the light changes from red to green. Let the other car start first. Wait till the other car gets half way across the street. And then slam your foot down on the gas pedal!"
- Creates personal involvement and anticipation

**4. Show Testing**
- Let the reader visualize proving the performance themselves
- Example from spark plug ad: "Now, if you have an automatic transmission — put your car in drive and let your engine idle. If your car stood still with spark plugs, it will now move forward at from 4 to 6 miles an hour..."
- Builds confidence through empirical verification

**5. Stretch Benefits in Time**
- Extend reader's vision into weeks and months of continuous benefits
- Example from plant food ad: "First, the amazing English pellets give your plants a tremendous new burst of growth! New plants and old — they send out dozens of hidden shoots and buds! Some of your plants may actually DOUBLE in height and breadth the very first month! But this is just the beginning!"
- Creates long-term value perception

**6. Bring in an Audience**
- Introduce other actors to provide fresh perspectives
- Can be celebrities (testimonials), average people (case histories), or observers
- Example from reducing ad: "And then, day by day, faster and easier and safer than they had ever known before, the ugly excess fat around their bodies melted away!..."
- Adds credibility and relatability through others' experiences

**7. Show Expert Approval**
- Bring in professionals, the sophisticated, the discriminating
- Example from rose ad: "Just picture the astonishment on these experts' faces when in the first month alone, Nearly Wild produced over 15 times more blossoms than the average of all other roses combined..."
- Uses elements of surprise, competition, and discovery to sharpen the image

**8. Compare, Contrast, Prove Superiority**
- Lay disadvantages of old product side by side with advantages of the new
- Example from book club radio ad: "Yes! While your friends were paying $3.95 for their copies of Frank Yerby's run-away best seller, The Golden Hawk, Dollar Book Club members were getting the same exact book for only 99¢..."
- Creates dramatic contrast that reinforces value

**9. Picture the Black Side**
- Paint the problem in full black color before applying the salve
- Example from spark plug ad: "If that plug has been in your car three thousand miles or more, then what you'll see on the bottom of that plug is FILTH! The Firing Point of that plug — the most important single point in your car — will be choked, strangled and fouled with BLACK, FILTHY CARBON!"
- Creates repulsion from the problem, driving prospect toward your solution

**10. Show How Easy It Is to Get These Benefits**
- Stress simplicity of application contrasted with tremendous benefits
- Example from engine additive ad: "One week from today, you are going down to your car. You are going to lift up its hood — you are going to take the black, shining can we send you — and you are going to simply pour its contents into the oil filler pipe of your car! That's all the work you will do!..."
- Eliminates fear of difficulty or complexity

**11. Use Metaphor, Analogy, Imagination**
- Present facts in more dramatic form using imagination
- Example from Sherwin Cody English Course: "Suppose he himself were standing forever at your elbow. Every time you mispronounced or misspelled a word... suppose you could hear him whisper, 'That is wrong, it should be thus and so.'..."
- Makes abstract benefits concrete through vivid comparison

**12. Summarize Before You're Done**
- Use the "catalog" technique to list benefits or applications
- Two types: horizontal (expanding to more applications) and vertical (deepening desire)
- Example from blow torch kit: "ALL IN ONE! Blow-Torch, Paint-Remover, Soldering Iron! Professional-type Propane Torch also doubles as fast, safe Paint Remover and precision Soldering Iron! Does 101 jobs around the house..."
- Creates effect of magnitude, infinite possibilities, and breadth of value

**13. Put Your Guarantee to Work**
- Transform guarantee into climax of your ad — final summary of product performances
- Example from plant food ad: "USE THEM TO CREATE SUPER-FLOWERS! Place one tiny pellet besides each of your hydrangeas, zinneas... any kind of flower that you want super-blooms! And if you don't see fantastic new growth within a few short weeks..."
- Uses guarantee as persuasion tool, not just risk reducer

### Application to Campaign Advertising

Schwartz distinguishes between mail order (which compresses all intensification into a single ad) and national advertising (which builds desire throughout a series of advertisements):

- **The Challenge:** Maintaining continuity while providing enough variation to keep readers interested
- **Solutions Include:**
  1. Central Campaign Idea with open repetition (Colgate's "Gardol — the Invisible Shield")
  2. Rule and Example approach (Toni's "WHICH TWIN HAS THE TONI?" with changing images)
  3. Product as focal point (Volkswagen campaign with simple, consistent product presentation but varied messages)

> "Each advertisement is sufficient to interest a prospective buyer. Together, especially when concentrated in the same media and reaching the same audience week after week, the series builds an overwhelming impression of novelty and value."

## Chapter 8: Identification — The Second Technique

**Definition:** Identification is the process of building a saleable personality into your product that resonates with how prospects want to define themselves.

**Core Concept:** Beyond functional desires, people have a longing for identification — the need to express and be recognized for certain character traits and achievements. Your product can satisfy these by becoming an instrument for achieving roles or acknowledgment that roles have been achieved.

**Schwartz's Key Insight:**

> "People don't change: only the direction of their desires do. They cannot be made to want anything, nor is it necessary to create want. All that is necessary is to be able to channel those wants into the proper products that offer legitimate satisfaction for them."

### The Two Types of Roles Prospects Desire

**1. Character Roles**

- Usually expressed by adjectives or adjectives-turned-nouns
- Examples: "progressive," "chic," "charming," "brilliant," "well-read"
- Part of the prospect's personality that they value and want to develop
- Once mastered, must be acknowledged, valued, and admired by others
- Products can:
  - Help achieve mastery (a philosophy book helps be "well-read")
  - Speed up mastery (a Speed-Reading Course)
  - Symbolize mastery to invoke acknowledgment (bookshelves)
- These roles are difficult to test or measure, making them subject to fantasy and projection
- Their subconscious, non-verbal nature makes them easy to accept without questioning

**2. Achievement Roles**

- Status roles created by society and offered to those who earn them
- Usually expressed by nouns serving as titles
- Examples for men: "Executive," "Home Owner," "$20,000-a-year-Man," "Five Handicap"
- Examples for women: "Wife," "Fashion Setter," "Career Woman," "Good Mother"
- These roles are invisible achievements that must be translated into physical symbols
- Products become status definers that announce achievements and document success
- Example: A newly-wed woman requires new sheets, furniture, and even a new home to express her transformation

### How to Put Longings for Identification to Work

**1. Identify the primary image** your product already has in the mind of your prospects

- Examples: Cigarettes are viewed as virile; piston rings as precision-machined; household appliances as time-saving
- These existing images form your starting point

**2. Build new images onto this foundation:**

- Either intensify the primary image (if favorable)
  - Example: Marlboro took the virility naturally associated with cigarettes and intensified it through (1) virile men (2) in virile situations (3) with primitive symbols of virility (tattoos)
- Or use the primary image as a bridge to bring in more favorable images
  - Example: Chesterfield's "Blow Some My Way" ad retained the man smoking (the accepted primary image) but subdued him to lead viewers to a more appealing overall image of romance

### Limits to Identification

- Prospects won't accept unrealistic identifications
- You cannot force your market beyond what they accept as true
- You must build bridges from what they already believe to what you want them to believe
- Example: Men who wouldn't identify with a yachting costume accepted the Marlboro image after it was introduced through the acceptable virility concept

### Product-Based Identification Sources

**1. Product Appearance**
- Start with functional appearance and build in identification
- Borrow from glamour versions (sports cars, racing cars)
- Add images unrelated to structure but meaningful to prospects

**2. Product Background**
- Borrow from the context where your product originated
- Example: Johnson & Johnson's Micrin mouthwash used the shape of medical containers to suggest clinical effectiveness

**3. Product Components**
- Use elements within your product that suggest prestige or specialness
- Example: Electronic components suggest cutting-edge technology, precision

## Chapter 9: Gradualization — The Third Technique

**Definition:** Gradualization is building a bridge of belief from facts already accepted by your prospect to the ultimate facts you want them to believe about your product.

**Core Concept:** Belief is immensely important for the success of your advertisement. It cannot be created by force, but must be built step by step from existing beliefs. Gradualization is the process of arranging your claims in a sequence that builds acceptance.

### The Nature of Belief

- Belief is a complex fusion of thought and emotion
- It provides emotional security to the prospect
- Most adults formed their belief channels as children
- Asking prospects to shatter established beliefs is asking them to become children again

**Schwartz's Rule:**

> "If you violate your prospect's established beliefs in the slightest degree — either in content or direction — then nothing you promise him, no matter how appealing, can save your ad."

### The Architecture of Belief

- Every claim has two sources of strength:
  1. The content of the statement itself
  2. The preparation made for that statement in preceding copy
- You can strengthen claims by:
  1. Increasing the intensity of content (bigger promises)
  2. Changing the sequence/position of statements (better preparation)
- Effective advertising creates a "stream of acceptances" leading to conviction

### Awareness as Readiness to Accept

- The Five Stages of Awareness (Chapter 2) can be redefined as stages of "readiness to accept"
- Your headline must be capable of being accepted at first glance as true
- This is why the most powerful claim doesn't always make the most powerful headline
- Mail order advertisers test different headlines, often finding less ambitious claims perform better

### Example Analysis: TV Repair Manual Ad

Schwartz analyzes in detail the structure of a TV repair manual ad, showing precisely how it builds belief through Gradualization:

**1. The Challenge:** Two facts needed to be believed:
- That owners could save money by making their own TV repairs
- That they were capable of making those repairs
- The direct claim headline ("Save up to $100 a Year on Your TV Repairs!") failed

**2. The Solution:** A headline using universal resentment
- "WHY HAVEN'T TV OWNERS BEEN TOLD THESE FACTS?"
- This tapped into suspicion without making direct claims

**3. Building the Stream of Acceptances:**
- First two sentences created a "Habit of Agreement" through inclusion questions
- Only then was the first claim introduced (saving money on repairs)
- Believability enhanced through grammatical construction ("Was your... Then...")
- Universal symptoms described (repair bills, service fees) to gain more acceptance
- Mechanism left deliberately ambiguous until reader was prepared for it
- Test reports from manufacturers introduced at the precise moment reader demanded a solution
- Logical construction established ("If your set were properly cared for...")
- Only after all this preparation was the idea that owners could make repairs themselves introduced

### Belief-Building Devices

**1. Inclusion Questions**
- Questions that permit immediate identification with your story
- Example: "Do you find it increasingly difficult to cope with the world around you..."
- Once prospects identify with these questions, they accept your recommendations as relevant

**2. Detailed Identification**
- Cataloging symptoms or problems that are your prospect's reasons for desiring your product
- Example: "Let me explain. I don't care whether your child is six years old or twenty — boy or girl — in grade school, high school or college. It makes no difference how badly that child is doing in school today..."
- Establishes that you understand their problems intimately

**3. Contradiction of Present (False) Beliefs**
- Directly challenging existing beliefs, but only with strong authority
- Example: "From this moment on, forget everything you have ever heard or read about what age 'must do' to your appearance."
- Most effective when you can immediately offer a compelling alternative

**4. The Language of Logic**
- Interlacing promises with words that signal reasoning
- Words like "reason," "therefore," "simply because," "the fact that," etc.
- Example: "Most persons do not write or speak good English simply because they have never formed the habit of doing so..."
- These words carry conviction after centuries of conditioning in courts, politics, and science

**5. Syllogistic Thinking**
- Building chains of logical reasoning where one statement inevitably leads to another
- Example from spark plug ad showing how larger spark = more powerful explosion = more power/economy
- Creates a feeling of inevitability — the product must work

**6. Other Belief Forms**
- Contingency structures ("If...then...")
- Repetition of proof through echoing
- Promise-belief-promise variation
- Paragraph parallelism (using same structure for accepted and new claims)

## Chapter 10: Redefinition — The Fourth Technique

**Definition:** Redefinition is removing objections to your product by giving it a new definition that transforms drawbacks into assets or makes them irrelevant.

**Core Concept:** Many products have aspects that repel prospects. Redefinition provides ways to overcome these negative perceptions by changing how prospects think about the product.

### Three Categories of Drawbacks and Their Solutions

**1. Products That Seem Too Complicated → Simplification**

- Example: The TV Repair Manual
  - Problem: Owners feared TV repairs were too technical
  - Solution: Redefined "repairs" as "minor adjustments on outside controls"
  - Approached through analogy: "Your TV set is a great deal like your body"
  - Used terminology shift: "warning signals" and "symptoms" instead of "breakdowns"
  - Created clear distinction between rare major breakdowns and common minor maladjustments
- This approach works especially well for breakthrough products that actually simplify processes
- Requires laying a base for acceptance by redefining the entire field

**2. Products That Aren't Important Enough → Escalation**

- Involves redefining the benefits of the product to shift them from less desirable to more desirable areas
- Example: English course ad
  - Old approach: Focus on grammar and punctuation
  - New approach: "Automatically gives you a power-packed vocabulary — to make your ideas crackle with excitement... to hold others spellbound with the power of your speech"
  - Shifted from correctness to persuasion power
- Can also increase perceived importance by showing what hinges on the product
  - Example: "Yes. You pay $2,000... $3,000... $4,000 for your car. And a single 99¢ part robs you of the real power and enjoyment that car should give you."
- Or show how frequently the product will be used
  - Example: "Your Entire Life is Spent Trying to Get Others to Do What You Want — Without Friction"

**3. Products That Cost Too Much → Price Reduction**

- Switching comparison to make price seem less by relating to a more expensive standard
- Example: Fire injector spark plugs ($1.49 each)
  - Compared to hand-made versions at $5 each
  - Emphasized total cost savings ("30 or 40 dollars was more than the average driver could afford")
  - Used benefit calculation ("pay for themselves 12 times in one year")
  - Added conditions ("If you will check your car's performance... and tell your friends")
  - Used phrases implying favor or discount ("prepared to do," "can have," "only")

### Multiple Reinforcing Techniques

Schwartz emphasizes how multiple techniques are often used together:

- In the spark plug example, he identifies 10 separate bargain-reinforcing phrases in just two paragraphs
- The combination creates a feeling of value far stronger than any single technique could provide

## Chapter 11: Mechanization — The Fifth Technique

**Definition:** Mechanization is verbally proving that your product does what you claim by demonstrating how it works.

**Core Concept:** Your prospect often demands to know "How does it work?" Mechanization answers this question by providing a logical demonstration of your product's function, making your claims believable.

### The Psychology Behind Mechanization

Your prospect engages in a silent dialogue with your copy, making demands including:

1. "Tell me more" (more information, more image, more desire)
2. "Oh yeah? Who says so?" (demands for proof)
3. "How does it work?" (demands for mechanism)

Good copywriters anticipate these questions and provide answers at exactly the right moments.

### Three Stages of Mechanism

**1. Name the Mechanism** (for well-known mechanisms)

- Appropriate when your prospect already understands and accepts the mechanism
- Simply name it and move on to competing on other factors like price
- Example: "TAKE FOOLPROOF PHOTOS WITH THE XENOPHON 1750. With Electronic Light Setter... Push-Button Zomar Lens... Magazine Load... Only $135."
- Used primarily in catalog or retail copy for familiar products

**2. Describe the Mechanism** (for less familiar mechanisms)

- Required when prospects don't understand the mechanism or are skeptical
- Classic formula: Promise — Reason Why
- Example from 1926 Rinso ad:
  - **Promise:** "Who else wants a whiter wash — with no hard work?"
  - **Built-up desire:** "Millions of women do it every week. They've given up washboards for good..."
  - **Mechanism:** "The secret is simply Rinso — a mild, granulated soap that gives rich, lasting suds even in the hardest water. Just soak the clothes in the creamy Rinso suds — and the dirt and stains float off."
- The mechanism must be sold as hard as the primary claim to be believed

**3. Feature the Mechanism** (when it becomes the main selling point)

- Used in highly sophisticated markets where promises sound alike
- Corresponds to the Third Stage of Market Sophistication
- Headlines focus on mechanism rather than result
- Examples: "FLOATS FAT RIGHT OUT OF YOUR BODY," "FIRST WONDER DRUG FOR REDUCING"
- Offers a new way to get what your prospect has wanted but failed to achieve

### Mechanism in Price Reduction

- Price cuts must be justified with mechanisms/reasons
- Example from Robert Collier letter selling shirts:
  - "A short time ago one of the old, reliable mills that makes the finer qualities of woven Madras for shirts began sending out S.O.S. calls."
  - "They had kept their plant going steadily for months, thinking that the usual demand would easily take care of their excess output."
  - "But, with the weather so unseasonably, the usual demand didn't materialize. And there they were, heavily overstocked — and needing money."
- This mechanism makes the price cut both believable and dramatically more effective

## Chapter 12: Concentration — The Sixth Technique

**Definition:** Concentration is proving to your prospect that alternate ways of satisfying their desire are ineffective compared to your product.

**Core Concept:** In competitive markets, you may need to crack existing product images and loyalty before channeling desire toward your product. Concentration accomplishes this by showing weaknesses in competing products while demonstrating your product's superiority.

### The Psychology of Concentration

- Your prospect's skepticism means simply attacking a competitor will evoke dislike
- The attack must be for their benefit, showing your product eliminates a weakness they suffer from

> "If you can show your customer that this attack is for his own good, in his service, because your product will eliminate this weakness, then you have a sales story he will accept."

### Two Structural Approaches to Concentration

**1. Direct Contrast Approach (Bad-Good, Bad-Good)**

- Example: Fire injector spark plug ad
- Structure: "A spark plug jumps a spark..." (weakness) vs. "A fire injector fires on..." (strength)
- Each paragraph follows this contrast pattern
- Uses parallel sentence structure to sharpen contrast: "A spark plug..." vs. "A fire injector..."
- Reinforces with stronger imagery: "thin skimpy spark" vs. "heavy, powerful flame"
- Integrates definition/redefinition: spark firing is "most wasteful" vs. surface firing is "most efficient"

**2. Time Sequence Approach (Before-After)**

- Example: Reducing pill ad
- First describes the negative experience with current products:
  - "For years doctors have known that ordinary reducing plans... are completely passive!"
  - "So what happened? If you were overweight, you struggled to do the job of reducing BY YOURSELF!"
  - "And then your will power snapped! You broke your terrible diet... And the fat flowed back — heavier and uglier and more deadly than ever before!"
- Then presents the solution as a new time sequence:
  - "And so you tried another passive plan. And another. And another. And then if you were like the men and women whose fantastic case histories were reported by leading medical journals — perhaps you went to your doctor and asked him for an easy way out..."

### Efficiency of Concentration

Schwartz notes that Concentration doesn't always require long copy blocks:

- It can be as short as two or three sentences or even a single phrase
- Example: "SHRINKS HEMORRHOIDS WITHOUT SURGERY" (implied weakness in other products with compensating promise)
- The effectiveness comes from the emotional reaction produced, not the length of copy

## Chapter 13: Camouflage — The Seventh Technique

**Definition:** Camouflage is borrowing believability from sources that already have the prospect's trust by adopting their formats, phraseology, and tone.

**Core Concept:** People buy publications for their content, not their ads, and they trust these publications to tell them the truth. By blending your ad into the style and format of trusted media, you can borrow some of their credibility.

### Three Ways to Borrow Believability

**1. Format Adaptation**

- Adopt the physical appearance of the publication where you're advertising
- Examples from Wall Street Journal adaptation:
  - Using Journal-style headline type rather than bolder, more modern alternatives
  - Employing dual sub-headlines in the Journal's old-fashioned style
  - Including the two bars above and below the first sub-head
  - Placing subheads to the extreme left of the column
  - Using a line drawing instead of a photograph
- This approach can double a standard ad's pulling power

> "A corny, old-fashioned, rather ugly advertisement has been developed for this specific medium — which has about twice the believability, twice the pulling power, and twice the staying power of the same exact copy and illustration presented the mass-produced way."

**2. Phraseology Adaptation**

- Adopt the verbal style and stereotyped phrases of trusted media
- Example from newspaper-style ad: "SKIN SPECIALIST DEMONSTRATES HOW TO RINSE AWAY YOUR BLACKHEADS By Claire Hoffman New York, N.Y. — A leading doctor today showed an audience of men, women and skin-troubled teenagers how to clean oily skin..."
- For direct mail, adopt the style of important correspondence like refund checks or confidential reports
- For broadcast, use news formats, clipped phrases, analyst tone, documentary feel

**3. Mood Adaptation**

Use tonal approaches that break away from "hard-sell" advertising stereotypes:

**a. Understatement**
- Simplicity, lack of color words, fewer adjectives, no superlatives
- Example from Volkswagen ad: "You never run out of air. You also won't have any worries about draining or flushing the radiator in spring. There is no radiator. Or hoses. Or water pump. Or rust."
- Short sentences that fall rather than rise in tone
- Self-deprecating humor that builds trust

**b. Deadly Sincerity**
- Pointing out flaws in an offer to make benefits more believable
- Example: "TO THE MAN WHO WILL SETTLE FOR NOTHING LESS THAN THE PRESIDENCY OF HIS FIRM. And who is willing to make the incredible sacrifices necessary to get there in the shortest possible time. Here is the most realistic handbook ever written for you — and you alone. This is a private advertisement. It is not meant for ninety-nine men out of every hundred."
- Creates tone of utter frankness that distinguishes the ad from others

## Chapter 14: The Final Touches

### Verification: How to Offer Authorities and Proof

- Providing statistics, tests, testimonials, authorities, awards, etc.
- **Key principle:** placement of proof is as important as content
- Proof should be:
  - As short as possible
  - As dramatic as possible
  - As specific as possible
- Proof is also selling copy — it must make prospects want the product more
- Proof is most effective when the reader unconsciously demands it
- Example from spark plug ad: Authority reference (U.S. Air Force) introduced immediately after Concentration copy to reinforce both the believability copy and the authority itself

### Reinforcement: How to Make Two Claims Do the Work of Four

- In emotional writing, 1+1 can equal 10
- Two emotional images, properly joined, can have 10 times the impact of either one alone
- Example from Avis campaign: "We're only second" + "We try harder" = far greater impact than either alone
- Another example from book ad: Frankly acknowledging flaws before stating benefits makes those benefits far more credible

### Interweaving: How to Blend Emotion, Image and Logic

- Every effective sentence should contain promise, image, and logic
- Example from fire injector ad: "With ordinary spark plugs you are using, or should be using premium gas (disadvantage) which costs from 4 to 8 cents more than ordinary gas (disadvantage), and despite this (disadvantage) you're getting inefficient (disadvantage), wasteful (disadvantage) gas consumption (disadvantage)."
- Followed by: "With fire injectors regular gas (promise) will give you up to 8 miles more per gallon (promise), up to 31 more horsepower (promise), plus easier starting (promise) in all weather (promise)."
- This packs multiple persuasive elements into every sentence

### Sensitivity: How to Give Your Reader What They Demand Step by Step

- The skill of anticipating when readers want to shift direction
- Recognizing when they're satiated with one type of copy and want another
- **Examples of different ad architectures:**
  1. Promise → Definition → Documentation → Promise
  2. Promise → Elaboration → Redefinition → Promise
  3. Promise → Promise → Definition → Promise
  4. Authority → Promise → Concentration → Promise
- Each structure meets different demands based on product and market
- The key skill: sensing when copy has "gone dead" and finding a new direction

### Momentum: How to Draw Your Reader Deeper Into Your Copy

**Two types of momentum-builders:**

**1. Momentum Phrases**
- Actual invitations to read on, typically in transition sentences
- Examples:
  - "They paid up to $22.50 a person to learn priceless techniques like these:"
  - "You can learn them all, in your own home next week-end, without risking a penny. Here's how."
  - "Here is the information you will find in this book."
  - "And yet it's only the beginning."
  - "For example —"
  - "Read the thrilling answer below."

**2. Incomplete Statements or Teasers**
- Based on creating interest in a point, raising a question, and implying an answer later
- Examples:
  - "And you will do it often using nothing more than ordinary tap water, your own ten fingers, and the contents of your garden and your refrigerator."
  - "Caught! 120 Fish in One Hour!"
  - "Just picture the scene as these college horticulturists began this amazing flowering test of roses... AND THEN, NEARLY-WILD STARTED TO BLOOM!"

### Mood: How to Pack Your Copy With the Right Emotion

- Words and rhythms set the mood of your copy
- Different copy requires different emotional tones:
  - For visual products: color words, sensory details, and emotion-definers
  - For power and momentum: staccato rhythm, power words, and man-to-man tone
  - For sincerity: absence of adjectives, self-kidding, straight-from-the-shoulder phrasing
- These elements work below the surface level, influencing readers without them noticing
- Must match the level of sophistication of your audience
- Read copy aloud to check rhythm and emotional impact

# Practical Applications of Breakthrough Techniques

## Applying Schwartz's Methods in Modern Contexts

While Schwartz wrote "Breakthrough Advertising" in the 1960s, his principles remain remarkably relevant across all media today.

### Translating to Digital Media

- **Headlines in Email Subject Lines:** The 5 awareness states apply directly to email marketing — most-aware subscribers need only product name and price, while unaware prospects need identification headlines
- **Landing Pages:** Follow the gradualization sequence, building belief systematically from the headline through all content blocks
- **Social Media:** Use identification techniques to create shareable content that helps prospects express their desired roles
- **Search Advertising:** Match ad copy to search term awareness level — informational searches indicate problem-aware prospects, brand searches indicate product-aware prospects
- **Video Content:** Structure scripts using intensification techniques, reinforcing the same desire from multiple perspectives

### Modern Campaign Integration

Schwartz's insights on campaign structure apply to today's multi-channel marketing:

- Maintain central campaign ideas across platforms
- Use different verbalization techniques for different channels
- Ensure consistent product personality across touchpoints
- Build belief through graduated exposure across platforms
- Apply proper format camouflage for each medium

### Adaptations for Changing Market Psychology

- Today's consumers are more sophisticated and skeptical overall
- Markets move through sophistication stages more rapidly
- Many markets remain in perpetual fifth-stage sophistication
- Digital tools allow more precise targeting of splinter markets
- Testing capabilities make refinement of appeals more scientific

## Testing Schwartz's Principles

Schwartz was a strong advocate for testing to determine what works. Modern marketers can test his principles:

- **A/B Test Headlines:** Compare headlines written for different awareness stages
- **Test Copy Length:** Determine how much intensification your specific market needs
- **Measure Mechanism Impact:** Test ads with and without detailed product mechanisms
- **Evaluate Identification Elements:** Compare results with different personality traits emphasized
- **Test Gradualization Sequences:** Try different arrangements of claims and proofs

# Ethical Considerations in Using These Techniques

Schwartz's techniques are powerful persuasion tools that come with ethical responsibility:

## The Power and Responsibility of Persuasion

- These techniques work because they tap into real human desires and beliefs
- With great persuasive power comes the responsibility to sell products that genuinely deliver
- The highest form of copy is that which matches genuine solutions to genuine needs

## Avoiding Manipulation

- Intensification becomes manipulation when promised satisfactions can't be delivered
- Identification becomes harmful when it exploits insecurities rather than fulfilling aspirations
- Gradualization must lead to honest conclusions, not mislead through logical fallacies
- Redefinition must genuinely reframe, not misrepresent
- Mechanization must explain real processes, not pseudoscience

## Schwartz's Ethical Framework

- Focus on channeling existing desires toward legitimate satisfaction
- Always respect the prospect's intelligence
- Build on established beliefs rather than attempting to shatter them
- Present honest proof that your product delivers
- Create advertising that stands the test of time through satisfied customers

# Breakthrough Advertising — Final Insights

## The Revolutionary Nature of Schwartz's Approach

Schwartz's work represented a fundamental shift in advertising thinking:

**From focusing on the product to focusing on the market**
- Traditional advertising centered on product features
- Schwartz centered on existing market desires
- This shift made advertising exponentially more effective through the "Amplification Effect"

**From creating clever ads to creating effective ads**
- Schwartz wasn't interested in creativity for its own sake
- He measured value solely by sales results
- This pragmatic approach anchored creativity to measurable outcomes

**From intuitive creativity to analytical creativity**
- Rather than relying on random inspiration, Schwartz developed systematic analysis
- He created frameworks that could be applied to any product-market situation
- This allowed for replicable success rather than hit-or-miss creative efforts

**From broadcasting messages to building persuasion architectures**
- Traditional advertising focused on "getting the message out"
- Schwartz built comprehensive persuasion structures
- These structures guided prospects through precisely calibrated emotional and logical steps

## Why These Principles Endure

Schwartz's techniques continue to work decades later because they're based on fundamental aspects of human psychology:

### Consistent Human Motivations

People remain driven by the same core desires:
- **Physical needs** (health, comfort, security)
- **Social needs** (status, belonging, recognition)
- **Emotional needs** (excitement, peace of mind, confidence)

The specific expressions of these desires evolve, but the underlying motivations persist.

### Unchanging Decision-Making Processes

- People still move through predictable stages of awareness
- Markets still progress through identifiable stages of sophistication
- Prospects still need to believe before they buy
- The same psychological principles of persuasion remain effective

### The Enduring Relationship Between Desire, Identification and Belief

- These three dimensions of the human mind continue to determine buying behavior
- Products must still satisfy functional needs, project desirable identities, and be believable
- The interrelationship between these dimensions remains consistent across generations

### The Timeless Power of Structured Persuasion

- Well-structured persuasion remains effective regardless of media evolution
- Techniques like gradualization work because they align with how people naturally accept new ideas
- The principles of intensification match how people experience and strengthen desire
- Mechanisms of identification reflect how people use purchases to define themselves

# The Complete Breakthrough Advertising Process

When synthesized into a comprehensive process, Schwartz's approach follows these steps:

## 1. Market Analysis
- Identify the mass desire that motivates your market
- Determine your prospect's state of awareness (Stages 1–5)
- Assess your market's level of sophistication (Stages 1–5)
- Understand which phase of the product lifecycle you're in

## 2. Product Analysis
- Distinguish between physical and functional attributes
- Identify the performance that best satisfies the dominant desire
- Find the unique "point of difference" that distinguishes your product
- Determine the product's existing personality in the market

## 3. Headline Creation
- Choose the appropriate headline based on awareness and sophistication
- Use verbalization techniques to strengthen impact
- Ensure the headline reaches prospects at their current level of belief
- Make it impossible for the right prospect to ignore

## 4. Body Copy Structure
- Develop a strategic progression of promises, claims, and proof
- Anticipate and answer questions before they arise
- Shift approaches when reader attention demands it
- Create a seamless flow from attention to action

## 5. Apply the Seven Breakthrough Techniques
- **Intensify** desire through vivid, varied imagery
- **Build identification** through product personality
- **Gradualize** belief through careful preparation
- **Redefine** objections into advantages
- **Mechanize** claims with logical demonstrations
- **Concentrate** on the shortcomings of alternatives
- **Camouflage** your approach in trusted formats

## 6. Fine-Tune with the Final Touches
- Verify claims with perfectly placed proof
- Reinforce impact through strategic juxtapositions
- Interweave emotion, logic, and imagery
- Maintain momentum throughout
- Create the perfect emotional mood

# The Ultimate Lesson

Perhaps the most valuable insight from Breakthrough Advertising is Schwartz's core philosophy:

> "Every new market — every new product — every new advertisement is a fresh new problem that never existed before on the face of this earth. Past advertising successes can only serve us as guideposts — as indicators of general directions. They can never solve today's specific problems for us."

This humility before each new marketing challenge — combined with the powerful analytical framework to solve it — represents Schwartz's greatest contribution to advertising.

The true breakthrough in advertising comes not from following formulas but from seeing each situation with fresh eyes, armed with timeless principles of human psychology and persuasion.

# Final Reflections from Schwartz

In the closing section of Breakthrough Advertising, Schwartz reminds us:

> "I have tried to write a scientific study of advertising... The first part of this book is about analysis, applied to the profession of copy writing. Its basic thesis is this: Every new market — every new product — every new advertisement is a fresh new problem that never existed before on the face of this earth."

He concludes with this powerful insight about the purpose of all advertising:

> "We are all primarily conceptual midwives, helping give birth to new markets for our products. All the other functions we or our businesses perform — the manufacturing, distribution, service, financing, and all the rest — are simply adjuncts to this vital central process."

This understanding — that advertising's essential function is not merely to sell products but to create and develop markets — represents the deepest truth Schwartz uncovered, and remains his most enduring contribution to marketing thought.
