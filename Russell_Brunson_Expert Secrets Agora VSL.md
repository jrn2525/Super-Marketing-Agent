### The Promos That Built Empires

I N 1978, a 30-year-old copywriter named Bill Bonner sat down at a typewriter in Baltimore and wrote a sales letter for an investment newsletter. That letter ran in a magazine. It generated enough new subscribers to fund a second product. Then a third. Then a fourth.

Forty-five years later, the company Bonner built - Agora - generates over a billion dollars a year in revenue, publishes dozens of newsletters across finance and health, and has employed or partnered with most of the copywriting legends of the last half century. Michael Masterson ran divisions inside it. John Forde wrote for it.

Porter Stansberry started there before launching his own publishing empire. Dr. Richard Gerhauser and Jim Rickards and Ian King all reach millions of people through its imprints.

And the engine that built all of it - the engine that still generates most of the revenue today - is a specific kind of video sales letter.

You’ve seen these VSLs whether you realized it or not. They’re the long, story-driven presentations that start with a mysterious building or an old photograph or a vial of unknown liquid, and sixty minutes later offer you a newsletter subscription for $49.

They are some of the most consistently effective pieces of direct-response writing ever created. And they are not improvisation. They are a structure.

A structure you can learn. And, more importantly, a structure you can copy.

## Chapter One

### The Agora Method

B EFORE WE GET INTO THE STRUCTURE, I want to give you a quick mental model of what we’re actually building. Because if you understand what an Agora VSL is trying to do - why it exists, why it’s shaped the way it’s shaped, and why it keeps working after fifty years - every specific technique in the rest of this book will make sense.

An Agora-style Video Sales Letter is a long-form narrative sales message. Typically forty to ninety minutes of spoken content. Sometimes shorter. Sometimes much longer. Its primary job is to turn a cold, skeptical viewer into a newsletter subscriber or book buyer, and to do it well enough that the same viewer keeps buying for years.

That’s a much harder job than most people realize. A cold viewer lands on the sales page knowing nothing about you. They’ve probably been burned before by bad newsletters, empty promises, or so-called gurus. They’re reading the first sentence with their thumb already hovering over the back button. And you’re asking them, sixty minutes from now, to hand over their credit card.

The only way that works is if you earn it, step by step. Attention first. Then trust. Then belief. Then desire. Then the sale.

The 14-element structure you’re about to learn is essentially a belief-change sequence ending in a transaction. Each element does one specific piece of the persuasion work. Skip any of them, and the sequence breaks.



<!-- Page 11 -->



Why This Format Works Most sales messaging you see today is short. A social media ad. A two-minute pitch video. A landing page with four bullet points and a button. The assumption is that attention is scarce, so you should ask for the sale as fast as possible.

That assumption is correct for warm audiences. If someone already knows you, trusts you, and wants what you sell, a short message is the right tool. Don’t waste their time.

But cold audiences are the exact opposite. With a cold audience, short messages fail because they haven’t earned any of the belief required to justify a yes. A two- minute pitch to a cold viewer gets skepticism, not conversion. You can’t shortcut trust.

What long-form VSLs do differently is they take the time to actually build the belief. An hour of teaching, story, proof, and framing. Not an hour of selling - an hour of earning the sale.

This is why the Agora format has survived every change in media, platform, and attention economy for fifty years. It doesn’t depend on tricks. It depends on the oldest persuasion engine there is: a well-told story that leads to a well-earned

## Chapter Two

### The Big Idea And The Rule Of One

E VERY SINGLE HIGH-CONVERTING VSL I’ve ever studied rests on one single, powerful idea. Not three ideas. Not “two big things I want to share with you.” One.

This is the Rule of One, and it’s the foundational principle of everything else we’re going to build. If you get this part right, a lot of other decisions make themselves. If you get it wrong, no amount of clever copywriting can save the finished piece.

The Rule of One comes from Michael Masterson and John Forde’s book Great Leads: The Six Easiest Ways to Start Any Sales Message. Masterson ran marketing and training divisions inside Agora for decades, and John Forde wrote some of the most successful Agora promotions of all time. Between the two of them, they probably have more data on what actually converts cold traffic than anyone else on Earth.

And when they wrote the book on how to start a sales message, the principle they put right at the front - before discussing a single tactical technique - was the Rule of One.

“When you lead with one powerful concept that creates a single compelling emotion, your copy becomes dramatically more effective.” - Michael Masterson, Great Leads Why the Rule Exists The viewer can only hold one big idea in their working memory across a 60-minute VSL. That’s a cognitive constraint, not a stylistic preference. Two big ideas force the viewer to mentally reset context every time you switch between them, and every reset is a chance for them to click away.



<!-- Page 16 -->



More importantly, the emotional arc of a VSL - worry, hope, conviction, urgency, decision - only works for one subject. If you’re trying to run two emotional arcs in parallel, the viewer ends up with emotional whiplash. They feel manipulated even if the individual claims are all true.

And the close itself only works with one idea. The close is a compression: “if you believe X, then Y is the obvious next step.” One X, one Y. When you try to compress two X’s into one Y, the causal chain breaks and the whole thing falls apart.

So: one big idea. One promise. One villain. One mechanism. One curiosity object. One primary product offer. Everything in service of the one thing.

The Big Domino Test Here’s the test I use to know whether a candidate big idea is strong enough. It’s a sentence-completion exercise. Fill in the three blanks:

### Thebigdominotest

If I can make my audience believe that [BIG IDEA] is the key to [WHAT THEY WANT MOST], and it’s only attainable through [MY MECHANISM OR PRODUCT], then every other objection becomes irrelevant and they have to give me their money.

If that sentence is true - if believing the big idea logically commits the audience to buying - the Big Domino is set correctly. If the viewer could still shrug and say “interesting, but I’ll pass” after believing the idea, the idea is too weak. Sharpen it until the Big Domino is unambiguously set.

Here are three examples of big ideas from real Agora VSLs that pass the test:

• “One Nevada-based company controls the rare earth magnet supply that

every electric vehicle motor in the Western Hemisphere needs - and it’s about to go vertical as the EV market grows twelve times over the next decade.”

• “Greek islanders on Ikaria routinely live past 100 in robust health because

they unknowingly use four specific longevity compounds that mainstream medicine has ignored for seventy years.”



<!-- Page 17 -->



• “There’s a $150 trillion trust fund hidden in Title 30 of the U.S. Code that’s

about to be unlocked by a recent Supreme Court decision - and the companies positioned around it are the opportunity of the decade.”

Notice what they all share. Each one names a specific thing (a company, a compound, a trust fund). Each one names a specific outcome (market cap growth, longevity, generational wealth). Each one implicitly positions the product as the only way to participate. And each one, if believed, leaves the viewer with only one reasonable next step: buy the thing that gives them access.

New Opportunity vs. Improvement Offer There’s one more distinction that separates a big idea that converts from one that fizzles. Masterson drew this line sharply, and it’s been reinforced by every top copywriter who followed him: the difference between a new opportunity and an improvement offer.

An improvement offer says: my product is a better version of what you’re already using. Better, faster, cheaper, more efficient. An improvement offer competes on commodity axes. You can always be out-competed on price, on features, on convenience.

A new opportunity says: my product gives you access to something you couldn’t get any other way. A different vehicle. A different category. A different paradigm. A new opportunity has no direct competition because it doesn’t exist yet in the prospect’s mind.

Every successful Agora VSL sells a new opportunity. Not “better stock tips” but “a proprietary 4-metric system for finding pre-breakout companies before Wall Street catches on.” Not “another health supplement” but “a long-lost compound rediscovered from Soviet cosmonaut research.” Not “a new retirement calculator” but “access to a $150 trillion trust fund hidden in federal law.”

### Thetest

If your first-draft big idea uses “-er” words - better, faster, cheaper, smarter, easier - it’s an improvement offer. Reframe it as a new opportunity before you go any further.



<!-- Page 18 -->



The reframe question is: what’s the one thing your product does that none of the alternatives do? What category would you have to invent to describe it accurately?

The answer to that question is your new opportunity. Applying the Rule of One to Every Element Once your big idea is locked in and passes both tests, it becomes the filter for every other decision in the VSL. Every element must serve the one big idea. CTA / Close Does this provide the risk-free path to the big idea’s result? If any element’s answer is “no, it’s about a different idea,” revise that element. Agora VSLs that convert are the ones where all 14 elements tell the same story from 14 different angles. Coherence is the whole game.

What This Looks Like in Practice Let’s make this concrete. Imagine you’re a financial newsletter editor and your first draft of a big idea is: “AI stocks are going to be huge, and also there’s a big infrastructure boom coming, and the Fed is about to cut rates which will help small caps.”

That’s three big ideas smashed together. Each one could probably support its own VSL. Trying to serve all three in one script produces a muddled narrative with no clear emotional arc and no single Big Domino.

The fix is to pick the strongest one and cut the others. You might land on: “Seven specific AI infrastructure sites are being built across America right now, with $113 billion in funding, and the companies at the center of each are about to see the single largest wealth transfer in American history.” That’s one big idea. It passes the Big Domino test. It’s a new opportunity, not an improvement. The Fed angle and the small-caps angle can live in your newsletter content later. They don’t belong in this VSL.

The hardest part of writing an Agora-style VSL is usually not the writing itself. It’s the discipline to cut everything that doesn’t serve the one big idea. Almost every first- time writer resists this. They think more ideas means more value. The opposite is true. More ideas means less conversion.



<!-- Page 20 -->



Lock in one idea. Run the Big Domino test. Make sure it’s a new opportunity, not an improvement. Then - and only then - move on to the next piece of Foundation: understanding exactly who you’re talking to.

## Chapter Three

### Know Your Audience

A BIG IDEA BY ITSELF ISN’T ENOUGH. The same big idea, delivered to two different audiences, needs to be presented completely differently. A promise that lands like a revelation for one viewer will sound like a sales pitch to another. The difference isn’t the promise. The difference is where the viewer is starting from.

Eugene Schwartz, the legendary copywriter who wrote Breakthrough Advertising back in 1966, mapped this out better than anyone before or since. He identified five stages of customer awareness, and he showed that the right opening for a sales message depends entirely on which stage your reader is at when they encounter you.

Sixty years later, his framework is still the single best tool for figuring out how to start a sales message. And Michael Masterson built his entire Six Lead Types framework on top of Schwartz’s foundation. Before we get to the leads, let’s get the awareness levels locked in.

Schwartz’s Five Awareness Levels The five levels run from “hot and ready” to “doesn’t even know they have a problem,” and they describe the full spectrum of where a viewer can be when they land on your sales page.

Level What They Know

1. Most Aware Knows you, knows the product, may

already be a customer. Just needs the deal.



<!-- Page 22 -->



Level What They Know

2. Product Aware Knows your product exists. Hasn’t bought

yet. Needs a compelling reason why now.

3. Solution Aware Feels the problem. Knows solutions like

yours exist in general, not yours specifically.

4. Problem Aware Feels the problem. Doesn’t know solutions

exist. Or knows vague ones that haven’t worked.

5. Unaware Hasn’t identified the problem yet. Largest

audience, hardest to sell to. Most Agora-style VSLs are aimed at viewers at levels 4 and 5 - problem aware or unaware. That’s because the whole point of running a long-form video to cold traffic is to reach people who aren’t already in the buying conversation. If they were product aware, you’d just email them a discount code. You’re spending 60 minutes of their time precisely because they need to be walked from “I don’t know this is a solvable problem” to “I’m pulling out my credit card right now.”

That’s a big psychological distance to cover. It’s why the format is so long. And it’s why the opening of the VSL - the first 3 to 5 minutes - is the single most important section to get right.

### Thekeyinsight

Cold traffic to an Agora VSL is almost always Problem Aware or Unaware. If your audience is Solution Aware or warmer, you probably don’t need a 60- minute VSL at all - a shorter direct-response piece will convert better.

Masterson’s Six Lead Types Now here’s where the framework gets practical. Each of Schwartz’s awareness levels calls for a specific kind of opening, and Masterson and Forde identified exactly six that cover the full spectrum. They published them in Great Leads, and



<!-- Page 23 -->



they’ve become the canonical framework every serious direct-response copywriter uses. The six types run from most direct (for hot audiences) to most indirect (for cold audiences). Here’s how they map: Awaren ess Level Default Lead Type Also Consider Most Aware Offer Lead Promise Lead Product Aware Promise Lead Offer Lead Solution Aware Problem-Solution Lead Big Secret Lead Problem Aware Big Secret Lead Proclamation Lead Unaware Story Lead Proclamation Lead Let me walk you through each one briefly. In the chapter on the opening (Chapter 4), we’ll get into exactly how to structure each type. For now, I just want you to have a mental model of what each lead is and when it’s appropriate.

Lead 1: The Offer Lead Most direct. Opens with the deal on the table - price, product, guarantee, all in the first few sentences. Works because the audience is already warm and ready, so skipping the preamble honors their readiness. Gene Schwartz called this the easiest lead to write: if you have a good deal and a receptive audience, it’s almost impossible to fail.

Sample opener: “For the next 48 hours, you can claim full access to my Insider’s Portfolio for just $1. Here’s what you get…” Lead 2: The Promise Lead Direct. Opens with the boldest, most specific claim the product can make. Names the product within the first 60 to 90 seconds. Works because the product-aware audience is close to the buying threshold and just needs the promise to be compelling enough to close the gap.

Sample opener: “Over the next 30 minutes, I’ll show you how to add $2,000 to $5,000 a month to your retirement income using a single type of investment Wall Street doesn’t want you to know about.”



<!-- Page 24 -->



Lead 3: The Problem-Solution Lead Middle of the spectrum, leaning direct. Opens with a specific, visceral description of the viewer’s pain, agitates it briefly, then pivots to the promise. Works because acknowledging the pain first builds empathy, and the pivot lands with maximum impact because the pain is fresh.

Sample opener: “If you’re over 55 and you’ve noticed your energy dropping, your sleep getting worse, and your workouts feeling harder than they used to - you’re not imagining it. And it’s not ‘just getting older.’” Lead 4: The Big Secret Lead Middle, leaning indirect. Opens with the existence of a secret without revealing it - teases hard-to-come-by knowledge, a formula, or a system. Names the branded mechanism term but doesn’t explain it yet. This is the dominant lead type in Agora finance VSLs, because problem-aware audiences are drawn to insider knowledge like a magnet.

Sample opener: “There’s a specific type of company most investors have never heard of - and the ones who find them early have made 500%, 1,000%, even 2,500% gains while everyone else sits in S&P index funds.”

Lead 5: The Proclamation Lead Indirect. Opens with a startling, emotionally compelling statement - a bold prediction, a shocking fact, a contrarian claim. Works because the proclamation short-circuits the viewer’s normal skepticism by being so bold that attention is compelled before judgment engages.

Sample opener: “On July 23rd, 2026, the single largest transfer of wealth in American history will begin. And 99 percent of Americans will miss it entirely.”

Lead 6: The Story Lead Most indirect, most powerful on the coldest traffic. Opens mid-scene with a specific person, place, or historical moment. Builds the narrative for 60 to 120 seconds without naming what it’s about. Then bridges to the Big Claim. This is the dominant lead type in Agora health VSLs, and in any ambitious cold-traffic finance VSL.

Sample opener: “On a tiny rocky Greek island, stuck out in the sea far from land, lives a community of seniors who appear to be cheating death.”

How to Choose Start with awareness. Where is your audience - really - when they land on the page? Be honest. If you’re running cold ads to a broad audience, they’re Problem Aware at warmest, Unaware at coldest. If you’re emailing a list that’s been on your newsletter for two years, they might be Product Aware or even Most Aware.

Then pick the default lead for that awareness level from the table above. Nine times out of ten, the default is the right call. The “also consider” column is for situations where the default doesn’t quite fit - usually because of something specific about the product or the presenter.

One rule that holds across every awareness level: when you’re uncertain whether to go more direct or more indirect, go more indirect. The downside of being too indirect for a warm audience is smaller than the downside of being too direct for a cold one. Cold viewers click away. Warm viewers will stay through an extra minute of story.

### Thedefault

For cold-traffic Agora VSLs, the default lead is either a Story Lead (coldest audiences) or a Big Secret Lead (problem-aware audiences). If you’re not sure, start with one of these two and test.

• A clear picture of your target audience - specifically who they are, what they

want, what they’ve tried, and how they feel about it.

• One big idea that passes the Big Domino test and is framed as a new

opportunity, not an improvement.

• A read on where that audience sits on Schwartz’s awareness spectrum.

• A choice of which of Masterson’s six lead types will open the VSL.

## Chapter Four

### The Opening

T HE OPENING OF AN Agora VSL is the most important five to ten minutes of the entire script. If viewers leave in those first few minutes, nothing else you wrote matters. Your credibility section doesn’t land. Your mechanism doesn’t get heard.

Your offer never gets made. The whole VSL is dead on arrival. That’s why we’re spending an entire chapter on what’s technically just three of the fourteen elements. The Hook. The Big Claim. And the Curiosity-Gap Teaser.

Together, these three are what I call the Opening, and they’re the elements that have to be absolutely airtight before the rest of the script has a chance to work.

The Three Jobs of the Opening The Opening has three distinct jobs, and they happen roughly in order, though they can interleave. Let me name them clearly so we know what we’re building toward.

Job 1: Stop the Scroll (Hook, first 30-90 seconds) Interrupt the viewer’s expectations so hard they can’t look away. This is not “intrigue.” It’s not “introduce the topic.” It’s a pattern interrupt. Something that makes the viewer go “wait, what?” within the first thirty seconds of the video.

Job 2: Name the Promise (Big Claim, minute 1-4) State the specific, concrete, numerically anchored outcome on offer. This is what holds the viewer for the next hour. Without a clear promise, they’ll drift. With a specific, almost outrageous promise, they’ll stay to find out whether it’s real.



<!-- Page 28 -->



Job 3: Plant the Mystery (Curiosity-Gap Teaser, minute 2-8) Introduce the unanswered question that can only be resolved by watching to the end. The vial on the desk. The building in Memphis. The compound with the code name. The viewer needs something unresolved in their head that they can’t let go of.

By the time these three jobs are done, the viewer should be thinking: “What IS that thing? And how do I get the result he just promised?” That’s the mental state you’re trying to leave them in when you transition into Credibility.

The Six Hook Variants Now, the specific shape of your Hook depends on which of Masterson’s six lead types you chose in Foundation. That decision tells you which variant to use.

I’m going to walk you through all six, with a sample opener for each. Pick the one that matches your Foundation, and model your Hook on that pattern.

Variant 1: The Offer Hook For Most Aware audiences. The rarest Hook type in cold-traffic VSLs, but worth knowing. Opens with the deal explicitly on the table.

### S A M P L E O P E N E R - O F F E R H O O K

For the next 48 hours, I‘m giving you full access to my Insider‘s Portfolio for just $1. Plus three free reports worth $597. Plus my monthly research newsletter for a full year. Here‘s why I‘m doing this right now, and why you need to act before midnight Friday...

Total length: 300-500 words. Everything about this VSL will be compressed because the audience is already sold. You’re not persuading, you’re closing.

Variant 2: The Promise Hook For Product Aware audiences. The boldest claim of the VSL appears in the first sentence. Product is named within the first 60-90 seconds.

### S A M P L E O P E N E R - P R O M I S E H O O K



<!-- Page 29 -->



Over the next 30 minutes, I‘m going to show you how to add $2,000 to $5,000 a month to your retirement income using a single type of investment Wall Street doesn‘t want you to know about. No options trading. No day trading. No complicated strategies. Just one simple approach that has already delivered real returns for over 8,000 of my readers.

Length: 400-700 words. Name the product and the specific outcome early. Move to Credibility quickly.

Variant 3: The Problem-Solution Hook For Solution Aware audiences. Lead with the hot-button pain. Agitate briefly. Pivot to the promise.

### S A M P L E O P E N E R - P R O B L E M - S O L U T I O N H O O

K If you‘re over 55 and you‘ve noticed your energy dropping, your sleep getting worse, and your workouts feeling harder than they used to - you‘re not imagining it. And it‘s not ‘just getting older.’ Here‘s what‘s actually happening.

And here‘s the one thing most doctors miss entirely... Length: 500-800 words. Don’t dwell in the pain. Sixty to ninety seconds of agitation, then pivot. Staying too long in the problem is the common failure mode.

Variant 4: The Big Secret Hook For Problem Aware audiences. The dominant Hook type in Agora finance VSLs.

Teases hard-to-come-by knowledge, formula, or system.

### S A M P L E O P E N E R - B I G S E C R E T H O O K

There‘s a specific type of company most investors have never heard of. The ones who find them early have made 500%, 1,000%, even 2,500% gains while everyone else sits in S&P index funds. I call them Breakout Companies. And over the next forty minutes, I‘m going to show you exactly how to spot them before Wall Street does.



<!-- Page 30 -->



Length: 600-1,000 words. Name the branded term but don’t explain it yet. Frame as “here’s why you haven’t heard of this.”

Variant 5: The Proclamation Hook For Problem Aware or Unaware audiences. Shocks with a bold fact, prediction, or statement.

### S A M P L E O P E N E R - P R O C L A M A T I O N H O O K

On July 23rd, 2026, the single largest transfer of wealth in American history will begin. And 99 percent of Americans will miss it entirely. I know how that sounds. I promise I‘m not exaggerating for effect. By the end of this presentation, you‘ll understand exactly why this is happening, and exactly how to position yourself in front of it.

Length: 500-900 words. Substantiate with one compelling fact within the first thirty seconds. Specificity, specificity, specificity. Vague proclamations land as hype; specific ones land as news.

Variant 6: The Story Hook For Unaware audiences. The dominant Hook type in Agora health VSLs, and in any ambitious cold-traffic finance VSL. Most indirect, most powerful on cold traffic.

### S A M P L E O P E N E R - S T O R Y H O O K

On a tiny rocky Greek island, stuck out in the sea far from land, lives a community of seniors who appear to be cheating death. Residents of this island routinely live to 100, 105, even 110. And they don‘t live their final decades hunched over in wheelchairs or confused in nursing homes. They work their gardens. They drink wine with friends. They swim in the ocean. Some of them still climb trees. What are they doing that the rest of the world has missed?

Length: 700-1,200 words. Build the story for 60-120 seconds without naming what it’s about. Introduce the mystery or suppression element. Then bridge to the Big Claim.



<!-- Page 31 -->



### Thestoryleadrules

Story leads work when the story is (1) specific, with a concrete place or person; (2) dramatic, with stakes or mystery; (3) relevant, with a clear bridge to your product; and (4) true. Invented stories get caught and destroy credibility. Find real stories. We’ll cover exactly how to find them in Chapter 10.

Writing Rules for the Opening A few rules that apply regardless of which variant you’re using:

• No greetings before the Hook. “Hi, I’m [name]” never appears in the first 30

seconds of any top-performing VSL. Presenter introduction comes at the end of the Hook, after the promise has landed.

• The first sentence does the heaviest lifting. Spend disproportionate time on

it. If the first sentence doesn’t earn the second, nothing else matters.

• Name something specific and unexplained in the first 60 seconds. A vial. A

building. A person by first name. A compound with a code name. A date. The specificity is what makes the curiosity sticky.

• Say the headline number aloud twice in the first 5 minutes. Repetition

anchors it in working memory. “$150 trillion… that’s enough to make every American family a millionaire… $150 trillion…”

• Never resolve the curiosity gap in the Hook. The reveal is Element 10, at 60-70

percent of the VSL. If you name the company, the compound, or the specific mechanism this early, the viewer has no reason to stay.

• Specificity beats superlatives, always. Kill every “incredible,” “amazing,”

“massive,” “huge” that isn’t backed by a specific number or fact. Specifics do the convincing; adjectives just sound like selling.



<!-- Page 32 -->



The Bridge Out Every section of a VSL ends with a bridge line that sets up the next section. The Opening is no exception. Your Hook / Big Claim / Curiosity-Gap Teaser section should end with a transition into Credibility and Origin.

The canonical bridge line: “But before I get into any of that, you deserve to know who I am and why I’m the one bringing you this.”

You can phrase it differently, but the shape is always the same. A promise to deliver the payoff, followed by a pause to introduce the presenter. That pause is critical. It lets the viewer catch their breath before the Credibility dump, and it signals that the presenter is confident enough to step out of the claim and into their own story.

### Theopeningchecklist

Before moving on, verify: (1) no greeting in the first 30 seconds; (2) a specific unexplained noun in the first 60 seconds; (3) the Big Claim is in the first 3 minutes with number + timeframe + catalyst; (4) the Big Claim number is stated aloud at least twice; (5) the curiosity object is named but not resolved; (6) the section ends with a bridge line into Credibility.

Once all six boxes are checked, you’ve got an Opening that can carry the rest of the VSL. Now it’s time to earn the trust.

## Chapter Five

### Building Trust

A FTER THE HOOK COMES the moment that separates amateur VSLs from professional ones. The viewer has been pulled in. They want to know the promise is real. They want to know the curiosity object is what you say it is. But before they can hear any of that, they need to decide whether to trust you.

This is where the Credibility Bio and the Origin Story do their work. Elements 4 and 5 of the framework. Together, they convert the viewer’s attention into trust, and they turn the presenter from “some guy making claims” into “someone who’s on my side.”

I’m going to cover them together because they’re always written together. Splitting them creates seams. Handled as one unit, they produce the single most important psychological pivot in the entire VSL.

The Two Parallel Jobs Two things are happening in parallel when a viewer watches this section of the VSL.

Both need to land, and both need to land completely. The Credibility Job Convert the viewer’s “is this guy legit?” skepticism into “okay, this guy knows what he’s talking about.” Achieved through specific credentials, named past calls, media validation, and - this one is counterintuitive - a specific moment of past failure or doubt. Authority gets built through facts. But real trust gets built when the presenter shows they’re human.



<!-- Page 34 -->



The Origin Job Convert the viewer’s “is this guy just trying to get rich off me?” skepticism into “this → guy is on my side.” Achieved through a specific narrative structure: insider state → → disillusionment decision to leave mission to help regular people.

By the time this section ends, the viewer should be thinking: “I trust this person. Show me what you’ve got.” That’s the mental state the Villain / Problem / Mechanism section picks up from.

The Structure, in Four Parts

## Part Amicrocredibilityfirst60Seconds100200Words

The first lines of the Credibility section establish the essentials in one concise pass: presenter name, current role or title, and the single most impressive credential relevant to this VSL’s Big Idea.

### M I C R O - C R E D I B I L I T Y E X A M P L E

My name is Ian King. I‘m a 20-year Wall Street veteran and one of the world‘s leading technology investment experts.

That’s it. Don’t bury it. Don’t stack it with five more facts. One sentence, then move.

## Part Bcredibilitystack24Min300500Words

Now expand with the full stack. There’s an optimal order of impact:

• Institutional affiliation: specific Wall Street firms, specific government roles,

specific academic credentials, specific medical institutions.

• Named past calls with specific numbers and dates: “I called Tesla in August

2019 and we saw 736% gains.” “I testified before the Treasury in 2007 warning of the financial crisis.”

• Media validation: CNBC, Fox Business, Forbes, Wall Street Journal, specific

quotes from specific publications.



<!-- Page 35 -->



• Trust from insiders: “Morgan Stanley and UBS analysts follow my work.” “Bill

Gates and Eric Schmidt followed my research.”

• Reader volume: “Over 100,000 subscribers across 30 countries.” Scale as

social proof. The key rule: distribute these credentials, don’t dump them. A paragraph with five credentials in a row reads like a résumé. Spread them across four to six sentences with breathing room. Each sentence should land one credential cleanly, not three mashed together.

## Part Cthehumilityanchor90Sec3Min200400Words

This is the single most underused element in amateur VSL scripts, and one of the most powerful. Every top-performing Agora VSL includes a specific, concrete moment of past failure or doubt somewhere in the Credibility / Origin arc. Not generic humility. A specific scene.

### Samplehumilityanchor

My first year on a trading desk was the most humbling period of my professional life. I lost money. A lot of it. Every trade I put on went the wrong way. After about ten months, I walked into my mentor‘s office and told him I wanted to quit. He asked me to sit down. And I‘ll never forget what he said. He looked at me and said: ‘Now that you‘ve figured out every way to lose, you can figure out how to win.’

Why humility works:

• Pre-empts the viewer’s “this guy’s too perfect” skepticism.

• Creates relatability (the presenter was once where the viewer is).

• Makes the eventual wins feel earned, not lucky.

• Disarms the “why should I trust you?” reflex before it fires.



<!-- Page 36 -->



### Therule

Every VSL needs at least one humility anchor, and it must be scene-level specific. “I’ve had some failures” is not a humility anchor. “In March 2003, I blew up a $40,000 account in three weeks of bad trades, and my wife didn’t speak to me for a month” is a humility anchor.

## Part Doriginstory24Min300500Words

Four beats, in order. Every Origin Story hits all four. Skip any and the structure collapses. Beat 1: The Before State Presenter was an insider. Wall Street, conventional medicine, government, corporate America. Making good money. Playing the system successfully. Name the firm, the role, the income bracket if relevant.

Beat 2: The Disillusionment Moment A specific incident. Not “I started to realize…” but “One afternoon in [specific year], I was sitting in [specific place] when [specific thing happened]…” The more concrete and scene-based, the better.

Beat 3: The Decision to Leave The pivot point. Walking away from the firm or the practice or the career. A mentor figure often appears here. Ian King has the head of his trading firm. Dr. Gerhauser has his realization after years of treating patients with pharmaceutical interventions that didn’t address root causes.

Beat 4: The Mission Statement What the presenter does now, and why. Explicitly for “regular people” or “Main Street Americans” or “seniors like you and me” or “hardworking folks who deserve better.”

The bridge out of Origin goes naturally into the Villain section that follows. The typical line: “And that’s why I get so angry when I see what Wall Street is doing to regular Americans right now…” or “Which is why I have such a problem with what Big Pharma has been doing to seniors for the last thirty years…”



<!-- Page 37 -->



The Three Requirements Any Credibility / Origin section that skips any of these three things falls flat:

• Real credentials you can verify. Never invent or embellish. One fact-check

from a skeptical reader and the whole VSL’s credibility dies.

• At least one humility anchor. Specific, scene-level, concrete.

• An explicit rejection of the insider identity. “I left Wall Street behind.” “I don’t

want to go on CNBC every morning.” “I’m not just another doctor in a white coat.” This positioning is what converts the insider credential into underdog alignment with the viewer.

Most VSLs fail on the last one. Writers spend all their effort on credentials and forget to position the presenter as having chosen the viewer’s side. Without that positioning, all the credentials read as “impressive person who doesn’t relate to me.” With it, the credentials read as “impressive person who’s chosen to help people like me.” That’s an enormous difference.

### Thebridgeout

End your Credibility / Origin section with a line that sets up the Villain. “And that’s why I’m so angry about what’s happening right now…” Or: “Which is why what I’m about to show you matters so much.” The bridge is what keeps the emotional momentum flowing.

Once the viewer trusts you and knows whose side you’re on, they’re ready for the core teaching. Which is where we head next.

## Chapter Six

### Installing Belief

I F THE HOOK GOT their attention and Credibility earned their trust, this next section is where the real work happens. The intellectual and emotional heart of the VSL. The place where “I trust this guy” becomes “I understand why this works, and I want in.”

Three elements operate together in this section. The Villain. The Sharpened Problem. The Mechanism. These get written as one unit because they form a single belief-installation phase. Split them apart and you lose the momentum.

By the end of this section, the viewer should have three new beliefs installed:

• The reason I haven’t gotten this result already is not my fault. It’s the Villain’s

fault.

• If I don’t act now, the window closes. The damage continues. The insiders

take it all.

• I understand HOW this works. The mechanism makes sense. It’s real.

Install those three beliefs cleanly and the viewer is ready to buy. Fail to install any one of them and the rest of the VSL can’t recover.

The Villain The first element of this section is the Villain. And this is where a lot of writers get skittish. They worry that naming a villain sounds paranoid or divisive. They try to soften it. They use vague phrases like “misaligned incentives” or “some bad actors.”



<!-- Page 39 -->



Resist that instinct. Every high-converting VSL names a specific external villain. Not because it’s manipulative, but because it’s accurate. There are real structural forces that keep regular people from getting good investment advice, good health information, good financial products. Naming them clearly is a service, not a smear.

A good villain has three properties:

• Named or named category. Not “bad actors” but “Wall Street” or “Big

Pharma” or “the FDA” or “the Federal Reserve.”

• Stated motive. Why does the villain do what it does? Profit from chronic

disease, information asymmetry that favors institutional clients, regulatory capture, protection of existing revenue streams.

• Specific evidence. Named wrong predictors, named suppression events,

named regulatory decisions. Don’t just claim the villain does bad things. Prove it with specifics. Here’s what a villain section actually sounds like when it’s written well:

### Samplevillainsection

Here‘s how the game is actually played. Wall Street makes its money from big institutional clients. Hedge funds, pension funds, endowments. These clients pay the highest fees. So the best research, the earliest ideas, the pre- breakout companies - those all go to the institutions first. By the time a regular retail investor sees a stock on CNBC, the institutional money has usually been in for six months or more. The gains the everyday person sees are the last wave. The institutions already had the first 500%. In 2018, GM Vice Chairman Bob Lutz said rare car collectors should buy a Tesla Model S because he was convinced Tesla was going bankrupt. Tesla has since 10x‘d.

Morgan Stanley analyst Adam Jonas said in 2019 that Tesla could drop to $10. The stock is over $600. These are the people you‘re competing with for information.

### O N E V I L L A I N , N O T F I V E



<!-- Page 40 -->



The Rule of One applies to the Villain too. One villain. Multiple villains fracture the narrative. Pick the one most relevant to your Big Idea and stay with it.

The Sharpened Problem The Problem has been implicit since the Hook. Now it gets sharpened into now-or- never urgency. This is where you give the viewer a reason to act today instead of bookmarking and coming back later.

There are three urgency vectors you can pull from. Use one or two, not all three. Stacking all three reads as fear-mongering and destroys credibility.

Vector 1: External Deadline A named date when things change. Supreme Court decision. Government announcement. Earnings call. Product launch. Regulatory change. The specificity is what makes it credible. “Sometime soon” is not an urgency. “July 23rd” is.

Vector 2: Market-Moving Event “Once Wall Street catches on, the price spikes.” “Once the processing facility goes live, revenue surges.” “Once the mainstream press covers this, it’s already too late.”

This works when there’s a real catalyst that’s genuinely close at hand. Vector 3: Personal Cost of Inaction Health: disease progresses, aging accelerates, chemical exposure compounds.

Finance: retirement underfunded, inflation eroding savings, missed bull run. The viewer’s life is getting worse by the day until they act.

Whichever vector you choose, the Sharpened Problem has a specific pattern: name the urgency, then immediately pivot to “but here’s the good news, there’s a solution that works if you act now.” Don’t dwell in despair. Dwell in motivated urgency. The viewer needs to feel the pressure, but they also need to feel that there’s a way out.



<!-- Page 41 -->



The Mechanism The Mechanism is the longest content section of the entire VSL. For a medium- length VSL, it runs 900 to 1,400 words - 6 to 10 minutes of spoken content. That’s 10 to 15 percent of the total runtime. It’s the intellectual heart of the entire piece.

Here’s what the Mechanism must accomplish:

• Give the strategy a proprietary, branded name. “The Innovation Breakout

Curve.” “The Alpha Molecule System.” “The Warburg Method.” “The American Birthright.”

• Break it into 3-5 named components. Not more, not fewer. Three to five is the

memorable sweet spot.

• Teach it at the strategy level, not the tactics level. Why it works, not how the

viewer would execute it alone.

• Illustrate with one walked-through past example, where each component is

explicitly demonstrated. Why It Needs a Proprietary Name When you give a mechanism a proprietary name, three things happen at once.

First, it becomes memorable. The viewer can say it back in their head, which means they can refer to it later. Second, it becomes ownable. It’s your system, not a generic approach anyone could copy. Third, it becomes a shortcut. Once you’ve named it, you can reference it repeatedly without explaining it each time.

The name should feel coined, not generic. “A systematic approach to finding emerging companies” is generic. “The Innovation Breakout Curve” is branded. The difference matters.

Why Components Need to Be Named A Mechanism with unnamed components is just vibes. It sounds impressive but the viewer can’t hold it in their head. When each component has a name - “Metric One: Tipping-Point Industry,” “Metric Two: Revenue Growth Above 20%,” “Metric Three: Cash Reinvestment,” “Metric Four: The Catalyst” - the viewer can follow along, and more importantly, remember it later.



<!-- Page 42 -->



The named components also create an implicit promise. If the mechanism has four specific metrics, then by buying the product, the viewer is going to learn what those metrics are and how to apply them. That’s a concrete value proposition, not a vague one.

The Visual Metaphor Every good mechanism has a visual metaphor that anchors the whole thing. Ian King’s Innovation Breakout Curve uses an S-curve with three zones. Gerhauser’s Alpha Molecules imagery uses the immune system as a “sticky flypaper” catching free radicals. Dr. Inglis’s cancer protocol uses cancer cells as “sugar addicts” starving without glucose.

The metaphor does two things. It simplifies the concept so the viewer can grasp it in a moment. And it stays accurate enough that the viewer doesn’t feel condescended to. If you can’t find a visual metaphor for your mechanism, your mechanism may not be well-defined enough yet. Keep working on it until it is.

Teaching Strategy, Not Tactics This is the place where more VSLs fail than anywhere else. The writer knows the mechanism works. They’re excited about it. They want to share everything they know. So they explain exactly how to execute it.

That’s the mistake. If you teach the full how, the viewer thinks “I can do this myself” and leaves without buying.

What you teach in the Mechanism section is why. Why the approach works. Why each component matters. Why the combination produces results. What happens when a component is missing. What you don’t teach is the specifics of executing it on the viewer’s own.

### Theteachingrule

Teach strategy, sell tactics. The VSL shows why the mechanism works. The product delivers how to execute it. If the viewer leaves thinking “I can replicate this without buying,” you revealed too much.



<!-- Page 43 -->



Walking Through a Past Example After naming the Mechanism and walking through its components, immediately illustrate with one specific past example. Ideally this is one of your strongest past wins from Foundation.

Walk through the past example with explicit reference to each component. “SolarEdge in 2019 met all four of my metrics. Metric One: residential solar was entering mass adoption. Metric Two: SolarEdge had just reported 43% year-over- year revenue growth. Metric Three: they had $800 million in cash reinvestment.

Metric Four: California had just mandated solar on new construction. So I recommended it at $82. Twelve months later, 169% average gain.”

This walkthrough serves a double purpose. It proves the Mechanism is real (the viewer just saw it work). And it rehearses the Mechanism in the viewer’s head (the repetition helps them remember it).

Don’t walk through every past win this way - save the rest for the Proof section that comes next. One detailed walkthrough in the Mechanism section. That’s the pattern.

The Bridge Out End the Mechanism by reattaching it to the current opportunity. “Which is why, when I applied these four metrics to [current opportunity], I found something remarkable.” Then hand off to Proof.

Your viewer now believes three things: someone is responsible for their past failures (the Villain), the window is closing (the Sharpened Problem), and the Mechanism is real and comprehensible (the Mechanism). What they haven’t yet seen is the stacked evidence that this has actually worked before.

That’s the next chapter. Proof, and the moment the curiosity gap finally breaks.

## Chapter Seven

### Proof And The Reveal

B Y THIS POINT IN THE VSL, you’ve done a lot of work. You’ve hooked the viewer, earned their trust, named their villain, sharpened the urgency, and taught them your proprietary mechanism. They believe the approach makes sense. They understand how it works.

What they don’t yet have is evidence. That’s what this chapter covers. Elements 9 and 10 of the framework. The Proof stack and the Discovery / Reveal. Together, these two elements convert “this sounds plausible” into “this is real and I need in.”

Why Proof and Reveal Belong Together I write these two elements as one unit for the same reason I paired Credibility and Origin. They’re always adjacent. They serve complementary jobs. And writing them separately breaks the momentum.

The Proof Job Convert the viewer’s “sounds plausible, but is it really this reliable?” skepticism into “okay, this actually works.” Achieved through stacked evidence. Past wins. Case studies. Testimonials. Institutional validation. Media mentions. One tier of proof is thin. Three tiers stacked together is overwhelming.

The Reveal Job Pay off the curiosity gap that was planted in the Hook. Show the vial. Name the compound category. Reveal the building’s purpose. Give the viewer enough that



<!-- Page 45 -->



they feel rewarded for watching - but withhold the specific actionable information, so they still need to buy the product to act.

That balance is the trickiest part of the entire VSL. Give too little and the viewer feels cheated. Give too much and they can act without buying. Most writers err on the side of giving too much. We’ll spend time on this.

The Three Tiers of Proof Stack your proof in three tiers for cumulative effect. If you have assets for all three, use all three. If one tier is empty, skip it rather than fabricate - fake proof gets caught, and faked proof destroys everything upstream.

Tier 1: Past Wins and Case Studies Three to five specific past successes, each told as a mini-narrative of 80 to 150 words. Tight, specific, factual.

For finance VSLs: “I recommended [Company] in [Month, Year] at $[price]. Within [timeframe], my readers had the chance to [sell at X% / hold for Y%]. That’s a [specific %] gain.”

For health VSLs: “[First name + last initial] came to me in [year] with [specific condition]. We worked together on the [mechanism protocol] for [timeframe].

Within [specific period], [specific outcome].”

### S A M P L E P A S T W I N - F I N A N C E

In August of 2019, I recommended SolarEdge to my readers at $82 a share. The company had just reported 43% year-over-year revenue growth.

California had just mandated solar on new construction. All four of my metrics lit up. I told my readers to buy. Within twelve months, we sold half of our position in July 2020 for a 110% gain. The other half in October 2020 for a 227% gain. That‘s a 169% average gain - in less than a year.

Tier 2: Testimonials and Reader Letters Three to five testimonials in quick succession, each 40 to 80 words. Often introduced as “And it’s not just me - let me share what a few of my readers have written in…”



<!-- Page 46 -->



The pattern is rhythmic repetition: “Michael G. wrote… Dorothy M. wrote… Cliff F. wrote…” Each testimonial on its own feels cherry-picked. Three or four in a row feels systemic.

### Sampletestimonialtier

Cliff F. wrote in recently: ‘I‘ve been following your research for three years. After SolarEdge, I was hooked. Thank you for showing a small-time investor like me that these opportunities actually exist for people like us.’ Michael J. wrote: ‘I invested $8,000 in Tesla when you called it. Twelve months later, that position was worth over $60,000. I honestly can‘t thank you enough.’ Marissa

C. wrote: ‘I‘m a retired teacher. I don‘t have a lot of money to work with. But the

returns on my small positions have given me a cushion I never thought I‘d have at this stage of my life.’

### Testimonialformat

First name + last initial. Not full names (legal exposure). Not just initials (reads as fake). First name + last initial is the sweet spot. Always real people. Never invented.

Tier 3: Institutional Validation This tier varies by niche. For finance, it’s usually the “smart money is already in” pattern. Named institutional investors with position sizes.

For health, it’s named research institutions and specific peer-reviewed studies. A Stanford study. An Institute of Aging paper. A Mayo Clinic position.

For any niche, it can include media validation - specific outlets with specific quotes. USA Today. Barron’s. The Wall Street Journal. Forbes.

### Samplesmartmoneystack

## Chapter Eight

### The Offer And The Price Anchor

M OST WRITERS DRAMATICALLY UNDERESTIMATE how much work the Offer section does. They think of it as a footer - “the part where you list what they get and name a price.” That framing is catastrophically wrong.

Top-converting Agora VSLs spend 20 to 30 percent of total runtime on the Offer and Price. That’s a minimum of 12 minutes in a 60-minute VSL. Sometimes 20 minutes or more. The Offer section is not a footer. It’s a full act - equal in importance to the Mechanism.

Here’s why. The viewer has, by this point, been persuaded intellectually. They believe the framework. They believe the mechanism. They want the specifics. But wanting is not buying. Between “I want this” and “I’m going to hand over my credit card” is a psychological distance that has to be closed deliberately.

The Offer section closes that distance. Through cumulative value stacking. Through careful price anchoring. Through positioning that makes $47 feel like a rounding error against $995 of value. That’s the work happening here, and it deserves real time and real writing craft.

## Chapter Nine

### The Close

T HE FINAL SECTION OF AN AGORA VSL IS THE CLOSE. Scarcity, Guarantee, CTA, Sign-off, and optional P.S. Elements 13 and 14 of the framework. Roughly 7 to 10 percent of the total runtime.

This is the section most writers get wrong in the most predictable way. They feel the urgency of being at the end, so they push too hard. They stack every scarcity tactic they’ve ever heard of. They cram in five more bonuses. They repeat the CTA eight times in the final three minutes.

Don’t. The close is the most delicate section of the entire VSL, and the instinct to push harder at the end is exactly wrong. The close works when it feels inevitable, not forced. When scarcity is real, not manufactured. When the guarantee is so generous that refusing to try feels irrational. When the CTA is repeated enough to land, but not so much that the viewer feels bullied.

Here’s how to do it right. The Two Barriers That Remain By this point, the viewer has accepted your big idea, bought into your mechanism, seen your proof, understood the reveal, and absorbed the stack value. They know exactly what they’re being offered and how much it costs.

Yet they’re not yet clicking. Two specific psychological barriers stand between them and the buy button:

• “Maybe I’ll come back later.” Defeated by Scarcity - real, credible reasons

they must act now.



<!-- Page 58 -->



• “What if it doesn’t work for me?” Defeated by the Guarantee - financial risk

removed entirely. Once those two are handled, the last job is just to point them at the button - multiple times, in slightly varied ways - and close with a warm, mission-anchored sign-off.

Scarcity - Use One Type, Not Four There are four scarcity types. Pick the truest, strongest one for your offer. Using multiple stacked scarcities reads as manufactured and damages credibility.

Type A: Quantity Scarcity A specific number of slots. “My publisher will only allow us to offer this pricing to the next 500 new subscribers. Once we hit that number, the discount comes down.”

Works when there’s a real business reason for the cap - fulfillment capacity, customer support load, research integrity with too many subscribers moving markets.

Type B: Time Scarcity A specific expiration. “This discount is only available for the next 24 hours. After midnight tomorrow, the price goes back to $199.” Works when paired with a reason why - end of promotional period, end of quarter, specific event.

Type C: Event Scarcity The opportunity itself has a deadline. “The Mar-a-Lago announcement is expected within the next 30 days. Once that hits the news, the window to get positioned at today’s prices is closed. This isn’t about my offer - it’s about the market itself.” This is the strongest scarcity type because it’s external and verifiable.

Type D: Publisher Scarcity The publisher or company imposes the limit. “My publisher only lets me run this kind of deep discount once every 18 months.” Works for established publishers with a history of the practice.

health, especially with physical books. Longer builds more trust but increases operational burden.

• Any-reason refund. “For any reason at all.” No conditions. No hoops. No fine

print.

they refund, they keep everything you sent them.

## Chapter Ten

### Finding Stories That Sell

W E’VE SPENT NINE CHAPTERS ON STRUCTURE. Fourteen elements, seven sessions, start to finish. But structure alone doesn’t make a VSL work. Structure is the skeleton. What makes the VSL come alive is the story at its center.

Specifically, the curiosity object. The vial. The mysterious building. The 1944 Berlin laboratory. The Greek island where people live past 100. The compound extracted from grape skins. Without a compelling curiosity object at the heart of the Hook, even a perfectly structured VSL falls flat.

And this is where writers most often get stuck. They’ve got the product. They’ve got the big idea. They’ve done the Foundation work. But they can’t find the story. “My product is a financial newsletter,” they think. “There’s no story there. It’s just picks and analysis.”

That’s wrong, and the best tool I’ve ever seen for proving it wrong comes from a copywriter named Mike Schauer. Mike runs a site called Swiped.co that catalogues great sales copy, and in one of his paid reports he laid out a four-step research process for finding stories to sell any product. I’m going to walk you through his method in this chapter, because it’s the single best tool I know for unsticking a stuck VSL.

The Product Origin Story Schauer calls his technique the Product Origin Story. But he doesn’t mean what most people mean by an origin story. He’s not talking about how you, personally, came up with the product. He’s talking about something much more useful.



<!-- Page 65 -->



Schauer’s insight is that every product contains, buried in its ingredients or methodology or philosophy, some connection to a story that already exists in the world. A historical figure who did something parallel. An ancient practice that foreshadowed the modern technique. A suppressed discovery. A forgotten invention. These stories are already out there, sitting in Wikipedia articles and obscure history books and half-remembered news archives. The copywriter’s job is to find the right one and make the connection explicit.

This is not making up stories. It’s finding real, true stories that happen to illustrate the product’s underlying logic. And then telling those stories cinematically, so they land for the viewer with the emotional weight of a good documentary.

### Thekeyinsight

You’re not inventing stories. You’re finding stories that already exist and making the connection to your product explicit. This is research, not fiction.

Why Story Leads Work So Well Before we get into the method, one quick note on why this matters so much for Agora-style VSLs specifically.

Cold traffic is allergic to direct sales pitches. The moment a viewer realizes they’re being sold to, their defenses go up. Every claim gets read through a filter of skepticism. Every credential feels like bragging. Every proof point feels like cherry- picking.

Stories bypass that filter entirely. Humans are wired to process narrative with suspended judgment. We’ve been sitting around fires telling each other stories for a hundred thousand years, and our brains don’t apply the same skeptical screening to a good story that they apply to a sales claim. By the time the viewer realizes they’re being sold to, they’re already emotionally invested in the outcome.

This is why Story Leads are the dominant Hook type in Agora health VSLs and the most ambitious Agora finance VSLs. Not because stories are a trick. But because stories are how humans have always made sense of new information.

Schauer’s Four-Step Process Here’s the method. Four steps. Do them in order. The whole process takes about an hour if you commit to it.

Step 1: Deconstruct the Product Take the product apart conceptually. For each component, ingredient, methodology, or philosophical essence, write down what it’s about at a non- technical level.

For a health supplement: the active ingredients, the extraction method, the philosophical approach (natural versus synthetic, ancient versus modern). For a stock pick: the underlying industry, the technology, the historical precedent for the opportunity, the analogous past moves. For a newsletter: the research methodology, the philosophical stance, the demographic it serves.

The key rule: deconstruct to topics that are NOT technology-specific or industry- specific. Those have limited history. Universal topics have rich history.

### Deconstructionexample

Product: A rare earth magnet-based investment opportunity. Surface level (don‘t use): ‘neodymium magnets,’ ‘EV motors,’ ‘semiconductor supply chain.’ Deconstructed level (do use): ‘strategic minerals,’ ‘supply monopolies,’ ‘foreign dependence,’ ‘technology bottlenecks,’ ‘resource scarcity,’ ‘national security,’ ‘mining ghost towns.’ Notice how the deconstructed level opens up centuries of history. The surface level only opens up the last twenty years.

The trigger phrases matter - they’re what shift your search results from “modern news coverage” to “historical stories with narrative weight.”

Step 4: Dramatize Using the Five Rules This is where the story gets transformed from a Wikipedia summary into a VSL Hook.

Schauer identifies five specific techniques that top copywriters use to turn raw historical material into cinematic narrative.

### Thefivedramatizationrules

1. Romanticize and enhance. Tell the truth, but the dramatic version. 2. Simplify

ruthlessly. Only the juicy parts. 3. Make it feel brand new - like a discovery, a cover-up, an untold story. 4. Brand the lesson with a proprietary name. 5.

Embed promises and benefits - remember, you’re using the story to sell. Let me walk through each briefly.

Rule 1: Romanticize and Enhance Tell the truth, but pick the dramatic version of every true detail. Don’t invent. But do select. If the original account says “the research was conducted over several years and produced interesting findings,” the dramatic version says “for seven years, in a concrete bunker in Siberia, Soviet scientists documented something that would change our understanding of human longevity forever.” Both are true. Only one has narrative weight.

Rule 2: Simplify Ruthlessly No dry facts. No filler. No “background context” that doesn’t serve the story. A two- hour documentary compresses to 90 seconds of narrative. Cut anything the viewer doesn’t need to feel the emotional shape of the story.

### Format, Length, And Advanced Techniques

T HE 14-ELEMENT FRAMEWORK YOU’VE learned in the first nine chapters is universal. It applies to every Agora-style VSL regardless of niche, audience, or presenter. But there are still decisions to make about how the VSL gets packaged: how long it runs, what format it uses, and which optional techniques you layer on top.

This chapter covers those decisions. Three format choices. Four length targets. Six advanced techniques that top-performing VSLs use selectively. Nothing in this

choices instead of defaulting to whatever feels natural. Format Choice Agora-style VSLs come in three main formats. The choice depends on the size of your claim and the skepticism level of your audience.

Format A: Solo Presenter One presenter delivers the entire VSL. Usually filmed in a simple setting - office, library, studio. Full first-person singular throughout. This is the most common format and what I’d recommend as your default.

Solo works best when:



<!-- Page 74 -->



• The claim is substantial but not extraordinary. “$2,000 a month in additional

retirement income” is solo-appropriate. “$150 trillion wealth transfer” probably isn’t.

• The presenter has a strong personal brand that carries weight on their own.

• The VSL is medium-length (40-70 minutes). Solo can get monotonous over

90 minutes.

• The offer is a single-product subscription, not a bundle of multiple experts.

Format B: Interview (Two-Person) A host asks questions; an expert delivers content. The host plays the audience’s surrogate, asking the questions the viewer would ask. This format is the best tool for claims that are too large for a solo presenter to carry credibly.

Interview works best when:

• The claim is very large or hard to believe. A $150 trillion trust fund; a 10,000%

upside; a medical breakthrough that mainstream medicine rejects. When the claim is big, an interrogating host makes the claim feel vetted rather than boasted.

• Positioning an expert who isn’t self-promotional enough to carry a solo VSL.

Some of the best researchers are terrible at self-marketing. A host fills that gap.

• Longer VSLs (70-120 minutes). The two-voice structure breaks up monotony.

The host’s questions should do specific jobs at specific moments:

• Specifically skeptical at key claim moments: “Jim, if this is real, why hasn’t

anyone else talked about it?”

• Clarifying at technical moments: “So walk us through exactly how this works

for someone who’s never heard of Title 30 before.”

• Emotionally on-behalf-of-viewer at objection moments: “For someone

hearing this for the first time, Jim, this sounds almost too good to be true.” Format C: Roundtable (Three-Person) Host plus two experts, often with controlled disagreement on minor points. Used for very ambitious VSLs or bundled offers from multiple experts. This is the least common format, and also the most logistically complex.

Roundtable works best when:

• Very cold traffic requires maximum social proof. Multiple experts validating

the thesis carries more weight than one.

• Bundling multiple newsletters from multiple authors into one offer. The

roundtable gives each expert their due while keeping the VSL cohesive.

• Very long VSLs (100 minutes and up). Three voices can sustain attention

longer than two. The “controlled disagreement” pattern is worth noting. Two experts appear to disagree on a secondary point but agree on the Big Idea. “I actually see it differently

- I think it’s even more bullish than Jim does” is the classic version. This creates

verisimilitude without fracturing the core thesis.

Six Advanced Techniques These are optional techniques observed in top-performing Agora VSLs. None are required. But they’re worth understanding so you can layer them in when they fit.



<!-- Page 77 -->



Technique 1: The Mini-VSL in the P.S. After the main CTA and Scarcity / Guarantee section, a brief three-to-eight-minute mini-narrative introduces one additional bonus report. It has its own micro-Hook, micro-Claim, and micro-CTA that loops back to the same order button.

Why it works: a substantial fraction of viewers drift during the final CTA repetitions. The Mini-VSL re-engages them with a fresh hook at the end, catching viewers who were “almost there” and pushing them over.

Best used for: Long and Very Long VSLs (100-plus minutes), especially when the additional bonus has its own compelling backstory that wouldn’t fit in the main narrative.

Canonical example: Jim Rickards’s $150 Trillion Birthright Trust VSL uses a Mini-VSL at the end introducing a Russian uranium-themed bonus report.

Technique 2: Two-Tier Pricing The main offer is presented at the standard discount (say, $49 for one year). Then a premium tier is offered - longer duration, additional bonuses, or advanced service level - at a higher price with aggressive social proof like “80 percent of subscribers chose the Premium option.”

Why it works: the anchor-and-alternatives effect. When presented with a standard option and a premium option, a significant fraction of buyers will choose premium, raising average revenue per subscriber without alienating the price-sensitive.

Best used for: offers where a subset of subscribers actively want more (more picks, more research, more service). Newsletters with tiered model portfolios are a natural fit.

Pricing ratios: premium is typically 2-4x the standard. Bonuses stacked on premium should be genuinely better - longer subscription, higher-tier research, additional reports - not just padding.

Technique 3: Physical Book Deliverable The hero deliverable is a printed, bound, mailed book rather than a digital PDF.

Often features uniquely numbered first editions, a Certificate of Authenticity signed by the presenter, branded packaging, and sometimes included physical supplements.



<!-- Page 78 -->



Why it works: tangibility converts. Physical objects feel more valuable than digital ones. The waiting period for delivery creates post-purchase anticipation that reduces refund rates. Certificate-of-Authenticity framing converts the purchase into a collectible.

Best used for: health VSLs especially. A 300-plus page “encyclopedia” book justifies the $99 retail value and premium subscription price. Less common but effective in finance for high-ticket offerings above $199.

Canonical examples: The Secrets of Underground Medicine (Natural Health Response), The Doctor’s Book of Survival Home Remedies (various health publishers).

Technique 4: The Humility Anchor A specific past-failure moment woven into the Origin Story pre-empts the “too perfect” skepticism. I covered this in Chapter 5 because it’s essentially required, not advanced - but it’s worth restating here.

Optimal placement: mid-Credibility Stack, before the Origin Story proper. 100 to 200 words of specific scene-level detail.

Technique 5: The “Tried That” Frame A brief section listing the alternatives the viewer has already tried (index funds, standard supplements, conventional doctors), framed not hostilely but sympathetically. “You’ve done everything reasonable; the reason it hasn’t worked isn’t your fault, it’s that the fundamental approach is incomplete.”

Why it works: pre-empts the objection “I’ve tried these kinds of things before.” By naming the tried-and-failed alternatives first, the VSL positions itself as the next logical step.

Best used for: audiences the user knows have prior failed attempts. Works well in retirement-planning and weight-loss spaces especially.

Technique 6: Specific Date Targeting The VSL references a specific upcoming date by which the opportunity must be acted on - an earnings call, a Fed meeting, a legislative deadline, a product launch.

The date becomes the urgency anchor.



<!-- Page 79 -->



Why it works: specific dates are more urgency-productive than vague “soon” framings. The viewer can mark the calendar. The urgency becomes verifiable, which paradoxically makes it more credible, not less.

Best used for: finance VSLs with real external catalysts. Don’t invent deadlines - if there’s no real date, don’t force one. Invented deadlines are the single fastest way to destroy credibility when they pass without the predicted event.

Element 7: The Sharpened Problem The urgency vector here is Personal Cost of Inaction - aging accelerates every day, mitochondrial function declines, the biological clock keeps ticking whether you intervene or not. Without dwelling in despair, Gerhauser pivots: “but here’s the good news, these four compounds work fast, and you can start in the next 30 days.”



<!-- Page 92 -->



Element 8: The Mechanism The proprietary mechanism is what Gerhauser calls “The Superhuman Protocol,” built around the four compounds. Each compound has a branded name (the Alpha Molecule, R9, Gray Salt, and the fourth held for the book). The visual metaphor: the immune system as sticky flypaper, with the compounds acting as enhancers. Each compound gets its own segment: what it is at a category level, why it matters biologically, what happens without it, and a teaser for the specific brand or source that’s disclosed in the book.

Element 9: Proof Tier 1 (case studies): specific patients named with first name + last initial, specific conditions (fatigue, cognitive decline, joint pain, inflammation markers), specific timeframes, specific outcomes. Frank M., 74, Stage II prostate markers. Barbara T., 68, fibromyalgia for 18 years. Each told as a narrative with clear before-and-after.

Health equivalent of honest-losses disclaimer included: “Results vary. Not every patient responds the same way. This is not a guarantee of any specific outcome.”

Tier 2 (testimonials): reader letters from subscribers who have used specific protocols from the newsletter.

Tier 3 (institutional): peer-reviewed studies from named institutions - Stanford, the Institute of Aging, published papers in specific journals. Plus the cultural/historical validation of the Ikarian population as a Blue Zone.

Element 10: The Reveal Each of the four compounds is category-revealed. Alpha Molecule is described as a specific class of antioxidants. R9 is revealed as an extract from a specific grape variety (but the brand and sourcing stay in the book). Gray Salt is described as a mineral-rich salt variety used in specific European coastal communities (but the specific source and dosing stay in the book). The viewer leaves the reveal knowing what the categories are - enough to feel satisfied - but not enough to act without the physical book.

Element 11: The Offer Stack Hero: “The Secrets of Superhuman Lifespans,” a physical book, 300+ pages, uniquely numbered first edition with a Certificate of Authenticity signed by Dr.

Gerhauser ($99 retail value). Bonus digital guides on specific protocols, each with stated values of $49-$99. Subscription: Natural Health Response newsletter,



<!-- Page 93 -->



$99/year normal, which is where the “free with subscription” framing of the book lives. Element 12: Price Framing Gerhauser uses the health-style price framing: the $99 book is free as a gift when you subscribe to the $49 newsletter trial. Total stack value around $300. Per-day math: “That’s about 13 cents a day. Less than a cup of coffee.” Publisher-advocate frame: the subscription trial is cheap because Newmarket Health wants subscribers for life.

Element 13: Scarcity Type: Publisher Scarcity - the deeply discounted first-year pricing is offered only during the launch window, and the publisher has limits on how many physical books are printed in each first-edition run.

Element 14: Guarantee + CTA + Close Guarantee: 60 days (longer than finance VSLs because the compounds need time to show results), any reason, full refund. Keep-the-book even after refund - the physical book is explicitly the subscriber’s to keep as a thank-you. Three CTAs distributed through the close. Sign-off: “Dr. Richard Gerhauser, M.D., signing off.

Thank you for trusting me with your time today.” Longer P.S. about acting while the compounds are still available from the specific sources in the book.

What to Notice Three things are specific to this health VSL that are worth studying. First, the physical book deliverable changes the entire economics. The book functions as both the hero and as proof of seriousness - a subscriber who’s going to receive a 300-page printed book in the mail feels differently about the purchase than someone getting a PDF. Waiting for delivery creates post-purchase anticipation that reduces refund rates. The Certificate of Authenticity framing makes it a collectible, not just content.

Second, the Story Lead setup carries further than a Big Secret Lead would. Gerhauser doesn’t mention his credentials until minute six. He doesn’t mention the product until minute twenty. The entire first section is just the Ikaria story, and that indirect setup works because the audience is Unaware - they didn’t come to the



<!-- Page 94 -->



page ready to buy a supplement. They came curious. The story has to warm them into the buying conversation before any other element can land.

Third, the Villain work is more structurally careful than in finance VSLs. Naming “Big Pharma” as a villain without tipping into conspiracy-theory territory requires real care. Gerhauser does it by being specific about incentives rather than intent. The pharmaceutical industry isn’t evil - it’s just structured around chronic-disease revenue. The FDA isn’t corrupt in a cartoon way - it’s subject to specific captured incentives. That careful framing keeps the villain credible to a cold audience that might otherwise write the whole pitch off.

If you write health VSLs, this is the template to internalize. The structure is identical to the finance template in Appendix A. But the textures - physical deliverable, Story Lead, personal health crisis humility anchor, the careful handling of Big Pharma - are specific to health. Study both appendices, and you’ll see how the universal structure adapts to each niche.
