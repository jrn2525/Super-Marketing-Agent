Cost Per Acquisition (CPA) - How Much Does It Cost to Get a Customer? There are two key numbers: CPA and ACV. CPA, or cost per acquisition, is how much you spend to get a customer. For instance, we pay Mark Zuckerberg to show ads on Facebook, and when someone clicks, that's our CPA.

Average Cart Value (ACV) - How Much Money Does That Customer Spend in the Funnel? The second number, ACV or average cart value, is how much money you make from each customer in your funnel. These two numbers are crucial. If you understand and balance them, everything becomes easier.

Let's go back to my example. If I give money to Zuckerberg, he shows my ad, and I get a customer. That's CPA. Then the customer goes through my funnel, buying products, which determines the average cart value. Understanding these numbers and how they interact is essential for success in funnel building. When customers go through your funnel, they may buy the first item, an upsell, or maybe nothing at all. On average, how much money do you make from each customer? That's the key.

## Secret #2

### Find The Control

F INDING THE FUNNEL CONTROL IS all about identifying and modeling the type of funnel that is currently working. This is the essence of Funnel Hacking. Instead of guessing, Funnel Hacking allows you to base your strategy on what's proven to work.

Companies like Agora use this method. They test different versions of their offers written by various copywriters. Then, they find out which one performs best and use that as their control. The winning version becomes the control because it has proven to be the most effective. This approach minimizes guesswork and maximizes the chances of success. By Funnel Hacking, you replicate and improve upon what's already working, ensuring a higher likelihood of success.

At Agora, the winning copywriter gets all the royalties, and they discard the rest. For a project, they might hire five copywriters, each paid around $25,000.

The one whose sales letter wins gets a 10-15% royalty on all sales. The process involves taking the best-performing sales letter - the control - and having all five copywriters rewrite the intro to try to beat it. If someone does, they become the new control and receive the royalties. One of my friends had a winning control for eight or nine years before someone finally beat his headline. This is the big leagues, where the stakes and the rewards are high.

Trey Lewellen, a fellow funnel hacker, had the control, generating millions of dollars a month. Instead of guessing or reinventing, I asked Trey to show me his entire funnel. With his proven structure, I went back with Steven Larson, and we built out the funnel.

When we acquired Zuma Juice, a supplement company, I knew I needed a solid funnel. I remembered their original launch, which had no upsells and ultimately failed. To avoid this, we started by Funnel Hacking. I bought 15-20 top supplement products to understand what was working. This wasn't just

This principle is the foundation of understanding ACV. The higher your ACV, the more you can afford to spend on customer acquisition, thus outpacing your competition.

- $37, $97, $297. Adding these together, the potential ACV could be $430.35.

The more offers within your funnel, the more chances you have to increase ACV, thus allowing you to spend more on customer acquisition.

## Secret #5

### Earnings Per Click (Epc)

I N THE WORLD OF DIGITAL marketing and sales funnels, there's a crucial metric that often goes overlooked - earnings per click (EPC). This metric is a cornerstone of your funnel's performance and can make the difference between a wildly successful campaign and one that barely breaks even.

Despite its importance, it's not discussed nearly enough. Earnings per click is prominently displayed on the ClickFunnels dashboard, but many marketers don't fully understand how to leverage this metric. EPC is the amount of revenue you generate for each click that enters your funnel. For instance, if your average cart value is $100 and people click into your funnel but only one person buys everything, your EPC could still be low if not enough people make it past your landing page.

This is a common scenario: a marketer might boast about a high ACV, but they struggle with profitability. The culprit often lies in a low EPC, indicating a bottleneck at the entry point of the funnel. Essentially, EPC is a diagnostic tool that reveals whether your funnel's gateway (the landing page) is functioning effectively. A low EPC suggests a brick wall - your landing page is not converting visitors into buyers. Conversely, a high EPC indicates a smooth entry into the funnel, akin to an open gate.

To optimize your EPC, you need to focus on the performance of your landing page. This is where the majority of your split testing and tweaks should occur. If your landing page acts as a brick wall, very few visitors will proceed through your funnel, no matter how enticing your subsequent offers are.

The first and most impactful test involves experimenting with radically different landing page designs. Before fine-tuning headlines or other elements, start



<!-- Page 29 -->



with starkly contrasting page layouts. For example, compare a minimalist design with a highly graphical one, or a single-step form with a multi-step process. These large-scale changes can lead to significant shifts in conversion rates.

We recently tested our current DotCom Secrets book funnel against a new design that mimicked an Amazon product page. The hypothesis was that familiarity with Amazon's layout might boost conversions. While we're still awaiting final results, such radical tests can provide insights that smaller tweaks might not reveal.

Once you establish a new control landing page, the next step is headline testing. The headline is often the first thing visitors see and can significantly impact their decision to stay or leave. Create several headline variations and test them against each other. The winning headline becomes your new control, setting the stage for further optimization.

If your EPC remains low despite an optimized landing page and compelling headlines, the next variable to test is your offer. This could mean bundling your main product with additional items or creating entirely new offers. For instance, testing an offer of a free eBook alone versus an eBook bundled with a T-shirt can yield different results. The goal is to find an offer combination that maximizes your EPC.

The last variable to test is your lead. The "lead" refers to the initial part of your sales message - the first 20 seconds of a video or the top section of your landing page. This segment is crucial because it sets the tone and hooks the visitor's interest. A strong lead can significantly boost your EPC by ensuring more visitors engage with your content. A highly recommended resource on crafting compelling leads is the book Great Leads, which provides in-depth strategies for refining the initial part of your sales message.

• Step 4 - With a solid headline in place, focus on your offers. Test different

combinations - perhaps a single product versus a product bundle, or a physical item alongside a digital download. Measure which offer configuration generates the highest EPC.

• Step 5 - Finally, tweak the lead section of your landing page. Experiment

with different opening statements, video intros, or top-page content. The aim is to hook visitors immediately and encourage them to proceed through the funnel.

## Secret #6

### Let The Market Vote With Their Credit Cards

O NCE YOU'VE SET UP YOUR funnel and optimized it using all the previous secrets, it's time to let the market vote with their credit cards. The only way to know if your funnel will work is to let the market test it and see if customers are willing to pull out their credit cards and make a purchase.

To put this into practice, take the best version you've created of your funnel, complete with optimized pages and offers. Then, determine an initial budget for testing. A good rule of thumb is to invest an amount equal to your potential average cart value. For example, if you have a webinar funnel selling a $1,000 product, start with $1,000 in ad spend to see if the funnel can generate a sale and break even. If your book funnel has a potential average cart value of $300, invest $300 in ads to test its effectiveness.

Once you've set your budget, let the ads run and then analyze the results. If you have a simple funnel, diagnosing the performance is straightforward. First, look at the ad performance. Did the ads generate clicks and traffic? If the cost per acquisition (CPA) is lower than your average cart value (ACV), then the ad is performing well. If not, you may need to create new ads or adjust your targeting.



<!-- Page 33 -->



Next, examine the landing page conversion rate. A high conversion rate indicates that the landing page is effectively turning visitors into leads. If the conversion rate is low, the landing page might need tweaking. The key metrics to track include the percentage of visitors who opt in, the average cart value, and the conversion rates for each step in the funnel.

For example, if the landing page has a 41% conversion rate but the order form conversion is only 1%, it's clear that the order form is the problem. Diagnosing which part of the funnel needs fixing involves looking at these numbers and identifying where the drop-off occurs.

To address issues in the funnel, revisit the fundamental elements: the hook, the story, and the offer. The hook is what grabs attention, the story is what engages and convinces, and the offer is what converts. If a page isn't converting well, one of these elements is likely the culprit. By tweaking them, you can improve the performance of your funnel.

This iterative process of testing, analyzing, and tweaking is the core of optimizing your funnel. By letting the market vote with their credit cards and then looking at the data, you get real feedback on what's working and what's not. It's a continuous cycle of improvement that leads to a highly effective and profitable funnel.

This secret is about relying on actual customer behavior to guide your funnel optimization. ## Secret #7

### Funnel Stacking

Most entrepreneurs focus on a single funnel, and while this can work initially, it becomes increasingly difficult to maintain profitability as ad costs rise. Many successful entrepreneurs find themselves struggling because their once-profitable funnels are now barely breaking even due to these increasing costs. This is where funnel stacking comes into play.

Funnel stacking involves creating multiple funnels that guide customers through a series of increasingly valuable offers. Instead of relying on a single funnel to generate all your revenue, you develop a sequence of funnels that collectively maximize customer value. By moving customers from one funnel to the next, you can afford to spend more on customer acquisition, outspending your competitors and dominating your market.
