# JOHN NICHOLS EMAIL VOICE STYLE GUIDE

**Operator-grounded, peer-like, action-biased, scannable. The coaching voice in writing.**

---

## Overview

This guide is the companion to the coaching voice guide. The voice does not change. The medium does.

In coaching, John can ask diagnostic questions in real time, read body language, and adjust on the fly. In email, he can't. The reader is on a phone, between customers, with eight other things demanding attention. So the voice stays the same (operator, peer, warm, action-biased) and the playbook adjusts: shorter paragraphs, one idea per block, subject lines that earn the open, P.S. that pulls real weight, sign-offs that sound like a person.

Two anchors carry through from coaching:

- **"It is always the right time to do the right thing."** The ethical anchor.
- **"Focus on the doing."** The action anchor. This one works overtime in email because email readers default to inaction.

The Four Basics also carry through, applied to the email itself: **Greet, Educate, Process, Close.** *Every email. Every time. No exceptions.*

John's biggest social proof asset is 23 years of UPS Store operating, scaling from one store in Lenoir City in March 2003 to eleven locations at peak. Every email sequence should be able to pull from that well. Stories, scenes, names, years. The franchise material is the engine.

## Voice & Tone (Email-Specific)

### Core Characteristics (carried from coaching)

**Operator-grounded.** Stories from the counter, the schedule, the manager who underperformed. Real franchise material.

**Conversational and peer-like.** Reads like you're sitting across a table, not standing at a podium.

**Action-biased.** Quick Start. Wants the reader doing something this week.

**Empathetic and self-aware.** Shares the bad hire, the snap decision that flopped, the store that drained him. Vulnerability is the rapport engine.

**Principle-driven directness.** When something needs saying, it gets said.

**Warm and playful.** Yellow on the Color Code. The inbox shouldn't feel heavy.

### Email-Specific Shifts

**Scannable above all else.** John has dyslexia, his readers are usually multi-tasking, and most opens happen on mobile. Long paragraphs lose. Short ones win. Bold the one or two sentences that have to land if the reader skims.

**Anticipate the reader's question.** No real-time diagnostic possible. So write to the question the reader is silently asking: *"Why should I care about this email today?"*

**Earn every section.** Subject line earns the open. Opener earns the next sentence. Story earns the CTA. CTA earns the click. The reader can leave at any point. Don't give them a reason.

**Shorter wherever possible.** Coaching can be long and storied. Email defaults to medium. Long emails are reserved for big moments (program launches, deep stories, year-end reflections).

## The Email Anatomy: The Four Basics, Applied

Every email has four jobs. They mirror the customer-facing framework John teaches.

### Greet. Subject Line, Pre-Header, Opener

The first 5 to 10 seconds. The reader is in the inbox, scrolling. The Greet has to make them stop, open, and read past the first sentence.

- **Subject line.** Short, curiosity-driven, often operator-coded.
- **Pre-header.** Functional inbox real estate (80 to 100 characters). Extends the subject. Adds a hook.
- **Opener.** First sentence has to validate the open. Drop them into a scene, ask a real question, or name the thing they're already feeling.

### Educate. The Story or Insight

The middle of the email. This is where trust gets built. Not by lecturing, by showing.

- A real franchise story (named place, year, what happened).
- A reframe of something the reader is probably wrestling with.
- A teaching moment from the Four Basics, the Time Freedom Scorecard, or another framework.

If there's no story, there better be a sharp insight. If there's no insight, there better be a question that makes them pause. Without one of the three, delete the email.

### Process. The Framework or Takeaway

The bridge from "interesting story" to "here's why it matters to you." One paragraph. Names the framework, the principle, or the lesson. Connects the story to the reader's situation.

### Close. The CTA, P.S., and Sign-Off

The reader is now ready to act, OR they're 90% gone. Either way, the Close has to be specific and easy.

- **One primary CTA.** Specific link or instruction. Real product, real action.
- **P.S.** Doing real work, not just repeating a mantra (see P.S. Patterns).
- **Sign-off.** Warm, human, not Victorian.

**Every email. Every time. No exceptions.**

## Sequence-Specific Guidance

The voice carries. The pressure, length, and CTAs shift by sequence.

### Time Freedom Scorecard Nurture (Top of Funnel)

**Audience.** Mixed. Single-unit and multi-unit. Just took the scorecard.

**Goal.** Build trust. Sort them toward the 5-Day Challenge (single-unit) or the 120-Day Program (multi-unit) based on engagement signals.

**Tone.** Welcoming, story-rich, no pressure. The get-to-know-you phase.

**Length.** Medium. 200 to 400 words.

**Pattern.** Open with a real franchise story → reframe their scorecard result → soft CTA toward the next product OR a reply prompt that helps you segment.

### 5-Day Challenge Enrollment

**Audience.** Single-unit owners primarily. Aspirational multi-unit.

**Goal.** Register for the challenge.

**Tone.** Inviting, low-stakes, action-biased. *"It's five days. You can do five days."*

**Length.** Shorter. 150 to 300 words.

**Pattern.** Hook with a result (what someone gets from the challenge) → one franchise story or reader scenario → clear CTA → P.S. with a softener or social proof.

### 5-Day Challenge Daily Emails

**Audience.** Active participants.

**Goal.** Show up. Complete the day. Move toward the upsell at the end.

**Tone.** Energetic, accountability-flavored, daily check-in.

**Length.** Tight. 100 to 200 words.

**Pattern.** Day theme → one specific instruction → CTA to take today's action → P.S. with the next-day teaser.

### 120-Day Program Enrollment

**Audience.** Multi-unit owners primarily.

**Goal.** Book a discovery call. Enroll.

**Tone.** Higher stakes, more substantive, credibility-forward.

**Length.** Longer. 400 to 700 words. Operator stories with specifics.

**Pattern.** Big-picture hook (the multi-unit problem they're hitting) → real franchise story with stakes → the framework or transformation → CTA to book a call → P.S. with a callback or a challenge.

### Year-Long (Franchise Freedom Year) Enrollment

**Audience.** 120-Day grads, qualified leads.

**Goal.** Renew or upgrade.

**Tone.** Relational, results-driven, partner-feeling. They already know him.

**Length.** Variable. Often shorter (trust is built).

**Pattern.** Open with a callback to their progress or a shared memory → describe what the next year unlocks → simple CTA → warm sign-off. Less selling, more inviting.

### Weekly Newsletter / Broadcast

**Audience.** The whole list.

**Goal.** Stay top of mind, deliver value, soft conversion.

**Tone.** Conversational, story-of-the-week, casual.

**Length.** Medium. 250 to 500 words.

**Pattern.** One real story or one strong reframe → tie to a Four Basics or coaching principle → soft CTA (scorecard, latest podcast, a reply prompt).

## Subject Line Patterns

Short, curiosity-driven, operator-coded. Calibrated to feel like a peer texted you, not like a brand emailed you.

### Categories and Examples

**A. Operator scenes.**
- "The hire I should've fired six months earlier"
- "What my Saturday counter taught me"
- "The day I almost lost my best manager"

**B. Direct questions (curiosity, not interrogation).**
- "Are you running the store or is it running you?"
- "Quick question about your Greet"
- "Is this what's keeping you stuck?"

**C. Curiosity gaps.**
- "I made this mistake at 3 stores"
- "The number that changed how I hired"
- "Wish I knew this in 2009"

**D. Direct utility.**
- "Free 5-minute scorecard inside"
- "5 days. One framework. Real results."
- "Your next step (no fluff)"

**E. Personal/intimate.**
- "Mary Ellen said something last night"
- "Confession from year 2"
- "Reading my journal from 2007"

### Formulas

- **Operator scene + lesson tease.** *"What [scene] taught me about [thing]"*
- **Direct question to the reader's reality.** *"Are you [doing X] or [doing Y]?"*
- **Confession opener.** *"The mistake I made when..."*
- **Year + lesson.** *"What I knew at year 5 that I wish I'd known at year 1"*

### Rules

- Keep it short (under 50 characters when possible, especially for mobile previews).
- Lowercase is acceptable and often warmer.
- Avoid moral lecturing. *"Will you stand up for what's right?"* reads as activism, not coaching.
- One idea per subject line.
- Curiosity over completeness. Don't give the punchline in the subject.

---

## Pre-Header Patterns

The pre-header is the second half of the inbox preview. It does real work.

### Categories and Examples

**A. Extension of the subject.**
- Subject: *"The hire I should've fired six months earlier"*
- Pre-header: *"And the conversation that finally made me act"*

**B. Tease the payoff.**
- "Plus the one move that recovered a $400 sale"
- "Inside: the framework I use weekly"

**C. Personal note.**
- "I'm still kicking myself about this one"
- "This took me 11 years to figure out"

**D. Functional utility.**
- "5-minute scorecard. Free. No spam."
- "Reply with one word and I'll send it."

### Rules

- 80 to 100 characters maximum.
- Don't repeat the subject. Extend it.
- Skip the moralizing.
- Lowercase is fine.

## Body Style: Formatting & Scannability

Because of John's dyslexia and his readers' mobile habits, scannability is non-negotiable.

### Paragraph Rules

- 1 to 3 sentences per paragraph. Never more.
- Single-sentence paragraphs are welcome for emphasis.
- Lots of white space between paragraphs.

### Formatting

- **Bold** the 1 to 2 sentences that must land if the reader skims.
- *Italics* for a quoted phrase, internal thought, or signature term.
- No dashes anywhere (see Writing Notes).
- Bullet lists for steps, frameworks, or "here's what changed" moments.

### Mobile-First

- Assume the reader is on a phone.
- The first 2 sentences need to land before the reader has to scroll.
- Avoid wide image headers that push real content below the fold.

### One Idea Per Block

If a paragraph has two thoughts, split it. The reader's brain handles one thought at a time.

## Story Patterns: The Franchise Engine

Stories from John's 23 years are the credibility and rapport engine. Every story should hit at least three of the following:

1. **Specificity.** Named place, year, person (or composite person).
2. **Stakes.** What was on the line.
3. **Mistake or tension.** Something went wrong or could have.
4. **Lesson.** What it taught him.
5. **Connection.** How it applies to the reader.

### Story Categories

- **The bad hire.** Hires that should have been fired earlier. Models accountability.
- **The good hire that surprised.** Models trusting your gut.
- **The angry customer.** Models the Four Basics in action.
- **The store that drained you.** Models knowing when to cut losses.
- **The decision you made fast.** Models the 50/50 snap-decision rate (transparency).
- **Mary Ellen moments.** Models marriage and partnership.
- **The selling-the-stores arc.** Models seasons ending and new ones starting.
- **The faith moment.** Models prayer as a real decision tool (use selectively).

### Story Rules

- Open with a scene, not a setup. *("It was 2009. Saturday morning rush. Lenoir City store.")*
- Get to the tension fast.
- The lesson lands at the end, in one or two sentences. No padding.

## CTA Patterns

Real product CTAs. No activism phrasing.

### CTA Formulas

- **Take the scorecard.** *"Take the 5-minute scorecard at timefreedomscore.com."*
- **Join the challenge.** *"Doors open Monday. Grab your spot here."*
- **Book the call.** *"Book a 20-minute call with me. Link below."*
- **Reply prompt.** *"Reply with the word 'ready' and I'll send the next step."*
- **Read the next thing.** *"Here's the full breakdown."*

### CTA Rules

- One primary CTA per email. Two only if they're the same action with different paths (a button + a text link).
- Action verb first.
- Specific. *"Click here"* is dead. *"Take the 5-minute scorecard"* is alive.
- Tell them what happens after they click.

### What to Avoid

- *"Take action for justice."* (Activist coding.)
- *"Stand up for your values."* (Activist coding.)
- *"Click here."* (Dead.)
- *"Learn more."* (Vague.)

---

## Link Placement

- **Once early** if the email has a strong hook and the link delivers on it.
- **Primary CTA** after the story and the framework, near the end.
- **In the P.S.** as a soft second touch.

For shorter emails (challenge daily, urgency reminders), the primary CTA can come earlier.

---

## P.S. Patterns

The P.S. is the most-read line in any email. It deserves real work.

### Pattern Library

**A. Secondary CTA.** Different action than the main CTA.
> *"P.S. If a call feels like too much, just reply with where you're stuck. I read every one."*

**B. Story callback.** Brings the opening story back home.
> *"P.S. That manager I almost fired? She ran my best-performing store for the next 6 years."*

**C. Funny or warm aside.** Lets the reader feel the human.
> *"P.S. Mary Ellen says I tell this story too much. She's not wrong."*

**D. The intimate beat.** Something that lands because it's said quietly at the end.
> *"P.S. Year 23 looks different than year 1. The work doesn't stop, but the reasons get clearer."*

**E. Anchor reminder.** Used sparingly.
> *"P.S. It is always the right time to do the right thing."*

**F. Curiosity tease for the next email.**
> *"P.S. Tomorrow I'll show you the exact play I used to recover a $1,200 customer."*

### Rules

- Don't always do the same one. Variety builds anticipation.
- Don't always end with the anchor. It loses force when it's automatic.
- Make the P.S. earn its line.

---

## Sign-Off Patterns

Warm, human, peer-like. Not Victorian.

### Approved Sign-Offs

- **John**
- **Talk soon, John**
- **More to come, John**
- **In your corner, John**
- **Doing the work alongside you, John**

### Avoid

- *"Sincerely, John Nichols"* (too formal, reads as funeral notice).
- *"Best regards."* (corporate).
- *"Yours in service."* (clergy).
- *"Onward!"* (LinkedIn coach).

### Rule

The sign-off should feel like the way you'd end a text to a friend who's also a client. Warm, brief, and like there's another conversation coming.

## Signature & Footer

A coach building a brand needs a working signature, not just a name.

### Standard Block

```
John Nichols
Founder, XpansionCode
Take the Time Freedom Scorecard → timefreedomscore.com
```

### Optional Additions

- Headshot (small, clean, recent).
- Logo (subtle, not dominant).
- Calendar link if the email warrants it.
- One social handle if it's actually maintained.

### Rules

- Keep it tight. The signature is real estate, not a billboard.
- One link beyond the email's primary CTA, max.
- No motivational quotes in the signature.
- No tracking pixel disclosures or footer copy that screams "marketing automation." It breaks the peer-to-peer feel.

## Faith in Email

Same approach as coaching. Faith is foundational, not background.

**Explicit.** Reserved for segments who've signaled openness. Newsletters with a faith-forward subject line. Sequences for clients who came in through a faith-based connection point. Direct references to prayer, scripture, or devotional practice.

**Implicit.** The default for the broader list. The values (integrity, service, stewardship, courage under pressure) carry without the language.

**Rule of thumb.** If you're not sure whether to bring faith forward, don't. The implicit version doesn't lose the principle, and the explicit version risks alienating readers who haven't signaled. Newsletter and sequence segmentation should do the sorting over time.

## Always Do

* Anchor every email in a real franchise story or a sharp insight.
* Write for the phone, not the desktop.
* Bold the 1 to 2 sentences that have to land if skimmed.
* One primary CTA. Specific. Real product.
* Use the P.S. to do real work.
* Sign off warm. *"John"* works. *"Sincerely, John Nichols"* doesn't.
* Pull from the 23 years of operating. That's the engine.
* Share misses, not just wins.
* Keep paragraphs to 1 to 3 sentences.
* Use both anchors when they fit ("right thing" and "focus on the doing").
* Run the Greet, Educate, Process, Close lens on every email before sending.

## Never Do

* Use dashes (em or en). Hard rule.
* Lecture or moralize. You're a coach, not a campaigner.
* Send an email without a story, an insight, or a sharp question.
* Use activist-coded CTAs (*"Stand up," "Take a stand," "Join the cause"*).
* Default to *"Sincerely"* or any corporate sign-off.
* Bury the CTA so far down nobody reaches it.
* Repeat the same P.S. mantra every send.
* Use jargon, leadership-book language, or generic motivational phrases.
* Front-load the email with throat-clearing (*"I hope this finds you well..."*).
* Bring faith forward when the segment hasn't signaled openness.
* Write paragraphs longer than 3 sentences.
* Use *"Click here"* or *"Learn more"* as link text.

## Coaching Patterns Carried Into Email

Several patterns from the coaching guide translate directly. Others adjust.

**Greet, Educate, Process, Close.** Carries directly. Now applied to email structure itself.

**Reframe Pattern.** Carries. *"It's not just a [symptom]. It's a [principle]. And here's why that matters."*

**Story-to-Lesson Pattern.** Carries. Email is actually the better medium for this because the reader can't interrupt.

**Self-Awareness-First Pattern.** Carries. Sharing your own miss in an email is more vulnerable than in coaching. Use it deliberately.

**Diagnostic-Before-Advice Pattern.** Adjusts. Can't ask in real time. Replace with rhetorical question or a *"you might be wondering..."* opener.

**Action-Closer Pattern.** Carries. Every email closes with one specific next action.

**Uncertainty-as-Exploration Pattern.** Carries selectively. Works well in newsletter and intimate email. Less appropriate in enrollment sequences where the reader needs confidence.

---

## Writing Notes

* **No dashes.** Hard rule. Em dashes and en dashes get replaced with periods, commas, semicolons, colons, or parentheses.
* **Real names, places, years.** *"My Lenoir City store in 2009"* beats *"one of my locations."*
* **No leadership-book language.** Cut *"synergy," "alignment," "buy-in," "leverage"* as a verb, *"circle back," "double down."*
* **Short sentences are welcome.** Fragments work in email. They land.
* **Lowercase is okay.** In subject lines and pre-headers especially. Warmer.
* **Read it out loud before sending.** If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it.

## Final Test

Would John send this email to a franchise owner he respects? Would the reader, sitting in their store between customers, get to the end and either click the CTA, reply, or quietly think *"yeah, he gets it"*? If the email feels like a campaign, a sermon, or a corporate broadcast, rewrite it. If it feels like a peer who's been there, knows what's hard, and is pointing toward the next right move, it's right.
