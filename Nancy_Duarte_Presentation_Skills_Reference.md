# Presentation Skills Reference — The Duarte Method™ Framework

> **Source attribution:** This reference is adapted from the *Pocket Guide to Presentation Skills* by Duarte, Inc. The four-pillar framework (Strategy, Story, Visuals, Delivery), along with named tools such as the Big Idea™, the Audience Needs Map, and The Glance Test™, are concepts developed by Duarte. This document paraphrases their guidance for use as an internal reference. For original training and the full method, see Duarte's published materials.

## Overview: The Four Pillars

The Duarte Method organizes presentation craft into four interdependent pillars. A strong presentation needs all four — weakness in any one undermines the others.

| Pillar | Focus | Core Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategy** | Understanding the audience | *Who am I talking to and what do they need?* |
| **Story** | Crafting interesting content | *What narrative will move them?* |
| **Visuals** | Amplifying key messages | *How do I make them see what I'm saying?* |
| **Delivery** | Honing presence | *How do I show up to land this?* |

---

## Pillar 1: Strategy — Understand Your Audience

Strategy is the foundation. Every other decision (story arc, slide design, vocal pacing) depends on getting this right.

### Know Your Audience
Before writing a single slide, build a picture of who's in the room: their background, what they care about, what they're hoping to get from your time, and — critically — what might prevent them from accepting your ideas. Duarte's **Audience Needs Map** is the named tool for surfacing those obstacles systematically.

### Define Your Big Idea™
Articulate a single, sharp central message. The Big Idea™ is Duarte's name for the one-sentence point of view that anchors everything else. A well-formed Big Idea™ states:
- Your unique perspective on the topic
- What's at stake — the consequences of accepting or rejecting your idea

If you can't say it in one sentence, you don't have a Big Idea™ yet.

### Plan the Audience Journey
A presentation is a transformation, not a transmission. Map two states:
- **Before:** what the audience currently thinks, feels, and believes
- **After:** what you need them to think, feel, and believe by the end

Then work backward from the gap: what must you *say*, *show*, and *do* to move them across it?

### Structure Your Content
Sequence ideas in a logical order so the audience can follow without effort. Comprehension precedes persuasion — if they're working to understand you, they aren't being moved by you.

### Add Supporting Points
Back the central argument with relevant evidence: data, examples, case histories, expert references. Supporting points exist to serve the Big Idea™, not to display research.

## Pillar 2: Story — Craft Interesting Content

Strategy decides *what* to say. Story decides *how* it lands emotionally.

### Start With a Hook
Open with something that makes the audience want the next sentence. Almost any opening beats variations of "I'm excited to talk about..." — that phrase signals a generic presentation and gives the audience permission to disengage.

Strong hooks include: a provocative question, a surprising statistic, a brief story, a vivid scene, a contrarian claim.

### Tell a Story
Make the abstract concrete through anecdote, analogy, or personal narrative. Stories work because they:
- Attach emotion to information (which makes it memorable)
- Give the audience a stand-in to identify with
- Compress complex ideas into transportable form

### Contrast Opposing Ideas
Tension drives attention. Build it by setting two states against each other:
- Problem vs. solution
- Present vs. future
- What is vs. what could be
- Old way vs. new way

This is the engine of most persuasive speeches — the speaker pendulums between current reality and a possible better one.

### Provide a Call to Action
Tell the audience exactly what to do next. Vague closings ("hopefully this gives you something to think about") waste the persuasive momentum you've built. Specific closings ("by Friday, please send me your top three priorities") convert it.

### End With the New Bliss
Close by painting the picture of the better world that adopting your idea creates. People remember endings disproportionately — more than openings, far more than middles. The last image you leave is the one that lingers.

## Pillar 3: Visuals — Amplify Key Messages

Slides exist to help the audience *see* what you're saying — not to serve as your speaker notes.

### Use Images
Reinforce verbal points with photos, diagrams, charts, or illustrations. A relevant image carries information faster than a bulleted list.

### Keep It Simple
One idea per slide. If a slide contains two ideas, it should be two slides. Information overload at the visual level forces the audience to choose between reading and listening — and they'll do neither well.

### Space It Out
Negative space (the empty area around your content) is a design element, not waste. Cluttered slides feel anxious and reduce comprehension. Generous margins and breathing room raise perceived quality.

### Be Consistent
Use a coherent visual system across the deck: consistent colors, typefaces, grid alignment, and layout patterns. Consistency reads as professionalism and reinforces brand identity. Inconsistency reads as carelessness, regardless of how good the content is.

### Test Your Visuals
Before presenting, verify:
- Slides are legible from the back of the room (or at the smallest expected screen size for remote audiences)
- Color contrast meets accessibility standards
- Charts and diagrams remain readable when scaled

### The Glance Test™
Duarte's named heuristic: every slide should communicate its core meaning in **three seconds or less**. If a viewer needs longer than that to figure out what they're looking at, the slide is doing too much. Cut, simplify, or split.

## Pillar 4: Delivery — Hone Your Presence

Even excellent content fails when delivery undermines it. Delivery is craft, not personality — it's trainable.

### Practice Your Delivery
Rehearse multiple times, out loud, ideally standing up. Practice produces two outcomes that nothing else can substitute for: command of the material, and the relaxed confidence that comes from knowing you have it. Reading the deck silently is not practice.

### Manage Pre-Presentation Nerves
Most speakers benefit from a brief calming routine before going on — controlled breathing, a short meditation, or a physical reset. Duarte publishes a pre-presentation meditation specifically for this purpose.

### Mind Your Body Language
Choose gestures and movement intentionally. Random pacing distracts; purposeful movement reinforces. Maintain eye contact with the audience — find individual faces and hold them briefly rather than scanning the room.

### Speak Clearly
Enunciate. Project to the back row (or the equivalent for virtual presentations). Mumbled delivery signals low conviction even when the words themselves are strong.

### Pause for Impact
Strategic silence does work that words can't:
- It marks important points (the audience leans in during a pause)
- It gives listeners time to process what you just said
- It demonstrates control (rushed delivery reads as nervous)

Most speakers under-pause. The pause that feels too long to you usually feels right to the audience.

### Be Passionate
Visible enthusiasm is contagious. If you don't seem to care about the topic, the audience won't either. Conviction can't be faked, but it can be cultivated by reconnecting with *why* the topic matters to you before you walk on.

## Synthesis: The Three P's

Duarte summarizes successful presentations as the product of three things:

1. **Preparation** — the strategy and content work done before anyone sees you
2. **Practice** — the rehearsal that turns content into performance
3. **Passion** — the conviction that makes the audience care

A presentation that engages, informs, and inspires requires all three.

## Quick-Reference Checklist

**Before writing slides:**
- [ ] Do I know who's in the audience and what obstacles they bring?
- [ ] Can I state my Big Idea™ in one sentence with stakes?
- [ ] Have I mapped the journey from current state to desired state?

**While building content:**
- [ ] Does my opening hook earn the next sentence?
- [ ] Is there a story or analogy that makes the abstract concrete?
- [ ] Have I built contrast between the current state and the better future?
- [ ] Is there a specific call to action?
- [ ] Does the close paint the new bliss vividly?

**While designing slides:**
- [ ] One idea per slide?
- [ ] Does each slide pass The Glance Test™ (three seconds)?
- [ ] Generous negative space?
- [ ] Consistent visual system across the deck?
- [ ] Accessibility-checked?

**Before delivering:**
- [ ] Have I rehearsed out loud, multiple times?
- [ ] Do I have a pre-presentation calming routine?
- [ ] Am I planning intentional pauses at key moments?
- [ ] Have I reconnected with why this topic matters to me?

## Application Notes for AI Agent Use

When helping users build presentations, this framework is most useful when applied **in order**: Strategy → Story → Visuals → Delivery. Common failure mode: users want to start with slide design (Visuals) before they have a Big Idea™ (Strategy), which produces beautiful decks that don't move audiences.

The four pillars also map onto common presentation problems:
- "My audience seems disengaged" → usually a **Strategy** problem (wrong content for this audience) or **Story** problem (no tension/hook)
- "My slides look bad" → **Visuals** problem
- "I get nervous and forget my points" → **Delivery** problem (insufficient practice)
- "People liked it but didn't act" → **Story** problem (weak call to action or new bliss)

Diagnose which pillar is breaking before prescribing fixes.
