# 1. Newsletter Content System

## 1.1 Purpose
The newsletter is not a promotional email blast. It is the long-form authority and trust-building channel for Xpansion Code. It exists to:

- Build trust with the audience
- Teach practical leadership and business lessons
- Strengthen the Xpansion Code brand
- Deepen connection through email
- Create long-form authority content
- Turn one real story into multiple downstream assets

It should feel like John speaking directly to a single overworked business owner who is ready for something that actually works.

## 1.2 Audience
Same audience as all Xpansion Code content: the overworked owner-operator, represented internally by the persona **Mike Reynolds**. Mike is typically:

- Working too many hours
- Carrying too many problems himself
- Frustrated by team inconsistency
- Missing family time
- Hungry for better systems and better leadership
- Tired of empty motivation and generic business advice

Every newsletter should answer: *Why should Mike stop scrolling and read this?*

## 1.3 Role in the business
The newsletter helps John:

- Teach from the trenches
- Share real stories with real lessons
- Stay in front of the audience consistently
- Reinforce practical authority
- Build email-based relationship depth
- Support the podcast and social media systems
- Convert one story into a larger content ecosystem

## 1.4 Identity and voice
The newsletter must sound like John. That means:

- Direct
- Practical
- Warm
- Honest
- Experienced
- Faith-anchored without being preachy
- Grounded in real life, not theory

The reader should feel like John is writing to one real person, not broadcasting copy to a list.

## 1.5 Core promise
Anchor message that most newsletters ladder back to:

> **"You did not buy a business to buy yourself a job."**

Most newsletters should move the reader toward:

- Owner independence
- Time freedom
- Stronger standards
- Healthier culture
- Better systems
- Better leadership
- More truthful decision-making

## 1.6 Three themes
Most newsletter content fits one of these:

**1. The Trapped Franchise Owner** — pain-focused content that helps the reader feel seen. Examples: carrying the whole business, missing family moments, feeling trapped by the team, spending all day solving problems.

**2. The Systems That Set You Free** — solution-focused content showing the path out. Examples: the 4 Basics, standards, systems, delegation, processes, manager expectations.

**3. The Leader You Are Becoming** — transformation-focused content centered on identity and growth. Examples: becoming more decisive, protecting the standard, telling the truth faster, stepping out of the weeds, building leaders instead of dependency.

## 1.7 Primary framework: WYLA
The newsletter is primarily built on **WYLA**:

- **W** — What happened (the real event, real moment, real tension)
- **Y** — Why it matters (why the reader should care, what bigger truth this reveals)
- **L** — Lesson learned (the principle, the takeaway, the truth)
- **A** — Apply it now (one practical action, one useful challenge)

WYLA works well in newsletter format because it lets John teach through story rather than dumping advice.

## 1.8 Standard newsletter structure
Twelve parts in order:

1. **Franchise Facts** — opening trivia question (recurring front door). System picks one unused question and reveals the answer later in the newsletter.
2. **Clickbait email title** — attention-grabbing headline. Strong when it includes a promise, tension point, number, curiosity gap, or fitting emoji.
3. **SEO meta description** — short clean description for search and preview value.
4. **Introduction** — opening hook. Pull the reader into the story fast, make them care, set up the tension.
5. **Main Section 1: WHAT** — tell the real story with specific details.
6. **Main Section 2: WHY** — explain the bigger meaning to an owner-operator.
7. **Main Section 3: BRIDGE + LESSON** — use a bridge line or metaphor, then teach the principle clearly.
8. **Main Section 4: STRATEGY** — give the practical framework, method, or approach.
9. **Main Section 5: APPLY** — one clear challenge for the week.
10. **FAQ section** — five questions and answers that clarify the lesson and answer likely objections.
11. **Conclusion** — restate the lesson and land the emotional close.
12. **Resource links** — helpful links, related articles, podcast plug.
13. **Signature** — `Thanks,` newline `John R. Nichols`
14. **PS section** — short clever wrap-up. Lighter, sometimes funny, always human.
15. **Franchise Facts answer** — closes the trivia loop.
16. **About John footer** — full authority and bio section.

(Numbering above lists 16 segments because the original outline groups some of them; the agent should treat them as required components.)

## 1.9 Idea sources
Strongest newsletters come from:

1. **Real stories from John** — best source. Examples: firing a good person who refuses the standard, a team leadership issue, a family moment with a business lesson, a hard truth from inside the UPS Stores.
2. **Weekly podcast themes** — extend the existing podcast theme.
3. **Weekly strategy priorities** — if the brief says standards / leadership / owner independence, reinforce that.
4. **Recurring audience pain** — repeated problems are usually newsletter-worthy.

## 1.10 Quality criteria
**Strong newsletter has:** real story, clear point, emotional tension, practical teaching, natural voice, one strong main takeaway, useful application, clear connection to the owner's real life.

**Weak newsletter:** sounds too polished or too generic, gets too long without direction, teaches too many things at once, loses the story, becomes preachy, offers no practical takeaway.

## 1.11 Voice rules (explicit)
When writing as John:

- First person when John is speaking
- Short paragraphs
- Natural flow and spoken rhythm
- Simple words
- Real examples
- Warmth without fluff
- Truth without harshness for harshness's sake

**Hard rules:**
- Never use dashes or hyphens in John's writing voice
- Never use tables
- Avoid corporate language
- Avoid textbook tone
- Keep it accessible

## 1.12 Title and subject line guidance
Strong newsletter titles do one of:

- Create curiosity
- Create tension
- Promise insight
- Name a painful truth
- Offer a surprising lesson

Examples of title style:
- *the hard leadership conversation most owners avoid*
- *a good person can still be the wrong fit*
- *the standard you refuse to enforce is costing you more than you think*

## 1.13 Bridge section
One of the most powerful parts of the newsletter. The bridge sits between story and teaching. Usually a short reframe, metaphor, quotable line, or truth that shifts the story into a bigger lesson. Makes the newsletter memorable instead of merely informational.

## 1.14 Strategy and application requirements
The newsletter must help the reader do something with the lesson, so it should normally include:

- One clear principle
- 2 to 3 teaching points
- One practical framework or approach when needed
- One challenge for the week

This prevents the newsletter from becoming entertainment without transformation.

## 1.15 FAQ section purpose
FAQ is not filler. It exists to:

- Answer likely objections
- Clarify misunderstood points
- Deepen the value of the lesson
- Make the topic more useful and complete

## 1.16 Support assets the system also produces
Beyond the main email, the newsletter skill can also generate:

- Broadcast name
- Subject line
- Preview text
- 7 social media posts for X / Twitter
- 7 quotes
- 10 alternative titles
- 10 alternative descriptions
- Short summary blog post
- Blog SEO page title
- Blog SEO page description
- Short blog summary

One newsletter becomes many assets.

## 1.17 Connection to the rest of the content system
Newsletter is not isolated. One strong newsletter topic can also feed: podcast episodes, Facebook posts, LinkedIn posts, Instagram posts, reels, quote graphics, blog summaries, email teasers.

## 1.18 Repurposing tree
From one good newsletter, the agent can typically pull: multiple hooks, quote lines, social media posts, blog content, a podcast tie-in, short clips or teaser concepts.

## 1.19 Weekly strategy alignment
The weekly strategy brief decides what matters now. If the weekly theme is owner independence through standards and leadership, the newsletter should reflect that — focusing on systems, standards, delegation, leadership truth-telling, reducing owner dependence, buying time back.

## 1.20 Recurring business concepts that fit newsletters
- **The 4 Basics** — core Xpansion Code teaching system
- **The 3-solution manager standard** — managers bring 3 possible solutions, not just dump problems upward
- **Systems create freedom** — without systems, owner stays trapped
- **Standards protect profit and culture** — weak standards create drag everywhere
- **Owner independence** — the owner should not be the answer machine

## 1.21 Delivery and file format
- Markdown source file for editable content
- PDF export for final delivery
- Word document when useful
- PDFs go in the export folder
- Optional: send file back through chat as attachment

## 1.22 Typical newsletter deliverables list
From one story or topic the agent can produce:

- Clickbait-style title options
- Subject line
- Preview text
- Full newsletter draft
- FAQ section
- Conclusion
- PS section
- Blog versions
- Quote lines
- Social pull-through content
- Podcast tie-in content

## 1.23 Bottom line for the agent
Take real-life business tension and turn it into practical long-form content that teaches, connects, and moves the audience toward freedom. Not slick — true. Story first. Lesson second. Application always.

# 2. Podcast Content System

## 2.1 Purpose
The podcast helps overworked owners think differently, lead better, build stronger systems, and buy their time back. It should sound like John teaching from real life — not a polished radio host reading generic business advice.

## 2.2 Audience
Same as the rest of Xpansion Code: the overworked owner-operator, represented by **Mike Reynolds**. Mike is typically:

- Buried in daily problems
- Solving too many things himself
- Frustrated by weak team performance
- Missing family time
- Wanting time freedom and financial freedom
- Tired of theory without proof

Every episode answers: *Why should a tired business owner care enough to listen all the way through?*

## 2.3 Role in the business
The podcast helps John:

- Teach practical leadership and systems
- Build trust through real stories
- Prove he has lived what he teaches
- Create long-form authority content
- Generate ideas that can be repurposed into social and email
- Reinforce the Xpansion Code message of owner freedom and standards

## 2.4 Identity and voice
The podcast should reflect John's actual identity:

- Battle-tested, not theoretical
- Direct, not fluffy
- Warm, not robotic
- Strong but human
- Faith-anchored but not preachy
- Practical, not abstract

The listener should feel like John is sitting across from them telling the truth plainly.

## 2.5 Core promise
Same anchor as everything else:

> **"You did not buy a business to buy yourself a job."**

Episodes should move owners toward owner independence, time freedom, better leadership, stronger standards, healthier culture, more practical systems, clearer thinking.

## 2.6 Three themes
Same three buckets:

**1. The Trapped Franchise Owner** — episodes that name the pain (carrying the whole business, missing family time, weak standards, team frustration, drowning in decisions).

**2. The Systems That Set You Free** — episodes that teach the practical path (the 4 Basics, systems and procedures, delegation, standards, manager expectations, customer experience systems).

**3. The Leader You Are Becoming** — episodes about the personal shift (becoming more decisive, telling the truth faster, protecting the standard, moving from operator to leader, learning to stop rescuing everybody).

## 2.7 Primary frameworks
Two main frameworks for podcast content.

### 2.7.1 WYLA (story-driven episodes)
- **W** — What happened
- **Y** — Why it matters
- **L** — Lesson learned
- **A** — Apply it now

WYLA is the dominant framework because John's strongest content usually starts with a real story.

### 2.7.2 TUNE (concept-driven episodes)
- **T** — Tension (name the pain fast)
- **U** — Understanding (explain why the pain exists)
- **N** — New Possibility (show the better path)
- **E** — Empowerment (give a next step)

Use TUNE when an episode is more concept-driven than story-driven.

## 2.8 Standard episode structure
Core structure for an episode:

1. **Intro** — *"What is up everyone. My name is John Nichols and this is Xpansion Code Radio."* Then set context for why this episode matters now.
2. **Hook** — first real punch. Creates curiosity, tension, or recognition fast. Examples: *"A good person can still be the wrong fit."* / *"Your best people are paying for your hesitation."* / *"You did not buy a business to become the answer machine."*
3. **What** — tell the story or explain the real-life situation. Specific and real.
4. **Why** — expand the meaning. Why is this bigger than one moment? Why should the owner-operator listening care?
5. **Problem** — name the trap. What lie are they believing? What pattern is keeping them stuck? What cost are they paying?
6. **Lesson** — teach the principle clearly. John shifts from storyteller to coach.
7. **Strategy** — give the framework, sequence, or practical method.
8. **Apply** — one clear challenge for the week. Not a giant list. One action.
9. **Close** — restate the lesson, point them toward xpansioncode.com when it fits, and close with the signature line: *"It is always the right time to do the right thing."*

## 2.9 Typical episode deliverable bundle
When the agent builds an episode outline, it usually produces:

- Episode title
- Hook headline
- 2 to 3 hook options
- Intro notes
- What section
- Why section
- Problem section
- Lesson section
- Strategy section
- Apply section
- Close section
- Show notes draft
- Social clips plan
- Email snippet

When needed, expand into a full long-form script.

## 2.10 Idea sources
Strongest podcast episodes come from:

1. **Real stories from John** — best source. Examples: firing a good person who refuses the standard, a hard moment inside the UPS Stores, a failure / tension / breakthrough, a family or life moment that reveals a bigger truth.
2. **Live business problems** — issues John is wrestling with right now.
3. **Weekly strategy priorities** — owner independence, standards, leadership, time freedom.
4. **Repeating audience pain** — pain that keeps showing up.

## 2.11 Quality criteria
**Strong episode has:** one strong central idea, real tension, honest storytelling, practical teaching, clear structure, a takeaway the listener can use this week, John's actual voice and personality.

**Weak episode:** tries to teach too many lessons at once, sounds generic or over-polished, loses the story, stays too abstract, gives no real application, sounds like motivation instead of experience.

## 2.12 Voice rules (explicit)
The podcast should sound like spoken John:

- Short sentences
- Short paragraphs
- Natural rhythm
- Plain words
- Repetition when it helps the point land
- Direct truth-telling
- Warmth without fluff
- Practical wisdom over clever language

**Hard rule:** John does not use dashes in his writing voice. Avoid them in scripted podcast content.

## 2.13 Hooks for podcast episodes
Strong podcast hooks do one of:

- Name a hard truth
- Create tension
- Challenge a false belief
- Expose the cost of weak leadership
- Make the listener feel seen fast

Examples:
- *A good person can still be the wrong fit.*
- *Keeping the wrong employee is not kindness. Sometimes it is fear with better branding.*
- *If every problem in your business still climbs back to you, the standard is broken.*

## 2.14 Story handling
Stories are the backbone. A real story earns attention, creates trust, makes the lesson memorable, and proves the message came from experience. When John gives a raw story, the agent should not just summarize — it should shape it. Look for:

- The moment of tension
- The bigger business meaning
- The core lesson
- The one action listeners should take

## 2.15 Connecting episode back to audience
The story alone is not enough. Always connect back to Mike Reynolds. Ask:

- How does this show up in the life of an overworked owner?
- What pain does this story explain?
- What lie does this story expose?
- What freedom does this story point toward?

## 2.16 Strategy and application requirements
Each episode should produce:

- One core principle
- 2 to 3 teaching points
- One practical framework if needed
- One challenge for the week

## 2.17 Support assets around the episode
Each episode can also generate:

- **Show notes draft** — short clear description of the episode and why it matters
- **Social clips plan** — sharp lines and moments for reels, shorts, or teaser posts
- **Email snippet** — short teaser for email promotion
- **Newsletter tie-in** — expand the episode theme into a full newsletter
- **Social promotion** — Facebook post, LinkedIn post, Instagram post, carousel, quote graphics, reels and short clips

## 2.18 Repurposing tree
From one strong episode the agent can usually pull: 2 to 3 strong hook lines, multiple short clips, one teaching carousel, one social post per major platform, one email teaser, one newsletter angle, one quote graphic or punch line set.

## 2.19 Weekly strategy alignment
If the weekly theme is owner independence through standards and leadership, episodes should support stronger standards, better delegation, time freedom, truthful leadership, reducing owner dependence, systems that create breathing room.

## 2.20 Recurring business concepts that fit the podcast
- **The 4 Basics** — core teaching framework from the UPS Stores
- **The 3-solution manager standard** — managers bring 3 proposed solutions, not just dump problems uphill
- **Systems create freedom**
- **Standards protect profit and culture**
- **Owner independence** — owner is not the answer machine

## 2.21 Delivery and file format
- Markdown source file for editable content
- PDF export for final delivery
- Word document when useful
- PDFs go in the export folder
- Optional: send file back through chat as attachment

## 2.22 Typical podcast deliverables list
From one story or one podcast idea the agent can create:

- Episode title options
- Hook options
- Full episode outline
- Full podcast script
- Talking points
- Show notes
- Social clip plan
- Email snippet
- Newsletter tie-in
- Social promo content

## 2.23 Bottom line for the agent
Take real experience, real leadership lessons, and real business tension and turn them into practical long-form content that helps owners move from stress and dependence toward freedom and clarity. Truth. Story. Strategy. Application.

# 3. Social Media Content System

## 3.1 Purpose
This is the working playbook behind the posts, scripts, reels, carousels, newsletters (when produced from social), podcast support, and weekly content batches.

## 3.2 Audience
Built for franchise owners and small retail owners who are overworked, stuck in the weeds, and hungry for time, leadership, systems, and owner freedom. Persona: **Mike Reynolds**.

Mike is typically:

- Working too many hours
- Carrying too many decisions
- Missing family time
- Frustrated by team inconsistency
- Wanting more profit and freedom
- Tired of vague advice and motivational fluff

Content is *not* written for beginners who want theory. It is written for owners who want practical help that works in the real world.

## 3.3 Identity and positioning
John is not positioned as a generic business coach. He is positioned as a battle-tested operator who built and led real UPS Store businesses, learned hard lessons in the trenches, and now teaches practical freedom through standards, leadership, and systems.

Content should always feel: real, direct, practical, warm, experienced, grounded in actual life.

## 3.4 Core promise
Same anchor as the rest of the system:

> **"You did not buy a business to buy yourself a job."**

Most content points back to: owner independence, time freedom, stronger standards, better leadership, healthier teams, more consistent profit.

## 3.5 Three themes
Same three buckets used across the content system:

**1. The Trapped Franchise Owner** — pain-focused (working 60+ hours, always solving problems, missing family moments, feeling like an employee in their own business).

**2. The Systems That Set You Free** — solution-focused (the 4 Basics, SOPs, standards, manager expectations, delegation systems, customer experience systems).

**3. The Leader You Are Becoming** — transformation-focused (operator-to-leader, telling the truth faster, protecting the standard, becoming more decisive, building a business that breathes without constant owner rescue).

## 3.6 Primary frameworks
Two frameworks. The agent does not create content randomly.

### 3.6.1 WYLA (story-driven content)
- **W** — What happened (real event, details, moment)
- **Y** — Why it matters (bigger than one moment, why the audience should care)
- **L** — Lesson learned (truth inside the story, the principle, the leadership insight)
- **A** — Apply it now (one clear practical step, not five pages of theory)

### 3.6.2 TUNE (teaching / concept / short-form content)
- **T** — Tension (call out the pain or problem fast)
- **U** — Understanding (explain why it is happening)
- **N** — New Possibility (show another way)
- **E** — Empowerment (give a clear next step)

Use TUNE more often for short-form and concept-driven posts.

## 3.7 Content creation process
Standard four-step workflow:

**Step 1 — Find the real source material.** Best sources: a real story from John, a leadership lesson from the UPS Stores, an angle from the weekly strategy brief, a practical teaching point (e.g., the 3-solution manager standard), a live business frustration John is facing now. The stronger and more real the source, the stronger the content.

**Step 2 — Identify the core message.** Pin down one main idea before writing. Examples: *"a good person can still be the wrong fit"* / *"systems protect freedom"* / *"managers should bring 3 solutions"* / *"unclear standards create owner dependence."* If the core message is fuzzy, the content gets weak.

**Step 3 — Match the right format to the message.** Then decide what the idea should become: Facebook post, LinkedIn post, Instagram post, Facebook Live script, 60-second reel, 2 to 3 short reel clips, carousel, quote graphic line, podcast outline / script, newsletter, weekly content batch.

**Step 4 — Build it (with the appropriate format rules below).**

## 3.8 Content formats
Ten format types, each with its own rules.

### 1. Facebook posts
Story-driven. Can be longer. Should feel personal, direct, practical.

### 2. LinkedIn posts
Cleaner, more leadership-focused. Still John's voice but leans into business language and decision-making.

### 3. Instagram captions
Shorter and punchier. Hook matters a lot. First lines must stop the scroll.

### 4. Facebook Live scripts
Usually come from WYLA stories. Rough structure: Hook → What → Why → Bridge → Lesson → Apply → Close.

### 5. 60-second reels
Structure: Hook → Story → Lesson → Apply. Always create the full spoken script, not just bullet points.

### 6. Ultra-short clips
10 to 15 second punchy lines pulled from the larger message. Sharpest or most replayable statements.

### 7. Carousels
Visual teaching pieces. Usually 5 to 7 slides. Order: Hook → Story or problem → Lesson or solution → Apply → CTA.

### 8. Quote graphics
Short punch lines worth sharing. One sentence, maybe two.

### 9. Podcast support
Episode titles, hook options, full episode outlines, full podcast scripts, show notes, social clips plan, email snippet.

### 10. Newsletters
Turn a story or leadership lesson into a long-form newsletter that matches John's voice and business message.

## 3.9 The WYLA story engine
One real story from John can become a full content suite:

- One Facebook Live script
- One 60-second reel
- 2 to 3 short reel clips
- One carousel
- 1 to 2 quote graphics
- Podcast episode support if needed
- Newsletter support if needed

This is one of the biggest advantages of the system — one story, multiple pieces of content.

## 3.10 Voice rules (explicit)
When writing as John:

- First person when John is speaking
- Simple language
- Short paragraphs
- Sound spoken and natural
- Direct
- Warm but not soft
- Practical not fluffy
- Real-life examples
- Grounded in experience
- Repetition when it helps the point land
- End with a clear action

**Hard rule:** John does not use dashes in his writing voice. Avoid em dashes and that style of punctuation in deliverables written as John.

**Avoid:**
- Generic motivational talk
- MBA / textbook language
- Corporate buzzwords
- Fake hype
- Vague inspiration without practical value

## 3.11 Hook construction
Hooks are critical, especially for Instagram, LinkedIn, reels, and Facebook Lives. Strong hooks do one of:

- Create tension
- Name a hard truth
- Challenge a bad assumption
- Make the owner feel seen
- Expose the cost of staying the same

Examples:
- *A good person can still be the wrong fit.*
- *You did not buy a business to buy yourself a job.*
- *If every problem still climbs back to you, the standard is broken.*
- *Your best people are paying for your hesitation.*

## 3.12 CTA construction
No weak CTAs. The CTA should fit the content. Common types:

- Ask one honest question
- Try one clear action this week
- Comment or reply with a keyword
- Listen to the episode
- Go to xpansioncode.com
- Reflect on a leadership blind spot

The CTA should feel like the natural next step, not random promotion.

## 3.13 Weekly strategy alignment
Content does not exist by itself. Align to weekly priorities. If the weekly theme is owner independence through standards and leadership, build content around: standards, delegation, leadership truth-telling, reducing owner dependence, buying time back.

## 3.14 Recurring business concepts
- **The 4 Basics** — core teaching system from the UPS Stores
- **The 3-solution manager standard** — managers do not escalate problems without bringing 3 proposed solutions
- **Owner independence** — owner is not the answer machine for everything
- **Systems create freedom** — right systems create trust, repeatability, breathing room
- **Standards protect profit and culture** — weak standards cost money, morale, momentum

## 3.15 Quality criteria
**Strong content has:** a real story, a clear lesson, one strong point (not ten weak ones), visible tension, simple language, a practical takeaway, alignment with John's real-world experience.

**Weak content:** too generic, too polished or fake, tries to teach too many things at once, loses the real human story, ignores the real pain of the owner-operator, content for content's sake.

## 3.16 Delivery and file format
- Markdown source file for editable content
- PDF export for final delivery
- Word document when useful
- PDFs go in the export folder
- Optional: send file back through chat as attachment

## 3.17 Typical deliverables list
From one story or one strong teaching point the agent can create:

- One Facebook post
- One LinkedIn post
- One Instagram post
- Hook variants A / B / C
- CTA and destination
- Suggested posting order and time windows
- One positioning line
- 3 positioning variants
- One recommended winner
- Polished final version
- Newsletter
- Podcast support
- Carousel
- Reels and clips

## 3.18 Production brief workflow
On production days the agent can build a full production brief with:

- Campaign angle for the day
- 3 platform-ready posts
- Hook variants
- CTA and destination
- Suggested posting windows
- One-sentence positioning
- Punchy / balanced / premium variants
- Recommended winner
- Polished version
- 3 CTA options

## 3.19 Bottom line for the agent
Take John's real-life experience, real lessons, and real business principles and turn them into practical content that helps overworked owners move toward freedom. Not a fluff system — a truth + strategy system. Stronger story in, stronger content out.

The weekly strategy brief sets the theme. If the weekly theme is owner independence through standards and leadership, *all three* channels reinforce that with content on: standards, delegation, leadership truth-telling, reducing owner dependence, buying time back, systems that create breathing room.

## 4.10 Shared delivery and file preferences
- Markdown source file for editable content
- PDF export for final delivery
- Word document when useful
- PDFs go in the export folder
- Optional: send file back through chat as attachment

## 4.11 Shared repurposing principle
Every channel feeds the others. One real story can become:

- A newsletter (long-form authority)
- A podcast episode (deep teaching)
- Multiple social media formats (Facebook, LinkedIn, Instagram, reels, clips, carousels, quote graphics)
- An email snippet
- A blog summary

The system is one connected content engine, not three separate channels.

## 4.12 Quality criteria summary (universal)
**Strong (any channel):** real story, one strong point, visible tension, practical teaching, simple language, useful application, John's authentic voice.

**Weak (any channel):** too generic, too polished, teaches too many things at once, loses the story, gives no real takeaway, sounds like motivation instead of experience, content for content's sake.

## 4.13 Bottom line for the Dify agent
Across all three channels the job is the same:

> Take real-life business tension and real experience and turn it into practical content that moves overworked owners toward freedom. Truth → Story → Strategy → Application. Not slick — true. Not theoretical — practical. Not generic — Mike-specific.
