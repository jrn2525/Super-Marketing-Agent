## Introduction

### The Videos That Broke The Internet

T HE FIRST HARMON BROTHERS VIDEO I EVER SAW was the Squatty Potty ad. A unicorn defecating rainbow soft-serve ice cream into waffle cones held by cherubic children.

Ninety seconds in, I realized I was watching a product pitch for a toilet stool. I had no idea how I’d gotten there, but I was laughing, I was learning something, and by the end, I was almost ready to buy.

That video did over 40 million views. The Purple mattress ad did over 200 million. The Poo~Pourri video launched a $20 million brand. FiberFix, Chatbooks, Kodiak Cakes, Kizik, Covenant Eyes, Keto Chow, Cove. A single agency in Utah kept producing these absurd, hilarious, genuinely weird direct-response videos. And every single one of them did two things that shouldn’t be possible at the same time: they went viral like entertainment, and they converted like hard-sell infomercials.

For a long time, I thought the Harmon Brothers had some secret creative gift that couldn’t be replicated. Great writers, great directors, great actors. A combination of talent you can’t manufacture. Then I started actually studying the videos. I pulled transcripts. I watched them side by side. I took notes on what happened at the 30-second mark, the one-minute mark, the two-minute mark.

And here’s what I found. The videos aren’t magic. They’re a formula. Every Harmon Brothers video hits the same ten beats, in the same order, at almost the same percentages of runtime. They deploy the same nine joke mechanics. They cast from the same seven character archetypes. They follow the same direct-response discipline. CTAs at 50%, proof stacks after the first CTA, risk reversal in-world, and a tag joke that ends the video on a character moment instead of a sales line.

The creative gift isn’t in the talent. It’s in the structure. Anybody with the structure can write one of these.

That’s what this guide is for.

I’ve also included a swipe file of four fully annotated teardowns of real Harmon Brothers campaigns, so you can see the whole machine working at once.

## Chapter One

### The Short Film That Sells

H ERE’S THE SHIFT IN THINKING you have to make before anything else in this guide is useful. A traditional direct-response video is an infomercial. Its job is to sell, and everything in it exists to move the viewer toward a buy decision. If a moment doesn’t contribute to the sale, it gets cut. The viewer tolerates the format only because they’re already interested.

Nobody shares an infomercial. Nobody watches one for fun. A Harmon Brothers video is a short film that happens to have a product pitch baked into its DNA. The viewer shows up for the story, stays for the jokes, and, if the script is doing its job, converts at the end without feeling sold to. The format is 90 percent entertainment and 10 percent pitch, but the 10 percent of pitch is placed so precisely inside the entertainment that the viewer doesn’t perceive the seams.

The result is a video that does something normal ads can’t do: it spreads by itself. Viewers share it. News articles write about it. Your paid media spend buys the first wave, and word-of-mouth carries it to ten or twenty times the audience you paid for.

The Non-Negotiable Rule Every Harmon Brothers video has to do three things at once. Entertain, so viewers actually watch it. Sell, so it makes money. Be memorable, so it spreads past your paid audience.

If a script entertains but doesn’t sell, it wastes money. If it sells but doesn’t entertain, nobody watches. If it’s not memorable, it never scales past the paid-view mark. All three goals have to be present, and the writer’s job is to fit them into a single 3-minute piece without letting any of the three dominate.

The Cold Open That Taught Me Everything Consider the opening line of the Squatty Potty ad: This is where your ice cream comes from. The creamy poop of a mystic unicorn. Totally clean, totally cool, and soft-served straight from a sphincter.



<!-- Page 8 -->



Read that again. There’s no product mention. No brand name. No logo on screen. No setup explaining what the viewer is about to watch. In the first ten seconds of a paid commercial, the Harmon Brothers spent the advertising real estate on a unicorn and rainbow soft-serve. On paper, that’s insane. Every instinct a marketer has says to lead with the product.

But watch what it does: it buys your attention. You are now 10 seconds into a video about toilet stools and you have absolutely no idea it’s a video about toilet stools. Your brain is working to figure out what you’re looking at. You’re not defended. You’re curious. And for the next 2 minutes and 40 seconds, you stay curious, because the script keeps rewarding your curiosity with more absurdity, more jokes, and more information about a product you would have dismissed if it had been pitched to you in frame one.

Every principle in this guide follows from that single move. The cold open isn’t the pitch. The cold open is the cost of entry to a world the viewer chooses to stay inside. Once they’re inside, you can sell them, but only by staying true to the world you’ve built.

Why This Format Scales People sometimes assume that what makes a Harmon Brothers video work is the creative team’s particular sense of humor. It’s a reasonable assumption. The videos are funny in a specific way, and the writers are talented. But humor alone doesn’t explain the conversion numbers. Plenty of funny ads don’t sell.

The real reason this format scales is that it solves the single hardest problem in direct response: getting attention from a skeptical audience without triggering their skepticism.

By the time the viewer realizes they’re in an ad, they’ve already invested two minutes of attention and laughed at five jokes. The sunk-cost feeling of “I’ve already watched this much” carries them through the CTA. They convert because they were never in sales-resistance mode to begin with.

That psychological move, delaying the viewer’s sales-resistance by dressing the pitch in a story, is the engine. Everything else in this guide is mechanical. Once you understand the engine, the rest of the parts fall into place.

## Chapter Two

### The 10-Beat Skeleton

E VERY HARMON BROTHERS LONG-FORM EXPLAINER follows a predictable ten-beat arc. The timings below are percentages of total runtime. They scale cleanly from a 2:30 script to a 5:00 script without breaking.

I want you to memorize these beats. Internalize the percentages. Because once you see them in action, you won’t be able to un-see them. Every video in the swipe file at the back of this guide hits all ten.

The Ten Beats

1. Cold Open (0 - 5%) world establishment, confusion or delight

2. Pain Identification (5 - 15%) visual, specific, slightly cartoonified

3. Alternative Parade (15 - 30%) roast each current option

4. Product Introduction (30 - 35%) brief, triumphant, tagline-forward

5. Demo / Mechanism (35 - 50%) specific, visualizable, credibility numbers

6. First CTA (50 - 55%) bare, no pressure

7. Proof Stack (55 - 70%) competitor beat, stats, expert authority

8. Social Proof (70 - 80%) rating, review count, 2 to 3 named reviews

9. Risk Reversal (80 - 85%) guarantee, free shipping, in-world language

10. Recap + Final CTA + Tag Joke (85 - 100%) close with a character moment

Why the Structure Holds at Every Length The genius of this skeleton is that it’s proportional, not absolute. A 3-minute script and a 5-minute script hit the same beats at the same percentages. Only the word budget changes. This means you never have to redesign the structure when a client asks for a longer or shorter cut.

Speaking rate across the dataset is consistent at roughly 150 words per minute, accounting for jokes, reactions, pauses, and visual gag beats. That gives you a simple word-budget rule: multiply your runtime in minutes by 150, and that’s your target length.



<!-- Page 10 -->



A 3-minute script should come in around 450 words. A 4-minute script around 600. Come in significantly over or under, and your pacing will be wrong.

Critical Design Choice: Three CTAs Look at the ten beats again. Notice that the call-to-action fires at least twice. Once at the 50-percent mark, and again in the recap at the 85-percent mark. The best Harmon Brothers videos hit a third CTA somewhere in the proof stack, usually around 65 percent.

This is deliberate. The first CTA at 50 percent catches viewers who have heard enough and are ready to click. The second (and third) CTAs catch viewers who weren’t convinced at 50 percent but got pushed over the edge by the proof stack or the social proof. If you only have one CTA, you’re letting the half of the audience who need more evidence walk away.

When in doubt, add a CTA. Never have fewer than two. Critical Design Choice: Proof Comes AFTER the First CTA This one catches new writers off guard. Your instinct is to build your case first (stack the proof, the reviews, the competitor comparisons) and then ask for the sale. The Harmon Brothers do it backwards. Demo, CTA, proof, more CTA.

The logic is cold: by 50 percent runtime, the convinced viewers should be able to buy. Making them wait another 90 seconds through a proof stack they don’t need is attention they might lose. The proof is there for the half who aren’t convinced, and for them, it’s being delivered AFTER they heard the first pitch, which primes them to evaluate the proof through a buying lens instead of a reading lens.

Critical Design Choice: The Tag Joke The last structural move, and the single most replicable signature of the format, is the tag joke. After the final CTA, after the URL is repeated, after the tagline lands, the video does not end. It takes one more beat for a character moment that calls back something from the cold open.

The VidAngel ad ends with the devil character ordering “Get me Hitler” after failing to pitch his products. The Keto Chow Hansel-and-Gretel version ends with the villagers debating whether the drawing of the witch actually looks like her. The Purple video brings back the bear cub from the $5,000-remote joke. The Kizik toddler-king complains one more time about lactose.

These tag jokes aren’t decoration. They do real structural work: they convert the video from a commercial into a short film. Without the tag, the video ends on a sales note, and the viewer remembers being sold to. With the tag, the video ends on a character



<!-- Page 11 -->



moment, and the viewer remembers being entertained. That’s the difference between a video that gets skipped and a video that gets shared.

## Chapter Three

### Choose Your World

T HE SINGLE BIGGEST CREATIVE DECISION in a Harmon Brothers script is the choice of world. Before you write a word of dialogue, before you cast a single character, before you figure out what the jokes are, you have to decide what kind of story this is going to be.

Is it a fairy tale? A superhero origin? A domestic comedy about a harried mom? A courtroom drama? A personified argument with your car? The choice determines everything downstream. The characters, the humor register, the visual style, and most importantly, the metaphor that will carry the customer’s pain.

The Selection Test: Productive Externalization A world works when it productively externalizes the customer’s problem. The customer’s internal pain, the real, daily, private thing they’re frustrated about, becomes a dramatic, visually shootable situation on screen.

Shapermint’s customer problem is that she doesn’t feel beautiful enough in her clothes. The externalization: Venus de Milo, the literal goddess of beauty, tells her she’s fine. The customer’s internal self-consciousness becomes a conversation with a deity.

Kizik’s customer problem is that she’s tired of bending to tie shoes. The externalization: a toddler-king who demands constant royal subservience. The daily annoyance of laces becomes ridiculous submission to a tiny tyrant.

Keto Chow’s customer problem is that he keeps failing on a keto diet. The externalization: a fairy-tale prince literally keeps falling off a tower. Mundane diet failure becomes heroic catastrophe.

Here’s the test: if you removed the product from the script, would the world’s central conflict still be a recognizable metaphor for the customer’s real problem? If yes, the world is doing work. If no, the world is decoration.

The Six World Archetypes Every Harmon Brothers video lives inside one of six world archetypes. Pick one, and the entire creative framework comes with it.

World 1: Fairy Tale / Mythological



<!-- Page 13 -->



Purple. Squatty Potty. Keto Chow. Beehive. Shapermint. Truvani. Hallow. About a third of the Harmon Brothers canon lives in this world.

Use it when the pain is universal, the product solves a problem that feels quest-like (weight loss, beauty, sleep, belief), and the brand has broad appeal across age groups.

It’s the highest-shareability world because storybook logic translates across demographics. World 2: Superhero / Action-Movie Covenant Eyes (Colossal Man). Skull Shaver (the Smoothest Man Alive). FiberFix (the manly-man narrator). This world works when the customer’s pain involves a personal struggle they need strength to overcome. Addiction, intimidation, inability. The product becomes the ally or weapon that unlocks the hero’s power.

World 3: Domestic-Absurd Chatbooks. Kodiak Cakes. Cove. Ruby Love. Several Kizik variants. Use this world when the customer is a parent, spouse, or caregiver managing a household, and the pain shows up in daily life. The comedy is relatable rather than fantastical. Someone is trying to do a mundane task while the world falls apart around them.

World 4: Court / Institutional Parody Kizik’s toddler-royal court. ClickFunnels’ website funeral. Hallow’s liturgical framing.

VidAngel’s Heaven-versus-Movie-Purgatory setup partially. Use this when the pain involves something the customer is supposed to do but hates. A ritual, an obligation, a gatekept process. The comedy comes from dignity-versus-ridiculousness contrast.

World 5: Personified Concept Ruby Love’s period. Olive’s car. Truvani’s Mother Nature. No B.S.’s beauty industry.

Shapermint’s Venus. Use this when the customer’s pain has a clear external cause that can be personified. When you want to make peace with something the customer would rather avoid, or when you want to have the pain-cause speak for itself.

World 6: Genre Parody with a Twist FiberFix’s manly-man infomercial. Olive’s psychological-thriller-from-the-car’s-POV.

How to Match a World to a Pain If you’re stuck choosing between worlds, this mapping helps:



<!-- Page 14 -->



When the pain feels like a quest or journey, consider Fairy Tale or Superhero. When it feels like a daily grind, consider Domestic-Absurd or Court Parody. When it feels like an enemy to defeat, consider Superhero, Personified Concept, or Genre Parody. When it feels like a broken system, consider Court Parody or Personified Concept. When it feels like personal inadequacy, consider Fairy Tale or a sympathetic Personified Concept. When it feels embarrassing, Personified Concept tends to win. Giving voice to the taboo topic normalizes it.

Proposing Three Concepts When you’re building a script from scratch, or when you’re interviewing a client to build one, don’t commit to a single world before the client sees options. Propose three distinct world concepts drawn from different archetypes. Let them pick.

The reason: choosing the world is a decision about brand tone, not just story. A fairy tale feels whimsical; a superhero world feels bombastic; a domestic-absurd world feels warm and shared; a court parody feels sharp and disruptive. The same product in two different worlds will read to the audience as two different brands. That decision belongs to the client.

Three concepts from three different archetypes gives the client a meaningful choice without overwhelming them with variations. Two concepts tend to feel like a forced A-B choice. Four or more tends to feel like the writer hasn’t committed to anything. Three is the sweet spot.

## Chapter Four

### The 9 Joke Mechanics

H UMOR IN A HARMON BROTHERS VIDEO ISN’T DECORATION. It’s structural load-bearing. The jokes are what buys the attention, what keeps the viewer from reaching for the skip button, and what makes the video memorable enough to share.

Across the dataset, a joke, gag, or punchline lands every 8 to 15 seconds. That’s the joke density rule. Scripts that skew closer to 8 seconds feel manic. Chatbooks, Keto Chow, ClickFunnels. Scripts that skew closer to 15 seconds feel grounded. Purple, Covenant Eyes, Shapermint. Below 8 seconds, the viewer exhausts. Above 15, they drift.

The joke doesn’t have to be a full gag. It can be a character aside, a visual sight gag, a one-word reaction, or a fourth-wall glance. But the viewer’s expression should change roughly every 10 seconds.

Here are the nine recurring joke mechanics. A typical 3-minute script deploys six to nine of these. Use this as a checklist while writing. Are you getting enough variety?

Mechanic 1: Bathos (Grand to Mundane) Set up a line in heroic, epic, or formal register, then undercut with a punchline in the register of everyday annoyance. Noah the prophet delivers this perfectly in the Beehive spot: I warn the people of the great and terrible flood of the earth! But totally forgot about the smaller flood of our leaky water pipes.

Venus de Milo does the same move for Shapermint: “Did you really think the goddess of love doesn’t have love handles?” The Keto Chow prince: “My love, your sweetness pleasures my lips and tingles my tummy.” Works best in Fairy Tale, Court Parody, and Superhero worlds. Slot one bathos joke into the cold open or product intro for instant tone-setting.

Mechanic 2: Anthropomorphization The customer’s problem, a concept, or the product itself becomes a character with dialogue, emotions, and personality. Ruby Love’s period is a sentient entity with feelings.

Olive’s car is actively plotting revenge. Truvani’s Mother Nature is an irritable woman experiencing hot flashes. ClickFunnels’ website gets a funeral.



<!-- Page 16 -->



Give the personified thing one clear personality trait, resentful, friendly, confused, bureaucratic, and let the trait react to the product reveal. That reaction is usually where the joke lands.

Mechanic 3: Running Gag with Deferred Payoff Plant a minor joke in Beat 1 or 2, let it sit through the middle of the script, and bring it back in Beat 10 with a bigger laugh. Every script should have at least one of these.

Jeffrey from Chatbooks is on the roof, has a crossbow, holds his breath for 26 seconds. Each escalation lands bigger than the last. The Purple bear cub appears once early, then returns in the tag joke. T-shirt rat from the Harmon Brothers’ own ClickFunnels pitch becomes the final punchline character.

The callback is the single most effective structural device for making a video feel written rather than assembled.

Mechanic 4: Fourth-Wall Break A character acknowledges they’re in a commercial. The Purple ad does this during its most skeptical moment, the egg demonstration: And no, we didn’t fake it. How lame would that be? You’re lame for thinking that.

By the time the viewer can doubt the demo, a character has already voiced the doubt and dismissed it. It’s an inoculation move. Use it during the demo or proof beats, but limit to one or two fourth-wall breaks per script. The potency drops fast with repetition.

Mechanic 5: Self-Aware Absurdity The script comments on its own ridiculousness, making the viewer complicit in the bit rather than confused by it. Squatty Potty: Introducing the Squatty Potty. No, Squatty Potty is not a joke. And yes, it will give you the best poop of your life, guaranteed.

VidAngel: “YES, VidAngel is an Actual Real Product. And YES, you can really watch filtered movies for $1.” Skull Shaver, on the 90-second shave: “It’s the only acceptable time for a man to be done in 90 seconds.”

Especially valuable for brands with weird names, unusual product categories, or claims that sound too good to be true. Defuse skepticism by naming it first.

Mechanic 6: PG-Dark Comedy Children, injury, death, or existential threat are referenced, always in a cartoon register, always as domestic background rather than central joke. Beehive’s Terrence is drowning



<!-- Page 17 -->



while another character waves cheerfully. Keto Chow’s witch wants to eat the children while the villagers want to burn the witch. Cove’s toddler is running with scissors.

The rule: the darkness is domestic background, never the central joke. A chainsaw visible in the background is okay; a chainsaw being used on someone is not. A kid on the roof is okay; a kid falling off the roof is not (unless it’s a costumed fairy-tale prince falling harmlessly for the hundredth time, in which case it’s absurd rather than dark).

Used well, PG-dark comedy signals “we’re being honest about the chaos of real life” without ever making the viewer feel unsafe. Domestic-Absurd worlds use it most.

Mechanic 7: Escalating Absurdity Each beat raises the stakes or strangeness of the world’s premise. By the end, the world has become much wilder than when it started.

Squatty Potty’s escalation goes: unicorns exist, then unicorns poop ice cream, then there are multiple unicorns, then your doctor approves, then we are selling you the stool.

Beehive’s goes: Noah is here, Noah has a wife, Noah’s neighbors are drowning, Noah is fighting with Roger, Roger is dying, Roger says “I’m already done.”

Start with a premise that’s already one click past realism. Add one new escalation per major beat (not per line, that’s too fast). By the end of Beat 10, the world should feel three or four clicks weirder than Beat 1. The escalation should feel logical within the world’s rules, not arbitrary.

Mechanic 8: Genre Parody The world borrows the costume of a known genre and plays inside it with one key subversion. FiberFix as manly-man infomercial. Skull Shaver partial action-movie. The Keto Chow fairy tales. Beehive as biblical prophecy. Shapermint as Greek mythology.

Commit to the genre’s vocabulary, character types, and visual language completely. Identify the ONE subversion that makes it funny. Usually inserting an incongruous modern product concern into the genre’s register. Don’t parody multiple genres in one script.

Commit to one. Mechanic 9: The Callback Button The final joke of the video, delivered after the final CTA, that pays off a setup from Beat 1 or 2 and ends on a character moment. Every script should end on one of these.

Plant a weird detail in Beat 1 or 2. Write the entire script without referencing it again. In Beat 10, after the final CTA and tagline, bring it back, ideally with the stakes escalated. End the video on that moment. Don’t add another CTA after it.



<!-- Page 18 -->



This is the single most replicable structural signature of the Harmon Brothers format. It’s what converts the ad from pitch to short film.

## Chapter Five

### The Character Roles

H ARMON BROTHERS SCRIPTS CAST FROM A SMALL, consistent set of character roles. Not every script uses all seven. Most use three or four. But knowing these archetypes lets you build a cast quickly and makes sure your scripts have the variety of voices they need to sustain a full three minutes.

One more thing before we get into the roles. The most important character in any Harmon Brothers video is the narrator-in-costume, the voice that guides the viewer through the script while staying inside the world. This is where your brand voice lives. If you’ve built out an Attractive Character for your brand, the narrator-in-costume is the place to deploy it. Supporting characters can have their own voices; the narrator should sound like your brand.

Role 1: The Narrator-in-Costume The voice guiding the viewer through the script, dressed in the world’s clothes. Speaks directly to camera. Delivers most of the DR copy. Sets the tone for the entire video.

The unicorn prince in Squatty Potty. Venus de Milo in Shapermint. Mother Nature in Truvani. The redneck in FiberFix. Dr. Bar’Bra Bouldair in ThirdLove. Colossal Man in Covenant Eyes. Noah in Beehive.

This character is inside the world but aware of the viewer. They can break the fourth wall. They’re your closest analog to a host, except that a host stands outside the story while the narrator-in-costume stands inside it.

Role 2: The Straight Man / Skeptic The voice of the viewer’s doubt, asking the questions the audience is thinking. Often a secondary narrator or a character the primary narrator is trying to convince. Rapunzel’s customer in Keto Chow. The skeptical neighbor in Cove. The prince before he believes in keto.

Their job is to ask “wait, really?” so the viewer doesn’t have to. The answers they get feel earned rather than asserted. Keep their skepticism genuine. Never theatrical. If the skeptic sounds like they’re setting up the answer, the viewer reads the setup and the move fails.



<!-- Page 20 -->



Role 3: The Oblivious Partner A spouse, friend, or companion who is in the scene but doesn’t quite understand it.

Generates comedy through misread reactions and accidental honesty. The husband in Chatbooks (“Why is Dad wearing a dress?”). The off-screen prince in Shapermint (“What?” at Venus’s suitor admission). The partner in Kodiak Cakes who just wants to eat pancakes.

The obliviousness is affectionate, not hostile. They love the protagonist but keep missing context. Their lines are short reactions and confused questions. They should never upstage the narrator.

Role 4: The Reactive Child Kids who escalate the chaos of the world, trigger running gags, or provide the blunt honesty adults can’t. Jeffrey on the roof in Chatbooks. The gingerbread children in Keto Chow. The toddler-king in Kizik.

Children in these scripts exist slightly outside the adult logic of the scene. Their lines are short and unfiltered. Either matter-of-fact about scary things or scared of nothing-things.

Never cutesy. Role 5: The Personified Villain The competitor, the problem, or the old-way-of-doing-things, embodied as a character the viewer can hate (or pity). The medium bed as a “whoosy centaur” in Purple.

“Competitor” at the Keto Chow taste test. Satan in VidAngel. The shame cycle monster in Covenant Eyes.

Not evil, exactly. More incompetent or self-interested. The viewer should enjoy their defeat more than fear them. The villain’s dialogue should give reasons to dislike them rather than being narrated as bad. Let them condemn themselves.

Role 6: The Witnessing Chorus Villagers, neighbors, bystanders, or a crowd who witness the action and react. Creates running gags and social proof for the world’s logic. The villagers who want to burn the witch in Keto Chow. The gym members and rideshare riders in Covenant Eyes. Roger and Terrence in Beehive.

They exist primarily for reaction shots and group-joke beats. Short collective or individual exclamations. They react to the protagonists rather than initiate action. Use them to add texture to a world, not to carry scenes.

Role 7: The Product Reviewer



<!-- Page 21 -->



Named customers who appear in the social proof beat. Beverly J. in Keto Chow. Jaron and Julie in Truvani. Goddess Suzie in Shapermint. Kristen in Ruby Love.

First name plus last initial. Their review line is short, sensory, and emotionally specific. Never generic (“great product!”). Always mentioning a concrete outcome (“losts 50 pounds in four months,” “tastes like a milkshake”). The actor delivering the review is often one of the main cast in a different costume or framing, which is a useful production economy as well as a comic beat.

## Chapter Six

### Direct Response In Disguise

H ERE’S WHERE WE STOP TALKING about comedy and start talking about money. A Harmon Brothers video is, at the end of the day, a direct-response piece. It’s measured by conversions. The reason brands pay for these scripts isn’t that they’re funny. It’s that they sell. And they sell because underneath the world-building and the jokes, they are running the same DR playbook that every good direct-response asset runs. They just run it so well that you don’t notice it happening.

This chapter covers the DR levers that make the format convert, and why each one is usually performed rather than stated.

The Memorable URL Every CTA points to a URL you can remember. The URL is either the product name dot com, TryKetoChow.com, SkullShaver.com, CovenantEyes.com, OnPurple.com, or a campaign URL that reinforces the video’s hook: DeathOfAWebsite.com for ClickFunnels, BeehiveFlood.com for the Orbit flood sensor, FunnelChallenge.com for the 1-Funnel-Away campaign.

Never a subdomain. Never a long URL. The viewer has to be able to remember it after the video ends, which means it has to fit in a single short phrase. If you can’t say the URL in under two seconds, find a shorter one.

Specific Numbers Everywhere Specificity is the credibility tell. Vague claims, fast, affordable, proven, trigger skepticism.

Weirdly precise numbers feel true even when they shouldn’t. “8 million meals sold.” “9 out of 10 dispatchers.” “$99 could save you $99,000.” “14 grams of protein.” “Over 15 patents, created by an actual rocket scientist.” “26 seconds.” “$724 bonus.” The numbers don’t even have to be the most impressive version of the claim.

They have to be the most specific.



<!-- Page 23 -->



When writing proof beats, resist the instinct to round. “Thousands of five-star reviews” is weaker than “over 10,000 five-star reviews.” “Millions of sales” is weaker than “8 million meals sold.” The human brain reads the specificity as “someone actually counted this,” which reads as “this is not marketing.”

Performed Proof, Not Stated Proof This is the move that separates Harmon Brothers DR from ordinary DR. When a normal ad says “our product beat four competitors in taste tests,” the viewer has to believe the claim on faith. When a Harmon Brothers ad makes the same claim, a character named “Competitor” actually loses a taste test on camera. The viewer sees the test.

Purple doesn’t just claim its mattress is better at supporting pressure points. The viewer watches eggs survive while a character sits on them. Cove doesn’t just say its response time is faster. The script dramatizes a 911 dispatcher’s screen lighting up with the address.

Keto Chow doesn’t say its flavor wins. A character representing “Competitor” tastes both and loses on camera.

When you’re writing a proof beat, ask yourself how the claim could be shown. If it can be shown, show it. The performed version always lands harder than the stated version, because performed proof is dramatization, which the viewer reads through the entertainment frame. Which means they don’t engage their skepticism on the way in.

Named Customer Reviews First name plus last initial. Beverly J. Jaron. Julie. Goddess Suzie. The specificity makes the testimonial feel unfabricated even when, Seinfeld-style truth, it’s been lightly fabricated for the script.

The reviews are selected for being short, emotionally specific, and sensory. “Beverly J. lost 50 pounds in four months.” “Jaron says it tastes like a milkshake.” Not “it was helpful” or “I’d recommend it to a friend.” Concrete outcomes, not generic endorsement.

In production, the actor delivering the review is usually one of the main cast in a different costume or framing. This is both a comic beat and an economy. You didn’t have to hire new cast. But the viewer reads it as a satisfied customer.

Risk Reversal With a Number Every Harmon Brothers risk reversal includes a specific number. 30-day money-back guarantee. 60-day. 100 nights. 40 days and 40 nights (Beehive’s Noah world). The number makes the guarantee concrete.

A signature move: match the guarantee language to the world. Beehive’s risk reversal runs for “40 days and 40 nights” because Noah. Purple’s is called the “no-pressure



<!-- Page 24 -->



guarantee” because mattresses. The risk reversal wears the world’s costume, which prevents it from reading like a bolted-on marketing moment.

Price Anchoring Anchor your price against something concrete and dramatic. Purple: “Most high-end mattresses cost $5,000. But ours is only $1,000.” Orbit Beehive: “$99 could save you $99,000.” Olive: “Most olive customers pay just $60 a month. Compare that to $3,000 to replace a transmission.”

The anchor has to be specific. “Cheaper than the competition” is useless. “A $5,000 mattress vs. ours at $1,000” is devastating. The dollar figures do more work than the adjectives.

The Self-Aware Price Move The best price-anchor beats pair the number with a self-aware line. Purple doesn’t just say “$1,000 vs. $5,000.” The narrator says “We’re sorry about that. We’re the best.” The self-aware line acknowledges that the viewer might find the price surprising and preempts the objection with character.

This pattern shows up throughout the format: whenever the script is about to say something that might strain credulity, a price, a claim, a demonstration, it hands the objection to a character first. Once a character voices the skepticism, the viewer’s skepticism has nowhere to go.

## Chapter Seven

### The Creative Director Interview

E VERYTHING IN THIS GUIDE SO FAR HAS BEEN THEORY. This chapter is practice. If you sit down to write one of these scripts, for yourself, for a client, or as an in-house team, this is the workflow.

The workflow has three phases: interview, concept, write. Do them in order. Don’t skip phases. A script you wrote before the client picked a world is a script they won’t own, which is a script they won’t approve, which is a script you’ll rewrite.

Phase One: The Eight Questions Ask these questions one at a time. Wait for the answer before moving on. Push for specificity when answers are vague. Offer examples when the user is stuck. A Harmon Brothers script lives or dies on concrete detail.

Q1. The product, in one sentence. What’s the product, and in one sentence, what does it do? If they give you a feature list, push for the outcome. What does the customer’s life look like after they buy it?

Q2. The customer. Who is this for, not in demographic terms, but as a specific person? What are they doing when they see this ad? What’s frustrating them that day? What’s the moment this video interrupts? This is the most important question. A good answer unlocks the world-building.

Q3. The pain, concrete and visual. What’s the single most annoying, visceral version of the pain your product solves? Not the marketing version. The version they’d complain about to a friend. What does it look like in real life? Follow up if the answer is abstract. You need something a director could shoot.

Q4. The mechanism.



<!-- Page 26 -->



How does the product actually work, at the level of “here’s the thing it does that others don’t”? What’s the feature or mechanism that earns the demo beat, the thing you can show working? If the mechanism is purely software or abstract, ask what the experience of using it feels like.

Q5. The proof. What proof do we have that it works? Specific numbers, reviews, press, case studies. Even rough. You’ll clean it up. Who are the real competitors, even if we don’t name them? What are we positioning against? You need at least two or three specifics for the proof stack and social proof beats.

Q6. The offer. What’s the exact ask? Give me the URL, the price, any guarantee, any bonus, any urgency.

Is there a deadline or is this evergreen? You’ll use this three times. First CTA, risk reversal, and final CTA.

Q7. The length. How long should the video be? Default is 3:00, but 2:30, 3:30, 4:00, or 5:00 are all viable.

Shorter scripts are tighter and better for YouTube pre-roll. Longer scripts have more room for world-building and tend to convert better for considered purchases.

Q8. Constraints. Anything off-limits? Topics, words, or references to avoid? Any competitor names you can or can’t use? Anything legal would flag? Any must-include, a tagline, a brand line, a specific phrase the founder wants in?

Phase Two: Propose Three World Concepts Once you have the eight answers, propose three distinct world concepts drawn from the six archetypes we covered in Chapter Three. Each concept includes: a world-archetype label, a one- or two-sentence premise, the main character(s), how the world externalizes the customer’s pain, and the tonal register.

Pitch three concepts that feel meaningfully different from each other. Don’t propose three fairy tales. A fairy tale plus a superhero plus a personified concept lets the client feel the range and choose based on brand fit. Two concepts feel like a forced A-B. Four or more feels like you haven’t committed to anything.

If the client rejects all three, ask what didn’t land, tone, characters, metaphor? And pitch three new ones tuned to what they want. The worlds are cheap to generate; commit early to making the client happy. Don’t defend your original three if they’re not working.



<!-- Page 27 -->



If the client loves two and can’t choose, offer to write a short teaser of each. Cold open, pain, product intro, about 60 seconds of script for each version. The tonal difference becomes immediately obvious, and they’ll pick the one that feels right.

Phase Three: Write to the Structure Once the world is locked, write the full script using the ten-beat structure from Chapter Two. Keep your word budget on hand. Check your joke density, eight to fifteen seconds between laughs. Deploy six to nine of the joke mechanics from Chapter Four. Cast from the character roles in Chapter Five. Hit every DR lever from Chapter Six.

Deliver the script in shooting-transcript format. Character names followed by dialogue, action beats in brackets, on-screen text called out separately, beat headers with timecodes. This is what a production team can actually shoot from.

The Pre-Delivery Checklist Before you send the script, run through this list. This isn’t a best-practices list. It’s a floor.

Every one of the 30-plus Harmon Brothers videos in the dataset hits all of them.

• Cold open is product-free. No brand name, logo, or direct reference in the first five

seconds.

• Pain is visual. A director could shoot it from this script.

• Each alternative has a punchline. No bullet-point alternatives.

• Product intro is two sentences or less. Brief, triumphant, tagline-forward.

• Demo has specific numbers. Credibility through precision.

• At least two CTAs, ideally three. At 50%, in proof stack, and in recap.

• Reviews use first name plus last initial. Not “one customer.”

• Guarantee has a specific number. 30, 60, 100 days. Never “satisfaction.”

• Tag joke callbacks something from Beats 1 or 2. Structural wholeness.

• Six or more joke mechanics deployed. Variety over repetition.

• Joke every 8 to 15 seconds. Scan the script, count the laughs.

• Tagline appears exactly twice. At intro and in recap.

• No corporate jargon. “Utilize,” “solutions,” “experience,” “journey.” All out.

• Ends on a character moment, not a CTA. Last line should be in-world dialogue.

If any of those is missing, revise before delivery.

## Teardown One

### Purple Mattress

R UNTIME: 3:46 · VIEWS: 206 million · World: Fairy Tale (Goldilocks) + Genre Parody (science demo) · Narrator: Goldilocks as deadpan expert Beat 1 - Cold Open (0:00 - 0:15) What’s a super easy way to tell if your bed is awful? The egg test. Let me prove it.

Bold false-premise opening. Zero product mention. The “egg test” is mysterious. The viewer needs to know what it is. The entire rest of the video exists to satisfy that curiosity.

Beat 4 - Product Introduction (0:55 - 1:05) Introducing Purple - the only mattress that cradles your pressure points like a soft bed, while supporting everywhere else like a firm one.

One sentence. Tagline-forward. Musical sting. Two seconds of product positioning, and we’re out. Beat 5 - Demo (1:05 - 1:45) The egg test is performed. “Need proof? Let’s check double check. Triple check. All the checks!”

And then the fourth-wall break that defines the format: Yes, these raw eggs are raw. And no, we didn’t fake it. How lame would that be? You’re lame for thinking that.

This inoculates against skepticism DURING the proof moment. Masterful. The credibility specifics come right after: “15 patents, created by an actual rocket scientist, and uses a

comfort grid system.” Beat 6 - First CTA (~2:00) “Click here to buy your Purple at onpurple.com.” Bare. No sales stacking.

Beat 7 - Proof Stack (2:00 - 2:30) Price anchoring: “Most high-end mattresses cost $5,000. But ours is only $1,000. We’re sorry about that. We’re the best.” Self-aware absurdity plus price anchor stacked into a single move.

In-world naming (“no-pressure”), specific number (100 nights). The guarantee wears the world’s costume.

What This Teardown Teaches Jokes can be front-loaded into Beats 1 and 2 without exhausting the audience if they’re varied. Bathos, pop-culture reference, and anthropomorphization of mattresses all in 15 seconds. Fourth-wall breaks work best at the moment of highest potential skepticism.

Price anchoring benefits from a self-aware humor beat (“We’re sorry about that”). The tag joke can callback a joke from two minutes earlier and still land.

### Keto Chow

R UNTIME: 2:55 · WORLD: FAIRY Tale (Rapunzel) · Narrator: Rapunzel as narrator, the prince as reactive foil Beat 1 - Cold Open (0:00 - 0:10) Rapunzel and her prince. The prince falling off the tower while she screams. No product.

Beat 8 - Social Proof (2:15 - 2:30)
