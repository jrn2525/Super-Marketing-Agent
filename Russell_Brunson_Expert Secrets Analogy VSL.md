## Introduction

### The Video That Changed How I Sell

I WAS AT KNEADERS IN BOISE, eating their all-you-can-eat chunky cinnamon French toast - the two-inch-thick version drowned in homemade caramel, strawberries, and whipped cream - when the founder of a new startup pitched me on becoming a partner.

I’m pretty sure he got me sugar-drunk on purpose. The idea sounded great, and I agreed. A week later, he sent me the sales script he’d written for their front-end explainer video. I read through it and felt a little dirty. It was a traditional pitch. Every copywriting trick and sales tool was in there. And yet, as I was reading it, I didn’t want to sign up. Something was off.

Here’s what I realized. The problem wasn’t that the script was bad - it was that the script was selling. And the moment a viewer feels like they’re being sold to, a wall goes up. They stop listening. They start defending. The sale is already lost.

I sent the founder an email that basically said: “Your goal isn’t to try to sell anyone anything. Your goal is to guide them to their own decision. If you want people to adopt a new concept, you have to lead them to the answer - but you can’t give it to them. They have to come up with the idea themselves. You plant the idea in their minds with a story, and if they come up with the answer, they’ll have sold themselves.”



<!-- Page 6 -->



He wrote back asking me to take a shot at writing the script. I spent the next few hours writing what I now call the Epiphany Bridge, and we used it as the explainer video on launch day for a startup called Rippln.

Within six weeks, we had acquired 1.5 million users with zero dollars in advertising spend. That’s the day I figured out that explainer videos aren’t supposed to sell. They’re supposed to teach. They’re supposed to take the viewer on an emotional journey that leads them to their own epiphany - the same epiphany I had, that got me excited about the product in the first place. When the viewer has their own aha moment, the buying decision becomes theirs, not yours. You don’t have to close them. They close themselves.

Since then, I’ve written explainer videos for ClickFunnels, Pruvit, Actionetics, Backpack, and dozens of other products. And every one of them has followed the same framework. A few of them have generated hundreds of millions of dollars in sales. One of them - the original ClickFunnels explainer - basically built the company.

I call this framework the Analogy VSL. It’s a teaching video. It’s an Epiphany Bridge. It’s 90% educational story and 10% direct product pitch, and every piece of it is organized around one extended analogy that carries the viewer from problem to product.

In this guide, I’m going to walk you through the complete framework. You’ll learn the eight-beat skeleton, how to choose the analogy that carries your entire script, the “kinda like” bridge tool I use to teach complex concepts without losing the viewer, the signature phrases that make these videos sound the way they sound, and the direct response mechanics that make them convert. ## Chapter One

### The Teaching Video That Sells

B EFORE WE GET INTO THE STRUCTURE, I need you to understand the fundamental shift this framework makes.

Most explainer videos are built like compressed infomercials. They open with the problem, introduce the product, list the features, stack the benefits, show the reviews, and close with urgency. Every sentence is working to move the viewer one step closer to a buying decision. Nothing that doesn’t contribute to the sale makes it in.

That’s how most people think a sales video is supposed to work. And if you’re selling something simple - like a cheap physical product where the viewer already knows exactly what it is - it can work just fine.

But if you’re selling something complex - a SaaS platform, a supplement, a course, a service that requires the customer to see the world differently before they’ll buy - the hard-sell approach falls apart. The viewer puts up their defenses the second they realize they’re being pitched, and no amount of clever copywriting gets past those defenses.

The Analogy VSL solves this with a different idea: teach instead of sell. The 90/10 Rule Every Analogy VSL is 90 percent teaching and 10 percent pitching. Let me break that down.

The first 90 percent of your video is pure education. You’re teaching the viewer something they didn’t know. You’re using an analogy to reframe a problem they didn’t realize they had. You’re walking them through an emotional journey that gets



<!-- Page 8 -->



them to the same epiphany that got you excited about your product in the first place. The final 10 percent is the ask. You introduce the product briefly, show how it works, and make one clean call to action. No stacked urgency, no bonus piling, no fake scarcity. The viewer has already sold themselves by this point - your job is just to tell them where to click.

The ratio isn’t arbitrary. I’ve tested versions that pitched harder, and they converted worse. I’ve tested versions that skipped the teaching, and they converted worse.

Ninety-ten is the sweet spot, because it matches the viewer’s attention curve. By the time you’re ready to ask for the sale, they’re already nodding.

I wrote about this in Expert Secrets, but it’s worth repeating here because it’s the foundation of everything else in this book: If you are directly trying to sell something in your video, it’s not as strong, and it doesn’t create the emotion you need to really cause action. If you want people to adopt a new concept and want to get their buy-in, you have to lead them to the answer, but you can’t give it to them. They have to come up with the idea themselves.

That’s the whole game. When the viewer arrives at the answer on their own, they own the decision. When you arrive at the answer and hand it to them, they’re evaluating your sales pitch - and evaluation mode is the opposite of buying mode.

Every structural element in this framework is designed to keep the viewer in the driver’s seat. The Socratic questions in Beat 3 aren’t rhetorical flourishes - they’re consent builders. Each question the viewer answers internally is a small “yes” they’ve given themselves. By the time they get to the CTA, they’ve stacked a dozen small yeses, and the final yes feels like a logical next step, not a leap of faith.

The Proof: ClickFunnels Opens With McDonald’s Here’s how this looks in practice. When I wrote the original ClickFunnels explainer, I didn’t open with “ClickFunnels is a sales funnel builder.” I didn’t open with “Most websites don’t convert.” I didn’t open with the product or the pain at all.

I opened with McDonald’s: Did you know that it costs McDonald’s $1.91 in advertising just to get you into the drive-thru? And when they sell you a burger for $2.09, they’re only actually making just $0.18. But when they upsell you fries and a coke for $1.77 more, they make, and more importantly they keep, a $1.32 profit. Yes, 8 times the profit of the initial sale. It’s pretty cool, don’t you think?

Read those first fifteen seconds again. Notice what’s not there. There’s no product. There’s no brand. There’s no feature. There’s no “introducing ClickFunnels.” The viewer has absolutely no idea what this video is about.

And that’s the point. Those first fifteen seconds are buying the viewer’s attention, not pitching them. I’m teaching them something interesting about McDonald’s economics, with specific numbers that make the story feel researched. By the time the viewer figures out this video is about something online-business-related, they’ve already invested attention in the McDonald’s story - and they’re curious where it’s going.

For the next two and a half minutes, I extend the McDonald’s analogy across the viewer’s online business. Capture page equals drive-thru. Sales page equals burger. Upsells equal fries and coke. The analogy does all the teaching. By the time I introduce ClickFunnels at the two-minute mark, the viewer already understands what it is, because McDonald’s has been showing them the whole time.

That’s the Teaching Video That Sells. Let me show you how it’s built.

## Chapter Two

### The 8-Beat Skeleton

E VERY ANALOGY VSL I’VE ever written follows the same eight beats in the same order. I’ve used this structure for SaaS, supplements, affiliate platforms, viral marketing apps, coaching programs, and more. The sequence doesn’t change.

When I first started writing these videos, I didn’t know the beats existed - I was just following my gut. It wasn’t until I’d written twenty or thirty of them that I noticed I was doing the same thing every time. Once I named the beats, I could teach the framework to my team, and they could write explainer videos that sounded like I wrote them.

Here are the eight beats, with the percentage of total runtime each one takes up. These percentages are stable across a 2:45 script or a 4:30 script - the structure scales proportionally.

1. Analogy Hook (0 - 15%) - open with a familiar thing, not the product

2. The Bridge (15 - 25%) - connect the analogy to the viewer’s world

3. Problem Recognition (25 - 40%) - Socratic questions inside the metaphor

4. Friction Moment (40 - 50%) - “why does it have to be so hard?”

5. Origin Story (50 - 60%) - “we asked ourselves... that’s why we created X”

6. The Demo (60 - 80%) - “Let me show you how it works” → enumerated steps

7. Future Pace (80 - 90%) - “Just imagine...” paint life with the product

8. Belief Statement + CTA (90 - 100%) - “So what are you waiting for?”



<!-- Page 11 -->



Three Design Choices You Need to Understand Before we go beat by beat, there are three structural decisions baked into this framework that you need to internalize. These are the non-negotiables. If you skip any of them, the script breaks.

1. The product doesn’t appear until Beat 5

Most sales videos introduce the product in the first ten seconds. Brand on screen, product name spoken, logo everywhere. The viewer knows exactly what they’re being pitched from the first second.

In my explainer videos, the product doesn’t appear until the origin story in Beat 5 - which is typically halfway through the script. The first 50 percent of the video is pure teaching with no product mention at all.

This freaks clients out at first. “How is anyone supposed to know what we’re selling?” The answer is: they’re not supposed to know yet. If the viewer knew this was an ad for X, they’d put up their defenses. By keeping the product hidden, you keep them in learning mode. And by the time the product finally appears, they already understand the problem you’re about to solve.

2. The origin story is always a question you asked yourselves

When I introduce the product in Beat 5, I never position myself as a guru who figured out the answer. I position myself as someone who noticed the same problem the viewer notices and went looking for a solution.

In the Rippln script, the origin story reads: Well, a little while ago, we asked ourselves these two important questions. First, when I started a ripple, how far does it go? And second, why aren’t I getting paid from the ripple effect that I’m causing for these companies? These two questions kept us up at night, and at first, they made us angry, and then curious, and then a little excited, and that’s why we created Rippln. → → Notice the structure. Problem exists we noticed it too we created the solution.

The viewer is positioned as a co-discoverer, not a target. We’re on the same team - we both saw the same thing, and we were angry and curious about it together.

I use this same construction in almost every explainer video. “We asked ourselves a question.” “That really bothered us, and that’s why we created [Product].” “We



<!-- Page 12 -->



thought so too.” The phrasing is consistent on purpose. It’s the emotional turn that lets the product feel inevitable rather than invented.

3. One CTA at the end, and only at the end

Most direct response videos stack CTAs. They’ll hit you with one at the halfway mark, another during the proof section, another at the end, another in the P.S. The logic is that different viewers are ready at different moments, so you give them multiple chances to act.

I don’t do that in my explainer videos. I have exactly one CTA, and it comes in the final ten percent of the video. Everything before it is building belief to that single decision point.

The reason is that the Analogy VSL is usually not a standalone closing asset. It’s a top-of-funnel or mid-funnel belief-builder. The viewer’s next step after the CTA is almost always another funnel asset - a webinar, a free trial, an opt-in page - where the actual purchase happens. The explainer’s job is to earn the click, not close the sale. And a single, clean CTA earns the click better than a stacked urgency play.

If your explainer is genuinely the only sales asset in the funnel - if there’s no webinar or long-form sales page after it - you can add a second CTA in Beat 7 or 8. But for a standard funnel position, one CTA is cleaner and converts better.

## Chapter Three

### Choose Your Analogy

T HE SINGLE BIGGEST CREATIVE decision in an Analogy VSL is the choice of analogy. Get this right and the rest of the script practically writes itself. Get it wrong and no amount of clever wording can save you.

Everything in your video sits on top of the analogy. The problem gets explained through it. The product gets introduced in its terms. The emotional journey runs on its rails. If you remove the analogy from the script, the script collapses.

Here are the analogies I’ve used in five of my own explainer videos:

• Pruvit - building a campfire (three fuels: kindling, logs, coal)

• ClickFunnels - the McDonald’s drive-thru (upsell economics)

• Rippln - throwing a stone in a calm lake (the ripple effect)

• Actionetics - walking into a retail store (each visitor as a person)

• Backpack - picking a spouse (a commitment that’s hard to leave)

Notice how different these feel. A campfire is sensory and physical. McDonald’s is economic and transactional. A stone in a lake is visual and mathematical. A retail store is social and intuitive. Picking a spouse is emotional and existential. The analogy sets the tone and personality of the entire video, and each of those five explainers has a completely different vibe because of it.



<!-- Page 15 -->



The Four Tests a Good Analogy Must Pass Before I commit to an analogy, I run it through four tests. I came up with these tests after writing maybe fifty of these videos - the ones that worked all passed all four, and the ones that didn’t work had almost always failed at least one.

Test 1: Familiarity The analogy has to be something every viewer has direct personal experience with.

Not a metaphor from history or philosophy - something from daily life. Campfires, McDonald’s, throwing a stone in water, walking into a store, picking a spouse, driving a car, cooking dinner. These are things that nearly every adult in my audience has done. When I say “have you ever built a campfire before?” almost every viewer can pull up a memory. They’re already halfway into the analogy before I’ve even finished the sentence.

What fails this test? Analogies that require specialized knowledge or cultural fluency. Medieval siege warfare might be a great analogy for something, but only for the subset of viewers who know what a trebuchet is. Cricket references work for a cricket-watching audience and confuse everyone else. Stick to universal human experiences unless you have very high confidence in your audience’s reference set.

The quick test: would a 40-year-old suburban mom, a 25-year-old creator, and a 65-year-old retired engineer all have personal experience with this thing? If yes, the analogy passes.

Test 2: Mechanical Mapping This is the test most analogies fail. The analogy’s moving parts have to correspond to the product’s moving parts - not in a hand-wavy general way, but structurally and specifically.

Look at the Pruvit campfire. I didn’t just say “ketosis is kind of like a fire.” That’s a vague similarity, not a structural mapping. I said there are three types of fuel in a campfire - kindling, logs, coal - and there are three types of fuel in your body - carbs, proteins, fats - and they map one to one. Kindling burns fast like carbs. Logs burn medium like proteins. Coal burns long and hot like fats. The mapping is clean and specific, and it holds up across the whole script.

Same with the ClickFunnels McDonald’s analogy. Drive-thru = capture page. Burger = initial product. Fries and coke = upsells. Total ticket = customer lifetime value.

Four specific components on each side, mapped one to one. I can stay inside the



<!-- Page 16 -->



analogy for the entire script because every part of the product has a corresponding part in the analogy.

If you can’t name at least three clean one-to-one correspondences between your analogy and your product, the analogy isn’t deep enough. Find a different one.

Test 3: Visualization The analogy has to be something a viewer can see in their head without effort.

Physical, concrete, and ideally animated in motion. A campfire catches, grows, and burns. Ripples spread outward on water.

Customers walk through a door. Cars queue at a drive-thru. All of these are kinetic mental images - they’re moving. The viewer sees the analogy play out like a short film inside their head.

Abstract concepts fail this test. “It’s like freedom” or “it’s like empowerment” aren’t things you can see. Institutional processes fail too - “it’s like democracy” doesn’t give you a picture. Find something physical.

The test: close your eyes and describe the analogy playing out in motion. If the mental image is crisp and kinetic, it passes. If it’s static or abstract, keep looking.

Test 4: Emotional Hook The analogy has to evoke something. Curiosity, nostalgia, injustice, longing - something. An analogy that’s clever but emotionally neutral is dead on arrival.

Rippln’s stone-in-a-lake analogy evokes injustice. You’ve been throwing stones for free, making trillion-dollar companies rich, and you’re getting nothing. That feeling of “wait, this isn’t fair” is the emotional charge that carries the viewer into the rest of the video.

ClickFunnels’ McDonald’s analogy evokes envy. McDonald’s is making eight times more than you on every sale, and they’re doing it with a simpler product than yours.

How is that possible? The envy is what makes the viewer lean in. Backpack’s picking-a-spouse analogy evokes dread. The wrong choice haunts you. You’re about to make a commitment you can’t easily unwind. That pit-in-your- stomach feeling is the emotional charge.

If the analogy doesn’t make the viewer feel anything, it’s not doing the work. The analogy carries the emotional charge that numbers and features alone can’t carry.



<!-- Page 17 -->



How I Propose Three Concepts When I’m writing for a new product - mine or a client’s - I never commit to a single analogy without pitching alternatives first. I come up with three analogies from three different domains. Not three food analogies. Not three business analogies.

Three from different worlds so the user can feel the range of possibilities. Each one passes the four tests, and for each one I write the literal first line of the cold open so the user can hear how the analogy would sound in practice. Then I let them pick. The analogy choice is a brand-personality decision, not just a creative one, and that decision belongs to the person whose brand this is.

If they reject all three, I ask what didn’t land and pitch three more. The analogies are cheap to generate. Don’t defend your first three if they’re not working.

## Chapter Four

“KINDA LIKE” BRIDGES T HE PRIMARY ANALOGY IS THE ARCHITECTURE OF YOUR ENTIRE SCRIPT. But inside that architecture, there’s a second layer of analogy work that I use constantly, and it has to be called out separately because it does a different job.

I call it the “kinda like” bridge. I wrote about it in Expert Secrets, and it’s become one of my most useful tools - not just for explainer videos, but for podcasts, webinars, keynote talks, and anytime I’m explaining something complicated to anyone.

Here’s the problem it solves. Every so often, when you’re teaching, you’re going to have to use a word that some of your audience won’t know. Ketones. Algorithm.

Ketosis. Funnel. LTV. Conversion rate. Serotonin. Bioavailability. These are words that mean something specific to people who already know them, and mean nothing to people who don’t.

Here’s what I discovered: if the viewer hits a word they don’t understand, they stop paying attention to everything that comes after it. Their brain is still stuck on the last word, trying to figure out what it meant, and they’re not hearing anything new.

You’ve lost them. The “kinda like” bridge fixes this. Every time you use a word or concept that’s past a third-grade level, you stop and immediately bridge it to something the viewer already knows.

The Ketones Example Here’s the canonical example, from my Pruvit explainer. I was writing a script about a supplement that puts your body into ketosis, and the key mechanism was a thing called ketones. The problem? Most viewers have no idea what a ketone is.



<!-- Page 19 -->



First I tried just using the word. “The goal is to get ketones into your body.” I watched focus groups and early viewers zone out every time that word came up. They didn’t know what it meant, and they stopped listening.

So I rewrote it with a bridge: The goal is to get ketones into your body. Now what are ketones? Well, they’re kinda like millions of little motivational speakers running through your body that give you energy and make you feel awesome.

Think about what that bridge does. The viewer doesn’t need to know the chemistry of a ketone. They don’t need to understand biochemistry or metabolism. They know what a motivational speaker is - someone who gets you fired up - and they can easily picture millions of tiny ones running around inside their body. The mental image does the teaching. The technical term doesn’t matter anymore because the viewer has a working model of what’s happening.

Then, later in the same script, I needed to describe what it feels like to be in ketosis. That’s a vague thing - it feels good, it feels like you’re on, but “ketosis feels good” is a terrible line. So I bridged it: When you’re in ketosis, it’s kinda like the old video game Pac-Man. Remember? You spend the whole game running away from the ghosts. But every once in a while, you get a power pellet and suddenly you get tons of energy, and then you’re actually chasing the ghosts, and you feel ON. That’s what it feels like to be in ketosis.

Now the viewer has a visceral feeling of what ketosis is. They remember the Pac- Man power pellet. They remember that moment of reversal. And they associate that feeling with the product I’m about to sell them.

Two bridges, in the same script, both doing work a biochemistry lecture couldn’t do in an hour. The Formula A “kinda like” bridge has three parts, and all three have to be present: First, a concrete object or scene. Not “a helper” - “a personal trainer.” Not “a connection” - “a bridge between two rooms.” Specific, physical, visualizable.



<!-- Page 20 -->



Second, a visualizable action. Something has to be happening. Ketones run through your body. Pac-Man eats a power pellet. The drive-thru window takes your money. Static images are weaker than kinetic ones.

Third, an emotional register that matches the feeling. If you’re describing something energizing, the bridge should feel energizing. Motivational speakers are loud, fast, and exciting - that matches the feeling of ketones. A quiet still-life painting wouldn’t work for the same concept. Match the temperature.

When to Deploy One Any time your script introduces a term that might confuse some viewers, immediately follow it with a bridge. Specifically:

• Scientific or medical terms (ketones, cortisol, serotonin, bioavailability)

• Technical platform terms (API, algorithm, webhook, integration,

segmentation)

• Financial or business jargon (conversion rate, LTV, CAC, funnel, attribution)

• Branded product features with made-up names (Keto-OS, Actionetics,

Backpack)

• Anything past a third-grade reading level

The rule I give myself: if I’d have to explain the word to my kid, I need a bridge. Bridges Gone Wrong A couple of failure modes to avoid.

First, don’t use a bridge that’s more complex than the original word. I’ve seen writers bridge “API” with “middleware protocol handler.” That’s not a bridge - that’s doubling the problem. The bridge has to be simpler than the word it’s bridging.

Second, don’t use an abstract bridge. “A neural network is kind of like a sophisticated decision engine.” “Sophisticated decision engine” is as vague as “neural network.” Find something concrete - “a neural network is kind of like a stadium of 10,000 people voting on every question.” The bridge has to replace abstraction with concreteness.



<!-- Page 21 -->



Third, don’t use culturally narrow references unless you’re sure about your audience. “It’s like the Fosbury Flop in high jumping” is a great bridge for track-and- field fans and confuses everyone else. If in doubt, reach for universal references.

Oversimplification Is the Point Sometimes people push back on this technique because they feel like it oversimplifies. They’re worried it makes them sound unsophisticated, or that their audience will feel talked down to.

Here’s my counter: the whole goal of an explainer video is to get the viewer to understand something. Oversimplification is the point. The sophistication lives in the product. The explainer video’s job is to get the concept into the viewer’s head in the clearest, fastest, most emotionally resonant way possible. If a bridge makes the idea land harder, use the bridge.

Nobody has ever complained to me that my explainer videos were too easy to follow.

## Chapter Five

### The Signature Phrases

I F YOU READ MY EXPLAINER VIDEO SCRIPTS SIDE BY SIDE, certain phrases come up over and over. They’re not accidents. They’re verbal moves I’ve found work at specific moments, and I’ve used them enough times that they’ve become my voice.

If you’re writing in this framework, use these phrases directly. Don’t paraphrase them. The wording has been sanded down to what works.

the viewer is already in-journey Bridge Pivots (Beat 2) I use these to transition from the analogy to the viewer’s world.

## Chapter Six

### Direct Response In Disguise

F OR ALL THE TEACHING WE’RE DOING IN THESE VIDEOS, the Analogy VSL is still a direct response asset. Someone is paying to run it. It has to convert. It has to move product.

But the direct response work happens quietly. Unlike a hard-sell video that beats you over the head with urgency, social proof, and stacked bonuses, an Analogy VSL does its DR work in the spaces between the teaching. This chapter is about those DR moves - what they are, where they go, and how to deploy them without breaking the teaching frame.

One CTA, Not Three I already mentioned this in Chapter 2, but it’s worth repeating because it’s the most counterintuitive move in this framework.

Most direct response videos stack CTAs. First one at the 50 percent mark, another during social proof, another at the end, another in the P.S. The Analogy VSL has exactly one CTA, in the final ten percent of the video.

Why? Because this video is typically not the final conversion asset. The viewer’s next step after the CTA is usually another funnel page - a free trial signup, a webinar registration, an app download. The explainer’s job is to earn the click to that next page, not to close the sale. A single clean CTA earns the click better than three stacked ones.

If your explainer is genuinely standalone - no webinar, no opt-in, no follow-up sequence - then add a light second CTA in Beat 7 or Beat 8. But for almost every use case, one is enough.



<!-- Page 27 -->



Specific Numbers Everywhere Vague claims trigger skepticism. Specific numbers feel like someone actually counted. Look at the first line of my ClickFunnels explainer: Did you know that it costs McDonald’s $1.91 in advertising just to get you into the drive-thru? And when they sell you a burger for $2.09, they’re only actually making just $0.18.

Those three numbers do more credibility work than a page of adjectives. The specificity tells the viewer I actually looked this up. And because I sound researched, the rest of the claims in the video inherit that credibility. → → → → Use specific numbers everywhere you can. In Rippln: 7 49 343 2,401 16,000+.

Those exponential numbers make the math visceral. In Pruvit: “one-third of your daily nutrients,” “14 grams of protein.” In the ClickFunnels McDonald’s example: $1.91, $2.09, $1.77, $1.32. Resist the urge to round. “Over 10,000 five-star reviews” beats “thousands of reviews.” “$1,891” beats “about $1,900.”

Proof Is Implied, Not Performed Here’s where my explainer videos differ most from the Harmon Brothers style.

Harmon Brothers dramatize proof. They put a character named “Competitor” on camera and show him losing a taste test. They show eggs surviving the mattress.

They stage the social proof reviews as performed moments. My explainer videos don’t do that. When I reference proof, I reference it lightly and mostly during the origin story or future pace beat. Customer reviews and named testimonials are largely absent from my scripts - those live in other parts of the funnel. The landing page carries the reviews. The webinar carries the case studies.

The email sequence carries the social proof stories. The explainer video just points people toward that stack.

The one proof beat I do include in the video is a dream-outcome before/after. In my Pruvit script: Before Keto-OS, they’re not in ketosis. Within less than 60 minutes after taking Keto- OS, your body is in nutritional ketosis.

That’s it. Brief, concrete, stated rather than dramatized. The teaching has already done the heavy lifting.



<!-- Page 28 -->



Risk Reversal Is Often Absent Look through my five explainer scripts and you’ll barely find a guarantee mentioned. No money-back promises. No free-shipping commitments. Maybe a free trial referenced in one line.

That’s because the Analogy VSL is a belief-builder, not an objection-handler. Risk reversal lives on the checkout page. The explainer’s job is to get the viewer excited enough to click; the checkout page’s job is to get them across the finish line.

If your video is the only asset in the funnel - there’s no other page where you can put a guarantee - then add a single risk-reversal line to Beat 8. Otherwise, leave it out. The script runs cleaner without it.

The Offer Is Small and Trust-Forward When I do mention an offer, it’s small and low-friction. “Get your Keto-OS today.”

“Download the Rippln app.” “Let’s create your first funnel.” None of these are hard- close purchase asks. They’re first steps. Free trials, downloads, sign-ups, apps.

The philosophy is that you’ve just spent three minutes teaching the viewer something valuable. They’re in a positive-value exchange mindset with you. Don’t ruin that by asking them to reach for their credit card. Ask them to take the next small step, and let the next asset in the funnel handle the sale.

The Belief Statement Is the Sale The belief statement at the end of Beat 8 is doing more DR work than people realize.

When I say “Because at ClickFunnels, we believe that’s how it should be,” I’m telling the viewer that my brand has a moral point of view about how the problem should be solved. That moral positioning creates loyalty that pure product positioning can’t create.

It also frames the purchase as a values decision, not a feature decision. The viewer isn’t just buying software or a supplement - they’re voting with their dollars for a worldview. That’s how you get customers who stick around, who bring their friends, and who forgive you when you make a mistake. The belief statement plants the flag.

Don’t skip it. Ever.

## Chapter Seven

### The Creative Director Interview

E VERYTHING IN THIS BOOK SO FAR HAS BEEN THEORY. This chapter is practice. When I sit down to write an Analogy VSL - for one of my own products or for a client - here’s the exact workflow I use.

Three phases. Interview, concept, write. Do them in order. Don’t skip phases. A script you wrote before the client picked an analogy is a script they don’t own, which is a script that won’t get approved, which is a script you’re going to rewrite.

Phase One: The Eight Questions I ask these questions one at a time, in this order. I wait for the answer before moving on, and I push for specificity when answers are vague. An Analogy VSL lives or dies on whether the problem is sharp enough to map onto a clear metaphor.

Q1. The product, in one sentence. What’s the product, and in one sentence, what does it do? Not what it is - what it does for the customer. If they give me features, I push for the outcome.

Q2. The customer’s current wrong belief. What does your customer currently believe that’s stopping them from buying or succeeding? What’s the mental model in their head that isn’t working - the thing they think is true that’s keeping them stuck?

This is the most important question in the whole interview. An Analogy VSL is a belief-change engine. If we can’t articulate the current wrong belief, the script has no target. I push hard on this one. I don’t accept vague answers.



<!-- Page 30 -->



Good answers I’ve heard: “They believe they need a website to sell online.” (That was ClickFunnels.) “They believe word-of-mouth is free and there’s nothing they can do about it.” (That was Rippln.) “They believe ketosis only happens after weeks of strict dieting.” (That was Pruvit.)

Q3. The new belief you need them to adopt. What do you need them to believe instead? After watching this video, what’s the new mental model you want them walking away with?

The gap between Q2 and Q3 is the entire job of the script. Every beat exists to move the viewer from one belief to the other.

Q4. The mechanism. How does the product actually deliver on the new belief? What are the two to four steps I can walk the viewer through in the demo beat? I don’t need all the technical details - just the sequence a customer goes through to get the result.

Q5. The natural analogy candidates. This is the fun part. A script in this format is organized around one extended analogy that maps onto your product. Before I pitch three options, do you already have an analogy in mind, or are there any everyday experiences, objects, or processes you’ve caught yourself using when explaining this product to people? Things you’ve said “it’s kind of like...” about?

If the founder already has a strong analogy, I start there. Spontaneous metaphors that emerge from real explanatory work are often the strongest ones.

Q6. The offer and CTA destination. What’s the exact action you want them to take at the end? Give me the URL and the action. In this format, the offer is usually small and trust-forward - free trial, download the app, sign up for the webinar, start your first X. Not a hard-close purchase.

Q7. Funnel position. Where does this video sit in your funnel? Is this the first time viewers are meeting your brand, or have they already opted in, watched something, or attended a webinar? That determines how much proof and risk reversal the script needs to include.



<!-- Page 31 -->



Q8. Length and constraints. Target runtime? Default is 3:30, but anywhere from 2:45 to 4:30 is viable. And - anything off-limits? Topics to avoid, competitor names we can or can’t use, any must-include phrases or taglines?

Phase Two: Propose Three Analogy Concepts Once I have the eight answers, I propose three distinct analogy concepts drawn from different domains. Each one includes the analogy itself, the mapping (how the analogy’s parts correspond to the product’s parts), the emotional hook, and the literal opening line of the cold open so the user can hear how the analogy would sound.

Then the user picks. This is a brand-personality decision, not just a creative one. Three different analogies will produce three different feels, and the decision belongs to the person whose brand this is.

If they reject all three, I ask what didn’t land - tone, mapping, emotional register? - and I pitch three new ones tuned to what they want. The analogies are cheap to generate. I don’t defend my original three if they’re not working.

## Teardown One

### Pruvit

Runtime: ~4:30 · Analogy: Campfire (three fuels = three body fuels) · Nested analogy: Pac-Man power pellet Beat 1 - Analogy Hook Have you ever built a campfire before? If so, did you know that by building that fire, you’ve actually unlocked the power for unlimited energy, fat loss, and focus? Let me explain.

Classic “Have you ever” opener. Promises something unexpected. No product mention. The tease “you’ve actually unlocked the power...” sets a high-stakes payoff expectation without giving away the product.



<!-- Page 36 -->



Beat 2 - Bridge When you build a campfire, there are three types of fuel that you can use: kindling, logs, and coal... So how does knowing that give you unlimited energy, fat loss, and focus? Well, did you know that there are also three types of fuel that run your body?

The bridge makes the mapping explicit: three campfire fuels map onto three body fuels. The structural parallel is doing the teaching work.

Beat 3 - Problem Recognition (extended) The carbs, proteins, fats explanation becomes the problem recognition beat. Most people never reach the fat-burning stage - they keep throwing kindling (carbs) on the fire and never get to the coal (fats). The problem is framed entirely inside the campfire metaphor.

Beat 4 - Friction Moment So why is it so hard? One-sentence friction beat, followed by an explanation of why biohacking ketosis takes weeks.

Beat 5 - Origin Story Well, that’s a question we asked ourselves a few years ago, and at first, it seemed like an impossible question. But recently, some scientists we work with have stumbled upon a new formula that actually made it possible. We call this new formula the Ketone Operating System, or Keto-OS for short.

Signature “we asked ourselves” construction. Note the humility move - “it seemed like an impossible question.” Positions the product as a collaborative discovery with scientists, not a brilliant solo invention.

First “Kinda Like” Bridge - embedded in Beat 3 these little ketones are like thousands of little motivational speakers running through your body.

Canonical bridge example. “Ketones” is bridged instantly before the viewer has time to zone out.



<!-- Page 37 -->



Beat 6 - Demo Check out how this works. Before Keto-OS, they’re not in ketosis. Within less than 60 minutes after taking Keto-OS, your body is in nutritional ketosis.

Before/after framing substitutes for step-by-step demo - the product is a drink, so there isn’t a multi-step usage flow.

Beat 7 - Future Pace with Nested Analogy It’s kind of like playing the game Pac-Man, where most of the game you spend running away from the ghost. But every once in a while, Pac-Man would eat one of those power pellets, and everything suddenly changes. Instead of running away from the ghost, you’re on fire, and you start actually chasing the ghost, and the game becomes yours for the taking. That’s how it feels when you’re in ketosis. Life literally becomes yours for the taking.

Second nested analogy (Pac-Man) does the emotional work the first analogy (campfire) couldn’t do as well. Campfire explained the mechanism; Pac-Man sells the dream.

Beat 8 - Belief + CTA So do you want to experience what it feels like to be in ketosis? If so, get your Keto- OS today and find out what it feels like to unlock your energy, fat loss, and focus.

Pruvit closes without the full “at [Brand], we believe” statement - an abbreviated close. Notable variance from the pattern.

What This Teardown Teaches The primary analogy can be fully extended across multiple beats. “Kinda like” bridges land best on the most technical terms (ketones). A nested secondary analogy in Beat 7 can do emotional work the primary analogy can’t. Pruvit is the longest of my five scripts and shows how the structure scales without breaking.

Textbook belief and CTA close. Notice how the verb “ripple” ties the CTA back to the analogy - the action is literally the analogy in motion.

What This Teardown Teaches Specific numbers (7 to 49 to 343 to 2,401 to 16,000) make exponential math visceral.
