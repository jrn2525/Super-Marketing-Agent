## Introduction

### What The Soap Opera Sequence Actually Is M Ost Email Sequences Are Boring.

They follow a predictable format. Welcome email. Value email. Pitch email. Follow-up. Bump. The reader sees the pattern immediately, and the moment they do, they start deleting without reading.

A Soap Opera Sequence is something different. It is a story that spans multiple emails - a continuing narrative with characters, tension, open questions, and genuine emotional stakes. The reader is not being marketed to. They are being drawn into something. And because the story is never fully resolved in any single email, they keep coming back.

Russell Brunson learned this concept from his friend André Chaperon, and has credited it as one of the most important things he ever learned about email marketing: "For years I tried dozens of ways to build a quick relationship with people after they joined my list, but I always struggled until I learned a concept called the Soap Opera Sequence from one of my friends, André Chaperon. The goal is to create an instant bond between your Attractive Character and the person reading the email. If your first email is boring, you're done. They probably won't open the next one. But if you give them something interesting and hook them with an open storyline in the first email, then they will look forward to the next one, and the next, and the next."

- Russell Brunson, DotCom Secrets

That bond - built story by story, email by email - is what makes the Soap Opera Sequence different from everything else. It is not a drip campaign. It is a relationship.

Where It Fits in Your Funnel The Soap Opera Sequence is the first thing a new subscriber experiences after joining your list. Before Daily Seinfeld Emails. Before broadcasts. Before any of the ongoing relationship-building you will do later. The SOS is the introduction.



<!-- Page 6 -->



Think of it like a TV show. The SOS is the pilot episode. Its job is to make the audience fall in love with the characters so completely that they have no choice but to come back for the rest of the season.

InTraffic Secrets, Russell explains the transition: once a subscriber completes all the Soap Opera Sequences inside the value ladder, they are moved to the daily broadcast list. "In a perfect world, we'd have follow-up funnels that lasted 40 years, but the reality is that most good follow-up funnels are effective for 30-60 days." The SOS is the active selling phase. Daily Seinfeld Emails are what sustain the relationship indefinitely after that.

A Note on Length and Flexibility The five-email structure in this guide is the foundational blueprint - the simplest version that works. But as André Chaperon demonstrates throughout his Autoresponder Madness course, sequences can run three, seven, ten, or even thirty or forty emails.

The number of emails matters less than the principles governing them. Open loops pull readers forward. Nested loops create compounding tension. The Enhancement Frame introduces your offer without feeling like a pitch. The story structure determines whether someone opens your next email or not.

Master the principles. The length will take care of itself.

## Chapter One

### The Strategy Of Preeminence

B EFORE YOU WRITE A SINGLE word of your Soap Opera Sequence, you need to adopt a philosophy. Not a technique. Not a tactic. A philosophy that governs everything you do.

André Chaperon calls it the Strategy of Preeminence, a concept drawn from marketing legend Jay Abraham. Here is how Chaperon describes it in his own words, from Autoresponder Madness 2.0: "Following ARM strategies presumes that you embrace a certain philosophy - a way of doing business online. It is about delivering REAL VALUE to an audience. Building a long term relationship with that audience. Your crowd. It presumes the attitude that you look at everybody who you want to do business with... and decide you're not going to wait for money to change hands before you start contributing, guiding, counseling, advising and protecting them. Jay Abraham calls this the 'Strategy of Preeminence'. Since 2005 I've based everything I do in business to be inline with Jay's philosophy. The end result is that I create a crowd of followers that will buy my stuff over and over again. (Because I have already earned their trust.) It creates long term customers. And this, my friend, is where all the REAL MONEY is at."

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

Read that again. The decision to start contributing, guiding, counseling, advising and protecting people before any money has changed hands. That is the operating system.

That is what makes everything else work. What This Looks Like in Practice Chaperon illustrates the philosophy with a real exchange from the launch day of ARM 2.0.

A subscriber named Richie emailed asking whether he should wait to buy because other marketers were offering bonuses. Chaperon's reply: "Honestly... I'd suggest wait. I know I'll earn less for saying that - but you get more - and that, to me, is more important."



<!-- Page 8 -->



Richie's response: "I honestly don't know many marketers that would have answered like you did... Its very refreshing to hear the truth. Consider me a customer for life."

Chaperon's reflection: "A customer for life, eh? It's simple things like this. I stepped over $98 to earn a customer for life. Truth is - I would happily forgo $98 all day long to earn customers for life... People are inherently good - and if you treat them with respect and empathy - you will always win in the long run."

He sacrificed a $98 commission to give honest advice. He got a customer for life. That is the Strategy of Preeminence in one exchange.

The Two Goals - In Order Chaperon is explicit about the priority structure of every SOS, and he warns against reversing it: "PRIMARY objective -> build the relationship. Secondary objective -> earn money. Don't mix these two around. Don't be tempted. I promise, if you do, you'll get poor results, and what results you do get... won't be long term."

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

Goal One: Build the relationship with your Attractive Character. The sequence must create an instant, emotional bond between the reader and your persona. You want them to relate to your flaws, your struggles, and your ultimate epiphany.

Goal Two: Move the reader into a funnel. Once that trust is established, the sequence transitions the reader toward the sale - a webinar, a sales page, a free-plus-shipping offer, your core product. This transition should feel like the natural next step in a story, not a pivot into pitch mode.

If your sequence feels like you are selling, rewrite it. If it feels like you are telling a story that naturally leads somewhere, you have it right.

You Are Not a Marketer. You Are a Trusted Advisor. Peter Drucker: "The aim of marketing is to make selling superfluous. The aim of marketing is to know and understand the customer so well that the product or service fits him and sells itself."

The Soap Opera Sequence is the vehicle through which you earn that trust. By the time you introduce your offer, it should not feel like a pitch. It should feel like the obvious next step for someone who has come to see you as the person who understands them best.

## Chapter Two

### Know Your Audience Before You Write A Word

Y OU CANNOT WRITE AN EFFECTIVE Soap Opera Sequence if you do not understand the conversation already happening in your prospect's mind. Chaperon is emphatic about this: "Before I can start writing anything I need to perform some undercover work to determine what my audience wants to achieve. I can't write a word without FIRST knowing this. Knowing what to write about all comes down to knowing your niche. And what the people in your niche want to achieve. The end result that they are after... the messaging and the hot-buttons that they will respond best to."

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

Your backstory, your wall, your epiphany - all of it needs to mirror the journey your reader is on right now. If it does not feel like you are describing them, they will not feel seen. And if they do not feel seen, they will not bond with your Attractive Character.

The Four Core Questions Before outlining your sequence, answer these four questions for your specific audience.

Everything you write depends on these answers. Question 1: What is the end result your ideal customer wants to achieve?

Be specific. Not "make money" but "make $10,000 per month within 6 months so I can quit my job." Not "lose weight" but "lose 20 lbs before my wedding in 3 months." The more precise this answer, the more precisely your story can speak to it.

Question 2: What are the roadblocks standing between them and that end result? These are the specific steps and obstacles. Each roadblock can be broken down into micro-steps. These roadblocks become the leverage points around which your story is



<!-- Page 11 -->



built - because your backstory is the story of encountering the same roadblocks your reader is hitting right now.

Question 3: What can you give them that moves them one step closer? This is the free value you deliver inside the SOS - the insights, stories, and epiphanies that demonstrate your expertise and build trust before money changes hands.

Question 4: What is their core fear? The fear underneath the fear. For someone trying to build a business online: "I've been at this for 18 months and I'm getting nowhere. I'm not tech-savvy. I feel like I'll never crack this." This fear is the emotional engine of your story. When your backstory describes it, your reader feels understood. When your epiphany solves it, they feel hope.

Creating Your Customer Avatar Once you have answered these questions through real research - forums, surveys, social media, direct conversations - you create a detailed Customer Avatar: a fictional person who represents your ideal reader. Give them a name. Know their age, their situation, their fears, their dreams.

"There is absolutely no way you can effectively connect with your target market segment without first understanding and identifying EXACTLY what they are going through. You need to know the 'story' that they are telling themselves. You need to put yourself in their shoes."

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

Roadblock Mapping Once you have mapped the roadblocks, associate specific products - your own or affiliate offers - as the solutions to those roadblocks. This is the Soap Opera Sequence Outline. The roadblocks become the story arc. Each obstacle your Attractive Character encountered is one your reader is facing right now. When you show how you found your way through, the product is not the pitch - it is the logical solution.

### Do This Before You Write

Write out: (1) the end result your reader wants, (2) the specific roadblocks in their way, (3) the free value you will deliver in the sequence, (4) their core fear. This becomes the blueprint your story follows.

## Chapter Three

### The Open Loop: The Engine That Drives Everything

I F THERE IS ONE MECHANISM that separates a Soap Opera Sequence from a standard email sequence, it is the use of open and nested loops. This is the psychological engine that keeps readers opening every email, day after day.

"This is the secret-sauce advanced writing strategy that only NLP gurus, Hollywood series fiction writers, and New York Times Best Sellers know about. You certainly would have never heard about it in the Internet marketing niche. No one talks about this... because no one knows about this. That simple."

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

The Zeigarnik Effect The open loop technique is grounded in a documented psychological phenomenon called the Zeigarnik Effect: people remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than completed ones. There is a need to complete a task once it has been initiated. That cognitive effort is only resolved once the person returns to finish it.

In practical terms: when you leave a story unfinished, your reader's brain will not let them rest until they find out what happens next. They have to open the next email.

Chaperon uses the TV show 24 to illustrate it: "'OMG! Jack Bauer just got shot and killed! I think. That can't be true...' But the episode ends right there. It creates a cliffhanger.

Unresolved emotional tension floods over you. You HAVE TO 'tune in' next week. Your brain won't let ya."

Your emails work the same way. Every email that ends with an unresolved question plants a hook that only your next email can remove.

Mechanism 1: The Pattern Interrupt



<!-- Page 13 -->



The Pattern Interrupt is abruptly ending an email right before the payoff. You build a story to a point of high drama, name a concept or secret, and then cut off before delivering the answer.

Verbatim from Autoresponder Madness 2.0 - end of Episode 1 of Chaperon's dating sequence: Subject:Episode 1 End - ARM Example "Btw - there is something I learned in [Art Of Approaching] that is so powerful, it's literally like DRUGS for women. Anyone can do it. The result: It elevates your 'social level' in their eyes. It's this elevated 'level' that gives you rock star like power. Will tell you about this 'DRUG' effect shortly."

Chaperon names the concept ("DRUGS for women"), builds desire around it ("anyone can do it"), then deliberately withholds the reveal. Now compare this to the end of DCS Local Email 1 in the appendix: Subject:[DCSL] Email 1 End "So... what was that statement? It was only 6 words... and somehow, everyone else in the class missed it... I'll give you that statement tomorrow."

Same mechanic. The reader now has one unanswered question that will not let them rest. They will open tomorrow's email.

Mechanism 2: The Forced Open The Forced Open is what happens at the start of the next email. You close the previous loop - delivering the promised payoff - and then immediately open a brand new loop before the reader can disengage. Every email both closes and opens at least one loop.

Verbatim from ARM 2.0, Episode 2 subject line: "So powerful, it's DRUGS for women!" - the subject line itself is the payoff from the previous cliffhanger. The reader opens because they were promised this. But within the same email, a new loop opens in the P.S.

Watch Russell execute this in DCS Local Email 2: Subject:[DCSL] Ch 2 - the backstory "Ok, so I know that I promised to tell you the 6 words... But before I get to that, I need to give you some backstory. If I don't, then it's going to be hard for you to have the same epiphany that I did."



<!-- Page 14 -->



He acknowledges the promise ("I know that I promised"), explains the delay ("I need to give you some backstory"), and extends the tension one more day. By the end of Email 2 the reader still does not know the six words - but now they also want to know what happened after Russell had the epiphany. Two loops are open simultaneously.

Mechanism 3: Nested Loops Nested loops are multiple open loops running at once, opened before any of them are closed. You might open Loop A in Email 1, Loop B in Email 2, Loop C in Email 3, close Loop C in Email 4, close Loop B in Email 5, and finally close Loop A in Email 6. There are always multiple unresolved threads pulling the reader forward.

Chaperon demonstrates a particularly nasty version in Episode 4 of his dating sequence, where a loop opened in Episode 2 is not resolved but intensified: "This email had one goal, and one goal only. Building massive anticipation! It builds on the loop that was opened way back in email #2... but instead of it getting closed to relieve tension for the reader - it gets heightened instead. Nasty. Oh so very nasty!"

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

Three emails deep and the reader is still waiting for the original reveal. When it finally comes, the satisfaction is enormous - and the purchase link that follows feels completely natural.

The P.S. Method: A Sub-Story Running in Parallel One of Chaperon's most powerful techniques is running an entire sub-story exclusively in the P.S. section of each email. This sub-plot conditions readers to always read to the very bottom of every email.

"What I do is create an entire sub-story (like a sub-plot) that gets played out entirely in the P.S. area of the follow-up emails. These sub-stories live and die purely within the P.S.

Again - conditioning the subscriber to read the entirety of every email."

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

Look at the P.S. sections throughout the DCS Local sequence in Appendix A. Each one either extends the main story, adds urgency, or previews the next email's subject line - creating a small open loop of its own.

The ARM Proof: 30% Conversion on Email 27



<!-- Page 15 -->



At the end of Autoresponder Madness 2.0, Chaperon reveals that the entire 30-day training program was itself delivered as a Soap Opera Sequence. Day 27 - 27 emails deep - he promoted a $127 product with no sales letter and achieved a 30.43% conversion rate.

His explanation for why: "If you didn't already know me when you signed up for ARM - you do now, right? How do you feel about me now? I'm willing to bet that I'm way up there near the top of your list of 'trusted advisers'. There's a 'trust' I've established with you. That, my friend, is what a 'Soap Opera Sequence' does. That's its primary role. To build trust and rapport between your audience and yourself."

By Email 27, the accumulated effect of open loops, nested loops, P.S. sub-stories, and the Enhancement Frame had built a relationship deep enough that a simple email to a $127 product converted nearly a third of the list.

That is what mastering these principles produces over time.

## Chapter Four

### The Enhancement Frame: Selling Without Selling

B ECAUSE YOUR FIRST GOAL IS to build a relationship, you must avoid hard-selling inside the SOS. But you are also trying to move a subscriber into a funnel. How do you do both at the same time?

The answer is what Chaperon calls the Enhancement Frame - a method of introducing paid solutions as optional upgrades to the free value you have already delivered.

"DON'T shove the paid solution down their freakin' throat like you see all the gurus doing to you all day long. You've just given them a totally free solution to help them move forward - no strings attached. This is when you 'introduce' the paid solution. But you do it 'covertly'... Frame it as an 'ENHANCEMENT'."

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

The Three Steps Step 1: Deliver the Free Solution Teach the reader how to overcome a roadblock using a free, accessible method. The email should feel like a gift. They should be better off for having read it, whether or not they ever buy anything.

Step 2: Acknowledge the Limitation Point out gently what the free solution cannot do - not to make them feel bad, but to create honest context for the enhancement you are about to introduce.

Step 3: Introduce the Paid Product as the Enhancement Frame the product not as a necessity, but as the tool that makes the free solution faster, easier, or more powerful. The reader should feel like they are being shown an upgrade, not sold to.



<!-- Page 17 -->



Chaperon illustrates this with a simple example: "Sure - they can build a WordPress blog for free. But they'd be missing out if they didn't get the 'Thesis' premium theme. 'Thesis' will enable them to do this, that, and the next thing. Something that's impossible with a free WordPress theme. See how I'm framing the offer?"

The free solution is real and complete. The product is an enhancement that makes it better. The reader never feels sold to because they were never told they had to buy anything.

Why the Promotion Should Not Look Like a Promotion Chaperon pushes this principle further, describing a specific email in one of his sequences as "a promotion wrapped up as good solid content": "This is why the 'Soap Opera Method' is so powerful. Every email is within the 'context' of one big 'story'. There is a connection to previous emails (context)... and connections to future emails (open loops). Missing an email in the series would have a similar effect as missing an episode of Lost or 24."

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

When every email is a chapter in a story - when the reader is invested in finding out what happens - a promotional link inside that email is not an interruption. It is part of the story.

The offer is the solution to the problem the story has been building toward. The Drucker Standard Peter Drucker: "The aim of marketing is to make selling superfluous. The aim of marketing is to know and understand the customer so well that the product or service fits him and sells itself."

The Enhancement Frame is how you achieve that standard. The product fits so naturally into the story you have been telling that it does not need to be sold. It simply needs to be offered.

### The Test

Before sending any SOS email, ask: does this feel like a story that happens to mention a product, or does it feel like a pitch that happens to have a story attached? If it feels like the second thing, rewrite it.

## Chapter Five

### The Foundational Blueprint: Five Emails That Work

E VERYTHING IN THE PREVIOUS CHAPTERS - the Strategy of Preeminence, the audience profiling, the open loops, the Enhancement Frame - feeds into the actual structure of the sequence. Here is the foundational five-email blueprint from DotCom Secrets, the simplest version that contains all the necessary elements.

Think of this as your starting template, not your ceiling. Russell notes that André Chaperon has built sequences of forty or fifty emails. The five-email version is the minimum viable story arc. What you learn here scales to any length.

### The Key Principle

Each email must do two things: close at least one loop that was opened in the previous email, and open at least one new loop that pulls the reader into the next email. The chain is what keeps them reading.

Email 1: Set the Stage This is the first email a new subscriber receives, ideally delivered immediately after they opt in. Its job is not to teach, sell, or establish credibility. Its job is to create a single irresistible open loop.

Introduce your Attractive Character. Set expectations for the sequence (frequency, format, what's coming). And end with a very specific promise about what tomorrow's email will contain - specific enough that the reader cannot help but look for it.

The loop Russell opens inDotCom SecretsEmail 1: he discovers six words in his economics class that will change everything. He does not tell you the words. He promises them tomorrow.

The loop Russell opens in the DCS Local sequence (Appendix A):"Those 6 words would change my life forever... Anyway, I'll give you that statement (which is the key you need



<!-- Page 19 -->



to become free) tomorrow." Notice both sequences use the same mechanic: a specific number (six words), a massive promise (change your life, become free), and a deliberate delay (tomorrow).

The specificity of "six words" is what makes it irresistible. The reader does not just want to know the concept - they want to know the exact phrase.

Keep this email warm, personal, and fast. Under 300 words is ideal. Tell them what to expect (how often you will email, the subject line format to watch for).

End with a very specific teaser about tomorrow - name something, promise something, withhold the details.

The P.S. is the perfect place to name tomorrow's subject line explicitly. Email 2: Open with High Drama - The Backstory and the Wall Email 2 is where the story really begins. And as Russell explains inDotCom Secrets: "I learned from Daegan Smith that you ALWAYS start any good story at the point of high drama. Most people mistakenly start their stories at the beginning, but usually stories don't get good until the middle, so it's better to start at the good part, and then you can go back and fill in the backstory after readers are hooked."

Drop the reader directly into the most emotionally charged moment of your story. The moment of crisis. The scene with maximum tension. Then go back and explain how you got there.

The backstory should mirror where your reader is stuck right now. If you are selling a business opportunity, go back to when you were broke, frustrated, and doing everything wrong. If you are selling a health product, go back to when you were at your most desperate. You want the reader to think: "That is exactly where I am."

Then hit the wall. The moment where everything seemed impossible. Where you had tried everything and nothing worked. This is the emotional low point of the story - and it is also where your reader currently lives.

End the email at the wall. Do not give the solution. Open the loop: "I finally found the answer. I'll show you exactly what it was tomorrow."

Start in the middle of the story - the moment of maximum drama. Go back and fill in the backstory that leads to that moment.

Mirror the reader's current situation in your backstory.



<!-- Page 20 -->



Hit the wall - make it real, make it painful, make it relatable. End without the solution. Promise it tomorrow. Name the next subject line in the P.S.

Email 3: The Epiphany - The Core Offer Email 3 is the turning point. You close the main loop from Email 2 by revealing the epiphany - the realization or discovery that changed everything for you. And that epiphany directly connects to the product or offer you are moving them toward.

Russell describes it inDotCom Secrets: "By now the reader is so hooked in, they want to know (and hopefully buy) your solution. Most of the time, your epiphany email will lead back to your core offer - whatever you're selling that solves the problem."

The epiphany should feel inevitable. Like: of course that is the answer. You were trying to do everything yourself - of course the key was learning to delegate. You were trying to get traffic through ads - of course the key was a better funnel. The reader should think: "I should have seen that."

Introduce the offer using the Enhancement Frame. Show them what you discovered. Then show them how the product accelerates, deepens, or simplifies that discovery for them.

Close the loop from Email 2 with the promised reveal. Frame the epiphany as a natural "aha" moment, not a product pitch.

Introduce the offer using the Enhancement Frame - free value first, product as enhancement. Open a new loop (hidden benefits) to pull them into Email 4.

Email 4: Hidden Benefits By this point, your reader has heard your story, felt your pain, experienced your epiphany, and been introduced to your offer. If they have not purchased yet, it is not because they do not want the result. It is because they have not fully seen how the result connects to the things they care most about.

Russell explains: "Often, it's the hidden benefits that really grab the reader and move them to take action. So, you want to come up with parables (stories) that demonstrate those hidden benefits. Show them how you can take time off whenever you want, and explain how that makes you feel. When the reader thinks,Man, I want that for myself!That's when they click the link and buy your products."



<!-- Page 21 -->



Hidden benefits are the secondary outcomes - the ones the reader has not thought about yet. Sure, you will make more money - but you will also be able to quit the job you hate. Sure, you will lose weight - but you will also have the energy to play with your kids again. Sure, you will build a business - but you will also stop worrying about the next round of layoffs.

The primary benefit sells the product. The hidden benefit sells the life change. And it is the life change that people actually buy.

Identify 2 to 3 hidden benefits that go beyond the obvious outcome. Use a parable or story to demonstrate each one (show, do not tell).

Make the reader feel the benefit emotionally before they understand it logically. Begin seeding urgency - hint that the offer or opportunity will not be available forever.

Email 5: Urgency and Call to Action Email 5 is the end of the introductory sequence. Its job is to force a decision. Up to this point, you have been building desire and trust. Now you introduce the final element: urgency.

FromDotCom Secrets: "The goal is to give the reader one last push to go take action right now. You do that by adding urgency into the equation and then using a call to action (CTA). Whatever the reason, your urgency needs to be real. Fake urgency will backfire on you, and you'll lose all credibility."

The urgency must be authentic. A webinar starting tomorrow. A discount expiring at midnight. A limited number of spots. A bonus being removed. Whatever the constraint, it must be real.

If your product does not have a natural deadline, create one. A limited-time bonus. A cohort that closes. A special price that expires. Be creative but be honest. Your readers will test your credibility by checking whether the urgency was real.

State the urgency clearly and early in the email. Briefly recap the story arc - remind them of the journey you have been on together.

Make the CTA unmistakably clear. One link. One action. No ambiguity. Close with warmth, not pressure. You are inviting them, not demanding.

## Chapter Six

### Scaling Your Sequence

T HE FIVE-EMAIL BLUEPRINT IS THE FLOOR, not the ceiling. Once you understand the principles, you can expand a sequence to 7, 10, 30, or even 50 emails - and the longer the sequence, the deeper the relationship you can build before introducing the offer.

The Emotion, Logic, Fear Framework InTraffic Secrets, Russell expands on the psychological structure of the full follow-up sequence. He identifies three types of buyers, each requiring a different close: "The most powerful way to get someone to take action is by using emotion. People buy things emotionally and then try to justify their purchase logically... That's why most of our ads lead with emotional stories. The lead of any sales letter, the stories in my webinars, the first emails in our follow-up funnels... always speak to people's emotions."

He continues: "The next 30 percent of buyers are a little bit harder to convince. They're the analytical buyers. They may have felt something emotionally, but they need to be able to logically convince themselves that the purchase is right for them... So under this top section of the page, I transition my message to speak toward their logic."

And finally: "The last 20 percent of buyers aren't as motivated by emotion or logic, but they are motivated by a fear of missing out (FOMO). The only thing that will get them to take the leap is for them to be afraid that you're going to take it away."

- Russell Brunson, Traffic Secrets

This maps directly to how you structure the arc of a longer sequence: Emails 1 through 3 (or more, in longer sequences): Primarily emotional. Story, backstory, empathy, the wall, the epiphany.

Emails 4 through 5 (or the middle of a longer sequence): Shift to logic. Hidden benefits. Proof. Comparisons. Why this is a smart decision.

Final emails: Urgency and scarcity. Real deadlines. Real consequences. Fear of missing out.



<!-- Page 23 -->



If you have three emails in a sequence, one covers emotion, one covers logic, one covers fear. If you have ten emails, you might spend the first five on emotion, two on logic, and three on urgency. The ratio is flexible. What matters is that all three closes are covered, because each one will convert a different group of buyers.

Seasons: The SOS as a Series InTraffic Secrets, Russell introduces the concept of treating each Soap Opera Sequence as a "Season" in an ongoing series: "Then we transition to the first funnel we're going to promote in our sequence. We start 'Season Two' of our Soap Opera Sequence with the stories behind the new product/funnel that we're introducing them to. ## Chapter Seven

### Storyboarding Your Sequence

T HE HARDEST PART OF WRITING a Soap Opera Sequence is not the writing. It is the architecture. Knowing how the story connects across emails. Knowing where to open each loop and exactly which future email will close it. Knowing the emotional arc from first email to last.

Most people try to write their SOS email by email, in order, without ever seeing the whole story at once. The result is usually a sequence where the loops feel random, the tension does not build properly, and the offer appears out of nowhere.

Chaperon's solution fromAutoresponder Madness 2.0: "For an SOS I create 7 blank plain-text documents. 1.txt, 2.txt, 3.txt... I then position the emails across my screen... That's my blank canvas. Many times when I write - other than having a rough direction to work to - the story has not yet clearly formed in my mind.

But magical things start to happen when one just 'starts to write'. Story ideas form. Ideas are created. Bits here. Pieces there. Having a 'blank-canvas' that spans the entire screen, allows me to copy/cut bits... and then paste them somewhere else (for later). 'Viewing' my story across multiple days (emails) allows me to 'insert' open loop elements in strategic places. It allows me to create cliffhangers because I can see the BIG PICTURE in one view."

- André Chaperon, Autoresponder Madness 2.0

The Pre-Write Process Before opening a single email file, answer these questions in writing: What is the main loop I will open in Email 1 and close in Email 3?

What secondary loops will I open in Emails 2 and 3 that close later? What is the emotional arc? Where is the lowest point? Where is the turning point?

What is the single offer I am leading toward, and at which email do I introduce it?



<!-- Page 26 -->



What is the urgency mechanism in the final email, and is it genuine? What P.S. sub-story will I run throughout, and how does it end?

Once you have answered these, you have the architecture. The writing becomes much easier because you know where every sentence is going.

Finding Your Attractive Character Story The foundation of any Soap Opera Sequence is the Attractive Character story. This is your origin story - the one that takes your reader from "who is this person?" to "I feel like I know them."

The structure of the origin story, as Russell teaches in the Secrets trilogy: The backstory: where were you before? Establish your life at the point your reader recognizes as their current situation.

The wall: what was the moment of maximum struggle? What had you tried that failed? What made you consider giving up?

The discovery: what did you find, learn, or realize that changed everything? This is the epiphany - and it must connect directly to the product or system you are offering.

The transformation: what did your life look like after the discovery? Be specific. Be visual. Let the reader see the life they want.

The key is specificity. Vague backstories produce vague empathy. Specific backstories produce the feeling of being understood. The reader should think: "He was exactly where I am right now." And when they feel that, the bond is formed.
