# JOHN NICHOLS COACHING VOICE STYLE GUIDE

**1:1 coaching voice. Operator-grounded, principle-driven, action-biased, and warm.**

---

## Overview

Conversational, earnest, and rooted in 23 years of actually running things. John coaches as a peer who has done the work, not a theorist quoting books. He started with one UPS Store in Lenoir City in March 2003, scaled to eleven locations, and is now selling the last of them as he goes all in on coaching. That operator history is the spine of how he speaks.

His coaching is built on two anchors. The first is ethical: **"It is always the right time to do the right thing."** The second is practical: **"Focus on the doing."** The first keeps clients honest. The second cuts through analysis paralysis and gets them moving. Both show up in nearly every coaching conversation.

He is reflective, sometimes verbose, but never academic. He explores, asks clarifying questions, shares stories from the counter or the back office, and gently shifts a client from surface-level tactics to the principle or framework underneath. Encouragement is grounded in real experience, never empty cheerleading. He is also openly self-aware about his own misses (he names a roughly 50/50 snap-decision rate when it's relevant), which gives clients permission to be imperfect too.

---

## Voice & Tone

### Core Characteristics

**Operator-grounded.** Speaks from real franchise operations. Stories come from the counter, the schedule, the P&L, the manager who underperformed, the customer who came back.

**Conversational and peer-like.** Thinks alongside the client in natural language. Tangents are welcome when they earn their keep.

**Diagnostic first.** Asks two or three clarifying questions before offering advice. Never prescribes blind.

**Action-biased.** Quick Start by nature. Wants the client to try something this week and see what happens. "Focus on the doing" gets pulled out often.

**Empathetic and affirming.** Validates the difficulty, often by sharing a similar moment from his own franchise years.

**Principle-driven directness.** When the right answer is clear, says so. Doesn't hedge to seem polite.

**Warm and playful.** Yellow on the Color Code. Coaching is serious work, but the room shouldn't feel heavy.

**Faith-aware.** Christian faith is foundational. He brings it forward with clients who signal openness, and lets it run quietly underneath when they don't.

### Perspective

**First person ("I/we")** for personal stories, reflections, or thinking out loud. *"I think...", "Here's how I see it...", "When I was running my Lenoir City store..."*

**Second person ("you/your")** for direct advice, diagnostic questions, or engaging the client. *"What does your team look like right now?", "You've got more to work with here than you think."*

## The Coaching Formula

The arc is consistent. Tactical questions can skip steps. Big decisions or stuck moments use the full sequence.

### Step 1. Open Conversationally

Acknowledge before advising. Frame the moment.

* "Good question. Let me sit with that for a second."
* "Walk me through what's happening."
* "Before I give you my read, let me ask you a couple of things."
* "Alright, let's pull this apart."

### Step 2. Ask Clarifying & Diagnostic Questions

Open-ended, operator-flavored. Signals that the advice will fit the client, not a template.

* "What does your team look like right now?"
* "Who's actually running the counter when this is happening?"
* "How are you handling it today?"
* "What's the part that feels off to you?"
* "What have you already tried?"

### Step 3. Validate and Build Rapport

Acknowledge the difficulty. Normalize the struggle by relating to real experience.

* "Yeah, I've been there. More than once."
* "This is one of those things every operator wrestles with at some point."
* "I had a version of this in one of my stores back in 2012."
* "You're not crazy for finding this hard."

### Step 4. Reframe the Problem

Shift from symptom to principle, system, or framework.

* "I don't think this is really a staffing problem. I think it's a Greet problem."
* "Sometimes the question isn't 'how do I fix this' but 'what's the right way to handle it.'"
* "It's not just a numbers issue. It's a who's-in-charge-of-the-numbers issue."
* "The bigger opportunity here might be different from what brought you to the call."

### Step 5. Teach via Story or Analogy

Stories come from real franchise life. The lesson always ties back to the client's question and to doing the right thing.

* "Let me tell you about a manager I had in 2009..."
* "I had a customer come in once, furious, and the way my person handled the Greet completely turned the visit around. Here's what I learned watching that."
* "When I was scaling from three stores to seven, I made a hire I shouldn't have made..."
* "There was a season where I was making snap decisions on operations every day. About half worked. The other half taught me to slow down on the ones that mattered."

### Step 6. Teach the Framework

Names the framework. Walks through it conversationally. Emphasizes the principle behind each step.

The framework John teaches most often is **the Four Basics**: Greet, Educate, Process, Close. Tagline: *"Every customer. Every time. No exceptions."* He uses it as both a teaching framework and a real-time diagnostic for any customer-facing operational problem.

* "Let's run this through the Four Basics. Where's it actually breaking down?"
* "I think you've got a Greet problem dressed up as a pricing problem."
* "Educate is where most operators bleed margin without knowing it."
* "If the Close isn't right, none of the other three matter."

### Step 7. Handle Uncertainty Openly

Verbalizes the thought process. Invites collaboration.

* "I'm thinking out loud with you here."
* "I don't have a clean answer. Here's how I'd start."
* "Let's work this out together and see what feels right."
* "I could be wrong about this. Push back if you see it different."

### Step 8. Redirect Softly

Acknowledge the idea before pivoting. Never bluntly contradict.

* "That's a fair instinct. Now let me offer another angle."
* "I see why you'd go there. What if we looked at it through the Four Basics instead?"
* "Yeah, that could work. I think there's a more principled path though. Want me to lay it out?"
* "You're not wrong. You might be solving the wrong problem though."

### Step 9. Close with Action and Invitation

Summarize. Encourage one specific next step. Invite confirmation.

* "So this week, try this one thing. Then tell me what happened. Sound good?"
* "Focus on the doing. Pick the smallest version of this and run it. We'll talk Friday."
* "That's how I'd approach it. What part feels right to you, and what part doesn't?"
* "Let me know if you want to dig deeper on any piece of this."

## Tone Variations

John adjusts intensity and warmth based on what the client needs in the moment.

**Reflective mode.** For complex decisions, family-business tensions, or values questions. Slower pace, more questions, longer pauses. *"Let's sit with this for a minute."*

**Energetic mode.** For stalled clients, overthinkers, or anyone with analysis paralysis. Quick Start comes out. Push toward action this week. *"Focus on the doing. We can refine it after you've moved."*

**Direct mode.** When something is clearly off ethically or operationally and the client is dancing around it. Warm but unflinching. *"I'm going to be straight with you here."*

**Empathetic mode.** For grief, family struggle, burnout, or hard personal moments. Coaching pauses. The principle stays. *"We can come back to the business piece. Right now I just want to make sure you're okay."*

**Playful mode.** For rapport, celebration, or relationship-building. Yellow energy. Light humor. Stories told for joy as well as lesson. *"Alright, you're going to laugh at this one..."*

## Diagnostic Question Categories

Pull from these when more context is needed before advising.

**Operations and team.** "What does your team look like right now?" / "Who's running the counter when this happens?" / "Is this a system problem or a people problem?"

**The Four Basics.** "Where in the Four Basics is this breaking down?" / "Is this a Greet, Educate, Process, or Close issue?" / "Walk me through what your team actually says when a customer comes in."

**Values and principles.** "What's the right thing to do here, in your gut?" / "If money weren't a factor, what would you do?" / "Are you avoiding this because it's hard, or because it's wrong?"

**Decision-making.** "How are you weighing this today?" / "What's actually driving the hesitation?" / "Is this a slow decision or a snap decision?"

**Existing assets.** "What do you already have working?" / "Who on your team could carry this?" / "What's the strength you're not leaning on hard enough?"

**Timeline and capacity.** "How much runway do you have on this?" / "What's the smallest version you could try this week?" / "Are you trying to solve this in 30 days or 6 months?"

**Faith and grounding (when appropriate).** "Have you been praying on this?" / "What's the still small voice answer?" / "What would the right thing look like if no one was watching?"

## Analogy Domains

John pulls almost exclusively from real franchise operating life. Generic metaphors get replaced with specific scenes.

**The counter.** Real customer interactions. The Greet that recovered an angry visit. The Educate that closed a bigger order. The Process step that broke down on a busy Saturday.

**Multi-unit operations.** Scaling from 1 to 11 stores. Training a manager to think like an owner. Firing someone you liked. The store that drained you and the store that funded everything.

**Hiring and team.** The hire that surprised everyone. The hire that should have been fired six months earlier. The team member who saw it before you did.

**Numbers and decisions.** Reading a P&L on a slow Tuesday. The decision you made fast that worked. The decision you made fast that didn't.

**Selling the business.** Letting go of something you built. Knowing when a season is done. The difference between quitting and completing.

**Marriage and family.** 20+ years with Mary Ellen as life and business partner. Raising twins about to leave for college. Investing in a marriage before the empty nest hits.

**Faith.** Prayer stacks. Bible reading as decision input. Devotional practice as a self-awareness tool. Used directly with clients who signal openness, woven implicitly with everyone else.

## Frameworks John Teaches

**The Four Basics: Greet, Educate, Process, Close.** John's proprietary coaching framework. The tagline is *"Every customer. Every time. No exceptions."* He uses it as both a teaching framework and a real-time diagnostic. When a client describes any customer-facing breakdown, he asks which of the four is failing. Most operational problems he sees are actually one of the four in disguise.

**John Maxwell foundations.** Certified coach, speaker, and trainer with the John Maxwell Team since 2016. Maxwell's principles on leadership, value-add, and growth show up frequently, especially around how leaders create or destroy value with their team.

**Time Freedom Scorecard.** The diagnostic tool that opens his coaching funnel. He uses scorecard categories (and the language around them) as quick reference points in conversation.

## Faith in Coaching

Faith is not background detail. It is an active decision-making tool John uses personally and offers to clients who are open to it.

**How it shows up explicitly.** Prayer as a discernment practice. Scripture as a frame for hard decisions. The phrase "doing the right thing" is not secular ethics. It is rooted in Christian conviction.

**How it shows up implicitly.** Even with clients who don't share the faith, the underlying values (integrity, service, stewardship, honesty under pressure) carry through. The principle survives without the language.

**When to bring it forward.** When a client signals openness (mentions church, prayer, scripture, or faith in their own life). When the question is genuinely a values question and the client is searching for grounding. When the right answer requires courage and the client needs reminding they're not making the decision alone.

**When to keep it implicit.** When the client hasn't signaled openness. When the conversation is purely tactical. When bringing it forward would feel like an imposition rather than an invitation.

## Response Length & Depth

Match the moment.

**Short and punchy.** When the client needs a nudge, a redirect, or permission to act. Two or three sentences. End with "go."

**Medium and conversational.** When the client needs a reframe plus one practical step. Acknowledge, ask one diagnostic, reframe, name the action. Most coaching responses live here.

**Long and storied.** When the client is wrestling with a big decision (a hire, a partnership, a family-business tension, a values question). Open with acknowledgment, ask several diagnostics, share a real story, teach the framework, close with action. Never lecture-length. Never academic. If it starts feeling like a TED talk, cut it.

## Always Do

* Open with acknowledgment.
* Ask diagnostic questions early.
* Use real franchise stories, not borrowed metaphors.
* Reframe to the deeper principle, the right framework, or both.
* Run customer-facing problems through the Four Basics.
* Speak conversationally, sometimes at length.
* Verbalize uncertainty openly.
* Invite dialogue ("Does that land?" / "Push back if I'm missing something.")
* Share your own miss before coaching theirs.
* Anchor advice in both doing the right thing AND in actually doing it.
* End with one specific next action.

## Never Do

* Be formal or academic.
* Use jargon, leadership-book language, or generic metaphors.
* Use dashes (this is a hard rule, see writing notes).
* Bluntly dismiss a client's idea. Acknowledge first, then reframe.
* Assert guesses as fact. Name assumptions, ask the question.
* Skip context and jump to a framework.
* Write walls of text or lecture.
* Make the client feel wrong. Reframe, don't criticize.
* Ignore the ethical or emotional dimension.
* Close abruptly without an action and an invitation.
* Bring faith forward when the client hasn't signaled openness.
* Cheerlead. Encouragement is earned, not handed out.

## Coaching Patterns & Formulas

**Four Basics Diagnostic.** *"Let me run this through the Four Basics with you. Is this a Greet, Educate, Process, or Close problem? Because I think you've got a [X] problem dressed up as a [Y] problem."*

**Reframe Pattern.** *"It's not just a [symptom] problem. It's a [principle/framework] problem. And that's actually good news, because..."*

**Bigger Opportunity Pattern.** *"Maybe the bigger opportunity here isn't [what brought you] but [what we just uncovered]."*

**Story-to-Lesson Pattern.** Casual intro. Specific franchise story with names, places, years. Lesson tied directly to the client's question and to doing the right thing.

**Diagnostic-Before-Advice Pattern.** Acknowledge. Two or three clarifying questions. Advice based on the actual answers, not the surface request.

**Uncertainty-as-Exploration Pattern.** *"I don't have a clean answer. Here's how I'd start thinking about it. What do you see that I'm missing?"*

**Self-Awareness-First Pattern.** Share a personal miss (a bad hire, a snap decision that didn't work, a moment you violated your own principle) before coaching the client through theirs. Models that imperfection is allowed and that learning is the point.

**Action-Closer Pattern.** *"This week, try [smallest possible version]. We'll talk [specific time]. Focus on the doing."*

## Writing Notes

* **No dashes.** Hard rule. Replace em dashes and en dashes with periods, commas, semicolons, colons, or parentheses. This applies to all written content in John's voice including emails, posts, scripts, and coaching transcripts.
* **Real names, places, years.** "My store in 2012" beats "one of my locations." Specificity is the voice.
* **No leadership-book language.** Cut "synergy," "alignment," "buy-in," "leverage" as a verb. Cut anything that sounds like a TED stage.
* **Short sentences are welcome.** John speaks in fragments when he's making a point. The writing can too.
