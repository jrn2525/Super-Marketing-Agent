## Introduction

The Emails That Changed Everything When I first started building my email list, I had absolutely no idea what to say.

I would sit down at my computer and stare at a blank screen, paralyzed. What did I have to say that was important enough for someone to want to open? What could I possibly write that was worth someone's time?

So I did what most people do. I started sending content emails. Packed, detailed, educational emails that took me days to write. Tips. Strategies. Step-by-step breakdowns of exactly how I was building my business. I thought that was the answer. If I gave people incredible value for free, they would trust me. And if they trusted me, they would buy from me.

The results were... okay. Open rates were decent. But clicks were low. Sales were even lower. And the more I wrote, the harder it got. I was burning myself out trying to produce content, and the returns just weren't there.

Then I discovered something that changed everything. My emails switched from 100 percent content to 90 percent entertainment and just 10 percent content. And you know what happened? My readership went up. My open rates went up. My clicks went up. And most importantly, my sales went up. Way up.

That's when I realized the truth: after someone has gone through your initial email sequence and bonded with your Attractive Character, they don't need more content.

What they respond to is stories. Entertainment. They respond to, well... nothing. That was the moment the Daily Seinfeld Email was born in my world.

Since then, I've sent thousands of these emails. Some of them have made over $100,000 in a single send. And every one of them follows the same simple framework. It's a show about nothing that sells everything.

In this guide, I'm going to teach you the complete system. You'll learn the framework, the email types, the subject line formulas, the voice principles, and how to build a story bank so you never run out of ideas. I've also included real-world swipe file examples from the masters of this art, including my friend Ben Settle, whose insights from the Email Player's Cookbook have shaped how I think about daily email.

## Chapter Two

The Framework: Hook, Story, Offer Every Daily Seinfeld Email follows the same three-part framework. It doesn't matter what you're writing about, what type of email it is, or how long or short the email ends up being.

These three parts are always present.

### The Framework

Hook. Story. Offer. In that order, every time.

## Part Onethehook

The hook is your subject line combined with your opening line. Its only job is to get the email opened and keep the reader reading past the first sentence.

The subject line is the front door. If it doesn't work, nothing else matters. Nobody sees the story. Nobody gets to the offer. The email is dead on arrival.

I'll go deep into subject lines in Chapter 5. But here's the key principle to understand right now: the subject line should create curiosity, not summarize the email. The moment someone knows what the email is about before they open it, they have no reason to open it.

The opening line is equally critical. Most people start their emails with some version of "I wanted to share something with you today..." That's the written equivalent of clearing your throat before speaking. Nobody wants to read it.

Here's what works instead: drop the reader directly into the middle of something already happening. Action. Dialogue. A scene. A bold statement. Something that makes them lean forward.

Start in the middle of the story. Not before the story starts.

## Part Twothestory

This is the meat of the email. And it should be exactly what it sounds like: a story. Not a lesson. Not a tips-and-tactics breakdown. Not a mini-training. A story.



<!-- Page 12 -->



Something happened. You were somewhere. You saw something, heard something, experienced something, or remembered something. The story can be about your day, your past, your customer, something you read, or a current event. The content is almost irrelevant. What matters is that it's interesting and that it connects to your offer.

Here's what most people get wrong: they tell the story and then they sell. The story and the offer feel like two separate things, bolted together awkwardly at the end. What you want is for the story to make the offer feel inevitable. By the time you get to the offer, the reader should be thinking, "Of course. That's exactly what I need."

The practical rules for writing the story: Keep paragraphs to one to three sentences. White space is your friend.

Write like you talk. If you wouldn't say it out loud, don't write it. Be specific. Use real names, real numbers, real dates, real places.

Build toward something. Don't resolve the tension too early. Stay in the entertainer's seat, not the teacher's seat.

## Part Threetheoffer

Every story must connect to something you're selling. Every single one. This is non-negotiable. If you just send out entertaining emails and don't tie in your products or services, you won't make a dime. Not even if you're the best storyteller in the world. EVERY EMAIL and each story must be tied back into some type of offer for your audience.

The offer doesn't have to be aggressive. In fact, it usually works better when it's soft. Use transitions like "Anyway...", "Which reminds me...", or "Here's why I'm telling you this..." to bridge from the story into the offer.

The offer could be your core product. It could be an affiliate product. It could be a webinar, a blog post, a podcast episode, or a free download. What matters is that there's a destination. Every email should have one link, one action, one place you want your reader to go.

## Chapter Three

The Daily Commitment The name says it all: Daily Seinfeld Emails. Not weekly. Not when you feel inspired. Not twice a month if you're feeling ambitious.

Daily. I know that makes a lot of people uncomfortable. I used to feel the same way. I was terrified of emailing too much. I thought my subscribers would unsubscribe if I emailed them every day. I thought it was rude. Pushy. Too much.

So here's what I did: I tested it. The Frequency Journey I started emailing once a month. My response rates were terrible.

Then I switched to twice a month. My income more than doubled. Then I tried once a week, then twice a week, then every other day. Every single time I increased my frequency, my results went up. Not down. Up.

And what I've found now is this: if I don't email my list every day, I lose money every day. Read that again. I lose money every day I don't email.

Here's why: the people on your list signed up because they want to hear from you. Your Attractive Character has built a relationship with them. When you disappear for a week, two things happen. First, they forget about you. Second, they start hearing from whoever is willing to show up. And that person might be one of your competitors.

Consistency is how you own a mind. Not by being the loudest, but by being the most present. The Entertainer's Solution Here's what makes the daily email sustainable: you don't have to write great content every day. You don't have to produce something brilliant. You don't have to educate anyone.



<!-- Page 18 -->



You just have to entertain them. And here's the key insight: when you're entertaining people, they don't get annoyed at the frequency. Think about your favorite podcast. If a new episode came out every single day, would you unsubscribe? Probably not. You'd be excited.

The Daily Seinfeld Email works the same way. If your emails are genuinely fun to read, people will actually look forward to getting them. Some of my best subscribers have told me that reading my daily email is part of their morning routine. That's the goal. Not to be tolerated. To be looked forward to.

The Ben Settle Model I originally learned the daily email concept fromBen Settle, one of the most respected email copywriters in the world and the author of theEmail Player's Cookbook. Here's what's remarkable about Ben: for the more than 10 years I've known him, he has emailed his list every single day about the same single core product, his Email Players newsletter.

Same product. Same offer. Every day. For over a decade. And he's built one of the most loyal, engaged, and profitable email lists in the marketing world.

How is that possible? Because every single day he shows up with a different story, a different hook, a different angle on the same core idea. The offer never changes. But the email always does. And because the emails are so entertaining, his subscribers don't just tolerate the daily frequency. They pay $97 a month to stay on his list.

That's the power of doing this correctly. You're not a marketer who sends emails. You're a personality they want in their inbox.

What to Do When You're Stuck The most common objection I hear is: "But what do I write about every single day?"

I'll go deep into building your story bank in Chapter 7. But here's the short answer: your life is the material.

Something happened to you today. Or yesterday. Or you remembered something from five years ago that just clicked. You saw something on the news. You overheard a conversation. Your kid said something hilarious. Your dog ate something it shouldn't have.

None of that is an email yet. But all of it could be. The skill is in finding the business parallel. And the more you practice, the faster it comes.



<!-- Page 19 -->



Ben Settle said something I've never forgotten about this. In theEmail Player's Cookbook, he credits this wisdom to his mentor Matt Furey: "Nothing bad happens to you when you're an email marketer. When something bad happens to you, that's an email."

Everything that happens in your life is material. All you need to do is show up and write it down.

## Chapter Four

The Ten Email Types One of the most important things I've learned about daily email is that variety keeps a list alive. If every email you send follows the exact same structure, your readers start to feel like they can predict what's coming. And the moment they can predict you, they stop paying attention.

The solution is to rotate through different email types. Each type has a different feel, a different energy, and a different way of approaching the offer. Together, they give your Attractive Character the range to stay interesting day after day.

I'll start with the three foundational types fromDotCom Secrets, and then add the additional types thatBen Settleteaches in theEmail Player's Cookbook. Where any overlap exists between the systems, I've merged them into a single, clean type.

### The Rotation Rule

Never use the same email type two days in a row. Rotate through different types to keep your list engaged and your Attractive Character multidimensional.

Type 1: The Episode (Story Email) This is the most common type and the one that most closely mirrors an actual Seinfeld episode. You tell a story about something that happened in your life, or that happened to someone you know, and you connect it to your offer. Russell calls this the "Episode" style.

Ben Settle calls it simply storytelling. The story can be big or small. A conversation at the grocery store. A moment with your kid. A call with a client. A mistake you made. A win you had. It doesn't need to be dramatic. It just needs to be real, specific, and tied to something you're selling.

The Episode type is your daily workhorse. When in doubt, tell a story about your day. As Ben explains in theEmail Player's Cookbook: "You can almost never go wrong by telling a story. It can be a long story, a short story, it doesn't matter. Our brains are very much hard-wired for stories."

Type 2: The Epiphany



<!-- Page 21 -->



This type is structured around a mindset shift. You had a belief, the belief turned out to be wrong (or incomplete), and discovering that changed something for you.

The structure is: "I used to believe X. Then something happened. And now I understand Y." The offer is whatever product, tool, or system helped you make that shift, or helps your reader make the same shift.

The Epiphany type is powerful because it doesn't just sell a product. It sells a new way of thinking. And that's the kind of thing people remember.

Type 3: The Soft-Teach (Educational) This type looks like a content email on the surface, but it's not. The key distinction is this: you tell people WHAT to do, but not HOW to do it. The "how" is in the product.

For example, you might write an email about the five things most people do wrong when building a funnel. You name all five things. You give a brief explanation of each. But you don't give them the step-by-step fix. For that, they need your product.

The result is an email that feels valuable and generous, but also creates hunger. The reader knows they have a problem. They know what it is. But they still need you to solve it.

Type 4: The Inspiration Email Sometimes the best email isn't about a business principle at all. It's about a moment, a person, a story, or a piece of art that genuinely moved you. Ben Settle writes about inspiration as a powerful email type in theEmail Player's Cookbook: "Find out what inspires you and then talk about it. It doesn't have to be from a book. Everybody gets inspired by something, and chances are if something inspires you, it's going to inspire other people who are like you."

Inspiration emails work because they connect on a human level that goes deeper than strategy. They remind people why they started. They remind them what they're working toward. And because you're the one delivering that feeling, they associate it with you.

Type 5: The Q&A This type is one of the simplest to write and one of the most consistently effective. Take a real question from your audience, share it, and answer it. As Ben explains: "All you do is take questions people are sending you, and when you do emails this way you will get more dialogue with your market, and those questions now become great fodder for future emails."



<!-- Page 22 -->



Q&A emails build community. They show readers that you're paying attention to them. And they generate their own content, because every question you answer in an email tends to prompt three more questions in reply.

Type 6: The Checklist Ben describes the Checklist type as his go-to email for days when he's low on ideas or energy: "Here's a very easy way to do emails. In fact, if you're stuck for ideas or you're kind of tired, do a checklist. In this checklist you can tell them stuff they're doing wrong. Now they're going to want to know how to do it right, and that's where you kind of segue into your product."

Checklists work because people are conditioned to engage with lists. The format feels accessible and easy to consume, even when the subject matter is complex.

Type 7: Current Events This type connects your email to something happening in the world right now. Ben writes: "Something might have happened today that will inspire a great email for tomorrow. An event can happen that can make buying your product very attractive all the sudden, or it can just make for a good email, because people like to talk about current events."

Current event emails feel urgent and timely. They show your readers that you're paying attention to the world, and they connect familiar events to your unique perspective. The key is always to find the business or marketing angle, not just to comment on the news.

Type 8: Controversy This is one of Ben's signature types and one of the most powerful tools in the email writer's arsenal: "Controversy is great. You want to get people riled up every now and then. The funny thing is, those who agree with you will like you more, and those who don't agree with you, they're still probably going to stick around anyway. Most people shy away from controversy. It'll make you stick out, and people will respect you for that."

Take a real position. Disagree with conventional wisdom. Say the thing that most people in your industry won't say. The readers who agree with you will feel deeply validated. The readers who disagree will keep reading to see what you say next. Either way, you have their attention.

Type 9: The "What It's Not" Email



<!-- Page 23 -->



This is a great email if you want to tap into somebody's sense of curiosity. As Ben explains: "What you do is you write an email where you tell people what the secret or the main benefit you're trying to give in the email is not. You're giving information away. But it's soft information, because you're not really teaching them anything other than you're telling them what something isn't... It really makes people curious about what the answer is."

The rhythm of this type is everything. Short staccato sentences. "It's not this. It's not that. It's not the other thing either." By the end, the reader is desperate for the actual answer, and that's when you point them to the offer.

Type 10: Charitable / Survey Use this type 1 to 2 times per year. Ben has found it powerful for goodwill and engagement: "Every now and then you can survey your list. A lot of people want to give their opinion on this kind of stuff. They're waiting for people to ask them what they think.

It's good for them to tell you, and it's good for you to hear, to have that dialogue with them." The Charitable variant is when you do something for someone else and invite your readers to participate. The key is that both of these types build the kind of trust that pays off over months, not immediately.

## Chapter Five

Subject Line Mastery The subject line is the most important real estate in email marketing. I'm not exaggerating when I say that a great subject line can double, triple, or even quadruple the results of an identical email. The message inside might be perfect. The story might be your best work. The offer might be exactly what your reader needs. But if the subject line doesn't get the open, none of that matters.

Here's the only job the subject line has: create an itch that can only be scratched by opening the email.

Not to describe what's inside. Not to promise value. Not to be clever for its own sake. Just to create enough unresolved curiosity or tension that the reader has no choice but to click.

The 4 to 5 Word Rule Here's something most email marketers don't think about: the majority of people read email on their phones. And on a phone screen, or in a compressed browser window, you typically see only the first 35 to 50 characters of a subject line before it cuts off.

That's roughly four to seven words. So your first four to five words have to do all the heavy lifting. If your hook doesn't land in those first few words, you've lost the reader before they've even decided whether to open.

Test every subject line by reading just the first four words. Do those four words alone make you want to know more? If not, rewrite from the front.

The Lowercase Rule Look at these subject lines from my own campaigns: How my addiction will save you...

The backwards way I wrote this book... Wanna hangout? ...can I read you something?



<!-- Page 25 -->



my weird niche funnel... Notice anything? Most of them start with a lowercase letter. That's intentional. A lowercase subject line signals "this is a person writing to me," not "this is a brand broadcasting at me." It reads like a text message from a friend, not a marketing campaign.

In a crowded inbox full of properly formatted corporate subject lines, a lowercase opener stands out because it looks personal. And personal gets opened.

## Chapter Six

Voice, Structure, and Writing Principles Here's the truth about most of the email marketing advice out there: it focuses almost entirely on strategy and almost not at all on craft.

Strategy tells you what to send. Craft determines whether anyone actually reads it. This chapter is about craft. Specifically, the principles that separate emails that get read, clicked, and acted on from emails that get deleted.

Write Like You Talk This is one of Ben Settle's core principles in theEmail Player's Cookbook, and it's a principle I've embraced completely: "Write like you talk. That means you're probably not going to use perfect grammar all the time. In email you can get away with this all day long. Don't even worry about it. It means you might use your own unique slang. Be yourself! Don't be like your favorite guru or like me or anybody. Be you. Be your unique person. What is it about you that makes you peculiar in the way you speak and talk and act? That's how you are and that's how you should be in your emails."

The emails that perform best are the ones that sound exactly like how you would talk to your best friend. Loose. Casual. Honest. Real.

If you wouldn't say it out loud, don't write it. If you would, go ahead. Never Hard-Teach This is one of the most important distinctions in daily email, and it's the mistake I see most often. Hard-teaching means giving away the full solution in the email. Telling people not just what to do, but exactly how to do it in complete detail.

Ben Settle explains why this is a problem in theEmail Player's Cookbook: "By hard teaching in my emails for five or six years, I'd get a lot of great feedback: 'Oh, I loved that lesson! Man, that's good stuff.' And it's almost like you get a pat on the back



<!-- Page 29 -->



from all your colleagues, but you're not getting a lot of sales, and that's kind of the whole point here. You have to finance your business."

"Give them that little cube of cheese. Let them get hungry on that. Let them get hungry, and then they see where they can buy more. Not only are you doing yourself more of a service, because you'll be making more sales, but you'll be doing them more of a service because people tend to value what they buy over what they get for free."

The email is the sample. The product is the full meal. Give them enough to make them hungry. Don't give them enough to make them full.

Short Paragraphs. Always. This is the single easiest change most people can make to immediately improve their emails.

Long paragraphs are a wall. When readers encounter a wall of text in their inbox, their eyes glaze over. They might skim the first line. They might scroll to the end to see if there's a point. But they don't read.

Short paragraphs are doors. One sentence. Two sentences. Three at the most. White space everywhere. Each paragraph should feel like a short step forward, not a barrier to push through.

Look at the two example emails in Chapter 2. Count the number of paragraphs that are a single sentence long. Most of them. That's not an accident. That's craft.

Be Specific. Always. Vague emails don't sell. Specific emails do. "I got into a lot of debt" is vague. "I maxed out a $25,000 credit card in less than two months buying traffic" is specific.

"We had a great week in sales" is vague. "We did well over six figures in sales this week, without any affiliates, without any product launches, and while I was at the wrestling room most of the week" is specific.

Specificity creates credibility. When you give your reader real numbers, real names, real dates, and real details, the story feels true. And true stories are persuasive in ways that vague ones can never be.

The rule: wherever you're tempted to be general, get specific instead.

Building Your Daily Story Bank The most common reason people stop sending daily emails is not lack of strategy. It's not a poor understanding of the framework. It's this: they run out of things to write about.

They sit down, stare at a blank screen, and think, "What am I supposed to say today?" Here's the thing: you're never actually out of stories. You're just out of the habit of noticing them.

Your Life Is the Material InDotCom Secrets, I give a list of prompts for finding daily email stories. These are the questions I ask myself when I'm stuck: What's going on in your Attractive Character's life?

What happened that's embarrassing? How are you getting through the holiday season? Where are you planning your vacation this year?

What did you buy recently that you regret? What did you buy recently that you just adore? What made you scream with rage yesterday, that you're laughing about today?

What crazy antics did your kid or your dog get into yesterday? What funny thing happened in your past that teaches a lesson?

Any one of these questions can generate an email. The story doesn't have to be profound. It doesn't have to be dramatic. It just has to be real and interesting.
