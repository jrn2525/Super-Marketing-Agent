# Slide Design & Presentation Visuals — Reference Guide

> **About this reference:** This document synthesizes presentation-design principles from multiple traditions — Nancy Duarte's publicly-discussed frameworks, Garr Reynolds' *Presentation Zen* approach, Edward Tufte's data-visualization principles, classical design fundamentals (Robin Williams' CRAP), cognitive science (Sweller, Mayer), and accessibility standards. It is intended as a working reference for an AI marketing agent, not a replacement for the original works. For depth, the source books should be consulted directly: *Slide:ology* and *Resonate* (Duarte), *Presentation Zen* (Reynolds), *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information* (Tufte), *The Non-Designer's Design Book* (Williams).

---

## Part 1: The Conceptual Foundation

### The Slide Is Not the Document

The single most consequential mistake in presentation design is treating slides as text documents projected on a wall. Slides and documents are two different media with two different jobs. A document is read silently at the reader's pace; a slide is seen while a speaker talks. When you put document-density text on a slide, you force the audience to choose between reading and listening — and they'll do neither well.

Garr Reynolds calls these hybrid objects "slideuments" — they fail as both slides and as documents. The remedy: if the audience needs detailed reference material, hand it out as a separate document (a "leave-behind"). The slides themselves are visual amplification for the talk, not the talk itself.

### The Two Channels of Communication

Cognitive science (notably Allan Paivio's dual coding theory and Richard Mayer's multimedia learning research) shows that humans process visual and verbal information through two separate channels. Information arriving on both channels simultaneously, when properly coordinated, produces dramatically better comprehension and retention than either channel alone.

This has direct implications for slide design:
- **Picture superiority effect:** images are remembered better than words
- **Redundancy effect:** when slides duplicate the speaker's words verbatim, comprehension *decreases* (the audience tries to do both)
- **Coherence principle:** every element on a slide that doesn't serve the message actively interferes with it

Practical translation: slides should *complement* what the speaker is saying, not transcribe it. The slide carries the visual half; the speaker carries the verbal half.

### Cognitive Load

John Sweller's cognitive load theory identifies three types of mental load:
1. **Intrinsic load** — the inherent difficulty of the content itself
2. **Extraneous load** — wasted effort caused by poor presentation (clutter, ambiguity, distractions)
3. **Germane load** — productive effort spent building understanding

Good slide design minimizes extraneous load so the audience's mental capacity goes to germane load. Every gratuitous animation, every unnecessary bullet, every decorative element that doesn't serve meaning is extraneous load tax.

## Part 2: The Strategic Layer (Before You Design Anything)

### Storyboard Before You Design

Almost all bad decks come from skipping this step. The temptation is to open PowerPoint or Keynote and start building. Resist it. Storyboard first — on paper, sticky notes, or a whiteboard. The storyboard process forces you to think about flow, structure, and what each slide is *for* before you commit to any visual choices.

A useful storyboarding workflow:
1. Write the **Big Idea** (your one-sentence point of view) at the top
2. List the major beats you need to hit, in order
3. For each beat, write what you want the audience to *think*, *feel*, and *do*
4. Sketch one rough thumbnail per slide — title and rough visual concept
5. Walk through the storyboard out loud, talking as if presenting

If the talk works on sticky notes, it'll work on slides. If it doesn't, no amount of design polish will fix it.

### The Big Idea (Duarte's framework, publicly discussed)

Duarte's concept of the **Big Idea™** — a single sentence that articulates your unique point of view and the stakes — predates the slide design phase. Without a Big Idea, slide design has no anchor. The Big Idea answers two questions:
- What is your unique perspective on the topic?
- What's at stake if the audience does (or doesn't) accept it?

Every slide either advances the Big Idea or it doesn't belong.

### The Audience Journey

Before designing, map two states:
- **Current state:** what the audience thinks, feels, and believes right now
- **Desired state:** what you need them to think, feel, and believe at the end

The slides exist to bridge that gap. If a slide doesn't move the audience along that journey, it's filler. Common filler: agenda slides at the start, "thank you" slides at the end, transitional slides that just announce the next section. These don't move the audience — they reassure the speaker. Cut them or replace them with content that does work.

## Part 3: Visual Design Fundamentals

### CRAP — The Four Principles (Robin Williams)

Robin Williams' four principles, originally articulated in *The Non-Designer's Design Book*, form the foundation of nearly all visual design:

**Contrast.** When two elements aren't the same, make them visibly different. Weak contrast (a 14pt heading next to 12pt body text) reads as a mistake. Strong contrast (a 36pt heading next to 14pt body text) reads as intentional hierarchy. Apply contrast to size, weight, color, shape, and position.

**Repetition.** Repeat visual elements throughout the deck — colors, fonts, alignment patterns, icon styles, image treatments. Repetition is what creates the perception of "a deck" rather than "27 unrelated slides." Inconsistent repetition reads as careless.

**Alignment.** Every element on the slide should have a visible reason for its position. Nothing arbitrary. Use grids and align elements to consistent edges or center lines. Misalignment by a few pixels is one of the most common reasons amateur slides look amateur.

**Proximity.** Group related items close together; separate unrelated items. Proximity creates visual relationships — items close to each other are read as belonging together. A label far from its chart, a caption far from its image, breaks the relationship and forces the audience to do extra work.

### Visual Hierarchy

Hierarchy directs the eye's path through the slide. The viewer should know within a fraction of a second:
- What to look at first (the dominant element)
- What to look at second (the supporting element)
- What's reference material vs. what's the main point

Tools for creating hierarchy:
- **Size:** larger elements read first
- **Weight:** bolder elements read first
- **Color:** saturated/contrasting colors read first
- **Position:** top-left and center-of-mass positions read first (in left-to-right reading cultures)
- **Isolation:** an element with whitespace around it reads first

A common error: treating every element as equally important. Without hierarchy, the eye has nowhere to land, and the audience defaults to reading everything left-to-right top-to-bottom — which is slow, exhausting, and often misses the point.

### Negative Space (White Space)

Negative space is the empty area around content. It is a design element, not waste. Generous negative space:
- Increases legibility
- Raises perceived quality (sparse is sophisticated; cluttered is cheap)
- Directs attention by isolating the focal element
- Reduces cognitive load

The typical instinct is to fill empty space. Resist it. If a slide feels sparse, it's probably right. If a slide feels full, it's probably overloaded.

### Signal-to-Noise Ratio

Every element on a slide is either signal (carries meaning) or noise (doesn't). The goal is to maximize signal and ruthlessly cut noise. Common noise:
- Decorative drop shadows, bevels, gradients that don't aid comprehension
- Background images behind text that reduce legibility
- Logos repeated on every slide
- Page numbers and dates the audience doesn't need
- Icons that decorate rather than inform
- Chart elements that don't carry data (3D effects, redundant gridlines, legends when direct labeling would work)

Tufte's related principle: **maximize the data-ink ratio** — the proportion of ink that actually represents data. Erase non-data ink within reason. Erase redundant data ink within reason.

### The Glance Test (Duarte's heuristic, publicly discussed)

A widely-cited Duarte heuristic: every slide should communicate its core meaning in three seconds or less. If a viewer needs longer than that to figure out what they're looking at, the slide is doing too much work. Apply the test by showing the slide briefly and asking what it said. If the answer is fuzzy or wrong, redesign.

## Part 4: Typography

### Type Choice

For projected slides, prefer **sans-serif typefaces** for body content. Serifs are designed for high-resolution print at close reading distance; they degrade at projection distances and lower resolutions. Common solid choices: Helvetica, Arial, Calibri, Lato, Open Sans, Source Sans Pro, Montserrat.

Serifs can work well for display headlines (large size mitigates the legibility issue) and for emotional/editorial tone. Mixing one serif (display) with one sans-serif (body) is a classic, reliable pairing.

Limit a deck to two typefaces — one for headings, one for body. Three at most. More than that fragments the visual identity.

### Type Size

The minimum readable size depends on the room and the projection setup, but practical floor levels:
- **Body text:** 24pt minimum (often larger)
- **Headings:** 36pt minimum
- **Display/key statements:** 48pt and up

If you find yourself dropping below 24pt to fit content on a slide, the slide has too much content. Cut content; don't shrink type.

Guy Kawasaki's "10/20/30 rule" suggests no more than 10 slides, 20 minutes, and minimum 30pt font for venture pitches — debatable as a universal rule, but the 30pt floor is a useful disciplinary forcing function.

### Hierarchy in Type

Establish clear type hierarchy:
- **Heading:** largest, boldest
- **Subheading:** noticeably smaller, often lighter weight
- **Body:** smallest, regular weight
- **Caption/footnote:** smaller still, often lighter color

The size jumps between levels should be obvious — typically 1.4–2× between adjacent levels. Subtle differences (16pt vs. 18pt) read as mistakes; bold differences (16pt vs. 32pt) read as intentional.

### Line Spacing and Reading Comfort

Default line spacing in slide software is usually too tight for projected reading. Increase line height to roughly 1.2–1.5× the font size. For body text, 1.4 is a reliable starting point. Tight line spacing makes blocks of text feel dense and difficult; generous line spacing feels open and inviting.

Avoid all-caps for body text — it slows reading by ~10%. All-caps works for short labels, headers, and emphasis.

## Part 5: Color

### Color as Meaning, Not Decoration

Color in slides should always be doing work — communicating hierarchy, indicating relationships, reinforcing brand, or directing attention. Decorative color (just to add visual interest) usually adds noise.

### The 60-30-10 Rule

A widely-used guideline for color proportion in any composition:
- **60%** dominant color (often a neutral background)
- **30%** secondary color (supporting structural elements)
- **10%** accent color (highlights, calls-to-action, focal elements)

The accent color should be the one that draws the eye to what matters. Reverse the proportions and slides feel chaotic.

### Color and Accessibility

Color blindness affects roughly 8% of men and 0.5% of women. The most common form (red-green) makes it impossible to distinguish red bars from green bars in a chart — a brutal failure mode for any data slide. Mitigations:
- Don't use color alone to convey meaning. Pair color with shape, position, label, or pattern.
- Use color-blind-safe palettes (ColorBrewer is the standard reference)
- Test slides in grayscale — if information disappears, color is doing too much work

Contrast ratios for text on background should meet WCAG standards: at minimum 4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text. Light gray text on a white background fails this routinely; dark text on dark backgrounds fails too. Free contrast checkers are widely available.

### Color and Mood

Color carries emotional and cultural associations. The associations vary by culture (white = purity in West, mourning in parts of Asia), so be cautious with international audiences. General Western associations:
- **Blue:** trust, stability, calm, corporate
- **Red:** urgency, passion, danger, energy
- **Green:** growth, health, money, environmental
- **Yellow:** optimism, caution, warmth
- **Black:** sophistication, luxury, formality
- **White:** simplicity, cleanliness, space

These are starting points, not laws. Context and combination matter more than any single color's "meaning."

### Brand Consistency

For business presentations, slide color should reinforce the brand identity, not freelance. Use the company's defined palette. If the brand has only logo colors and no presentation palette, build one: 1–2 brand colors + 2–3 neutrals + 1 accent for highlights.

## Part 6: Imagery

### Image Quality and Resolution

Pixelated, stretched, or compressed images broadcast amateurism. Use high-resolution images sized correctly for the slide canvas (typically 1920×1080 or larger for full-bleed). Never enlarge a small image to fill space — find a larger one or change the design.

### Stock Photography Pitfalls

Generic stock photography (the smiling diverse business team in a glass conference room, the lone hand holding a glowing lightbulb, the businessman jumping in front of a sunset) actively damages credibility. Audiences are now sophisticated enough to recognize stock instantly, and it reads as inauthentic.

Better alternatives:
- Original photography of your actual people, products, or work
- Editorial/documentary-style stock from sources that emphasize realism
- Illustration or iconography that doesn't pretend to be reality
- Conceptual or abstract imagery rather than literal-but-fake imagery

### The Image-Text Relationship

Three common patterns for combining image and text:
1. **Image dominant, text small:** the image carries the message, text labels or anchors it. Works for emotional, evocative slides.
2. **Text dominant, image supporting:** the text is the message, the image illustrates or sets tone. Works for content-heavy slides.
3. **Image and text equal:** used for direct comparisons (before/after, this-not-that). Both elements need to land equally.

The mistake is having neither dominant — text and image at equal weight, fighting for attention, with no clear focal point.

### Full-Bleed Imagery

Full-bleed (image extends to all edges of the slide) creates impact and emotional weight. It works well for:
- Section dividers
- Emotional anchors
- Dramatic statements paired with bold text overlay

When using full-bleed with text overlay, ensure adequate contrast: darken the image, use a partial overlay, or place text in a low-detail area of the image.

## Part 7: Data Visualization

### Tufte's Core Principles

Edward Tufte's foundational principles for displaying quantitative information:

**Show the data.** The data should be the visual star, not the chart's decoration.

**Maximize the data-ink ratio.** Every drop of ink should serve to display data. Erase ink that doesn't.

**Erase non-data-ink.** Within reason. Gridlines, borders, backgrounds, and 3D effects usually don't carry data.

**Avoid chartjunk.** Tufte's term for decorative graphical elements that don't communicate information — 3D bars, drop shadows, gradient fills, unnecessary borders, "moiré vibration" patterns, the cluttered default styling that comes out of Excel.

**Show comparisons.** A single number means little; comparisons reveal meaning. Always ask: compared to what?

### Choosing the Right Chart

Match chart type to the question being asked:
- **Comparison across categories** → bar chart (horizontal bars are often easier to read than vertical, especially with long category names)
- **Change over time** → line chart
- **Composition / parts of a whole** → stacked bar (usually better than pie chart, which is hard to read precisely)
- **Distribution** → histogram or box plot
- **Correlation / relationship** → scatter plot
- **Geographic patterns** → map (if geography is meaningful; if not, a regular chart is clearer)

The pie chart is overused. It's only effective with very few slices (2–4), large differences between slices, and when precise comparison isn't required.

### Direct Labeling

Whenever possible, label data directly on the chart rather than using a separate legend. Legends force the audience to look back and forth — extra cognitive work. Direct labels (placing the series name next to the line endpoint, for example) keep the eye on the data.

### Annotation

A chart that shows a number is reference material. A chart that *tells you what to think about* the number is communication. Annotate charts with:
- The headline insight ("Revenue accelerated after Q3 launch")
- Highlighting the data point that matters (color the relevant bar, gray out the rest)
- Brief callouts that explain anomalies or context

In a presentation context, every chart should have a one-sentence headline above it that states the insight, not just the topic. "Q4 Revenue by Region" is a topic. "Q4 revenue grew 40% in Asia, driven by new partnerships" is a headline.

### Small Multiples

When comparing the same metric across several categories or time periods, a series of small charts (small multiples) often outperforms one cluttered chart trying to show everything at once. Each small chart is simple; the comparison happens by eye-jumping across them. This is one of Tufte's most powerful techniques.

## Part 8: Animation and Transitions

### Animation as Function, Not Decoration

Animation should serve communication, not entertain. Useful animations:
- **Build:** revealing elements one at a time as you discuss them, so the audience focuses on the current point rather than reading ahead
- **Highlight:** drawing attention to a specific element (a chart series, a key number)
- **Transform:** showing change over time or process (before/after, step-by-step)
- **Connect:** showing how elements relate (an arrow appearing between two concepts)

### Transitions

Default to no transition or a simple cut. Slide-to-slide swooshes, spins, cubes, and dissolves rarely add meaning and almost always distract. The exception: a meaningful transition (fade-to-black between major sections) can mark structural shifts.

### Animation Restraint

A few specific failure modes to avoid:
- Animating every bullet point in to no purpose
- Animations that take too long (slow audiences down without aiding comprehension)
- "Spin," "bounce," or other attention-grabbing entrances that are louder than the content
- Building text letter-by-letter or word-by-word

The test: does removing this animation lose any meaning? If no, remove it.

## Part 9: Accessibility

Accessible slides are better slides — the constraints that make them readable for people with disabilities tend to make them more readable for everyone.

### Specific Practices

- **Color contrast:** meet WCAG AA at minimum (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text)
- **Don't rely on color alone:** pair color coding with shape, position, or labels
- **Alt text:** add alternative text descriptions for images when the deck will be shared as a file
- **Reading order:** ensure the slide's logical reading order matches the visual order (matters for screen readers)
- **Font size minimums:** 24pt body, larger when possible
- **Caption videos:** any embedded video should have captions
- **Avoid flashing:** rapid flashing (>3 per second) can trigger seizures

### Run an Accessibility Check

PowerPoint and Keynote both have built-in accessibility checkers. Run them before delivering. They catch most common issues automatically.

## Part 10: Practical Workflow

### The Design Process in Order

1. **Define the Big Idea** (one sentence, with stakes)
2. **Map the audience journey** (current state → desired state)
3. **Storyboard on paper** (sticky notes, whiteboard, sketch)
4. **Build a slide skeleton** (titles only, no design yet)
5. **Add content slide by slide** (one idea per slide)
6. **Apply visual design** (template, type, color, hierarchy)
7. **Add data and imagery** (charts annotated for insight, images chosen for purpose)
8. **Edit ruthlessly** (cut anything that doesn't serve the Big Idea)
9. **Run the Glance Test** on every slide
10. **Run accessibility checks**
11. **Rehearse out loud, multiple times**
12. **Get external feedback** before high-stakes delivery

### One Idea Per Slide

The single most effective discipline in slide design. If a slide contains two ideas, it should be two slides. Slides are nearly free; cognitive overload is expensive. A 40-slide deck with one idea per slide outperforms a 20-slide deck with two ideas per slide, even though the content is identical.

This also frees the speaker — pacing can match the audience rather than being forced by the slide.

### Edit by Subtraction

Most decks improve by removing content, not adding it. After a first draft, ask of every element on every slide: *if this disappeared, would the message be weaker?* If no, cut it.

The same principle applies to the deck as a whole: if a slide disappeared, would the talk be weaker? If no, cut the slide.

### The Leave-Behind Document

If the audience needs detailed information that doesn't belong on slides, create a separate leave-behind document — a PDF, one-pager, or report. The slides do their job (visual amplification of the talk); the document does its job (detailed reference). Don't try to make one artifact do both.

## Part 11: Common Failure Modes (Diagnostic List)

When a deck isn't working, check against these:

| Symptom | Likely Cause |
|---|---|
| Audience disengaged | No Big Idea; no audience journey; weak hook |
| Audience reading slides instead of listening | Slideuments — too much text on slides |
| Speaker reading slides aloud | Same problem from the other side |
| Slides feel cluttered | Negative space deficit; too many ideas per slide |
| Slides feel inconsistent | Repetition principle violated; no visual system |
| Slides look amateurish | Alignment issues; weak hierarchy; chartjunk; stock photo abuse |
| Charts confusing | Wrong chart type; no headline insight; legend instead of direct labels |
| Important points missed | No hierarchy directing the eye; no annotation |
| Audience didn't act | Weak call to action; no "new bliss" close |
| Content felt long | Too many filler slides (agenda, transitions, thank-you) |

## Application Notes for AI Agent Use

When a user brings a presentation problem to the agent, diagnose by pillar before prescribing. The Common Failure Modes table above is a quick diagnostic tool. The most common pattern: users blame slide design (visuals) for problems that actually live in strategy (no Big Idea) or story (no audience journey, weak hook, weak close). Fixing the visuals doesn't fix those.

For most business presentation work — sales decks, investor pitches, training presentations, internal updates — the highest-leverage interventions in order are:

1. Force articulation of the Big Idea (often surfaces that the user doesn't have one)
2. Map the audience journey (current → desired state)
3. Cut content ruthlessly (one idea per slide, leave-behind document for the rest)
4. Apply hierarchy (size, weight, color, position) to direct the eye
5. Replace chartjunk with annotated, direct-labeled charts
6. Add a specific call to action and a vivid close

Visual polish (typography, color refinement, custom imagery) matters but is the *last* layer, not the first. Polishing weak content produces a polished deck that still doesn't work.
