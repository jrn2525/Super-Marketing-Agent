## Introduction

### The Pitch That Bombed (And The Lesson That Saved Everything)

I WANT TO TELL YOU a story about the worst pitch of my career. Every year, we put on an event called Funnel Hacking Live. It’s our big event. Thousands of entrepreneurs fly in from around the world. We spend months planning it, and between the venue, the production, the speakers, and everything else, we invest millions of dollars to make it happen.

And every year, I get one shot to make a pitch from that stage. One presentation. One offer. The revenue from that pitch is what makes the entire event financially viable. If the pitch works, the year is a success. If it bombs, we’ve basically spent millions of dollars on a very expensive party.

The first year we did this pitch at our Orlando event, we absolutely crushed it. The offer was a brand new program, positioned as a completely new opportunity. We had a luncheon set up for maybe 250 people. Over 850 signed up. It was chaos. The catering team was scrambling, the sales team was overwhelmed, and my team and I just looked at each other like, What just happened?

The next year in Nashville, we ran the same offer, tweaked the presentation slightly, and crushed it again. Two years in a row. I was on fire.

And then I did what every entrepreneur eventually does. I tried to make it better. I changed the name of the program. I updated the positioning. I adjusted the pitch. And I walked out on that stage the following year feeling confident. This was going to be the biggest one yet.

I delivered the presentation. I made the offer. And then I did what I always do-I looked over at the sales table to see how far the line stretched.

It barely reached the table.



<!-- Page 6 -->



My stomach dropped. I knew immediately. The pitch had bombed. And not just a little-it had bombed. The line that usually wrapped around the room was a trickle. After spending millions on the event, the signature offer had fallen flat on its face.

That night, I sat in my hotel room trying to figure out what went wrong. The content was solid. The production was great. The audience was fired up. So what happened?

Here’s what happened: I had unknowingly shifted from a new opportunity to an improvement offer. Instead of presenting something fresh, exciting, and different, I had repositioned the same program as a better version of what they already had. I had added “ER” words to the pitch. It’s faster. It’s better. It’s smarter. And the moment I did that, I lost the magic.

The next year, I went back to the original positioning. I made it a new opportunity again. I gave it a new name, a new identity, a new energy. And we crushed it. The line wrapped around the room. The sales team was slammed. Everything worked again.

That experience taught me a lesson I will never forget, and it’s the reason I wrote this guide: The difference between an offer that crushes and an offer that bombs is almost never the product. It’s the positioning. And the most powerful position you can ever take is a new opportunity.

In this guide, I’m going to teach you everything I know about new opportunities. You’ll learn why they work, why improvement offers don’t, and exactly how to create and position a new opportunity for your business, no matter what market you’re in.

I’ll show you case studies from billion-dollar companies like Netflix, Dollar Shave Club, and Apple, as well as case studies from entrepreneurs like you who used this exact concept to build movements and change lives.

And I’ll give you the practical frameworks I use in every presentation, every webinar, every challenge, and every video sales letter I’ve ever created. This isn’t theory. This is the playbook.

Let’s go.

## Chapter One

### The Three Types Of Offers

E VERY OFFER YOU’VE EVER SEEN, every product you’ve ever sold, and every pitch you’ve ever made falls into one of three categories. Once you understand these three types, you’ll start to see why some offers explode while others barely get a pulse.

I taught this framework at our ClickFunnels Connect event in Salt Lake City, and I used a car analogy that I think makes this crystal clear.

Type One: Repair The first type of offer is a repair offer. Think about it this way. You have a car, and the engine is broken. A repair offer says, “I’m going to fix your engine.” That’s it. Something is broken, and I’m going to make it work again.

If you look at the history of advertising and entrepreneurship, this was the only kind of offer most people could make for a long time. Back in the early 1900s, the big companies like Sears and Ford were building the products. Entrepreneurs couldn’t compete with them on manufacturing. So what could an entrepreneur do? Fix things. Your fridge is broken? I’ll start a business and fix it. Your plumbing is leaking? I’m your guy.

A lot of people are still in repair businesses today. If you’re a consultant who comes in and fixes someone’s broken funnel, that’s a repair offer. If you’re a therapist who helps fix a struggling marriage, that’s a repair offer. If you’re an accountant who cleans up someone’s messy books, that’s a repair offer.

Repair offers are fine. There’s money in them. But traditionally, there’s not as much money in repair, because you’re always working within the constraints of something that already exists. You’re not creating anything new. You’re just making the old thing work again.

In my own business, repair happens at the very top of my value ladder. When someone pays $100,000 for a one-on-one consulting day with me, that’s often a repair engagement. They bring me their webinar, I look at it, and I fix it. “Here’s what’s broken.



<!-- Page 8 -->



Let’s fix this slide. Let’s rewrite this close.” That’s high-ticket repair. Type Two: Improvement The second type of offer is an improvement offer.

Going back to the car analogy: your car sucks. An improvement offer says, “I’m going to get you a better car.” Not fix the one you have. Get you a different, upgraded version.

This is where about 90 percent of people’s offers land. And honestly, this is what most business schools and marketing courses teach you to do. Find a mousetrap. Build a better mousetrap. That’s the whole philosophy of improvement.

Here’s how you can tell if your offer is an improvement offer. Look at how you describe it. If the letters “ER” show up anywhere in your pitch, you’ve got an improvement offer.

“It’s kind of like this, but better.” “It’s kind of like this, but faster.” “It’s kind of like this, but easier.”

“It’s kind of like this, but cheaper.” Better. Faster. Easier. Cheaper. Smarter. Stronger. Every one of those words is a red flag that you’re making an improvement offer.

Now, improvement offers aren’t bad. They’re better than repair. And there’s definitely a time and a place for them in your value ladder. Most of the offers on the back end of my business are improvement offers. My coaching programs like Inner Circle and Atlas are for people who are already in my world and want to improve what they’re doing. They’ve already bought in. They’re already committed. Now they want to do it better.

But on the front end? Where you’re trying to reach the masses? Where you’re trying to attract people who have never heard of you before? Improvement offers are the wrong play. And I’m going to show you exactly why in the next chapter.

Type Three: The New Opportunity The third type of offer is the new opportunity. And this is where the magic happens.

Back to the car analogy: Repair says, “Your car is broken. I’ll fix the engine.” Improvement says, “Your car sucks. Here’s a better car.”



<!-- Page 9 -->



A new opportunity says, “Why would you drive somewhere? Let’s fly. I’ve got a plane.” That’s a completely different thing. It’s not in the same genre. It’s not in the same category. It’s not a better version of the old thing. It’s a fundamentally different vehicle for getting the same result.

Repair fixes the engine. Improvement gives you a different engine. A new opportunity gives you wings.

And when you position your offer as a new opportunity, everything changes. The price resistance drops. The excitement goes up. The objections disappear. Because you’re no longer competing inside a crowded market of similar options. You’re creating something entirely new.

Where They Fit on Your Value Ladder Here’s something important. Your business will probably have all three types of offers at different points in your value ladder. That’s normal. That’s healthy.

The key is understanding where each type belongs. Front end = New Opportunity. When you’re trying to attract the masses, get attention, and bring new people into your world, a new opportunity is by far the most effective. It’s exciting. It’s fresh. It doesn’t require people to admit they failed at something else.

Middle of the ladder = Improvement. Once someone is in your world and has committed to the new opportunity, your coaching, courses, and programs can help them do it better, faster, and smarter. That’s improvement, and it works because they’ve already bought in.

Top of the ladder = Repair. At the highest levels, you’re doing personalized, one-on-one work. You’re looking at someone’s specific situation and fixing what’s broken. That’s premium, high-ticket repair.

Take ClickFunnels as an example. When we first launched a decade ago, it was a new opportunity. Nobody was talking about sales funnels the way we were. We weren’t saying, “We’re a better website builder.” We were saying, “Websites are dead. Funnels are the future.” That was a completely different vehicle, and it’s what allowed us to build a movement.

But here’s the thing. In today’s market, ClickFunnels is no longer a new opportunity. There are dozens of funnel builders out there. So when we position ClickFunnels now, it’s an improvement offer. “Our builder is faster. Our templates are better. Our editor is easier.”

Those are “ER” words. We’re in improvement territory.



<!-- Page 10 -->



That doesn’t mean the product is bad. It means the positioning has shifted. And that’s why we’re constantly looking for the next new opportunity to lead with on the front end, while ClickFunnels continues to serve people deeper in the value ladder.

The lesson is this: if you’re building your front-end offer right now, the one that’s supposed to bring new people into your world, it needs to be a new opportunity. Period. That’s the only positioning that will cut through the noise and reach the masses.

In the next chapter, I’m going to show you exactly why improvement offers fail at reaching the masses. And once you see it, you’ll never look at your offers the same way again.

## Chapter Two

### Why Improvement Offers Are Killing Your Business

I KNOW THIS IS A BOLD CLAIM. Improvement offers aren’t just “less effective.” They’re actively working against you. And the reason most people can’t see it is because improvement feels right. It sounds smart. “Build a better mousetrap” is practically entrepreneurial gospel.

But there are four fundamental reasons why improvement offers will always underperform new opportunities. And once you understand them, you’ll see why some of your past offers didn’t work the way you expected.

Reason #1: Improvement Is Hard Let me ask you something. Have you ever tried to lose weight? Most people have. And most people have tried multiple times. They’ve done keto. They’ve done paleo. They’ve done Weight Watchers. They’ve hired a personal trainer. They’ve bought the home workout DVDs. And for whatever reason, it didn’t stick.

Now imagine someone comes to you and says, “Hey, we’re going to lose weight together. We’re going to do it this time. It’s going to be great.”

Your first thought isn’t excitement. Your first thought is pain. Because you remember what it felt like the last ten times. You remember the hunger. You remember the soreness. You remember stepping on the scale and seeing the number go in the wrong direction. You remember quitting and feeling terrible about yourself.

All of that pain is sitting in your memory, and the moment someone presents an improvement offer, every bit of that pain comes flooding back. You’re not thinking about the result. You’re thinking about the process. And the process is something you’ve tried before and failed at.



<!-- Page 12 -->



That’s what you’re fighting against when you sell an improvement offer. You’re not just selling a product. You’re asking someone to re-enter a battlefield they’ve already lost on.

With a new opportunity, none of that baggage exists. They’ve never tried it before. They don’t have any negative memories associated with it. They don’t know what the pain looks like because they’ve never walked that road. Everything is fresh, exciting, and full of possibility.

Think about the first time someone presented you with the idea of entrepreneurship. I bet a lot of you experienced that at a hotel room meeting or a home party somewhere.

Someone told you about network marketing or online business, and you were electric. You were like, “Are you kidding me? I can get paid for this?”

That excitement existed because it was a new opportunity. You had no negative reference points. You didn’t know how hard it was going to be. If someone had told you upfront-“Hey, you’re going to be an overnight success, and it’s only going to take you twelve years, and you’re barely going to sleep, and your friends and family are going to question your sanity, and you’re going to come close to bankruptcy three times”-you never would have signed up.

But you didn’t know any of that. It was new. It was exciting. And you said yes. That’s the power of a new opportunity. And that’s what you lose the moment you position your offer as an improvement.

Reason #2: Desire vs. Ambition Here’s a question that will change how you think about your market: what percentage of people are actually ambitious?

My answer: maybe 2 percent. At most. Look at the people in my world. The ones who come to Funnel Hacking Live, who join Inner Circle, who show up to every event and implement everything they learn. Those people are ambitious. They’re the 2 percent. They’re the ones who will buy an improvement offer because they genuinely want to get better at something they’re already doing.

But everyone else? The other 98 percent? They don’t want to improve. They want to change. Here’s the distinction. Ambition says, “I want to get better at what I’m already doing.”

Desire says, “I want something different.”



<!-- Page 13 -->



All people have desire. Every single person on the planet desires a better life, more freedom, better health, stronger relationships, more money. But very few people have the ambition to grind through an improvement process to get there.

When you make an improvement offer, you’re selling to the 2 percent. You’re automatically excluding 98 percent of the world. You might still make money-the 2 percent will buy-but you’re fighting an uphill battle with a tiny addressable market.

When you make a new opportunity offer, you’re selling to desire. And desire lives in everyone. You’re no longer limited to the ambitious few. You’re speaking to the dreams of the masses.

Reason #3: Memories of Poor Past Decisions This one is subtle, but it might be the most important. For someone to accept an improvement offer, they have to first admit they failed.

Think about that. If you’re selling a course on how to build a better email list, the person buying it has to say, “Yeah, I’ve been building my email list wrong.” If you’re selling a program on how to improve someone’s marriage, they have to say, “Yeah, my marriage needs fixing, and it’s partly my fault.”

Nobody wants to admit that. Nobody wants to look in the mirror and say, “I made bad decisions.” It creates an immediate decrease in how they see themselves. And as we’ll talk about in the next chapter, that’s the single most powerful force working against you.

A new opportunity doesn’t require any admission of failure. There’s nothing to feel bad about. You’re not telling them they were wrong. You’re telling them, “Hey, this new thing exists, and you’re going to love it.” Their past is irrelevant. They get a clean slate.

That’s liberation. That’s freedom. And that’s what makes people say yes. Reason #4: Commodity Pricing (The Race to the Bottom) When you’re selling an improvement, you’re selling inside a crowded market. There are dozens, maybe hundreds of other people offering something similar. You’re all swimming in the same red ocean, fighting over the same customers, and the only way to differentiate is usually to drop your price.

That turns your offer into a commodity. And once you’re a commodity, it becomes a race to the bottom. Whoever charges the least wins-and nobody wins a race to the bottom.

My friend Dan Kennedy once told me something I’ll never forget:



<!-- Page 14 -->



“If you can’t be the number one lowest-price leader in your market, there is no strategic advantage of being the second lowest price leader in town. But there is a huge strategic advantage of being the most expensive.”

Read that again. If you can’t be the absolute cheapest, then being kind of cheap gives you nothing. But being the most expensive? That’s a real strategic position.

And you can’t be the most expensive when you’re fighting inside a red ocean of improvement offers. There’s always going to be someone willing to undercut you.

But when you present a new opportunity? You’re creating a blue ocean. There’s no competition because there’s nothing to compare you to. You’re the only one offering this thing. And all price resistance goes out the window because the customer can’t shop around. There’s no “but I can get something similar for less” because nothing similar exists.

That’s where you want to be. That’s where the real money is. And that’s where a new opportunity takes you.

## Chapter Three

### Status: The Only Thing That Makes People Move

A FEW YEARS AGO, my friend Perry Belcher explained a concept to me that immediately changed how I thought about selling. It changed how I structured my offers, how I wrote my copy, and how I interacted with my audience. It’s the single most important concept in this entire guide.

He told me that status is the only thing that causes people to move. That’s it. Status. Not logic. Not features. Not even price. Status.

When someone is presented with any opportunity-your offer, a competitor’s offer, a new job, a new diet, a new relationship-their subconscious mind is running one calculation and one calculation only: Is this thing I’m considering going to increase my status or decrease it?

That’s the question. Every time. In every situation. For every human being on the planet. What Status Really Means Now, when I say “status,” I’m not talking about what other people think of you. I’m talking about what you think of yourself.

Status, as I’m defining it here, is your internal self-perception. It’s how you see yourself. And almost every decision you’ve ever made has been filtered through this lens, whether you realize it or not.

What school did you go to? You picked a school that you thought would elevate your status. Who did you date? Who did you marry? You made those choices based on who you thought would elevate how you see yourself. What car do you drive? What neighborhood do you live in? What books do you read?

All status. And here’s the counterintuitive part. Status works on both ends of the spectrum. Some people drive a Ferrari because it increases their status. Some people refuse to drive a



<!-- Page 16 -->



Ferrari because pulling into their neighborhood in a $400,000 car would decrease their status with their friends and family. Both decisions are driven by the same force.

We are, whether we like to admit it or not, slaves to what we believe other people will think about us.

The Balancing Scale Here’s how status plays out in every buying decision. Imagine a scale in your customer’s brain. On one side is hope of increased status. On the other side is fear of decreased status. Every time someone considers your offer, that scale is tipping back and forth.

On the “hope” side: If this works, I’ll look smarter. I’ll feel wealthier. I’ll be healthier. My friends will be impressed. My spouse will be proud. I’ll feel good about myself.

On the “fear” side: If this doesn’t work, I’ll look stupid. I’ll have wasted money. My spouse will say ‘I told you so.’ My friends will judge me. I’ll feel worse about myself than I do right now.

Your job as the person making the offer is to load up the hope side and reduce the fear side. You do that with compelling stories, social proof, money-back guarantees, risk reversals, and done-for-you options.

But here’s the critical insight: the type of offer you make determines how heavy the fear side starts.

Why Improvement Offers Start with a Heavy Fear Side When you present an improvement offer, the fear side of the scale is already loaded before you even open your mouth.

Why? Because for someone to say yes to your improvement offer, they have to first admit that what they’ve been doing is wrong. They have to admit they failed. And that admission is an immediate decrease in status.

Think about it from the customer’s perspective. They’re sitting there thinking: I know that if I invest $1,000 or $10,000 with this person and it doesn’t work, I’m going to look stupid. I could lose money. I could embarrass myself. My wife is going to see the charge on the credit card, and if this doesn’t work, she’s going to think I’m an idiot. My friends are going to find out. My colleagues are going to judge me.



<!-- Page 17 -->



All of that is fear of decreased status. And you’re fighting against it from the very first moment. Now, when someone invests in my $50,000 Inner Circle program, that money leaving their bank account causes an immediate decrease in status. It’s a big hit. But they’re willing to take it because they believe the end result-what they’ll learn, who they’ll become, what they’ll build-will create an even bigger increase in status down the road.

That’s the gamble every customer is making. And with improvement offers, the fear side is so heavy that most people never tip the scale.

Why New Opportunities Start with a Light Fear Side New opportunities work because they remove the heaviest weight from the fear side of the scale: the admission of past failure.

When you present a new opportunity, you’re not asking anyone to admit they were wrong. You’re not asking them to confront their past decisions. You’re saying, “Hey, this brand-new thing exists, and it’s amazing.”

There’s nothing to feel bad about. There’s no baggage. There’s no shame. The fear side of the scale is light, and the hope side is heavy because everything about this new opportunity is exciting and fresh and full of possibility.

That’s why new opportunities convert at a fraction of the effort. The scale tips in your favor from the start.

What Elevates Status? So what factors actually increase someone’s status? While it’s different for everyone, there are a few that are nearly universal: Appearance of intelligence. Anything that makes someone look or feel smarter. When someone discovers your new opportunity and shares it with their friends, they look like the smart person who found something first.

Appearance of wealth, power, or happiness. People want to look successful, influential, and content. If your offer can create that perception, status goes up.

Physical appearance. Weight loss, fitness, style, grooming. Anything that changes how they look in the mirror.

Style and identity. Think Mac vs. PC. This isn’t about the computer. It’s about what it says about the person using it. Apple users feel a certain way about themselves. That’s status.



<!-- Page 18 -->



When you’re building your new opportunity, think about how many of these status elevators you can stack into the offer. The more ways someone’s status increases by saying yes, the easier the sale becomes.

## Chapter Four

### Why People Crave New Opportunities

W E’VE ESTABLISHED WHY IMPROVEMENT OFFERS FAIL. Now let’s talk about what makes new opportunities so irresistibly attractive. Because it’s not just that improvements are bad. It’s that new opportunities tap into something primal in the way people think and make decisions.

There are four core reasons people crave new opportunities. And once you understand them, you’ll be able to engineer offers that people feel compelled to say yes to.

Reason #1: New Discovery Human beings love to discover things. And more importantly, they love to share things they’ve discovered. When someone finds something new and exciting, the very first thing they want to do is tell someone about it.

Think about the last time a friend sent you a YouTube video and said, “You HAVE to watch this.” That’s discovery at work. They didn’t send it because the video was educational.

They sent it because sharing something cool that no one else has seen yet makes them feel smart. It makes them look like the person who’s “in the know.”

That’s an immediate increase in status. When you present a new opportunity, you’re giving your audience something they can discover and then share with their world. “Hey, have you heard about this thing?” That question is status fuel. The person asking it gets to be the expert, the insider, the one who found it first.

I’ve worked with teams who create viral videos for a living, and they’ve found that videos go viral when they are cool and new. Not when they’re educational. Not when they’re comprehensive. When they’re new. Because people want to be the first to show something new to their friends. Discovery is the engine of virality.

Improvement offers don’t have this quality. Nobody sends their friend a text saying, “Hey, I found a slightly better version of the thing we’ve been doing!” That’s not exciting. That’s



<!-- Page 20 -->



not shareable. There’s no discovery to brag about. Reason #2: No Pain of Disconnect This is the flip side of the “memories of poor past decisions” problem we talked about in the last chapter.

When someone takes an improvement offer, they have to disconnect from what they’re currently doing. They have to look at their current situation and say, “This isn’t working, and I was wrong for doing it.” That disconnect is painful. It decreases status. And it creates massive internal resistance.

With a new opportunity, there’s no pain of disconnect. They don’t have to admit anything was wrong. They can simply move on to something brand new. It’s not that the old thing was bad. It’s just that this new thing is different.

That distinction matters more than you think. Improvement offers sell through the pain. New opportunities sell away from the pain. One forces people to confront their failures.

The other lets them leave their failures behind without a backward glance. Which one do you think creates less resistance?

Reason #3: Dream Replacement One of the biggest reasons people struggle to make changes in their lives is the fear of losing their dreams.

That sounds backwards, but think about it. If someone has been dreaming of building a successful business, and they try one more method and it fails, then their dream might actually die. As long as they haven’t tried, the dream is still alive. The possibility still exists.

But if they try and fail again, they might have to face the reality that it’s never going to happen.

That fear keeps people stuck more than anything else. They’d rather hold on to the possibility of success than risk the certainty of failure.

In Proverbs 29:18, it says, “Where there is no vision, the people perish.” And that’s exactly what happens when dreams die. People stop trying. They give up. They settle.

A new opportunity solves this by giving people a new dream to replace the old one. You’re not asking them to risk their existing dream on one more attempt. You’re giving them an entirely new vision of what’s possible. The old dream can stay where it is. This is something completely different.



<!-- Page 21 -->



That’s incredibly powerful because it removes the fear of finality. Even if the old thing didn’t work, this new thing is a fresh start with a fresh dream.

Reason #4: Greener Pastures We’ve all heard the saying: “The grass is always greener on the other side of the fence.”

Most people treat that as a warning. “Don’t be fooled by the other side. Stay where you are. Be grateful for what you have.”

But as a marketer and an entrepreneur, I look at it differently. The grass does look greener on the other side. And instead of trying to convince people that their grass is fine, or offering to fix their grass, or trying to make their grass slightly greener-why not just take them to the other side of the fence?

That’s where they want to go anyway. Stop fighting human nature and start working with it. A new opportunity is the other side of the fence. It’s the greener pasture. It’s the fresh start that everyone craves. And when you position your offer that way, you’re not swimming upstream. You’re swimming with the current of human psychology.

Mass Movements Are Built on New Opportunities If you want proof that new opportunities are the most powerful force in human behavior, just look at history.

In one of my favorite books, The True Believer by Eric Hoffer, he writes that mass movements don’t appeal to people who want to improve their existing lives. They appeal to people who want to be “rid of an unwanted self.”

Think about that. The most powerful movements in human history weren’t built on improvement. They were built on replacement. A completely new way of seeing the world.

Every major movement, every cultural revolution, every paradigm shift followed this pattern. The leader didn’t say, “Let me help you get a little bit better at what you’re already doing.” The leader said, “Everything you’ve been doing is wrong. Here’s something entirely new. Follow me.”

When Steve Jobs stood on that stage in his black turtleneck, he didn’t say, “We built a CD player that can hold 36 CDs instead of six.” That would have been an improvement offer.

Nobody would have cared. Instead, he pulled out the iPod and said, “2,500 songs in your pocket. This is something completely different.”



<!-- Page 22 -->



That was a new opportunity. And it changed the world. When he came back years later with the iPhone, he didn’t say, “We made a better phone.”

He said, “This is a phone, an iPod, and an internet communicator. It’s not a better version of what exists. It’s something that has never existed before.”

Again: new opportunity. Again: the world changed. Your offer doesn’t have to change the entire world. But it does have to feel like a new world to the person considering it. That’s the standard. Not better. New.

## Chapter Five

### New Opportunities That Changed The World

T HE BEST WAY TO UNDERSTAND the power of a new opportunity is to study the companies that used them to completely rewrite their industries. These aren’t just business case studies. They’re proof of concept for everything we’ve been talking about.

In every case you’re about to read, the winner wasn’t the company that made a better version of the old thing. It was the company that replaced the old thing with something the market had never seen before.

Netflix vs. Blockbuster The year was 2000. Blockbuster Video had over nine thousand stores across the globe, six billion dollars in annual revenue, and roughly sixty thousand employees. They were the undisputed king of home entertainment. ## Chapter Seven

### How To Create Your New Opportunity

B Y NOW, you should be completely sold on why new opportunities work. The question is: how do you actually create one? And if you’re already selling something, how do you reposition it to become a new opportunity?

I’ve broken this down into three steps. They’re simple, but don’t confuse simple with easy. Each step requires real thought. Take your time with these.

Step #1: What Is the Result Your Dream Customers Are Trying to Achieve? The first step is to take your dream customers and look at the result they actually want.

Not your product. Not your method. Not your framework. The result. Start with the three core markets. Is your dream customer trying to get health, wealth, or relationships? Which one of those three desires is driving them?

Then get more specific. If it’s wealth, what does that actually look like for them? Are they trying to quit their job? Hit six figures? Build a business they can sell? Retire early? Create passive income?

If it’s health, are they trying to lose weight? Build muscle? Have more energy? Sleep better? Live longer?

If it’s relationships, are they trying to save their marriage? Find a partner? Be a better parent? Build deeper friendships?

There’s a question from a book called The Dan Sullivan Question that I use all the time. If you could sit down with your dream customer and ask them one question, ask this: “If we were having this discussion three years from today, and you were looking back over those three years, what has to have happened in your life, both personally and professionally, for you to feel happy with your progress?”



<!-- Page 33 -->



That question gets to the root of what someone really wants. Not what they say they want. Not what they think they’re supposed to want. What they actually dream about when they close their eyes at night.

You need to understand that result at a deep level before you can create a new opportunity. Because the new opportunity is the vehicle that gets them there. And if you don’t know the destination, you can’t build the vehicle.

Step #2: What Vehicle Are They Currently Using? Now that you know what they want, you need to understand what vehicle your dream customers are currently using to try to get that result.

Here’s the reality: you are rarely going to be the first solution someone tries. They’ve been on this path before. They’ve tried multiple different vehicles. And for whatever reason, those vehicles haven’t worked.

If they’re trying to lose weight and they find you, they’ve probably already tried keto, paleo, Weight Watchers, a personal trainer, meal delivery services, and maybe even surgery. They’ve been through the wringer.

If they’re trying to make extra income, they may have tried selling on eBay, building an Amazon store, starting a blog, doing freelance work, or joining a network marketing company. None of it stuck.

If they’re trying to grow their business, they’ve probably invested in SEO, paid ads, social media marketing, a sales team, and maybe a few courses or coaching programs.

You need to write down every single vehicle your dream customers have been using to try to get the result they want. And don’t just think about the obvious competitors.

Remember the three categories from earlier: Competing methods - all the other products, programs, and approaches in your market.

Doing nothing - the people who want the result but haven’t taken action. Inertia is a vehicle. It’s the vehicle of “I’ll figure it out someday.”

Doing something completely different - the people who want to make money but went back to school instead. Or the people who want to get healthy but decided to focus on their career. They’ve left the building entirely.

Map all of them. Be exhaustive. Because in the next step, you’re going to replace all of those vehicles with one new one: yours.



<!-- Page 34 -->



Step #3: The Opportunity Switch This is where the magic happens. Now that you know the result they want and the vehicles they’ve been using, you’re going to create a completely new vehicle that gets them the same result in a fundamentally different way.

The mistake most people make here is looking at the existing vehicles and trying to make them better. If they see that their customers have tried keto and it didn’t work, they create “Keto 2.0” or “Keto Made Easy” or “The Better Keto Plan.” That’s improvement. That’s “ER” words. And it won’t work.

Instead, you need to create something that doesn’t even sound like the old vehicles. Something so different that it can’t be compared to what they’ve already tried.

A simple way to frame this is with a Who/What statement: I’m going to teach [your sub-market] how to [your new opportunity].

“I’m going to teach real estate investors how to flip houses on eBay.” Those people are already in a vehicle (traditional real estate investing). You’re taking them to a new vehicle (eBay flipping).

“I’m going to teach people who want to lose weight how to become a Lady Boss.” They’ve tried every diet. You’re not giving them another diet. You’re giving them a new identity and a new lifestyle.

“I’m going to teach experts how to get their message out through funnels.” They’ve been trying websites, social media, blogging. You’re switching them to a completely different vehicle.

Notice that in each case, the switch can happen at different levels. Sometimes you’re switching them within their current market (from one diet to your new approach).

Sometimes you’re stepping back one level and switching them from an entirely different sub-market. We sold our ClickFunnels stuff to real estate investors, stock traders, and coaches-people from completely different niches-because we stepped back to the core desire (making money) and offered a new vehicle for getting there.

The key question to ask yourself is: Can I describe my offer without using any “ER” words? If I can’t say what I do without saying “better, faster, easier, cheaper,” then I haven’t found my new opportunity yet. I need to go back and reposition until I can describe it as something fundamentally different, not just incrementally better.

The Opportunity Stack Once you’ve created your new opportunity and started bringing people into your world, there’s one more concept you need to understand: the Opportunity Stack.

Here’s the rule: you get one and only one opportunity switch per business. That’s your front-end. That’s what brings people in. One switch. One new opportunity. One moment of “everything changes.”

After they’ve made the switch-after they’ve committed to your new opportunity-then you start stacking additional opportunities within that new opportunity.

Let me show you how this works with ClickFunnels. Our one opportunity switch is: “Stop building websites. Start building funnels.” That’s the switch. That’s the front door.

Once someone walks through that door and commits to the world of funnels, then we start stacking: ClickFunnels (the software) is an opportunity within the opportunity.

Funnel Hacking Live is an opportunity within the opportunity. Certified Partner program is an opportunity within the opportunity.

All of these products and programs exist within the universe of “funnels.” None of them require a second opportunity switch. The person has already switched. Now they’re going deeper into the world they’ve committed to.

That’s the stack. And it’s how you build a massive business on the foundation of one single new opportunity.
