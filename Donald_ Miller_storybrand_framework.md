# StoryBrand Framework — Marketing Agent Reference

A structured reference of Donald Miller's StoryBrand methodology for use as agent context. Written as an operational guide, not a reproduction of source material.

---

## Core Premise

Most marketing fails because brands talk about themselves instead of inviting the customer into a story where the customer is the hero. The brand's job is not to be the hero — it is to be the **guide** who helps the hero win.

Two foundational rules drive everything else:

1. **The customer is the hero, not the brand.** Every piece of marketing should be built around the customer's desire, struggle, and transformation — not the company's history, expertise, or self-image.
2. **People don't buy the best products; they buy the products they can understand the fastest.** Clarity beats cleverness. If the customer has to burn calories to figure out what you offer, they leave.

The human brain is wired to (a) survive and thrive, and (b) conserve energy. Marketing that ignores either of these dies. Story is the most efficient compression algorithm humans have for organizing information, so framing offers as story dramatically lowers the cognitive cost of understanding them.

---

## The SB7 Framework

Every effective brand message answers seven questions, in order. This is the StoryBrand 7-part framework (SB7).

### 1. A Character

The customer is the character. Define them by what they **want** — a single, specific, story-relevant desire that your product helps them achieve.

Rules:
- The want must be relevant to the product's value.
- The want should be narrowed to one primary thing per message. Multiple competing wants dilute the story.
- The want should be stated in language the customer would actually use, not internal company jargon.

Diagnostic questions:
- What does the customer want as it relates to our offering?
- Can we say it in under ten words?
- Is the want survival-relevant? (Status, resources, relationships, time, security, purpose, etc.)

### 2. Has a Problem

Stories are driven by problems. Define three layers of problem, plus the villain:

- **Villain**: A root cause to push against. Should be a single, clear, ideally personifiable antagonist (e.g., "hidden fees," "wasted weekends," "burnout culture"). Villains make the problem concrete and give the customer something to oppose.
- **External problem**: The tangible, surface-level problem the product solves (e.g., "my lawn is overgrown").
- **Internal problem**: How the external problem makes the customer feel about themselves (e.g., "I feel like a bad neighbor / I'm embarrassed when people drive by"). This is where most marketers stop short — and it's where most sales are actually made.
- **Philosophical problem**: Why it's wrong on a bigger level. The "this shouldn't be" statement. Connects the purchase to a sense of justice or rightness (e.g., "people shouldn't have to choose between a beautiful home and their weekends").

The internal problem is the most important lever. People buy solutions to internal problems and use external-problem language to justify it.

### 3. And Meets a Guide

The customer doesn't need another hero — they're already the hero, and they're stuck. They need a guide who has been where they're going. The brand positions itself as the guide by showing two things:

- **Empathy**: The brand demonstrably understands the customer's pain. Surface this with statements like "We know how frustrating it is to..." Empathy must be specific to be credible.
- **Authority / Competence**: The brand has the experience, results, or credentials to actually help. Surface this with:
  - Customer testimonials (especially before/after transformation language)
  - Statistics (years in business, customers served, results delivered)
  - Awards, certifications, press mentions
  - Logos of recognizable clients

Avoid overdoing authority — too much makes the brand look like the hero. The guide is competent but secondary; the spotlight stays on the customer.

### 4. Who Gives Them a Plan

A plan removes uncertainty. Confused customers do not buy. Two types of plan can be used, often together:

- **Process plan**: A 3–4 step path from where the customer is to where they want to be. Steps should be simple, action-verb-led, and reduce the perceived complexity of the purchase or onboarding.
  - Example structure: "1. Schedule a call. 2. We design your plan. 3. Enjoy [outcome]."
- **Agreement plan**: A list of promises or commitments that defuse specific fears the customer has about doing business with you (e.g., "No hidden fees. Cancel anytime. Money-back guarantee.").

Plans work because they make the next action feel small and safe.

### 5. And Calls Them to Action

Customers do not act unless challenged to act. A story without a call to action stalls. Use two distinct CTA types:

- **Direct CTA**: An ask for the sale. "Buy Now," "Schedule a Call," "Get Started," "Book Your Estimate." Must be repeated, prominent, and unambiguous. Should appear above the fold on the website and at the end of every email.
- **Transitional CTA**: A lower-commitment ask that builds trust over time. "Download the free guide," "Watch the webinar," "Take the quiz." These reciprocate value and stay in front of customers who aren't ready to buy yet. Transitional CTAs typically feed lead-nurture sequences.

The two are complementary, not interchangeable. Direct CTAs convert; transitional CTAs warm up the not-yet-ready audience.

### 6. That Helps Them Avoid Failure

Stories have stakes. If there's nothing to lose, there's no reason to act. Spell out — clearly but without overdoing it — what the customer's life looks like if they don't solve the problem.

Common failure stakes:
- Continued waste of time, money, or energy
- Lost opportunity / falling behind
- Embarrassment or social cost
- Ongoing frustration or burnout
- Risk to health, family, or business

Best practice: name 3–4 specific negative consequences. Too few feels low-stakes; too many feels manipulative or fear-mongering. Failure stakes work in tension with success vision (next section) — both are needed.

### 7. And Ends in Success

Paint a clear, specific picture of how the customer's life looks after they buy. Vagueness ("be happier," "succeed more") is forgettable. Specificity sticks.

Three categories of aspirational identity to draw on:

- **Status gain**: The customer becomes more respected, admired, accepted, or influential.
- **Completeness / wholeness**: The customer becomes more capable, prepared, equipped, or "themselves."
- **Self-actualization / acceptance**: The customer reaches an ideal self — wiser, more at peace, more loving, more powerful.

The success vision should be visualizable. The customer should be able to see themselves in the after-picture.

## The BrandScript

A BrandScript is the artifact produced by walking through SB7 for a specific brand or campaign. It serves as the source of truth for all downstream marketing copy.

A complete BrandScript has the following filled in:

- **Character**: Customer description + their primary want
- **Problem**:
  - Villain
  - External problem
  - Internal problem
  - Philosophical problem
- **Guide**:
  - Empathy statement(s)
  - Authority / competence proof points
- **Plan**:
  - Process plan (3–4 steps)
  - Agreement plan (commitments / promises)
- **Call to Action**:
  - Direct CTA
  - Transitional CTA
- **Failure**: 3–4 negative stakes
- **Success**: Specific after-picture (status, completeness, self-actualization)
- **Character Transformation**: Who the customer was before → who they become after (e.g., "overwhelmed homeowner" → "confident, in-control homeowner")

Character transformation is sometimes treated as an eighth element. It captures the identity shift the brand enables, which becomes the central message of long-form storytelling.

## Standard Application: The StoryBrand Website Wireframe

The framework prescribes a specific website structure that maps SB7 to layout sections. This is the canonical "StoryBrand homepage."

| Section | SB7 Element | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Header / above the fold | Character + want, hint of problem, direct CTA | Pass the "grunt test" in 5 seconds: what you offer, how it makes life better, how to buy. |
| The Stakes | Failure | Brief reminder of what the customer loses by not acting. |
| Value Proposition | Success (early) | 3–4 specific benefits, ideally visual. |
| Guide section | Empathy + Authority | "We get it" + proof points. |
| The Plan | Process plan | 3–4 simple steps. |
| Explanatory paragraph | Synthesis | Long-form text restating the story; rarely read but boosts SEO and reassures skeptics. |
| Direct CTA (repeated) | CTA | Above fold, mid-page, footer — minimum three placements. |
| Transitional CTA / lead generator | CTA | Free PDF, quiz, video, etc. for not-yet-ready buyers. |
| Junk drawer | — | Less-essential links (about, careers, blog) live in the footer, not the main nav. |

The "grunt test": a stranger looking at the homepage for five seconds should be able to answer (1) what do you offer, (2) how will it make my life better, (3) what do I need to do to buy it.

## Standard Application: Lead Generators (Transitional CTAs)

A lead generator is a free, high-value asset given in exchange for an email address. Effective lead generators:

- Solve a specific, narrow problem the target customer has right now
- Position the brand as the guide (empathy + authority)
- Are easily consumable (PDF checklist, short video, short quiz, short webinar)
- Have a title that promises one clear outcome

Common high-performing lead generator formats:
- "The X Mistakes Most [audience] Make When [doing thing]"
- "The [Number]-Step Checklist to [desired outcome]"
- "The [Free Tool/Template] for [specific result]"
- Quizzes that produce a personalized result
- Interview / webinar with a recognized authority

### Email Nurture Sequence

After a lead generator, a nurture sequence builds trust and moves toward a direct CTA. Typical structure:

1. **Value/nurture emails (5–7+)**: One specific tip or insight per email. Each email should solve a small problem and end with a soft transitional CTA.
2. **Sales emails (3–5)**: Once trust is established, run a focused sales sequence. Standard arc:
   - Email 1: Identify the problem
   - Email 2: Share the stakes (failure)
   - Email 3: Offer the solution and the plan
   - Email 4: Testimonial / case study
   - Email 5: Direct CTA with deadline or scarcity element

## The "One-Liner"

A one-sentence pitch that encapsulates the BrandScript. Used for elevator pitches, email signatures, social bios, and as a recurring tagline. Standard structure:

> **[Customer's problem] → [our solution] → [happy ending].**

Examples of the structure (illustrative, not from any specific brand):
- "Most homeowners dread holiday decorating. We design and install professional Christmas lighting so families can enjoy the season without the ladder."
- "Coaches struggle to scale their impact. Our coaching system gives them frameworks, content, and community so they can grow a profitable practice without burning out."

A good one-liner names the problem (creating intrigue), names the solution (creating clarity), and names the outcome (creating desire).

## Common Failure Modes the Framework Diagnoses

The framework is also a diagnostic tool. When marketing isn't working, check for these:

1. **Brand-as-hero language**: Copy that opens with "We are..." or "Founded in..." or "Our mission..." instead of speaking to the customer's want.
2. **No identifiable villain**: Vague antagonists ("inefficiency," "the old way") instead of concrete, hateable ones.
3. **Internal problem ignored**: Copy only addresses the external problem and misses the emotional stakes.
4. **Too much authority, too little empathy**: Awards and credentials without the "we get it" half. Reads as bragging.
5. **No clear plan**: Customer can't tell what happens after they click "Buy."
6. **Buried or absent direct CTA**: "Learn more" links instead of "Buy now," "Schedule," or "Get started."
7. **No stakes**: Marketing reads as "this would be nice" instead of "this matters."
8. **Vague success vision**: Generic outcome language ("be successful") instead of specific transformation.
9. **Multiple competing offers in one message**: Splits the customer's attention and kills conversion. One BrandScript per audience per offer.
10. **Inside-baseball language**: Industry jargon, internal product names, abstract value props that don't survive contact with a non-expert reader.

## Audience Segmentation

A single brand often needs multiple BrandScripts — one per distinct customer segment, because each segment has a different want, problem, and success vision. Do not try to serve all audiences with one BrandScript; the message will collapse to the lowest common denominator.

When building a BrandScript:
1. Pick one specific customer avatar.
2. Walk through SB7 as if writing only to them.
3. Repeat for each meaningful segment.
4. Use the relevant BrandScript to drive the relevant landing page, email list, and ad set.

## Tone and Voice Principles

- **Clarity over cleverness.** If a sentence is witty but confusing, cut it.
- **Specific over abstract.** "Save 6 hours a week" beats "Save time."
- **Customer language over internal language.** Mirror the words customers actually use in reviews, support tickets, and sales calls.
- **Short over long.** Long copy isn't bad; bloated copy is bad. Every sentence should justify its existence.
- **One idea per asset.** A page, an email, an ad — each should advance one point.

## Workflow for Producing a BrandScript

A practical sequence an agent can follow:

1. **Intake**: Gather information about the business — what it sells, who buys it, what customers say, what problems customers have, what makes the business credible.
2. **Pick the segment**: If multiple audiences exist, pick one to focus on first.
3. **Draft the seven elements**: Fill in each SB7 element in order. Don't skip ahead.
4. **Pressure-test for clarity**: Run the grunt test on the Character + Want statement. Verify the Villain is concrete. Verify the Internal Problem is named.
5. **Write the one-liner.**
6. **Generate downstream assets**: Homepage wireframe copy, lead generator title and outline, sales email sequence outline, social bios, ad copy.
7. **Review for the failure modes listed above.**

---

## Quick-Reference Checklist

For any marketing asset, the agent should be able to answer:

- [ ] Who is the customer in this asset, and what do they want?
- [ ] What villain/problem are we naming? (External, internal, philosophical)
- [ ] How are we showing empathy?
- [ ] How are we showing authority — without making ourselves the hero?
- [ ] What is the plan? Is it 3–4 steps?
- [ ] What is the direct CTA? Is it visible and unambiguous?
- [ ] What is the transitional CTA?
- [ ] What's at stake if the customer doesn't act?
- [ ] What does success look like, specifically?
- [ ] Can a stranger pass the grunt test in 5 seconds?

## Glossary

- **SB7**: The seven-element StoryBrand framework (Character, Problem, Guide, Plan, Call to Action, Failure, Success).
- **BrandScript**: A completed SB7 worksheet for a specific brand or campaign; the source of truth for all messaging.
- **Grunt test**: A 5-second test of whether a homepage clearly communicates what's offered, how it improves life, and how to buy.
- **Direct CTA**: An explicit ask for the sale.
- **Transitional CTA**: A lower-commitment ask (free resource, quiz, etc.) that builds the relationship.
- **Lead generator**: An asset (PDF, video, quiz) given in exchange for contact info, fulfilling a transitional CTA.
- **One-liner**: A single-sentence pitch encapsulating problem → solution → outcome.
- **Character transformation**: The identity shift from who the customer was before the brand to who they become after.
