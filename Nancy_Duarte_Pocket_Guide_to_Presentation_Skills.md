## Agent Reference Summary

This guide presents a compact presentation-building framework built around four pillars:

1. **Strategy** - Understand the audience and plan the transformation you want them to experience.
2. **Story** - Craft compelling content with tension, contrast, narrative, and a clear call to action.
3. **Visuals** - Use clean, simple visuals that amplify key messages and pass a quick-read test.
4. **Delivery** - Practice and deliver with presence, clarity, passion, and intentional pauses.

The central idea is that successful presentations require **preparation, practice, and passion**. The presenter should design the presentation around the audience’s needs, guide them from their current state to a desired future state, and end by showing the improved world created by adopting the idea.

# 1. Strategy

## Understand Your Audience

### Know your audience

Understand your audience’s background, interests, and expectations so you can tailor your content to resonate with them.

### Use the Audience Needs Map

Use the **Audience Needs Map** to overcome obstacles that might keep listeners from wanting to adopt your ideas.

### Define your Big Idea™

Make your central message clear by defining:

- Your unique point of view on the topic
- What is at stake if the audience adopts your idea
- What is at stake if the audience does not adopt your idea

### Plan their journey

Define how you want your audience to be transformed.

Ask:

- What are they currently thinking?
- What are they currently feeling?
- What do you need to say, show, and do to move them?

### Structure your content

Organize your presentation into a logical sequence so the content is easy to understand and remember.

### Add supporting points

Use relevant data and examples to support your main points and make your argument more convincing.

# 2. Story

## Craft Interesting Content

### Start with a hook

Grab your audience’s attention with a compelling opening that makes them want to hear more.

Avoid weak openings such as:

> “I’m excited to talk about...”

### Tell a story

Make your topic more relatable and accessible by sharing:

- An anecdote
- An analogy
- A personal story

### Contrast opposing ideas

Add dramatic tension by comparing opposing ideas, such as:

- Problem versus solution
- Present versus future
- What is versus what could be

### Provide a call to action

Encourage your audience to take the next step by closing the presentation with a clear call to action.

### End with the new bliss

Paint a picture of how much better the audience’s world will be when they adopt your ideas.

Important reminder:

> People will always remember the last thing you said more than what was in the beginning or middle.

# 3. Visuals

## Amplify Key Messages

### Use images

Reinforce key messages with graphics that help your audience “see” what you are saying.

Useful visual types include:

- Photos
- Diagrams
- Charts

### Keep it simple

Pare down your content to just **one idea per slide** to avoid information overload.

### Space it out

Do not fill every inch of the slide. Leave open space around your content and visuals to make your slides look less cluttered and easier to read.

### Be consistent

Use consistent visual elements to create a coherent look and reinforce your brand.

Consistent visual elements include:

- Colors
- Fonts
- Grids

### Test your visuals

Check that your slides are readable for everyone in the audience, whether you are presenting in a room or online.

Run slides through an accessibility checker.

### Use The Glance Test™

Ask:

> Do your slides pass The Glance Test™?

Your audience should be able to discern the meaning of your visuals in **three seconds or less**.

# 4. Delivery

## Hone Your Presence

### Practice your delivery

Rehearse your presentation multiple times so you know your material well. This will help you feel more comfortable and confident.

### Calm your nerves

The guide recommends using a pre-presentation meditation to calm your nerves and mind.

### Mind your body language

Choose your gestures and movements intentionally to reinforce your words.

Maintain strong eye contact with the audience.

### Speak clearly

Enunciate your words and speak at an appropriate volume so everyone can hear and understand you.

### Pause for impact

Use pauses strategically to emphasize key points and give your audience time to process information.

### Be passionate

Show enthusiasm and passion for your topic to keep your audience engaged and interested.

## Presentation Creation Checklist

When building a presentation, the agent should ask or infer:

1. Who is the audience?
2. What do they currently think and feel?
3. What transformation should happen by the end?
4. What is the Big Idea™?
5. What is at stake if they adopt the idea?
6. What is at stake if they do not adopt the idea?
7. What hook will open the presentation?
8. What story, analogy, or anecdote will make the idea relatable?
9. What contrast will create dramatic tension?
10. What evidence supports the main point?
11. What is the clear call to action?
12. What “new bliss” should close the presentation?
13. Does each slide contain one idea?
14. Do visuals pass the three-second Glance Test™?
15. Has the delivery been rehearsed for clarity, pauses, body language, and passion?

## Agent Prompt Template

Use this prompt pattern when applying the framework:

```text
Create a presentation using the Duarte-style four-part framework:

1. Strategy: Define the audience, their current beliefs/emotions, the Big Idea, and the desired transformation.
2. Story: Open with a hook, include a relevant story or analogy, contrast what is with what could be, and end with a strong call to action and new bliss.
3. Visuals: Keep each slide to one idea, use images/diagrams/charts to reinforce meaning, maintain consistent visual style, and ensure each slide passes a three-second glance test.
4. Delivery: Include speaker notes that support clear delivery, intentional pauses, strong body language, and passionate communication.
```
