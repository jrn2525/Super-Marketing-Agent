# JOHN NICHOLS VIDEO VOICE STYLE GUIDE

**The voice on camera. YouTube long-form, short-form vertical, course videos, sales videos, on-demand webinars, podcast video, live streams, ad creative.**

---

## Overview

Fifth in the set. Sister to coaching, email, writing, and stage. Same John. Same anchors. New medium with its own physics.

Video is not one format. It's at least eight, each with different runtime, different opening mechanics, different pacing, different CTA conventions. A 90-second short and a 60-minute podcast filmed from your office are both "video," but they're as different from each other as an email and a book chapter. This guide treats them that way.

Video is also half-spoken and half-visual. The voice carries through. Production decisions (lighting, framing, B-roll, captions, thumbnails) carry as much weight as anything in the script. A great script with bad audio loses. A decent script with sharp visual language wins.

Two anchors carry through:

- **"It is always the right time to do the right thing."** The ethical anchor.
- **"Focus on the doing."** The action anchor. Often the closing line of a video.

The Four Basics carry too. *Greet, Educate, Process, Close.* Now applied to the video itself. *Every viewer. Every time. No exceptions.*

The 23-year operator history is the credibility floor. On video, that history is also visual: store photos as B-roll, sales reports on screen, real franchise scenes the audience can see and not just hear about.

The John Maxwell certification (since 2016) lives in the background on most video formats and surfaces openly on faith-aligned channels and coaching-content videos.

## Voice & Tone (Video-Specific)

### Core Characteristics (carried)

**Operator-grounded.** Real franchise material in every video. Never theoretical.

**Conversational and peer-like.** The viewer is one person watching alone, not a room. Speak to them, not to a crowd.

**Reflective and honest.** Vulnerability is the trust engine. The camera sees micro-expressions. Honesty has nowhere to hide.

**Self-deprecating with restraint.** The 50/50 snap-decision rate, the bad hire, the persistence-vs-purpose confession. Used deliberately, not piled on.

**Values-driven.** Integrity, accountability, finishing well as throughlines.

**Faith-aware.** Implicit by default on broad-audience video. Explicit on faith-aligned channels or faith-tagged content.

### Video-Specific Shifts

**Two energies, both real.** Reflective John lands on long-form lessons and Sunday-night-style talking-head videos. Yellow Quick Start John lands on short-form, ads, course videos, and live streams. Most videos blend both. The Yellow energy is on camera whether the script names it or not, so don't over-write into reflective mode.

**One viewer, not a room.** Stage language like *"every operator in this room"* doesn't translate. Video adapts to *"every operator watching this"* or *"if you've ever run a store on a Saturday morning..."*

**Captions are the experience for most viewers.** Mobile, muted scroll is the default consumption mode for short-form and a real share of long-form. The script has to read as well as it sounds.

**The first second is the only second guaranteed.** On every format, the open earns the next moment. On short-form, the first 1.5 seconds are everything. On long-form, the first 30 seconds. On a sales video, the first 60. Throat-clearing kills any of them.

**Production carries half the message.** Lighting, framing, audio, B-roll, on-screen text, editing pace. The voice is half. The visual is half.

## The Video Anatomy: The Four Basics, Applied

Every video has four jobs. They mirror the customer-facing framework John teaches.

### Greet. The Hook

The first beat. Earns the watch.

- **Confession.** *"I'm going to tell you about the hire I should have fired six months earlier."*
- **Time anchor.** *"It was 2009. Saturday morning rush. My Lenoir City store."*
- **False belief named.** *"Most operators think persistence is the answer. I was one of them."*
- **Pattern interrupt.** *"Stop scrolling if you've ever sat in your store after close, lights off, wondering if it's still worth it."*

No hellos. No "thanks for tuning in." No "in today's video, we're going to talk about..." Drop the viewer in.

### Educate. The Story or Insight

Where trust gets built. Same patterns as stage and writing, with visual layered on top.

- Real franchise stories with year, place, person, stakes.
- Internal monologue surfaced. *"I'm sitting there thinking, if I just put in more hours, this store will turn."*
- Vulnerability with restraint.
- B-roll supports the story (store photos, sales reports, family photos when appropriate, screen recordings of vibe-coded apps).

### Process. The Framework or Reframe

The bridge from "this is John's story" to "this is your situation." Where John's IP appears on camera.

- The Four Basics, often taught in full on long-form and course video.
- The reframe as the central line. Repeated. Pulled out as on-screen text. Designed to be a clip.
- The principle, named clearly.

### Close. The Reflection or CTA

The last beat. Where the viewer either acts, reflects, or moves on.

- Reflective close (for content video).
- Direct CTA (for sales video, ad creative, course video).
- Soft direct (for long-form YouTube and podcast video).

CTA convention varies sharply by format. See Format-Specific Guidance below.

**Every viewer. Every time. No exceptions.**

## Format-Specific Guidance

The voice carries. The container shapes everything else.

### Long-Form Direct-to-Camera (YouTube, 10 to 20 min)

**Audience.** Search traffic, returning subscribers, recommended-feed discovery.

**Goal.** Substance. Authority. Channel growth. SEO presence on operator topics.

**Voice.** Reflective forward with Yellow moments. Closer to keynote pacing than to ad pacing.

**Structural recommendations.**

- One arc, one big idea. Don't try to teach three frameworks.
- Three to five real stories serving the central reframe.
- Callback structure: opening line returns at the close.
- Soft direct CTA at the end. Take the scorecard. Subscribe. Comment with their situation.
- Chapter markers in the YouTube description for skippable navigation.
- Length: 12 to 18 minutes is the sweet spot for franchise content.

### Short-Form Vertical (Reels / Shorts / TikTok, 30 to 90 sec)

**Audience.** Cold scroll. Mostly first-time exposure to John.

**Goal.** Discovery. Follower growth. Funnel top-up to YouTube long-form, scorecard, or newsletter.

**Voice.** Yellow forward. Tighter. More energy in the first second.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Hook in 1.5 seconds.** Visual lean-in plus verbal confession or contrarian claim. *"Most operators think more hours fix a struggling store. I tried that for two years."*
- **One beat. One payoff.** Don't try to land three points.
- **Often a clip from a longer talk or video.** Repurposing is fine. Repackaging matters.
- **Burn-in captions essential.** Most viewers watch muted.
- **One CTA, soft.** *"Follow for more from 23 years of operating."* or *"Comment YES and I'll send the breakdown."*
- **No outro music sting.** End on the line.

### Course Videos (5-Day Challenge, 120-Day Program, Year-Long)

**Audience.** Enrolled clients. Active learners.

**Goal.** Completion. Application. Retention. Movement to the next program.

**Voice.** Modular. Consistent across lessons. Action-pushing. Yellow with reflective moments.

**Structural recommendations.**

- Each lesson opens with a scene or a turning-point statement, same as a chapter.
- Frameworks named consistently across lessons. The Four Basics. The Time Freedom Scorecard categories.
- One framework or principle per lesson.
- Action push at the end. *"Now go do this exercise. Don't watch the next lesson until you have."*
- Length: 5 to 15 minutes per lesson. Longer is rarely better for course consumption.
- B-roll: vibe-coded app demos where applicable.

### Sales Video / VSL (sales page video)

**Audience.** Warm leads on the offer page.

**Goal.** Conversion to call, program, or challenge.

**Voice.** Vulnerable first, confident in the offer second. Same logic as the sales talk in the stage guide.

**Structural recommendations.**

- Open with a scene the viewer recognizes from their own life.
- Build credibility through specificity (23 years, 11 stores, the back-office moments).
- Position the offer as a natural extension of operating experience, not a packaged course.
- Address objections through story, not bullet points.
- One specific CTA. *"Book a call at [link]."* No fake scarcity. No countdown timers.
- Length: 5 to 12 minutes for most VSLs. Longer only when warranted by complexity.

### On-Demand Webinar Recordings

**Audience.** Post-event signups. Evergreen funnel viewers. Skip-around watchers.

**Goal.** Conversion or education.

**Voice.** Tighter than live webinar (covered in stage guide). Knows the viewer can leave or skip.

**Structural recommendations.**

- Chapter markers and timestamps.
- Tighter editing than live (cut the dead air, the false starts, the host-platform housekeeping).
- Production cues: on-screen text for key reframes and frameworks. Pull quotes. Section headers.
- Clear CTA at the end and at one mid-point.

### Podcast Video (Your Own Podcast Filmed)

**Audience.** Subscribers. Engaged community.

**Goal.** Relationship deepening. Content asset. Long-form trust building.

**Voice.** Most relaxed. Coaching voice resurfaces hardest, especially with guests.

**Structural recommendations.**

- Two-camera or single-camera setup. Filmed at the desk or in the office.
- Conversational, longer-form (30 to 90 minutes).
- Recurring intro/outro segments build familiarity.
- Soft CTA at the end. Newsletter, scorecard, next episode tease.
- Short-form clips from each episode become a feeder for short-form vertical content.

### Live Streams (Facebook / YouTube / Instagram Live)

**Audience.** Engaged community. Real-time viewers.

**Goal.** Relationship. Real-time response. Audience interaction.

**Voice.** Most informal. Yellow forward. Coaching voice in Q&A.

**Structural recommendations.**

- Lower production is fine. Phone or webcam plus decent audio.
- Acknowledge viewers by name when comments come in.
- Real-time CTA possible. *"Comment SCORECARD and I'll drop the link."*
- Replay handling: trim the dead air for evergreen viewing, leave authenticity in.
- Topic-led, not script-led.

### Talking-Head Ad Creative (paid)

**Audience.** Cold or warm paid traffic.

**Goal.** Conversion to scorecard, challenge, or call.

**Voice.** Yellow forward. Tightest. Hook discipline.

**Structural recommendations.**

- 15 to 60 seconds. 30-second is the workhorse.
- Hook in 1.5 seconds. Same as short-form vertical.
- One pain point. One reframe. One CTA.
- Burn-in captions.
- Multiple variations per concept for testing.
- CTA explicit. *"Take the 5-minute scorecard at timefreedomscore.com."*

## Hook Craft (Short-Form and Ads Especially)

The first 1.5 seconds decide whether the viewer stays. Hook craft is its own discipline.

### The Visual Hook

- **Lean forward.** Closes the distance.
- **Hand gesture.** Holding up an object (old store photo, sales report, scorecard).
- **Quick cut to a B-roll image** that supports the verbal hook.
- **Direct eye contact.** Lock the camera.

### The Verbal Hook (Patterns)

- **Confession.** *"I'm about to tell you the hire I should have fired six months earlier."*
- **Contrarian claim.** *"Most operators think persistence is the answer. I tried that for 11 years."*
- **Specific number.** *"After 23 years and 11 stores, here's the one thing I'd tell my younger self."*
- **Time anchor.** *"It was 2009. I was sitting in my back office. Lights off. Just the glow of the screen."*
- **Pattern interrupt.** *"Stop scrolling if you've ever wondered whether your store is running you."*
- **Direct address.** *"If you're a franchise owner watching this, this is for you."*

### Hook Rules

- No greetings. No name introductions. The bio goes in the channel description.
- The hook is the headline. If it doesn't earn the next second, the rest doesn't matter.
- Verbal and visual hook should reinforce, not compete.
- One hook per video. Don't open with two.

## Story Patterns for Video

Same patterns as writing and stage, with visual layered on top.

### The Wall

The turning-point story. *"The wall came when I realized I was holding on out of fear, not out of purpose."* On video, the wall moment lands hardest with a pause and a cutaway to a still image (store photo, sales report, family photo). The visual gives the viewer somewhere to put the feeling.

### The Epiphany

The insight story. *"The epiphany came during a conversation with Mary Ellen."* Often shot as a single continuous take with no B-roll, so the viewer reads the moment on John's face.

### The Mary Ellen Conversation

A specific subset. Mary Ellen as the wisdom mirror. Audiences feel the weight instantly because they recognize the dynamic.

### The Specific Year + Place

Always anchor with year and location. Visually paired with photos when available.

### The Bad Hire

The hire who should have been fired six months earlier. Models accountability. Visual: a redacted name in handwriting on a notepad, a stock photo of an empty workstation, the back-office still.

### The Store That Drained

The location that took more than it gave. Sets up *"Not every store is meant to be saved."*

### The 50/50 Snap-Decision Pattern

*"About half my snap decisions were the best calls I ever made. The other half taught me to slow down on the ones that mattered."* Lands a smile and trust at once. On camera the smile is visible, which makes it land harder than on stage.

### The Selling Arc

The current chapter. Selling the last 10 stores. *"Letting go isn't quitting. It's completing."* Visual: photos of stores, signing documents, an empty counter at end of day.

## Recurring Phrases & Anchors

Same phrase library as the rest of the set, with notes on visual treatment.

### Anchor Phrases

- **"It is always the right time to do the right thing."** Often pulled out as on-screen text.
- **"Focus on the doing."** Closing-line candidate. Pull-quote candidate.
- **"The work is in the doing, not the waiting."** Long form for long-form video.
- **"Every customer. Every time. No exceptions."** Four Basics tagline. Visual treatment as a graphic.

### Reframe Phrases

- *"Letting go isn't quitting. It's completing."*
- *"Not every store is meant to be saved."*
- *"I was mistaking persistence for purpose."*
- *"Accountability isn't just for the team. It's for me."*
- *"Waiting to act costs more than acting."*

### Turning-Point Phrases

- *"The wall came when..."*
- *"The epiphany came..."*
- *"If I just..."* (false belief setup)

### Time Anchors

- *"After twenty-three years in franchise operations..."*
- *"When I started with my first UPS Store in Lenoir City back in March 2003..."*
- *"By the time I had eleven stores..."*

### Use Rules for Video

- One reframe phrase as the central line. Build toward it. Hold it on screen as text when it lands.
- Anchor phrases earn one or two appearances per video. No saturation.
- Short-form: pull one phrase per video. That's the clip.

## Production Notes

The visual half of the message.

### Lighting

- Soft natural light from the side or front.
- Or a single key light with fill.
- No harsh overhead. No backlit silhouette unless deliberate.
- Warm color temperature, not cold.

### Framing

- **Talking head long-form.** Chest-up. Eyes in the upper third.
- **Short-form vertical.** Closer crop. Face fills more of the frame.
- **Podcast video.** Wider shot. Desk and background visible.
- **Sales video / VSL.** Same as long-form talking head.

### Audio

- Lavalier or shotgun mic. Never camera mic.
- Quiet room. No HVAC. No hum.
- Audio matters more than video quality. Bad audio loses viewers faster than bad lighting.

### Background

- Home office. Books visible. Warm and lived-in.
- Avoid corporate beige. Avoid sterile.
- Personal touches earn trust (family photo, store memorabilia, framed certificate).

### B-Roll Library

Build and maintain a library:

- Store photos (interior, exterior, signage, counter, back office).
- Sales reports and dashboards (blurred for any sensitive numbers).
- Family photos (Mary Ellen, the kids, the wedding ring shot).
- Screen recordings of vibe-coded apps (Time Freedom Scorecard, Faith Builder Journal, Amino Stack Builder, Imposition Labs, The Pricing Tool Kit, Xpansion Console).
- Texas Hill Country scenes if they fit a story.
- Generic franchise B-roll (counter scenes, customer interactions, retail moments) for fallback.

### Editing Pace

- **Long-form.** 5 to 10 second cuts on average. Slower for reflective beats.
- **Short-form.** 1 to 3 second cuts. Faster for hook section.
- **Course video.** 7 to 12 second cuts. Easier on the brain for learning.
- **Sales video.** Variable. Slow down for vulnerability beats.

### Color Grading

- Warm. Slightly saturated. Skin tones natural.
- Consistent across the channel.

### On-Screen Text

- Pull-quote treatment for reframe phrases.
- Lower-third name and title on first appearance.
- Section headers for long-form (e.g., "The Wall," "The Epiphany").
- Section headers function as built-in chapter markers.

## Captions and Accessibility

Captions are not optional.

### Why

- Most short-form viewers watch muted.
- A meaningful share of long-form viewers also watch muted.
- Captions improve completion rate and watch time.
- Captions are accessibility, not marketing nice-to-have.

### How

- **Short-form.** Burn-in captions. Animated word-by-word for hook section.
- **Long-form.** SRT files. YouTube auto-generated, then edited for accuracy.
- **Sales video / VSL.** SRT or burn-in based on placement.
- **Course video.** SRT minimum. Player-controlled captions.
- **Live streams.** Auto-captions where the platform supports them.

### Caption Style

- Match speech. Don't sanitize.
- Keep "ums" out. Keep texture in (so, now, here's the thing).
- Punctuation present. Sentence case.
- No all-caps unless the speaker emphasized.

## Thumbnails and Titles (YouTube Especially)

Half the click is the thumbnail. The other half is the title.

### Thumbnail Patterns

- John's face visible. Clear emotion (surprise, concern, focus, smile).
- Big bold text. 3 to 4 words max.
- High contrast. Readable at small size.
- Consistent style across the channel.
- Test variations when possible.

### Title Patterns

- **Operator language.** *"The hire I should have fired six months earlier."*
- **Specific number.** *"23 years. 11 stores. One lesson."*
- **Confession.** *"I was mistaking persistence for purpose."*
- **Direct question.** *"Are you running your store, or is it running you?"*
- **Curiosity gap.** *"The decision that nearly cost me everything."*

### Title Rules

- No clickbait that the video doesn't deliver. Burns trust faster than any other mistake.
- Front-load the hook. The first three words matter most.
- Avoid jargon and leadership-book language.

## Self-Awareness Patterns on Camera

Vulnerability lands harder on video because the viewer sees the face. Used deliberately, it's the trust engine.

### The 50/50 Snap-Decision Pattern

*"About half my snap decisions were the best calls I ever made. The other half taught me to slow down on the ones that mattered."* On camera, deliver this with a slight smile. The smile is what makes it land.

### The Bad Hire Confession

*"I had a hire I should have fired six months earlier."* Universal. Models accountability and the cost of avoidance.

### The Mary Ellen Truth

*"Mary Ellen said the thing I couldn't say to myself."* Models marriage and the wisdom mirror.

### The Persistence-vs-Purpose Confession

*"I was mistaking persistence for purpose."* Reframes a quality the viewer has been told their whole career to value.

### The Back-Office Moment

*"I'm sitting in the back office. Lights off. Just the glow of the computer screen."* On camera, this is whispered, not declared. The visual cue (a still or B-roll of an empty back office) lands the moment.

### Use Rule

One vulnerability beat per major story. Stacked vulnerability reads as performance. Spaced vulnerability reads as honesty.

## Viewer Reflection Patterns

Lines designed to make the viewer pause, look away, and answer in their head. Video lets these land harder than any other medium because the viewer can actually pause the video and sit.

### The Direct Question

- *"Are you holding on out of purpose, or out of fear?"*
- *"Are you running your store, or is your store running you?"*
- *"What's the call you've been avoiding?"*
- *"Who's the hire you know you need to make?"*

### The Recognition Line

- *"Every operator watching this knows that feeling."*
- *"If you've ever run a store on a Saturday morning, you know what I'm about to describe."*
- *"You've sat where I sat."*

### Use Rules

- One reflection prompt per video.
- After the question, hold for a beat. Don't fill the silence.
- Pair with a cutaway shot or on-screen text for the viewer to sit with.

## CTAs by Format

CTAs vary sharply by format. One playbook does not fit all.

### Long-Form YouTube

- Soft direct: *"Take the 5-minute scorecard at timefreedomscore.com."*
- Plus a subscribe ask near the end.
- Plus a comment prompt for engagement (*"Drop a comment with your year-one lesson."*).

### Short-Form Vertical

- One CTA only.
- Soft: *"Follow for more from 23 years of operating."*
- Or specific: *"Comment SCORECARD and I'll send the link."*

### Course Video

- Action push: *"Now go do this exercise. Don't watch the next lesson until you have."*
- Reinforce the framework name.

### Sales Video / VSL

- One specific CTA. *"Book a call at [link]."*
- No fake scarcity. No countdown timers.
- Repeat the CTA at the end and at one mid-point.

### On-Demand Webinar

- Mid-point CTA and end CTA.
- Specific: book a call, take the next program, register for the cohort.

### Podcast Video

- Soft. Newsletter, scorecard, or next episode tease.
- One per episode. Burying the listener in CTAs breaks the conversational feel.

### Live Stream

- Real-time. *"Comment X and I'll drop the link."*
- Acknowledge by name when delivering.

### Ad Creative

- Single specific CTA. *"Take the scorecard. Link below."* or *"Book a call. Link in bio."*
- Match the CTA to the funnel stage.

## Faith on Video

Same approach as the rest of the set, with platform considerations.

**Implicit by default.** Broad-audience YouTube, short-form for general operator audiences, ads, sales videos to mixed segments.

**Explicit.** Faith-aligned channels or playlists. Faith-tagged content. John Maxwell affiliated content. Faith-forward podcast episodes. Sermon-style talks.

**Rule of thumb.** When in doubt, implicit. The values carry. Reserve explicit for the contexts where it adds rather than narrows. A single, brief, unforced reference to faith on broad-audience video lets faith-aligned viewers feel seen without pushing past anyone else.

## Always Do

* Open with a confession, time anchor, or false-belief callout. No greetings.
* Pull from the 23 years of operating. Visuals when available.
* Match production to the format (lighting, framing, B-roll, editing pace).
* Caption everything.
* Treat thumbnails and titles as half the click for YouTube.
* Use the Four Basics as both teaching framework and video structure.
* Land at least one signature phrase per video.
* Use vulnerability with restraint. One beat per major story.
* Match faith calibration to the platform and audience.
* Bring Yellow energy and reflective energy both. Don't over-write into reflective mode.

## Never Do

* Open with hellos, thank-yous, or "in today's video..."
* Stack multiple frameworks in one video.
* Skip captions.
* Use clickbait the video doesn't deliver.
* Use jargon, leadership-book language, or LinkedIn-coach phrases.
* Pretend to have all the answers.
* Use fake scarcity, countdown timers, or urgency theater.
* Pile on hard CTAs from a long-form content video.
* Use dashes (em or en) in any script. Hard rule.
* Bring faith forward in venues that haven't called for it.
* Saturate signature phrases. Two or three per video at most.
* End on a music sting that drowns out the closing line.

## Coaching Patterns Carried to Video

Several patterns translate. Some adjust.

**Story-to-Lesson Pattern.** Carries strongly. Video is the visual version of stage storytelling.

**Self-Awareness-First Pattern.** Carries strongly. Camera magnifies vulnerability.

**Reframe Pattern.** Carries. On camera the reframe gets pulled out as on-screen text.

**Diagnostic-Before-Advice Pattern.** Resurfaces in podcast video and live stream Q&A. Otherwise replaced by rhetorical questions.

**Action-Closer Pattern.** Adjusts by format. Hard close on ads and sales video. Soft close on long-form content. Action push on course video.

**False Belief Pattern.** Carries directly from stage. Video is the right medium for the visual treatment of "false belief named, false belief dismantled."

**Viewer Reflection Pattern.** New on video. Stage prompts reflection but the audience can't pause and sit. Video viewers can.

## Writing Notes (for Video Scripts)

* **No dashes.** Hard rule. Em dashes and en dashes get replaced with periods, commas, semicolons, colons, or parentheses.
* **Real names, places, years.** *"My Lenoir City store in 2009"* beats *"one of my locations."*
* **No leadership-book language.** Cut *"synergy," "alignment," "buy-in," "leverage"* as a verb.
* **Read it out loud before recording.** If a sentence trips on the tongue, rewrite it.
* **Mark the cuts.** Editing notes go in brackets. *[Cutaway to store photo]*. *[Pull quote: 'Letting go isn't quitting']*. *[B-roll: scorecard demo]*.
* **Recurring phrases as anchors, not filler.** Two or three per video, threaded through.

## Final Test

Would the viewer pause the video, look away, and actually answer the question? Would the franchise owner who started watching skeptically finish thinking *"that guy's been there"*? Would the short-form scroller send the clip to their business partner? If the video feels rehearsed, polished into safety, or dressed in business-book vocabulary, rewrite it. If it feels like a 23-year operator on the other side of a camera, telling the truth about the hard parts and pointing toward the next right move, it's right.
