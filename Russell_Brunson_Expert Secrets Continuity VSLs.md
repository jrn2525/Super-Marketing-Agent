The Haunted Mansion in Baltimore Last year I took my Atlas group to Baltimore, Maryland. For those of you who aren’t from America, Baltimore is - how do I say this politely - kind of the armpit of the country. And my whole team was looking at me like, “Russell, why on earth are we going to Baltimore?”

Two reasons. Reason one: when I got started in this game twenty-five years ago, my first real mastermind was with Bill Glazer, and Bill Glazer was based in Baltimore. I flew to Baltimore three times a year for six years of my life, standing at the feet of Bill and the other guys I was learning from. That city changed my life. I have a soft spot for it even if the weather and the neighborhoods don’t.

Reason two: Agora Publishing is in Baltimore. If you don’t know who Agora is, they’re the biggest direct-response publisher in our world. They do about $1.5 billion a year selling a $49 financial newsletter. Yes, you read that right. $1.5 billion. One newsletter. $49.

I’ve been aware of Agora for twenty years. I’ve funnel-hacked them every single year. I was sure I understood their model. I was even sure I was using pieces of their model in my own businesses. I’d read everything public about them that I could find.

So I called the guys at Agora and said, “We’ve got a bunch of nerds who want to come together and talk about business. Can we come do our mastermind at your offices?” They were thrilled.

They let us in to this beautiful old building from the 1800s - a building that had at one point been a funeral home - and walked us through the rooms.

“This is the room where Mark Ford wrote the letter for X. This is where Porter Stansbury wrote the letter for Y.” Billions and billions of dollars of direct-response copy came out of those rooms.

It felt haunted. Some of the staff were telling ghost stories. We were all a little freaked out on the first day.

I had missed the entire point. What I Thought I Knew About Agora I thought I understood Agora’s model like this: they write a VSL. The VSL sells a newsletter. They run ads to the VSL. Simple.

Here’s how their business actually works. Each Agora brand has four to six “gurus.” James Altucher is one of the famous ones - crazy hair, face everywhere online. Each guru has their own newsletter. “But wait,” I said to the guys at Agora, “when one of your VSLs stops working, how do you fix it? Do you test new hooks? New offers? New price points?”

One of them answered with a line I haven’t been able to shake since: The offer never changes. The offer has been there for twenty-five years. The offer is always exactly the same. The only thing we change is the story on the front.

Wait. So you’re telling me you write a new VSL every six weeks, running to the same offer, with a different hook each time?

Yes, he said. That’s exactly what we do. And then he said something that reframed my entire view of how our industry works: We’re not in the optimization business. We’re in the hits business. We’re like a musician cutting thirty, forty, fifty songs. We just need one or two to hit and we’re famous. If a VSL doesn’t work, we don’t tweak it.

We kill it and move on to the next one. The winners get optimized. The losers get buried. That’s the whole business.

The Piece I Missed Okay. I can hear you. “Russell, this is cool, but what does it have to do with me selling my continuity?”

Everything. Because here’s the piece I missed for twenty years, and the piece I want to give you in this book.

Each of those VSLs that Agora writes is built around what they call a “premium.” In our world we’d call it a bonus. It’s not the product - the product is still the same $49 newsletter. The premium is a specific thing included with the newsletter that the VSL spotlights as if it IS the product.

So when Elon Musk is in the news, they write a VSL called something like “Elon’s $15,000 Supercomputer Play.” The VSL spends ninety minutes talking about Elon’s supercomputer company, the stocks, the opportunity, the trend. Reader comes in for the report. Gets the newsletter too. Twenty-five years later, they’re still a subscriber.

Six weeks later, another premium, another VSL. They have one product. One offer. One price. And they write dozens of VSLs against it - each one spotlighting a different premium from inside the offer stack as the hook.

The Agora Engine Before we get into the twelve-beat structure, I want to make sure you understand the engine underneath it. Because the structure only works if the engine is running.

Here’s the core insight. A continuity product - something the customer pays for every month or every year

- is a very different beast from a one-time purchase. With a one-time product, you’re trying to get

someone to make one decision. With a continuity, you’re trying to get someone to start a relationship.

And most of our industry has been selling continuities the wrong way. We treat them like one-time products. We write one VSL, one hook, one story, and we run it forever. When it stops working, we tweak the headline or test a new price. We’re in the optimization business.

Agora isn’t in the optimization business. They’re in the hits business. That distinction matters because it changes how you spend your time. An optimizer spends eight weeks A/B testing variations of a single VSL. A hits-maker spends eight weeks writing a brand-new VSL. At the end of eight weeks, the optimizer has a slightly better version of the same thing. The hits-maker has something completely new that might hit a totally different audience.

One Offer, Many Hooks The thing that took me twenty years to understand is that Agora treats the offer as sacred. They do not mess with the offer. The offer has been dialed in for twenty-five years. What they change is the story wrapped around it.

Here’s how it works in our world. Say you’ve got a continuity at $97 a month. Inside that continuity, you’ve got seven bonuses - let’s call them a software tool, a private community, a newsletter archive, live event recordings, a core training library, a Q&A coaching call, and a mindset challenge.

Most people, when they sit down to write a VSL for that continuity, try to sell all seven things at once.

Here’s the software, and you also get the community, and the newsletter, and the recordings, and the

Why This Works The psychology of the Stack Pivot is subtle. Let me walk you through what’s actually happening in the reader’s head.

When the reader lands on the VSL, they’re skeptical. They’ve seen sales pitches before. They’ve watched countless marketers try to stuff seven benefits into a two-minute video. They’ve built up defenses.

But this VSL doesn’t seem to be pitching a continuity. It’s talking about one specific thing. It’s telling a story about how that one specific thing came to exist. It’s showing, step by step, how the reader would use it.

The reader’s defenses start to lower, because they recognize what they’re reading - it’s a pitch for a single product, and they know how to evaluate those. They’re actively considering whether they’d buy this one thing.

Then, just as they’re making a mental commitment, the Stack Pivot happens. The single thing they were ready to buy turns out to come bundled with six other things at a price point that’s a fraction of what the single thing would be worth on its own.

The reader doesn’t feel tricked. They feel lucky. Because the one thing they wanted is still the primary thing they’re getting - they just get a bunch of other stuff for free.

And that’s why the Stack Pivot only works when the offer is honest. If the continuity doesn’t actually include the premium you spotlighted, or if the other bonuses are junk, the reader feels manipulated when they find out. They cancel. They warn their friends. You converted once and churned hard.

This framework only works when the offer is real. Don’t write this framework for a continuity that isn’t genuinely worth the stack. It will convert for a minute and then implode.

The Portfolio Approach Here’s the other thing that took me a while to see. Agora doesn’t write ONE VSL for each newsletter. They write dozens.

Each VSL spotlights a different premium. Each VSL uses a different hook. Each VSL runs to a different slice of the audience. When one VSL burns out on Facebook, they launch a new one next month. When a new premium gets added to the offer stack, they write a new VSL around it.

For a continuity with five or more bonuses, you should eventually have at least three different VSLs in rotation - each one spotlighting one of your strongest premiums. The reader who’s frustrated with their marketing software converts on the software VSL. The reader who’s lonely in their business converts on the community VSL. The reader who needs proof of what’s working converts on the newsletter VSL. Same continuity, three different paths in.

I’m not telling you to write ten VSLs this week. I’m telling you to write ONE VSL this week, get it working, and then plan to write another one around a different premium next quarter. Build a portfolio over time.

That’s the hits business.

The 12-Beat Skeleton Every Continuity VSL I’ve written follows the same twelve beats in the same order. I didn’t start with the beats - I started by writing a couple of these and then analyzing them side by side to see what the structure actually was. Once I saw it, I couldn’t un-see it. Both scripts hit every single beat.

Here are the twelve beats, with the percentage of total word count each one takes up. These percentages are stable whether the script is fifteen minutes long or thirty minutes long - the structure scales proportionally.

### Thetwelvebeats

1. Diagnostic Hook (0 - 5%) - name the reader’s frustration with unusual precision

2. “Actually It’s This” Reframe (5 - 12%) - pivot from the surface problem to the real problem

3. Two Worlds Setup (12 - 20%) - build the binary that organizes the whole argument

4. Personal Proof Story (20 - 28%) - “I’ve been on both sides of this wall”

5. Premium Introduction (28 - 38%) - introduce the spotlighted premium as THE solution

6. Premium Origin Story (38 - 48%) - where the premium came from

7. Premium Demo (48 - 58%) - walk the reader through the premium step by step

8. The Stack Pivot (58 - 65%) - “You can’t buy this on its own - it only lives inside...”

9. Full Stack Reveal (65 - 78%) - everything else the reader gets

10. Bribe Reframe (78 - 85%) - “Why am I giving you this much for this price?”

11. Urgency + Guarantee Stack (85 - 93%) - real urgency, then triple guarantee

12. Two Paths Close (93 - 100%) - imagine both futures, then say “maybe”

Three Rules You Have to Internalize Before we go beat by beat, here are the three structural rules that are not negotiable. If you violate any of these, the script breaks.

Rule 1: The product doesn’t appear until after Beat 7 The continuity name and price do not show up in the first fifty-eight percent of the script. Sometimes clients freak out about this. “How is anyone supposed to know what we’re selling?” The answer is: they’re not supposed to know yet. You’re selling them on the premium first. The continuity reveal happens at the Stack Pivot.

If you introduce the continuity too early, the reader spends the next half of the script evaluating whether they want a subscription. That’s a much harder sell than “do you want this one specific thing.” Keep the focus on the premium until the hinge.

Rule 2: Urgency comes BEFORE guarantees, never after Most direct response teaching tells you to resolve objections first and then build urgency. For a Continuity VSL, you do it the opposite way.

Here’s why. Urgency raises anxiety. Guarantees disarm it. If you put the guarantees first, you calm the reader down - and then you try to raise urgency, but now they’re calm and the urgency doesn’t stick. If you do urgency first, you raise anxiety, and then you immediately disarm it with the guarantees. The reader closes the VSL feeling like the decision is both urgent AND safe.

Sequence matters. Don’t invert. Rule 3: One CTA, at the very end The Continuity VSL is long - fifteen to thirty minutes read-aloud. You might think you should have multiple CTAs sprinkled throughout, giving the reader multiple chances to act. Don’t.

These VSLs have exactly one CTA, in Beat 12. The entire script builds belief toward a single decision point.

Multiple CTAs fragment the emotional buildup. The Two Paths close in Beat 12 is the moment - that’s when the reader decides.

### Chcahapptteerrtthrhereee

Choose Your Spotlight Premium The single biggest decision in a Continuity VSL is which premium to spotlight. Get this right and the first sixty percent of the script has natural gravity. Get it wrong and the setup has no pull, the Stack Pivot has no momentum to release, and the reader churns out before Beat 8.

If your continuity has five or more bonuses in the stack, you’re not going to spotlight all of them in one VSL.

You’re going to spotlight ONE per VSL. Which means the question becomes: which one deserves this script?

The Four Tests Before I commit to a spotlight premium, I run it through four tests. The premium has to pass all four. If it fails one or more, I either pick a different premium, compensate in other beats, or restructure how the premium gets described.

Test 1: Standalone Desirability Would someone pay for this premium separately, as a standalone product? At a price point that makes the reader feel like they’re getting a deal when they’re told it’s included in the continuity?

The Dream 100 Software passes this test strongly. “People have offered six figures to license it.” That’s the standalone anchor. When the Stack Pivot reveals the software lives inside a $97-a-month continuity, the math is devastating.

The Backstage Pass passes because it’s positioned as “the only other place I share at this level is my $50,000 Inner Circle.” The reader immediately knows what kind of premium this is - it’s a cheaper version of a very expensive thing.

What fails this test? A generic training library. “Fifty-four modules on how to build your business.” The reader has seen a thousand of those. No standalone anchor exists. Same for pure content that’s commoditized in your space.



<!-- Page 17 -->



The test: can you name a credible dollar figure someone would pay for this premium on its own? If yes, the premium passes. If you’re reaching for an answer, it fails.

Test 2: Clear Problem Fit Does the premium solve a specific, nameable problem that a subset of your audience is actively feeling right now? Not a general problem - a specific one.

The Dream 100 Software solves “I’ve read about Dream 100 in Russell’s books but I can’t actually execute it because finding the right influencers is a slog.” That’s concrete. I can picture the reader who has that problem.

The Backstage Pass solves “I’ve consumed all the free marketing content and I still can’t get my business to convert - I need to see what’s actually working right now, not what worked two years ago.” Also concrete.

A vague bonus - “five hundred dollars of marketing secrets” - doesn’t pass this test because the reader can’t picture what specific problem it solves.

The test: finish this sentence in one line. “The reader who buys this VSL is the one who’s frustrated about ___.” If the blank gets a specific answer, the premium has problem fit.

Test 3: Unique Story Hook Does the premium have an origin story dramatic enough to carry Beat 6 of the script? Because if it doesn’t, the whole middle of the VSL goes flat.

Three origin-story archetypes work well. The first is what I call the Mrs. X story. The premium came from someone with dramatic credentials who normally doesn’t share it publicly. There’s a big dollar or scale anchor, an initial refusal to release, and then a story of how the refusal got reversed. I use this for the Dream 100 Software - Mrs. X, $200 million in sales, turned down six figures to license, eventually agreed to share with my audience. Every element is doing work.

The second is the Inner Circle contrast. The premium is a cheaper, more accessible version of something that costs far more. “The only other place I share at this level is my $50,000 Inner Circle.” Works when you have a genuine high-ticket program the premium connects to.

The third is the proprietary tool release. The premium was built internally for your own team and is being released publicly for the first time ever. Works when there’s a genuine internal-use history and demonstrated results.



<!-- Page 18 -->



The test: can you write a 300-word version of Beat 6 right now? If the story flows easily and contains scarcity, drama, and stakes, the premium passes. If you’re struggling to come up with anything, it fails.

Test 4: Demo-able Can you walk the reader through the premium step by step in Beat 7, so they can picture themselves using it?

Software tools demo easily. The Dream 100 Software has six clean demo steps: open the software, pick a platform, set the filters, search, get the contact info, manage the outreach. Each step has a concrete action.

Communities and access-based premiums demo through content enumeration. The Backstage Pass doesn’t have steps - but it has “here’s what you’ll see inside” bullet points that serve the same function.

Pure mindset content is hard to demo. So is abstract methodology. If the premium is “a new way of thinking,” Beat 7 has nothing concrete to put on the page, and the script goes soft in the middle.

The test: can you write three to six enumerable demo steps right now? If yes, pass. If the premium’s usage is abstract or vibes-based, fail.

What to Do When a Premium Fails a Test Don’t write the VSL around a premium that fails multiple tests. Pick a different premium from the stack, or restructure how the premium is described so it passes.

If the premium only fails one test and mostly passes, you can compensate in other beats. A premium with weak standalone desirability but strong problem fit works if you lean harder into Beats 3 and 4 (the binary and the personal proof story). A premium with weak demo-ability can shift Beat 7 from enumerated steps to content enumeration.

What you cannot do is force a weak premium into the spotlight and hope the writing covers for it. The structure is load-bearing. If the foundation is soft, nothing you put on top of it holds.

Multi-VSL Strategy The payoff of spotlighting one premium at a time is that you can run multiple VSLs against the same continuity - each one converting a different slice of your audience.

At MarketingSecrets.ai, we started with two VSLs: the Backstage Pass angle (the community) and the Dream 100 Software angle (the tool). Same offer, same price, same stack. Different hooks. Over time we’ll add VSLs around other premiums. A Funnel Oracle VSL for readers frustrated with figuring out where to



<!-- Page 19 -->



start. A Live Event Recordings VSL for readers who want to see what actually happens in a room full of seven-figure marketers.

Each VSL converts the subset of the audience whose specific frustration matches the spotlighted premium.

Together, the portfolio converts a much wider audience than any single VSL could. Rule of thumb: for any continuity with five or more premiums in the stack, you should eventually have at least three different VSLs in rotation. Start with one. Get it working. Then write the second one around a different premium. Then the third. Build the portfolio over time.

The Stack Pivot The Stack Pivot is the structural hinge of the entire framework. Everything in the first fifty-eight percent of the script sets it up. Everything after pays it off. If you get the Stack Pivot wrong, the whole script breaks - even if every other beat is technically correct.

This chapter is dedicated to the Stack Pivot because it’s the hardest beat to write well and the one that most distinguishes the Continuity VSL from every other sales format.

What the Stack Pivot Actually Does For the first seven beats of the VSL, you’ve been selling the premium as if it IS the product. The reader has been making a mental commitment to want this specific thing. They’re calculating whether they’d pay for it. They’re imagining themselves using it.

The Stack Pivot reveals three things at once:

1. You can’t actually buy the premium on its own. There’s no checkout page for it.

2. The premium only lives inside this other thing - the continuity.

3. When you join the continuity, you get the premium AND everything else that’s inside.

That “AND everything else” is the Stack Reveal that follows in Beat 9 - but the Stack Pivot itself is the moment of reveal, the redirect from “premium” to “continuity.”

The Two Versions There are two versions of the Stack Pivot depending on what kind of premium you’re spotlighting.

Version 1: The Explicit Pivot



<!-- Page 22 -->



Use this when the spotlighted premium is a distinct product - a software tool, a specific report, a physical product - that could theoretically exist on its own. The reader has been imagining buying it as a standalone.

You have to explicitly tell them it’s not for sale. From my Influencer Secrets VSL: Now, I need to explain something important. You can’t buy this software. It’s not for sale on its own.

There’s no checkout page. No one-time purchase. When Mrs. X agreed to let me share this with my audience, she had one condition: “Don’t just SELL it to people. Put it somewhere they’ll actually USE it.” So here’s what we did. I put the Dream 100 Influencer Secrets Software inside my private membership called Marketing Secrets.

The key structural moves: Name the “it’s not for sale” part explicitly. “You can’t buy this.” “It’s not for sale on its own.” The reader needs to hear this crisply so the redirect lands.

Justify the constraint. In my Influencer Secrets VSL, I use the Mrs. X condition - she specifically asked that the software not be sold standalone because she didn’t want it ending up in the hands of people who wouldn’t use it. The justification makes the constraint feel principled rather than manipulative.

Reveal where it DOES live. “So here’s what we did. I put it inside...” This is the redirect. The reader was trying to figure out how to buy the premium; you’re telling them the answer is to join the continuity.

Version 2: The Subtle Pivot Use this when the spotlighted premium IS the access layer of the continuity - like the Backstage Pass, which is effectively a name for the community access that comes with Marketing Secrets. You can’t really say “the Backstage Pass isn’t for sale” because it’s literally the thing the continuity grants.

Instead, the Subtle Pivot reveals that the premium comes bundled with many other things. The Stack Pivot becomes an “and there’s more” move rather than a “you can’t buy this” move.

From my Marketing Secrets VSL: This alone is worth the price of admission. But we’re just getting started. Because access alone isn’t enough. You also need tools to execute what you’re learning. And you need training to fill in the gaps. So I’m including everything...

The same function gets served - the reader’s focus shifts from “the one premium I wanted” to “all the things I’m getting” - but the framing is different. The reader doesn’t feel redirected; they feel upgraded.

Common Failure Modes Three ways writers get the Stack Pivot wrong. Failure 1: Pivoting too early Writers who are nervous about “hiding” the continuity often pivot at the 40% or 45% mark. The reader hasn’t invested enough emotional energy in the premium yet - the reveal doesn’t land because they weren’t fully committed to the premium when it got yanked.

Pivot at 58 - 65%. Not earlier. Let the premium fully land first. Failure 2: Pivoting too late The opposite failure. Writers who are enjoying their premium-setup keep the setup going until 70% or 75%. By the time the pivot happens, the reader is fatigued. The Stack Reveal has no runway to land in.

The pivot needs Beat 9 (the Stack Reveal) and Beats 10 - 12 (bribe, urgency, guarantee, close) to breathe.

Don’t steal their space. Failure 3: Apologizing for the pivot Writers who feel a little guilty about the redirect will hedge: “I know this might seem like a twist, but actually…” Don’t do this. The reader doesn’t know they’re being redirected until you tell them. If you telegraph guilt, you make them suspicious.

Write the pivot with full confidence. The reader came for a premium, and they’re about to get that premium plus six other things at a fraction of what the premium alone would cost. That’s a better deal, not a worse one.

The Pivot Only Works If the Offer Is Real I said this in Chapter 1 but it bears repeating here. The Stack Pivot only works when the continuity genuinely includes the spotlighted premium, and when the total stack genuinely exceeds what the reader expected.

If the reader joins the continuity and discovers that the premium you sold them on isn’t actually inside, they cancel. If they discover that the “$23,000 of value” was padded with junk, they cancel. If they discover that the “Mrs. X exclusive software” is actually a cheap off-the-shelf tool with a fancy sticker, they cancel.

This framework requires an honest offer. The reader’s trust is the thing that converts the first subscription month, AND the thing that keeps them subscribed for the next eighteen months. You trade that trust carelessly and the continuity becomes a churn machine.

Write this framework for continuities that genuinely deliver what the stack claims. If your stack is thin, fix the stack before you write the VSL.

The Bribe Reframe, Urgency, and Guarantees After the Stack Pivot and the Stack Reveal, the reader has seen everything. They know what they’re getting. They know roughly what it’s worth. The next three beats handle the three final objections they’re about to have, in the order the objections arise.

The Bribe Reframe (Beat 10) By the time you’ve finished the Stack Reveal in Beat 9, the reader has seen a value stack that totals $23,000 or more against a monthly price of $97 (or whatever your price point is). Their skepticism kicks back in. “If this is worth $23,000, why is it $97? What’s the catch?”

The Bribe Reframe answers that question before they can use it as an excuse to not buy. The move is to directly name what you’re doing. Not dance around it - name it. ‘OK Russell… What’s The Catch?’ No catch. But I get why you’re asking. So let me be completely transparent with you. This insane offer? It’s a bribe. I’m bribing you to step inside Marketing Secrets and see what it’s actually like to be in the room.

Two things happen when you name it. First, the reader’s skepticism releases, because you’ve said the thing they were thinking. Second, your willingness to admit “yes, this is a bribe” signals that you’re not trying to hide anything - which paradoxically increases trust.

After the direct-name move, stack the reasons. Three to five numbered reasons explaining why you’re willing to over-deliver. The reasons follow predictable archetypes:

• Long-game framing (I want you as a customer for the next decade, not just today)

• Business-scale framing (I have real businesses; I don’t need this continuity to be my main income)

• Audience-selection framing (I want the right people in the room)



<!-- Page 26 -->



• Personal mission framing (I’m tired of watching good people stay stuck)

You don’t need all four archetypes. Three is enough. Five is the maximum - more than five and the reasons start to feel defensive.

Real Urgency, Not Fake Scarcity (Beat 11a) Most direct-response teaching tells you to build urgency with scarcity. Countdown timers. “Only 37 copies left.” “Price goes up in three hours.”

I don’t do that in my Continuity VSLs. It’s not because I’m above urgency - urgency is essential. It’s because fake urgency erodes the trust you’ve spent thirty minutes building.

Here’s how I handle it. I name the fake move explicitly and reject it, then I stack real urgency reasons.

Look - I’m not going to manufacture fake scarcity. There’s no ‘only 37 copies left’ countdown timer here.

This is a digital membership. I’m not going to pretend I’m running out of pixels. But there ARE real reasons why waiting is a bad idea...

That “but there ARE real reasons” pivot is the entire move. You’ve just told the reader you’re being honest with them. Now you give them reasons that are genuinely true.

The four real-urgency archetypes I use:

• Time-value urgency: every day of waiting is a day of access you miss. The continuity is live, not static;

what happens today only happens today.

• Grandfather-rate urgency: the price is going up in the future as the stack grows. I’m being honest - I don’t

know when or how much, but I know the people who join at today’s price get grandfathered.

• Intimacy-capacity urgency: the community only works if the room stays intimate. There’s a ceiling. When

we hit it, doors close.

• Platform-risk urgency: the reader’s own platform could be shut down tomorrow. The continuity is an

insurance policy that only works if they have it before they need it. Stack three to four. Each one is a short paragraph with a specific framing.

The Triple Guarantee (Beat 11b) Immediately after the urgency, the guarantees. Sequence matters. Urgency raises anxiety; guarantees disarm it.



<!-- Page 27 -->



I use a triple guarantee. Not because three is magic, but because each of the three guarantees addresses a different objection.

Guarantee 1: The Financial Guarantee Removes money risk. Standard structure: a free trial period (14 days is my default), cancel anytime during the trial for zero charge, one-click cancellation, no phone calls or guilt trips.

The psychological move is to lower the commitment threshold. The reader isn’t saying yes to $97 a month forever - they’re saying yes to trying it for two weeks for free. That’s a much easier decision.

Guarantee 2: The Experience Guarantee Removes “I’ll get lost and quit” risk. The reader has been burned by memberships before - signed up, logged in, got overwhelmed, never came back. This guarantee addresses that specifically.

The structure is to name the failure mode (“most memberships are graveyards”), identify what makes your continuity different (“you have the Funnel Oracle guiding you”, “you have a community of serious people alongside you”), and guarantee the outcome (“you will never feel lost, overwhelmed, or alone”).

Guarantee 3: The Results Guarantee Removes “what if it doesn’t work for me” risk. This is the boldest of the three. You’re guaranteeing a specific business outcome.

From my Marketing Secrets VSL: If you actually USE the Dream 100 software. If you follow what I’m sharing inside the Backstage Pass. If you reach out to influencers and start building partnerships. One deal will pay for YEARS of your membership.

And if you put in the work - actually USE what’s inside - and you STILL haven’t landed a single partnership after 12 months? Email me. Show me you did the work. And I’ll refund every penny you paid.

The Results Guarantee is bold because you’re putting your money on the line for their outcome. It also filters out customers who weren’t going to do the work anyway - they self-select out because they know they won’t qualify for the guarantee.

Use all three when you can. If you have to drop one, drop the Results Guarantee (it’s the most expensive to honor). Always keep the Financial and Experience guarantees.

Why This Order One more note on sequencing. The order is bribe → urgency → guarantee. Not bribe → guarantee → urgency. Not guarantee → bribe → urgency.



<!-- Page 28 -->



The logic: the bribe reframe releases skepticism about the offer being “too good to be true.” Once that’s released, urgency can build anxiety about waiting. Once the anxiety is high, guarantees resolve it.

If you reverse any of these steps, the emotional arc breaks. Guarantees delivered before urgency just calm the reader down without anything at stake. Urgency delivered before the bribe reframe just feels manipulative because the “too good to be true” objection hasn’t been resolved.

Stick to the sequence. Bribe. Urgency. Guarantee. In that order.

The Two Paths Close Most sales letters end with a push. “Click now.” “Buy today.” “Limited time.” The Continuity VSL doesn’t end with a push. It ends with a fork.

By Beat 12, the reader has seen the premium, the stack, the bribe reframe, the urgency, and the guarantees. Every rational objection has been addressed. What’s left is the emotional moment of decision.

The Two Paths close is designed to make that moment concrete. The Fork Structure The move is simple. Force the reader to visualize both futures - the one where they don’t act, and the one where they do. Make both futures specific. Not abstract benefits (“you’ll succeed”) but concrete daily realities (“tomorrow morning you’ll log in and see what I’m sharing this week”).

From my Marketing Secrets VSL: Right now, you’re standing at the door. You can walk through it. Or you can walk away. If you walk away… Tomorrow you’ll wake up and keep doing what you’ve been doing. Consuming content. Watching from the outside. Wondering what the people who are ‘making it’ know that you don’t. A year from now, you’ll probably be in the same place. Still learning. Still planning. Still waiting for the ‘right time.’ And the people who stepped through the door today? They’ll be a year ahead of you. If you walk through… Tomorrow you’ll log in and see what I’m sharing THIS week. You’ll open the Dream 100 software and find influencers you didn’t know existed. You’ll tell the Oracle where you’re stuck and get the exact training you need.

You’ll be in the room. Finally. And a year from now? You’ll look back at today as the day everything changed.

Notice the specificity. The cost of inaction isn’t “you’ll keep struggling.” It’s “you’ll be in the same place a year from now, still learning, still planning.” The reward of action isn’t “you’ll succeed.” It’s “tomorrow you’ll log in and see what I’m sharing this week.”

Specific futures land because the reader can picture themselves inside them. Generic futures don’t land because the reader can’t see themselves there.

The “Maybe” Close After the fork, the final ask. And here’s where most sales writing gets the psychology backwards.

Most closes push the reader toward a “yes.” “Are you ready to commit? Buy now.” The Continuity VSL doesn’t ask for a yes. It asks for a “maybe.”

I’m Not Asking You To Say ‘Yes.’ I’m asking you to say ‘maybe.’ Maybe this is the thing that finally breaks me out of Room #1. Maybe this is the access I’ve been missing. Maybe this is worth 14 days of my time to find out. That’s all I’m asking. 14 days. Free. Full access. See for yourself.

Why “maybe” instead of “yes”? Because the reader isn’t being asked to commit to a subscription forever.

They’re being asked to try a 14-day free trial. The decision isn’t “am I the kind of person who joins this membership?” - that’s an identity-level decision. The decision is “am I willing to test this for two weeks?” - that’s a try-it-level decision.

Nearly every reader who made it this far can say yes to the lower threshold. The commitment is small. The risk is zero. The upside is knowing whether this thing is right for them.

This move converts dramatically better than the hard “yes” close because it matches the actual decision the reader is being asked to make. You’re not asking them to commit to $97 a month for the rest of their life. You’re asking them to try the trial. Frame it that way.

The Literal Ask After the fork and the “maybe,” the literal instruction. What specifically do they click, what do they enter, what happens next?

Here’s What To Do Next. Click the button below. Enter your information. And start your 14-day free trial of Marketing Secrets. The moment you’re in, you’ll get instant access to everything. The Backstage Pass. The Dream 100 software. The Funnel Oracle. The full training vault. It’ll all be waiting for you.

Three commands, in order. Click. Enter. Start. Then a reminder of what they’ll find on the other side of the click. And a final line of connection: I’ll see you in the room.

Or, in the Influencer Secrets VSL: “I’ll see you on the inside.” That last line is doing emotional work that logic can’t do. It’s a promise. It’s a welcome. It positions the reader as someone who’s about to become part of something - not someone who’s about to make a purchase. The reader crosses the threshold expecting to be greeted.



<!-- Page 31 -->



Why This Close Is Different Every other VSL framework I’ve taught you ends differently. The Analogy VSL ends with a belief statement and “so what are you waiting for?” - which pushes the reader forward. The Harmon Brothers video ends with a tag joke and a character moment - which leaves the reader entertained. The Continuity VSL ends with a fork and a “maybe” - which leaves the reader in control.

The difference matters because the Continuity VSL is a longer-term relationship. You’re not asking them to buy a toothbrush. You’re asking them to start paying you every month. The relationship starts the day they click - and the way you close is the first impression of that relationship.

A pushy close makes the reader feel sold to. A fork-and-maybe close makes the reader feel chosen. They made the decision themselves. They own it. And because they own it, they show up differently on day one of the trial. They log in. They use the product. They stay.

The close is doing retention work, not just conversion work. Don’t rush it.

The Creative Director Interview Everything in this book so far has been theory. This chapter is practice. When I sit down to write a Continuity VSL - for the Marketing Secrets continuity or for a client - here’s the workflow I use.

Three phases. Interview, validate, write. Do them in order. Don’t skip phases. A script you write before you’ve chosen the spotlight premium is a script you’re about to throw away.

Phase One: The Ten Questions I ask these ten questions one at a time. I wait for the answer before moving on. I push for specificity when answers are vague. A Continuity VSL depends on concrete proof points, dollar anchors, and specific problems - vague answers produce weak scripts.

Q1. The continuity product What’s the continuity, the price, and the trial terms? I need the exact numbers. Is there a free trial? How long? What happens after the trial?

Q2. The full bonus stack Walk me through every bonus, premium, tool, and component included in the continuity. I need the whole stack because the Stack Pivot depends on the reader learning they’re getting more than just the premium we spotlight.

If the stack has fewer than four or five components, I flag it. This framework depends on the stack reveal being impressive. If the continuity is basically one thing, a different VSL framework is probably the better fit.

Q3. The spotlighted premium for this VSL Which premium from the stack are we spotlighting? Which one is going to be the hook - the thing the first sixty percent of the script treats as if it IS the product?



<!-- Page 34 -->



If the client says “all of them,” I push back. This framework requires ONE spotlight per VSL. If they want multiple angles, that’s multiple VSLs, each spotlighting a different premium.

Q4. The premium’s standalone desirability If we were selling just this premium on its own, as a standalone product, what would it cost? Who would want it? Is this premium desirable enough on its own that someone would pay for it separately? This is the critical filter. If the premium doesn’t pass this test, I strongly consider picking a different spotlight.

Q5. The reader’s current frustration Who is this VSL for, not in demographic terms, but as a specific person with a specific frustration? What’s the feeling they have but couldn’t quite articulate? This powers the diagnostic hook in Beat 1. Push for specificity.

Q6. The binary What are the two worlds? Every Continuity VSL organizes around a binary. Room #1 vs. Room #2. Rented land vs. owned land. Consumer vs. creator. Stuck vs. moving. What’s the binary that puts your reader on the wrong side of the wall and positions your continuity as the bridge across?

Q7. The premium’s origin story Where did this premium come from? Did you build it internally? Did it come from someone else? Is it normally only available at a higher price point? What’s the scarcity anchor? This is Beat 6. The stronger the story, the stronger the beat lands.

Q8. The premium demo Walk me through using the premium as if the reader just logged in for the first time. What do they click?

What do they see? What are the concrete steps? I’m looking for three to six enumerable steps. Q9. The personal proof story What’s the founder’s story that proves they’ve been on both sides of the binary? When they were stuck on the wrong side, what did that look like? What was the moment they crossed over? Three to five sentences, concrete and specific.

Q10. Constraints and voice Anything off-limits? Topics to avoid, competitors to handle carefully, must-include phrases? And what’s the read-aloud runtime target? Default is 20 - 25 minutes. Shorter converts colder traffic slightly better; longer converts warmer traffic and higher-ticket continuities.



<!-- Page 35 -->



Phase Two: Validate the Spotlighted Premium Before writing a word, I run the spotlighted premium through the Four Tests from Chapter 3.

Standalone desirability - would someone pay for this separately? Clear problem fit - does it solve a specific, nameable problem for a subset of the audience? Unique story hook - does it have an origin story dramatic enough to carry Beat 6? Demo-able - can I walk the reader through it step by step?

If the premium passes all four tests, I proceed. If it fails one or more, I flag to the client before writing, and we decide together whether to pick a different premium, compensate in other beats, or restructure how the premium gets described.

Don’t skip this step. A weak spotlight premium produces a weak script. Finding out on delivery is expensive; finding out before writing is free.

Phase Three: Write to the Structure Once the premium is validated, I write the full script using the twelve-beat structure. Word budget on hand. Specific signature moves at each beat. Stack Pivot at the 60% mark - not earlier, not later. Bribe reframe, then urgency, then guarantee. Two Paths close with a “maybe” final ask.

Delivery format is sales-letter - long-form text with section headings, subheadings, short paragraphs, and visual breaks. Works as a written sales page and as a spoken VSL. Single voice from first line to last.
