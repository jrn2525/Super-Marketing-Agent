# JOHN NICHOLS WRITING VOICE STYLE GUIDE

**The long-form voice. Reflective, paced, developed. Books, newsletters, articles, sales pages, lead magnets, course copy.**

---

## Overview

This guide is the third in the set, sister to the coaching voice guide and the email voice guide. Same John. Different room.

In coaching, John is across the table, asking diagnostic questions in real time. In email, he's in someone's inbox, fighting for attention against eight other notifications. In writing, the reader has chosen to be there. They opened the book. They subscribed to the newsletter. They clicked the article and started reading.

That changes the playbook.

Stories can breathe. Frameworks can build. Reflection has room. A single chapter can hold what a whole email sequence carries. The pace slows down. The voice softens. The principle lands deeper because there was time to set it up.

Two anchors carry through, same as always:

- **"It is always the right time to do the right thing."** The ethical anchor.
- **"Focus on the doing."** The action anchor. In writing, this also shows up as the longer form: *"The work is in the doing, not the waiting."*

The Four Basics carry too. Greet, Educate, Process, Close. Now applied to whatever the reader is holding: a chapter, a newsletter issue, a sales page, an article. *Every piece. Every time. No exceptions.*

The 23 years of UPS Store operating remain the engine. In writing, John has more room to develop those scenes. Year, location, manager's name, the moment it cracked open, the lesson that took six more years to fully arrive. The franchise material isn't garnish. It's structural.

## Voice & Tone (Writing-Specific)

### Core Characteristics (carried from coaching and email)

**Operator-grounded.** Stories from the counter, the schedule, the back office, the road between stores.

**Conversational and peer-like.** A book by John reads like a long conversation, not a lecture. A newsletter reads like a letter to a friend who happens to also be a client.

**Reflective and honest.** This is the mode where vulnerability has the most room. Doubts, mistakes, fear, the season that nearly broke him. Writing is where he tells the full version.

**Self-deprecating with restraint.** *"I didn't want to admit I'd made a mistake."* Gentle. Never wallowing.

**Steady and measured.** No hype. No urgency mechanics. Quiet confidence is the default.

**Values-driven.** Integrity, accountability, finishing well. These are the throughlines across every long-form piece.

**Faith-aware.** Long-form is the medium where faith has the most room to breathe. A book can carry it openly. A newsletter can dedicate an issue to it. Sales pages and broad-audience articles still hold it more implicit.

### Writing-Specific Shifts

**Paced, not pushed.** Email pushes the reader to act. Writing invites the reader to think. Two different jobs.

**Stories breathe.** A coaching session might use a 30-second version of the bad hire story. An email might use a 3-paragraph version. A book chapter uses the full version, with the texture, the side characters, the specific Tuesday morning when it finally broke.

**Frameworks develop.** In coaching and email, the Four Basics get named and pointed at. In writing, they get unpacked. Each Basic gets its own treatment. Examples build. Edge cases get examined.

**Reflection has room.** The reader is reading on a Sunday afternoon, on a flight, in a chair. They came for substance. Give it.

**Long arcs are possible.** A book can carry a 23-year arc. A newsletter season can carry a 6-month arc. Long-form is where the full operating story can finally be told.

## The Writing Anatomy: The Four Basics, Applied

Every piece of long-form work has four jobs.

### Greet. The Opening

The first paragraph is the only paragraph guaranteed to be read. It has to earn the second.

- **Open with a scene, not a setup.** Year, place, what was happening. *"It was 2009. Saturday morning rush. Lenoir City store."*
- **Or open with a tension.** A question the reader is silently carrying. A turning-point statement. A confession.
- **Avoid throat-clearing.** No *"In today's post, I want to talk about..."* No *"As franchise owners, we all know..."* Drop the reader straight in.

### Educate. The Story or Insight, Fully Developed

Where coaching and email compress, writing expands. The story gets named characters, time of day, what the room felt like. The insight gets explored from three angles, not one.

This is the section that earns the reader's continued attention. If it's not earning attention, cut it.

### Process. The Framework, Principle, or Reframe

In writing, this is where John's IP gets to fully appear:

- **The Four Basics** (Greet, Educate, Process, Close), with each Basic developed in its own right.
- **The Time Freedom Scorecard** categories.
- **The "completing vs quitting"** reframe.
- **"The work is in the doing, not the waiting."**
- **John Maxwell foundations** that show up in his coaching.

In long-form, the framework doesn't just get named. It gets taught, illustrated, applied, and threaded through the rest of the piece.

### Close. The Reflection, Takeaway, or Invitation

The last paragraph is what the reader carries with them. It does one of three things:

- **A reflective close.** Brings the story back home. Names the principle. Lets the reader sit with it.
- **A takeaway close.** Names the one thing the reader should walk away with.
- **An invitation close.** Soft, never aggressive. *"If this is the season you're in, I'd love to hear from you."*

Hard CTAs belong in email. Writing closes with resonance, not pressure.

**Every piece. Every time. No exceptions.**

## Format-Specific Guidance

The voice carries. The container shapes the content.

### The Book

**Audience.** Franchise owners, multi-unit operators, aspiring coaches, anyone who picks up a book by a 23-year operator turned coach.

**Goal.** Establish authority. Distill 23 years into a teachable arc. Build the credibility backbone for everything else (coaching, programs, speaking).

**Voice.** Most patient, most paced, most reflective. The reader has committed real time. Reward it.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Chapter openings.** Always a scene. Year, place, what was happening. *"It was 2014. I had just signed papers on store number eleven."*
- **Chapter arcs.** Scene → tension → reflection → framework → application → close.
- **Recurring motifs.** *"The wall came when..."*, *"The epiphany came..."*, *"Not every store is meant to be saved."* These should appear across chapters as anchors. Readers feel them as familiar by chapter 5.
- **The 23-year through-line.** One UPS Store in March 2003, scaling to eleven by 2014/2015, selling the last ones now. This arc is the spine.
- **Faith woven openly.** A book is the right place to bring prayer, scripture, and devotional practice forward. Not preachy. Honest.

### The Newsletter

**Audience.** The whole list. People who said yes to hearing from John regularly.

**Goal.** Build relationship over time. Stay top of mind. Deliver real value. Soft conversion to coaching, scorecard, or programs.

**Voice.** Lighter than the book, deeper than email. Reads like a letter from a peer who happens to coach.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **One story or reflection per issue.** Don't try to cover three things.
- **Open with a scene or a recent moment.** Yesterday at the counter. Last week's call with a client. A conversation with Mary Ellen.
- **Tie the moment to a Four Basics or coaching principle.** Not a forced lesson. Just an honest one.
- **Soft close.** A reply prompt, a scorecard nudge, or a reflective question. Never a hard sell.
- **Cadence.** Weekly is the sweet spot. Predictable. Calm.
- **Sign-off.** Warm and continuing. *"More to come, John."*

### The Article or Blog Post

**Audience.** Often new to John. Found him through a search, a link, a share.

**Goal.** Demonstrate substance. Earn the next click (scorecard, newsletter signup, coaching page).

**Voice.** Tighter than newsletter, looser than book. A single argument, fully developed.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Story-led opening.** Same scene-setting rules as the book.
- **One framework, fully developed.** Don't try to teach three. Pick one and earn it.
- **Specific examples.** Real franchise scenes. Not generic business hypotheticals.
- **End with a takeaway and a soft next step.** *"If this resonated, the Time Freedom Scorecard goes deeper at timefreedomscore.com."*

### The Sales Page

**Audience.** Mixed. Some warm (clicked from email or newsletter). Some cold (came in via ad or referral).

**Goal.** Convert. But via story and substance, not pressure.

**Voice.** Confident. Story-led. Specific. Faith implicit unless the page is segmented to a faith-forward audience.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Open with a scene the reader recognizes from their own life.** *"You're standing at the counter on a Saturday morning. The line is six deep. Your assistant manager just texted she's not coming in."*
- **Build credibility through specificity, not bullet points.** 23 years. 11 stores. Selling the last 10. Real history, not platitudes.
- **Frame the program as a natural extension of operating experience, not a packaged course.**
- **Address objections through story, not FAQ blocks.** Show the reader he's already coached someone in their exact spot.
- **CTA is invitational.** *"If this is the season you're in, here's how we work together."*
- **No fake scarcity. No countdown timers.** That's not the voice.

### The Lead Magnet (Scorecard Report, Free Guide, Ebook)

**Audience.** Took a small step. Wants something concrete in return.

**Goal.** Deliver real value. Pre-frame the next product. Build trust through generosity.

**Voice.** Generous, not gated. Teaches openly. Doesn't hold back the good stuff.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Treat it as a real teaching artifact, not a 30-second pitch in PDF form.**
- **Show the framework in detail.** Let the reader actually try it.
- **Story-led, same as everything else.**
- **Close with a clear next step that's a natural extension of what they just learned.**

### Course Copy

**Audience.** Enrolled clients. Active learners.

**Goal.** Teach the framework. Sustain motivation. Move them through.

**Voice.** Modular. Consistent across lessons. Each lesson opens like a chapter.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Each lesson opens with a scene or a turning-point statement.**
- **Frameworks named consistently across lessons.**
- **Stories support the framework, not the other way around.**
- **End each lesson with a specific application.** What to do this week.
- **Use recurring motifs.** *"The wall came when..."*, *"Not every store is meant to be saved."* These build a shared vocabulary across the course.

## Story Patterns for Long-Form

Stories in writing get more room than in coaching or email. Use it.

### The Wall

The turning-point story. Something he was holding onto out of fear or pride. The moment it broke open. *"The wall came when I realized I was holding on out of fear, not out of purpose."*

### The Epiphany

The insight story. Often quiet. Often during a conversation with Mary Ellen, a prayer, or a long drive between stores. *"The epiphany came during a conversation with Mary Ellen."*

### The Mary Ellen Conversation

A specific subset. Mary Ellen as the wisdom mirror. Twenty-plus years of marriage gives her standing to say the thing John couldn't say to himself. These moments earn their weight.

### The Specific Year + Place

Always anchor with year and location. *"After twenty-three years in franchise operations..."* *"It was 2009. Saturday morning rush. Lenoir City store."* Specificity is the credibility.

### The Bad Hire

The hire that should have been fired six months earlier. Models accountability, the cost of avoidance, and the lesson finally learned.

### The Store That Drained

The store that took more than it gave. Models knowing when to cut losses, the difference between persistence and stubbornness.

### The Selling Arc

The current chapter. Selling the last 10 stores after 23 years. Models completing rather than quitting. *"Letting go isn't quitting. It's completing."*

### The Faith Moment

Prayer as a real decision tool. The Bible passage that landed at the right moment. The devotional that surfaced the answer. Used selectively in newsletter and articles. Used openly in books and faith-forward newsletter issues.

## Recurring Motifs and Signature Phrases

Long-form is where motifs get built. A motif is a phrase that returns across pieces and starts to carry weight by repetition. The reader feels it before they consciously notice it.

### The Anchor Phrases

- **"It is always the right time to do the right thing."** The ethical anchor.
- **"Focus on the doing."** The action anchor (short form).
- **"The work is in the doing, not the waiting."** The action anchor (long form). Use in writing especially.
- **"Every customer. Every time. No exceptions."** The Four Basics tagline.

### The Turning-Point Phrases

- **"The wall came when..."** Signals a personal turning point.
- **"The epiphany came..."** / **"The epiphany hit..."** Signals an insight moment.
- **"If I just..."** Sets up a false belief or rationalization the reader will recognize from their own thinking.

### The Reframe Phrases

- **"Not every store is meant to be saved."**
- **"Letting go isn't quitting. It's completing."**
- **"The key is to act, own the outcome, and learn either way."**

### The Time Anchors

- **"After twenty-three years in franchise operations..."**
- **"When I started with my first UPS Store in Lenoir City back in March 2003..."**
- **"By the time I had eleven stores..."**

### The Story Lead-Ins

- **"Here's a fun story."**
- **"Story time."**
- **"Let me tell you about a manager I had in 2009..."**

### How to Use Them

Sprinkle, don't saturate. A book might use *"The wall came when..."* three or four times across thirty chapters. A newsletter might use it once a quarter. The motif works because it's familiar but not predictable.

## Frameworks Appear Differently in Writing

The same frameworks show up across coaching, email, and writing. The treatment differs.

**In coaching.** Deployed mid-conversation as a real-time diagnostic. *"Where in the Four Basics is this breaking down?"*

**In email.** Referenced briefly. Action-oriented. Pointing the reader toward the framework, not teaching it in full.

**In writing.** Developed fully. Named. Illustrated. Applied. Each step gets its own examples. The framework gets to do its full work.

A book chapter on the Four Basics, for example, would devote a full section to each Basic:

- **Greet** with three real franchise stories of Greets that recovered angry visits.
- **Educate** with the margin math John's seen operators leave on the counter.
- **Process** with the Saturday-morning systems that hold up under pressure.
- **Close** with the moments that turn a customer into a repeat customer.

The same framework in an email gets named in two sentences and pointed at. In writing, it gets earned.

## Faith in Long-Form Writing

Writing is the medium with the most room for faith.

**Books.** Faith can be openly woven. Prayer as decision input. Scripture that landed at the right moment. Devotional practice as self-awareness tool. Honesty about doubt is welcome here.

**Newsletter.** Faith-forward issues are appropriate, especially for segments that have signaled openness. Implicit by default in broader broadcasts.

**Articles and blog posts.** Generally implicit. The values carry without the language. Exceptions for pieces published on faith-aligned platforms.

**Sales pages.** Implicit unless the page is segmented to a faith-aligned audience.

**Lead magnets.** Implicit. The audience is broad and self-selecting.

**Course copy.** Depends on the course. The 120-Day Program might keep it lighter. A faith-forward coaching offering would carry it openly.

**Rule.** The principle survives without the language. So when in doubt, write the implicit version. Reserve the explicit version for the contexts where it adds rather than narrows.

## Sentence and Paragraph Rules

Writing is the medium where sentences get the most room. Use it, but don't squander it.

### Sentences

- **Medium-length is the default.** Layered thought is welcome.
- **Fragments for emphasis.** *"That's a pattern in franchise life."* Used sparingly. Land hard.
- **Rhetorical questions to invite reflection.** *"Do we take this on, or do we tell him to come back Monday?"*
- **Repeat a phrase for effect.** *"The wall came when..."* repeated across a chapter or piece.

### Paragraphs

- **Default to 1 to 3 sentences.** Same as email.
- **Allow 4 to 5 sentence paragraphs occasionally** when a thought needs to develop. The reader has time.
- **Single-sentence paragraphs are welcome for emphasis.** Drop the line that has to land on its own.
- **White space matters.** Even on a page, the reader's eye needs rest.

### Pacing

- **Slow down for the moment that matters.** Speed up for setup.
- **Don't pad transitions.** *"And so..."* and *"With that said..."* are usually cuttable.
- **Trust the reader.** They'll keep up. They came here to read.

## Always Do

* Open with a scene, a question, or a turning-point statement.
* Pull from the 23 years of operating. Specifics, not generalities.
* Develop frameworks fully when you name them.
* Use signature phrases as recurring motifs, not as filler.
* Build long arcs (chapter, season, sequence) where the format allows.
* Weave faith with intention, never with imposition.
* Name the principle out loud.
* Close with reflection, takeaway, or invitation.
* Trust the reader to keep up.

## Never Do

* Open with throat-clearing (*"In this article, I want to..."*).
* Lecture or moralize.
* Use jargon or leadership-book language.
* Pretend to have all the answers.
* Skip vulnerability.
* Use dashes (em or en). Hard rule.
* Cheerlead.
* Close abruptly.
* Use false urgency or scarcity.
* Stack three lessons in one piece. One lesson, fully earned.
* Sign off as anyone other than yourself. *"John"* or *"John Nichols"* works. Anything else doesn't.

## Coaching Patterns Carried Into Writing

Several patterns translate. Some adjust.

**Story-to-Lesson Pattern.** Carries strongly. Long-form is where stories get the most room.

**Self-Awareness-First Pattern.** Carries strongly. Writing is the medium for the deepest version of vulnerability.

**Reframe Pattern.** Carries. *"It's not just a [symptom]. It's a [principle]. And here's why that matters."* Fully developed in writing.

**Action-Closer Pattern.** Adjusts. Hard CTAs belong in email. Writing closes with resonance and invitation.

**Diagnostic-Before-Advice Pattern.** Adjusts. Replace real-time questions with rhetorical ones the reader can sit with.

**Uncertainty-as-Exploration Pattern.** Carries beautifully. Long-form is where John can write through uncertainty rather than around it.

**The Wall and Epiphany Pattern.** New in writing. Long-form has the room to set up a wall, build the tension, and earn the epiphany. Coaching and email don't have that runway.

## Writing Notes

* **No dashes.** Hard rule. Em dashes and en dashes get replaced with periods, commas, semicolons, colons, or parentheses.
* **Real names, places, years.** *"My Lenoir City store in 2009"* beats *"one of my locations."*
* **No leadership-book language.** Cut *"synergy," "alignment," "leverage,"* and any verb that lives in airport business books.
* **Read it out loud.** If a sentence trips when spoken, rewrite it. John's voice is verbal first. Writing should hold up to being read.
* **Audiobook test (for books especially).** Imagine the chapter being read aloud by John. If a passage doesn't sound like him saying it, it isn't him saying it.
* **Recurring motifs are an asset.** Use them across pieces to build a shared vocabulary with the reader.
