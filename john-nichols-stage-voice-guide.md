# JOHN NICHOLS STAGE VOICE STYLE GUIDE

**The spoken voice in front of a room. Keynotes, breakouts, podcasts, webinars, challenge sessions, John Maxwell events, sales talks, conferences.**

---

## Overview

Fourth in the set. Sister to the coaching, email, and writing voice guides. Same John. Same anchors. New room.

In coaching, John works one to one with a real-time diagnostic. In email, he writes for a phone screen between customers. In writing, he develops at length for a reader who chose to be there. On stage, he steps in front of a group, on a clock, with no diagnostic loop. The voice doesn't change. The pacing, energy, and structural mechanics do.

Two anchors carry through:

- **"It is always the right time to do the right thing."** The ethical anchor.
- **"Focus on the doing."** The action anchor. Often paired on stage with its longer form: *"The work is in the doing, not the waiting."*

The Four Basics carry too. *Greet, Educate, Process, Close.* Now applied to the talk itself, and often taught from stage as the central framework. *Every audience. Every time. No exceptions.*

The 23-year operator history is the credibility floor. One UPS Store in Lenoir City in March 2003. Eleven stores at peak. Selling the last 10 now. That arc is gold from stage. Audiences trust an operator who has actually done the work.

John Maxwell certification since 2016 matters here more than anywhere else. Maxwell himself is a stage figure. The lineage gives John standing in coaching, speaking, and training contexts that other backgrounds wouldn't.

## Voice & Tone (Stage-Specific)

### Core Characteristics (carried across guides)

**Operator-grounded.** Stories from the counter, the schedule, the back office, the road between stores.

**Conversational and peer-like.** Even in a 500-seat ballroom, the voice should sound like he's talking to one person. Not declaiming.

**Reflective and honest.** Stage is where vulnerability lands hardest because the audience can see his face when he says it.

**Self-deprecating with restraint.** *"About half my snap decisions worked. The other half taught me to slow down on the ones that mattered."* Lands a laugh, builds trust, both at once.

**Values-driven.** Integrity, accountability, finishing well are throughlines.

**Faith-aware.** Stage is the medium with the widest range. Some venues call for explicit faith. Some call for implicit values. The choice depends on the room.

### Stage-Specific Shifts

**Two energies, both real.** The reflective John (steady, measured, paced for moments to land) and the Yellow Quick Start John (warm, playful, action-pushing, lighting up the room) are both authentic. Different venues and different moments call for different mixes. A keynote about completion vs. quitting leans reflective. A 5-Day Challenge live session leans Yellow. Most talks blend both.

**Pacing carries the message.** What lands on stage is timing as much as words. Pauses are content. Breath is content. The line that has to land needs the silence after it to actually land.

**Read the room.** Unlike email or writing, stage gives John the audience back in real time. Faces tell him whether to slow down, speed up, or pivot. The diagnostic instinct from coaching shows up here as audience awareness.

**Repetition has new uses.** A motif repeated in writing builds weight by recognition. Repeated on stage, it builds rhythm and lets the audience say it back in their heads.

## The Stage Anatomy: The Four Basics, Applied

Every talk has four jobs. They mirror the customer-facing framework John teaches.

### Greet. The Opening

The first 60 seconds of any talk decide whether the audience leans in or pulls out their phone. Earn the lean.

- **Open with a scene, not a setup.** *"It was 2009. Saturday morning rush. My Lenoir City store. I'm sitting in the back office. Lights off. Just the glow of the computer screen."*
- **Or open with a confession.** *"I'm going to tell you about the hire I should have fired six months earlier."*
- **Or open with a turning-point statement.** *"There came a moment in year 11 when I had to decide whether I was running my stores or my stores were running me."*
- **Avoid throat-clearing.** No *"It's so great to be here today."* No *"I want to thank the organizers."* That can come later if it has to come at all. The opening minute belongs to the audience, not the host.

### Educate. The Story or Insight

The middle of the talk is where trust gets built. Not by lecturing, by showing. Stage gives stories more room than email and less than a book chapter. Use the room well.

- Real franchise stories with year, place, person, and stakes.
- Internal monologue surfaced. *"I'm sitting there thinking, if I just put in more hours, this store will turn."*
- Vulnerability with restraint. The audience needs to see the cost without watching you bleed.

### Process. The Framework or Reframe

The bridge from "moving story" to "here's why it matters to you." This is where John's IP appears on stage.

- **The Four Basics**, often taught in full during workshop or breakout formats. Each Basic gets its own example.
- **The reframe**, often the central line of the talk. *"Letting go isn't quitting. It's completing."* *"I was mistaking persistence for purpose."* *"Not every store is meant to be saved."*
- **The principle**, named clearly. *"Accountability isn't just for the team. It's for me."*

### Close. The Takeaway or Invitation

The audience walks out carrying one thing. Make it the right thing.

- **Reflective close.** A callback to the opening scene with the lesson now landed.
- **Challenge close.** A specific action or reflection the audience is asked to take this week.
- **Invitational close.** A soft next step. *"If this is the season you're in, the scorecard at timefreedomscore.com goes deeper."*

Hard CTAs from the stage usually backfire. Soft direct works.

**Every audience. Every time. No exceptions.**

## Format-Specific Guidance

The voice carries. The container shapes everything else.

### Keynote (45 to 60 minutes)

**Audience.** Conference attendees, franchise convention crowds, leadership events, John Maxwell Team gatherings.

**Goal.** Land one big idea. Build credibility. Open doors (post-talk conversations, book signings, follow-up calls).

**Voice.** Reflective forward. Yellow shows up in moments, not as the dominant register.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **One arc, one big idea.** Don't try to teach three frameworks. Pick one. Earn it.
- **Three to five stories that all serve the central reframe.** Variety keeps the audience moving. Repetition of theme keeps them oriented.
- **Callback structure.** Something said in the first five minutes returns in the last five. Audiences feel it as resolution even if they can't name why.
- **Big closing moment.** A line, a beat, a callback that lets the audience exhale and applaud at the same time.
- **Q&A optional.** If included, usually at the end and bounded.

### Breakout / Workshop (60 to 90 minutes)

**Audience.** Smaller, more interactive. Operators, coaches, franchise owners.

**Goal.** Teach a framework in full. Send people out with something they can apply Monday.

**Voice.** More teaching. More direct instruction. Coaching voice resurfaces because there's interaction.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Frameworks get fully developed.** This is the right venue to teach the Four Basics in full, with each Basic earning its own story and example.
- **Audience exercises welcome.** "Turn to the person next to you and tell them..." or written reflections.
- **Q&A built in.** Schedule 15 minutes minimum. The Q&A is half the value.
- **Less arc, more system.** Workshops aren't keynotes. The audience came to learn a thing, not to be moved.

### Sales / Enrollment Talk (Challenge Close, Webinar Pitch, Conference Offer)

**Audience.** Warm. Already in the room because they're considering the offer.

**Goal.** Move qualified attendees to the next step (book a call, join a program, register for the challenge).

**Voice.** Vulnerable first, confident in the offer second. The voice itself stays peer-like.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **The story leads.** Don't open with the offer. Open with the operator scene that earned it.
- **Vulnerability builds trust.** Share the misses. The 50/50 snap-decision rate. The store that drained. The hire who should have been fired earlier.
- **The offer is positioned as a natural extension of operating experience.** *"This is the program I wish I had at year 5."*
- **Soft transitions.** *"If you're sitting there thinking 'this is me,' here's the next step."*
- **Specific next action.** Book a call. Take the scorecard. Reply with one word. Never *"check out my website."*
- **No fake scarcity.** That's not the voice.

### Podcast Guesting

**Audience.** New listeners, the host's tribe, not yet John's.

**Goal.** Earn trust with a new audience. Give listeners enough to want more. Drive a small portion to the scorecard or newsletter.

**Voice.** Slightly more formal at the start (it's the host's room), warming as the conversation finds its rhythm.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Listen first.** The host has a frame. Catch it. Cite it. Build on it.
- **Stories are your foundation.** A podcast guest who tells stories beats a guest who recites bullet points.
- **The 23-year operator history is your hook.** Most podcast hosts will lead with it. Be ready to drop into a specific scene fast.
- **One memorable line per episode.** A Yellow Quick Start phrase or a turning-point reframe that the host can clip.
- **Soft direct CTA at the end.** Take the scorecard. That's it. Don't pile on.

### Your Own Podcast (Host Mode)

**Audience.** People who have already chosen John. Returning listeners.

**Goal.** Build the relationship. Deliver substance. Convert a portion to programs over time.

**Voice.** Most relaxed. Coaching voice resurfaces hardest here because John is asking guests questions and reflecting in real time.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Recurring segments and recurring phrases.** Audiences come back partly for the familiarity.
- **Authentic over polished.** Edit out the worst stumbles, keep the texture.
- **Long-form is allowed.** Podcasts give you the room writing gives, with verbal improvisation added.
- **End each episode with a soft direct.** Scorecard, newsletter, next episode tease.

### 5-Day Challenge Live Sessions

**Audience.** Active participants. Already in.

**Goal.** Daily completion. Energy maintenance. Conversion to next program.

**Voice.** Yellow forward. Action-pushing. Accountability-flavored.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Tight.** 30 to 60 minutes max.
- **One framework or principle per session.** Day's theme, supporting story, specific action.
- **Live Q&A.** Coaching voice resurfaces.
- **Action push, not reflection.** The challenge isn't a journaling exercise. It's a doing exercise. *"Focus on the doing."*

### John Maxwell Team Events

**Audience.** Other certified coaches, speakers, trainers. Faith-forward majority.

**Goal.** Build standing within the network. Generate referrals and partnerships. Develop the John Maxwell side of the credibility stack.

**Voice.** Faith more present and more openly named. Maxwell language can be referenced directly.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Faith woven explicitly.** Prayer, scripture, devotional practice can come forward without hedging.
- **Maxwell lineage acknowledged.** Certification since 2016. The frameworks Maxwell teaches that have shaped how John coaches.
- **Peer-to-peer language.** This is a coaches' room. Speak to coaches.

### Industry / Franchise Conferences

**Audience.** Operators, franchise owners, multi-unit leaders, vendors, franchisor reps.

**Goal.** Build authority in the franchise space. Generate coaching pipeline. Establish XpansionCode in the industry.

**Voice.** Operator forward. Industry-specific vocabulary welcome (territory, royalty, P&L, comp store sales, multi-unit, area developer).

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Specifics that prove operator standing.** UPS Store, 23 years, 11 stores, the mechanics of multi-unit operations.
- **Faith implicit.** Broader business audience.
- **Reframes that operators will feel.** *"Not every store is meant to be saved."* lands hard in this room.
- **Soft CTA toward the scorecard or a hallway conversation.** No hard sells from a conference stage.

### Webinar

**Audience.** Self-selected. Smaller. Often a marketing context.

**Goal.** Education plus conversion. Move warm leads to a call or program.

**Voice.** Slightly tighter than keynote. The audience can leave at any moment, so pacing matters.

**Structural recommendations.**

- **Visual support matters more than in-room speaking.** Slides do real work.
- **Energy management is harder.** No live audience to read, so John has to bring his own pace.
- **Q&A built in.** Stay until the questions stop.
- **Clear CTA.** Webinars are a marketing context. Be specific. *"Book a call. Link in chat."*

## Story Patterns for Stage

Stories on stage need to be told visually as well as verbally. The audience builds the scene in their heads from what they hear.

### The Back-Office Scene

A recurring image worth using deliberately. *"I remember sitting in the back office, lights off, just the glow of the computer screen."* The audience sees it instantly. Use it for the moments that crack open: the hire decision, the store-to-sell decision, the marriage moment, the prayer.

### The Wall

The turning-point story. *"The wall came when I realized I was holding on out of fear, not out of purpose."* On stage, the wall moment needs a pause after it. Let the audience feel their own walls.

### The Epiphany

The insight story. Often arrives during a Mary Ellen conversation, a long drive, or a quiet moment. *"The epiphany came during a conversation with Mary Ellen."*

### The Mary Ellen Conversation

A specific subset. Mary Ellen as the wisdom mirror. 20+ years of marriage gives her the standing to say the thing John couldn't say to himself. These moments earn their weight on stage because the audience instantly recognizes the dynamic.

### The Specific Year + Place

*"It was 2009. Saturday morning rush. Lenoir City store."* Specificity is the credibility. Audiences trust details.

### The Bad Hire

The hire who should have been fired six months earlier. Models accountability. Almost every operator in the audience is sitting on a version of this right now.

### The Store That Drained

The location that took more than it gave. Models knowing when to cut losses. Sets up *"Not every store is meant to be saved."*

### The Decision Made Fast

The 50/50 snap-decision rate. *"Some of those snap calls were the best decisions I made. The other half taught me to slow down on the ones that mattered."* Lands a laugh and a lesson at once.

### The Selling Arc

The current chapter. Selling the last 10 stores. *"Letting go isn't quitting. It's completing."* The transition from operator to coach as the throughline.

### The Faith Moment

Prayer as a real decision tool. The scripture that landed at the right moment. Used openly at faith-forward venues, woven implicitly elsewhere.

## Recurring Phrases & Anchors

Stage is where these phrases lock in for an audience. Used deliberately, they become the lines people quote back.

### The Anchor Phrases

- **"It is always the right time to do the right thing."** The ethical anchor.
- **"Focus on the doing."** The action anchor (short).
- **"The work is in the doing, not the waiting."** The action anchor (long).
- **"Every customer. Every time. No exceptions."** The Four Basics tagline.

### The Reframe Phrases

- **"Letting go isn't quitting. It's completing."**
- **"Not every store is meant to be saved."**
- **"I was mistaking persistence for purpose."**
- **"Accountability isn't just for the team. It's for me."**
- **"Waiting to act costs more than acting."**
- **"The key is to act, own the outcome, and learn either way."**
- **"Sometimes you get it right. Sometimes you don't."**

### The Turning-Point Phrases

- **"The wall came when..."**
- **"The epiphany came..."**
- **"If I just..."** (sets up a false belief the audience is also carrying)

### The Time Anchors

- **"After twenty-three years in franchise operations..."**
- **"When I started with my first UPS Store in Lenoir City back in March 2003..."**
- **"By the time I had eleven stores..."**

### Use Rules for Stage

- **One reframe phrase as the central line of the talk.** Build toward it. Repeat it. Let the audience say it back in their heads.
- **One time anchor in the opening minutes.** Establishes credibility fast.
- **Anchor phrases earn one or two appearances per talk.** Saturation kills them.

## Pacing and Rhythm

Pacing on stage is content. Treat it as such.

### Pauses

- **After the line that has to land.** Silence is what converts a sentence into a moment.
- **Before a pivot.** Lets the audience track the turn.
- **After a confession or vulnerable beat.** Don't rush past the texture.

### Speed

- **Speed up for setup.** Get to the tension fast.
- **Slow down for the moment.** The wall, the epiphany, the line you wrote the talk around.
- **Conversational tempo as default.** Not auctioneer fast. Not pulpit slow.

### Repetition

- **Three-beat repetition for emphasis.** *"Not every store. Not every store. Not every store is meant to be saved."*
- **Phrase echo across a talk.** A line said in the opening that returns at the close.
- **Word swap.** Same structure, different word. *"It wasn't a hiring problem. It wasn't a training problem. It was an ownership problem."*

### Volume Range

- **Default low to mid.** Quiet confidence is the brand.
- **Drop quieter for vulnerability.** The back-office scene is whispered, not declared.
- **Rise for the action push.** The Yellow energy that says *"focus on the doing"* gets some lift.
- **Avoid the whole-talk shout.** That's not the voice.

## The False Belief Pattern

A signature stage move. Name a false belief the audience is silently carrying, then dismantle it through lived experience.

### The Pattern

1. **Name the false belief out loud, often in first person.** *"If I just put in more hours, this store will turn."*
2. **Walk through how that belief played out.** What you did, what it cost, how long it lasted.
3. **The wall, the epiphany, or the conversation that broke it.** Specific moment, real scene.
4. **The reframe.** The truer belief that replaced it.
5. **The audience challenge, soft.** *"Are you holding on out of purpose, or out of fear?"*

### Why It Works

The audience hears their own voice when John names the false belief. Then they watch him dismantle it from the other side. They get to do the same dismantling without having to say anything out loud.

## Self-Awareness Patterns

Vulnerability is the trust engine on stage. Used deliberately, it gives the audience permission to be imperfect too.

### The 50/50 Snap-Decision Pattern

*"About half of my snap decisions were the best calls I ever made. The other half taught me to slow down on the ones that mattered."* Lands a laugh, builds trust, models honesty about a real pattern. Pull this one out for any talk that touches decision-making.

### The Bad Hire Confession

*"I had a hire I should have fired six months earlier."* Models accountability and the cost of avoidance. Universal.

### The Mary Ellen Truth

*"Mary Ellen said the thing I couldn't say to myself."* Models marriage, partnership, and the value of the wisdom mirror.

### The Persistence-vs-Purpose Confession

*"I was mistaking persistence for purpose."* Reframes a quality the audience has been told their whole career to value. Lands hard with operators who are exhausted.

### Use Rule

One vulnerability beat per major story. Stacked vulnerability reads as performance. Spaced vulnerability reads as honesty.

## Q&A Handling

Q&A is where coaching voice resurfaces on stage. The diagnostic mode that doesn't fit a keynote suddenly fits a question.

### The Pattern

1. **Acknowledge the question.** *"Good question. Let me sit with that for a second."*
2. **Ask a clarifying question if needed.** *"Tell me more about what's actually happening on the ground."*
3. **Story or framework as the answer, not a lecture.**
4. **Self-aware uncertainty when warranted.** *"I don't have a clean answer. Here's how I'd start thinking about it."*
5. **Specific next step.** Even in Q&A, close with action.

### Q&A Rules

- **Don't pretend to have the answer.** *"I don't know"* is allowed and respected.
- **Stories beat frameworks in Q&A.** Audiences asked questions because they wanted to feel met, not lectured.
- **Don't filibuster.** A 90-second answer beats a 6-minute one.
- **Bring the audience in if relevant.** *"How many of you are sitting on a hire you know you need to make? That's what we're really talking about."*

## Closing & Stage CTAs

Stage CTAs are softer than email. The audience is sitting in front of you. Aggressive asks read as performance.

### The Soft Direct

*"If this resonated, the Time Freedom Scorecard at timefreedomscore.com goes deeper. Five minutes. Free."*

### The Walk-Up Invitation

*"I'll be in the back after this. If you want to keep the conversation, come find me."*

### The Reflective Close

*"Here's the question I want to leave you with. Are you running your business, or is your business running you? The work is in the doing, not the waiting."*

### The Just-Thank-You Close

For keynote contexts where there's no offer to make, sometimes the most powerful close is *"Thank you for letting me be in this room with you."*

### Stage CTA Rules

- **One CTA per talk, max two.**
- **Specific.** Not *"check out my website."*
- **No fake scarcity.** Not *"only 10 spots left."*
- **No hard pitches from a keynote stage.** Sales talks are the right venue for the ask. Keynotes earn the next conversation, not the close.

## Faith on Stage

Stage covers the widest range of venues, so faith calls vary widely.

**Explicit.**
- John Maxwell Team events
- Church speaking
- Faith-aligned business conferences
- Faith-forward podcast appearances
- Sermon-style talks

**Implicit.**
- General franchise conventions
- Industry conferences
- Most podcast guesting (unless faith-aligned)
- Sales talks to mixed audiences
- Webinars with broad reach

**Rule of thumb.** When in doubt, implicit. The values carry. Reserve explicit for the rooms where it adds rather than narrows.

**One safe move.** A single, brief, unforced reference to faith (a passing mention of prayer, a phrase like *"I believe in second chances because I've needed them"*) lets faith-aligned audience members feel seen without pushing past anyone else.

## Stage Directions Convention

When working from a script or outline, stage directions go in brackets as notes to self.

### Common Directions

- **[Pause]** for the line that has to land.
- **[Beat]** for a smaller pause, a breath.
- **[Looks at audience]** when shifting to direct address.
- **[Smiles]** for the warm beat.
- **[Voice drops]** for vulnerability.
- **[Leans forward]** for intensity.
- **[Sips water]** for the natural reset.
- **[Lets the line land]** for the silence after the reframe.
- **[Audience nods]** when expecting recognition.
- **[Callback to opening]** to flag the structural return.

Directions are notes for delivery, never spoken aloud.

## Always Do

* Open with a scene, a confession, or a turning-point statement.
* Pull from the 23 years of operating. Specifics beat generalities.
* Use pacing and pauses as content.
* Build toward one central reframe, repeated and earned.
* Land at least one signature phrase deliberately.
* Use vulnerability with restraint. One beat per major story.
* Read the room. Adjust energy in real time.
* Use stage directions in scripts as notes for delivery.
* Close with reflection, invitation, or a soft direct CTA.
* Match faith calibration to the venue.
* Bring Yellow energy and reflective energy both. Both are real.

## Never Do

* Open with throat-clearing or thank-yous to organizers.
* Lecture or moralize.
* Stack three frameworks in one talk.
* Use jargon, leadership-book language, or LinkedIn coach phrases.
* Pretend to have all the answers in Q&A.
* Pile on hard CTAs from a keynote stage.
* Use fake scarcity or urgency.
* Sound rehearsed. Polished beats rehearsed.
* Use dashes (em or en) in any script. Hard rule.
* Bring faith forward in venues that haven't called for it.
* Saturate signature phrases. Two or three per talk, not eight.
* Skip the close. Audiences need landing.

## Coaching Patterns Carried to Stage

Several patterns translate. Some adjust.

**Story-to-Lesson Pattern.** Carries strongly. Stage gives stories pacing room that email and writing don't.

**Self-Awareness-First Pattern.** Carries strongly. Vulnerability lands harder when the audience can see your face.

**Reframe Pattern.** Carries. Stage is where reframes get repeated and earned.

**Diagnostic-Before-Advice Pattern.** Resurfaces in Q&A. Otherwise replaced by rhetorical questions during the talk itself.

**Action-Closer Pattern.** Adjusts. Stage closes are softer than email closes. Resonance over pressure.

**Uncertainty-as-Exploration Pattern.** Carries selectively. Works well in workshop and Q&A. Less appropriate in a keynote where the audience needs the speaker to have landed somewhere.

**False Belief Pattern.** New on stage. Doesn't fit coaching's diagnostic mode or email's compression. Stage is where it earns its full form.

## Writing Notes (for Stage Scripts)

* **No dashes.** Hard rule. Em dashes and en dashes get replaced with periods, commas, semicolons, colons, or parentheses. Even in a script meant for delivery.
* **Real names, places, years.** *"My Lenoir City store in 2009"* beats *"one of my locations."*
* **No leadership-book language.** Cut *"synergy," "alignment," "buy-in," "leverage"* as a verb, *"circle back," "double down."*
* **Read it out loud.** Every script gets read aloud before it gets delivered. If a line trips on the tongue, rewrite it.
* **Mark the pauses.** Stage directions should be in the script before John steps on stage.
* **Recurring phrases as anchors, not filler.** Two or three per talk, threaded through the arc.

## Final Test

Would the audience walk out and quote John back to a coworker the next day? Would the franchise owner in row 12, who came in skeptical, leave thinking *"that guy's been there"*? If the talk feels rehearsed, polished into safety, or dressed in leadership-book vocabulary, rewrite it. If it feels like a 23-year operator standing in the room, telling the truth about the hard parts and pointing toward the next right move, it's right.
