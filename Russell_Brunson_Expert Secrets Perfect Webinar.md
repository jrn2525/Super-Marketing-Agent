## Chapter One

### What The Perfect Webinar Actually Is

BE F OR E I WR OTE A S I N GL E S L I DE of my first webinar, I spent years just watching other people sell from stages.

I’d sit in the back of the room at events with a notebook in my lap, and I’d write down everything: what they opened with, how they transitioned into teaching, where they put the stories, how they handled the pitch, and what they said when the room went quiet.

I started to notice something interesting, which was that the best presenters all did basically the same thing. Not the same words, and not the same stories, but the same structure over and over again.

And the presenters who bombed? They were missing pieces. They’d nail the opening and forget the close, or they’d have a great offer and no story to support it, or they’d teach so much that by the time they pitched, the audience was thinking, “Cool, I can do this myself.”

That’s when I started to realize that selling one to many isn’t a performance. It’s a structure. One to One vs. One to Many Here’s something I want you to understand up front.

There’s one-to-one selling, which is what most people are used to. You sit across from somebody, you talk to them face to face, and you sell them one on one. You hear their objections in real time, you answer their questions, and you read their body language and adjust.

I’m not good at that. If you saw a picture of me trying to sell one on one, you’d laugh. I look awkward, and introverted Russell doesn’t know what to say. I’m fine on a stage in front of 9,000 people, but the second someone walks up to me off stage and says hi, I forget how to talk.



<!-- Page 9 -->



What I’m good at, and what I’m going to teach you in this book, is one-to-many selling. One to many is different. You’re not having a conversation, you’re giving a presentation. You can’t ask your audience what their concerns are, and you can’t stop and adjust to each person. Instead, you have to anticipate every concern before they think it, answer every objection before they raise it, and do it all at scale from a stage or a webcam in a way that works whether you’re in front of ten people or ten thousand.

That’s why the Perfect Webinar exists. It’s a script built to handle all of that for you. Every section exists because it solves a specific problem, every transition is where it is because of what the audience is feeling at that moment, and every trial close is a micro-agreement that stacks up so by the time you ask for money, the audience has already said yes hundreds of times in their head.

You don’t have to be a great salesperson to make this work. You just have to follow the structure. The Four Phases The Perfect Webinar runs 90 to 120 minutes and breaks into four phases, and each phase has a specific psychological purpose. If you skip a phase, your conversion drops. If you rush a phase, you leave money on the table. And if you overdo a phase, the audience checks out before the pitch.

Here’s the whole thing at a glance: Time Phase Purpose 0 - 10 min Missing Piece Story Hook the audience so they stay for the pitch Origin Story + New Kill the Old Way, introduce the new vehicle, 10 - 25 min Opportunity establish authority Break the three false beliefs that stop people from 25 - 60 min The Three Secrets buying 60 - 90 min Stack & Close Present the offer, anchor the price, ask for the sale The second close, where 90% of sales actually 90 - 120+ min Q&A + Objection Handling happen Let me walk you through what each phase is doing.

Phase 1: The Missing Piece Story. This is the first ten minutes, and it’s where most webinars die. Most people open with a sob story or a screenshot of their bank account, and the audience checks out. Instead,



<!-- Page 10 -->



you open with a case study about someone else, someone who’d tried everything, failed, and then found the one missing piece. The goal of this phase is to make every person in the audience think, “This could be MY missing piece.” If they don’t think that, they don’t stay.

Phase 2: Origin Story and New Opportunity. Now you reveal what the missing piece is, and you tell the story of how you discovered it. This is where you kill the Old Way with math and data, introduce a completely new vehicle, and position yourself as a reluctant hero who figured it out. By the end of this phase, the audience has claimed a new identity: they’re no longer someone who tried and failed, they’re someone who’s about to try something they’ve never tried before.

Phase 3: The Three Secrets. This is the teaching section. The audience has three objections that would stop them from buying, even if they love you: the first is that the strategy won’t work, the second is that they personally can’t do it, and the third is that some external thing (like time or money or a spouse) will stop them. Each Secret demolishes one of those objections, and each one teaches strategy, not tactics.

You show them the map from 30,000 feet, but you don’t give them the turn-by-turn GPS, because the GPS is what they buy.

Phase 4: The Stack and the Close. This is the section that transformed my sales from $30,000 per event to $180,000 in a single presentation. You present the offer as a cumulative stack, anchor the price against the full value, and use trial closes, guarantee language, and a fast action bonus to make buying the obvious next step. I tested removing this phase once, and sales bombed. Then I put it back, and sales crushed again. This phase is not optional.

Phase 5: Q&A and Objection Handling. Most presenters end at the price reveal. Big mistake. For my first ten or twelve years doing this, I did the same thing. Then I started extending my presentations with ten or fifteen minutes of after-selling. When I looked at the data, 90 percent of my sales came from after the price reveal. Not from the pitch itself. From the Q&A. So we’ve built a thirty-minute Q&A structure that works whether you’re live or automated, using pre-built questions, the “you’re probably thinking” technique, and a social proof parade that keeps the offer in front of the audience until the countdown clock hits zero.

That’s the Perfect Webinar in one page. The Close Starts at Minute One Here’s the thing most people don’t understand about this framework.

The close doesn’t start at minute 60 when you reveal the offer. The close starts at minute one, the second you say your first word.

Every story, every trial close, every teaching moment, every transition is part of the close. You’re not teaching first and then selling. You’re selling the entire time. You’re just not asking for money yet.



<!-- Page 11 -->



By the time you reveal the price, the audience should have said “yes” to you so many times that the price is just one more yes in a sequence they’re already deep inside.

If that sounds like manipulation, let me reframe it. Your audience came to the webinar because they have a problem. You have a solution. Your job isn’t to trick them into buying. Your job is to make sure they don’t leave without the solution just because you weren’t good enough at presenting it.

A webinar that doesn’t close people who should buy is doing them a disservice. That’s the mindset shift.

You’re not selling at them. You’re helping them decide. Strategy vs. Tactics This is the one rule that separates webinars that convert from webinars that fall flat. I’m going to repeat it in every chapter of this book because it’s that important.

Your webinar teaches strategy. Your product delivers tactics. Strategy is the map. The framework. The logic of why something works. You can teach strategy in a 90- minute presentation.

Tactics are the turn-by-turn directions. The click-by-click execution. The step-by-step implementation.

That’s what your product is for. That’s what people pay for. If you teach tactics in your webinar, here’s what happens. The audience thinks, “Cool, I’ve got it. I can do this myself.” And they leave. They don’t buy.

If you teach strategy, here’s what happens. The audience thinks, “I see the map. I get why this works. But I need the tactics to actually do it.” And they buy.

Every section of this framework is designed to make this distinction crystal clear. In the Three Secrets, you show them the shape of the strategy. In the Stack and Close, you show them what the tactical product looks like without giving away the actual tactics.

When you get this right, people buy because they feel like they need the tactics you’re selling. When you get it wrong, people leave because they think they already have what they need.

Time objections don’t matter because the cost of waiting is higher. Self-doubt doesn’t matter because your framework handles execution.

## Chapter Two

### The Foundation

Your New Opportunity, Headline, Three Secrets, and Stack Slide BE F OR E I WR OTE MY F I R S T VE R S I ON of the Perfect Webinar, I used to try to draft webinars the way most people try to draft webinars. I’d open a blank document and start writing from the beginning, going from the opening to the first story to the first teaching point to the second teaching point.

About twenty minutes in, I’d look at what I’d written and realize I was lost. I didn’t know where I was going, I didn’t know what the audience was supposed to believe by the end, and I didn’t even know what I was selling clearly enough to write toward it.

So I’d scrap it and start over, and then scrap it and start over again. Here’s what I figured out eventually: you can’t write a Perfect Webinar from the top down. You have to build the skeleton first.

The skeleton is four pieces: your new opportunity, your One Thing headline, your Three Secrets headlines, and your Stack Slide. Once you have those four pieces nailed down, everything else in the webinar hangs off of them. The Missing Piece Story points at the new opportunity, the Origin Story reveals the new opportunity, Secret One, Two, and Three each hold up one corner of the new opportunity, and the Stack Slide is what you’re actually selling.

In this chapter, we’re going to build all four pieces. This is the most important chapter in the book, because if you get this part wrong, nothing else in your webinar will work. But if you get this part right, the rest of the script practically writes itself.

Russell Brunson has famously brought strangers up on stage at live events, asked them five questions about their offer, and written these four pieces on a whiteboard on the spot. Then he recorded a complete webinar pitch for their product, flawlessly, right there in the room.

If you have these four pieces, you have a webinar. Let’s build yours.



<!-- Page 15 -->



The New Opportunity Here’s the single most important strategic decision in your business: you are not selling an improvement to what your audience is already doing. You are selling a completely new vehicle to get the result they want.

This sounds simple, but it’s not. Most people get this wrong, because they think they’re selling a new opportunity when what they’re actually selling is an improvement. They say things like, “a better way to build your email list,” or, “a faster way to lose weight,” or, “a more efficient sales system.”

Those are all improvement offers, and improvement offers convert badly. Here’s why. An improvement offer forces your audience to admit they’ve failed at the thing before, which lowers their status. It basically says, “You’ve been trying, but not hard enough.” People don’t want to buy that, because they don’t want to be reminded they failed.

A new opportunity does the opposite. It raises status, because it says, “You weren’t failing, you were just using the wrong vehicle. Here’s a completely different one.” Now they don’t have to admit defeat, and they can try something new and fresh with no baggage.

Let me give you some examples. I didn’t offer “better websites.” I said, “Websites are dead, here’s what replaces them. Funnels.”

Steve Jobs didn’t make a better CD player. He introduced the iPod and said, “1,000 songs in your pocket.”

Tesla didn’t make a better car. They made an electric car and created a new category. Atkins didn’t offer a better diet. He said, “Ketosis is a completely different way your body processes energy.”

See the pattern? In every case, the new opportunity is categorically different from what came before. It’s not faster, better, or cheaper. It’s new.

The test is simple. If you can describe your offer using any word ending in “er,” you have an improvement offer. Better, faster, cheaper, smarter, easier, those are all improvement words, and they all decrease status.

Your new opportunity has to be a different vehicle altogether.



<!-- Page 16 -->



If you read what you’ve written so far and it sounds like an improvement, don’t panic. You probably have a genuine new opportunity hiding inside what you’ve built, you just need to reframe it. Ask yourself this question: “What does my approach do that none of the alternatives do?”

Then ask: “If I had to give my method a completely new category name that didn’t exist before, what would it be?”

Keep pushing until the reframe clicks. The Big Domino Once you have your new opportunity, you can write your Big Domino statement. Remember, this is the single sentence your entire webinar is built around.

Fill this in: “If I can make them believe that [my new opportunity] is the key to [what they want most], and it’s only attainable through [my specific framework], then all other objections become irrelevant and they have to give me money.”

Once you’ve written this, read it out loud. If you genuinely believe your audience would buy the moment they believed that sentence, your Big Domino is set correctly. If you could still imagine them hesitating, the Domino isn’t sharp enough yet, so rewrite it.

Every headline, every secret, every story you write from this point forward has one job: to push that domino.

The One Thing Headline Your One Thing headline is your webinar title. It’s the registration page copy, the title slide, the subject line of every promotional email driving traffic to the webinar.

It has one job: to generate enough curiosity that someone sees it at 11 p.m. and thinks, “I need to be on that webinar tomorrow morning.”

Every headline has to pass four tests: The Curiosity Test. Would someone stop scrolling to register? Is something unresolved that they have to find out more about?



<!-- Page 17 -->



The Desire + Fear Test. Does the headline name what they want AND remove what they fear, in one sentence?

The Origin Story Test. Can you naturally follow this headline with, “Let me tell you how I discovered this…”? The headline should open a story, not close one.

The Compliance Test. If this is going in paid ads, does it avoid specific dollar amounts? Use percentages, multiples, or qualitative language instead.

The single most effective headline structure in the history of webinar marketing is what I call the How to Without formula. It wins about 70 percent of split tests: “How to [desired result] without [fear one], without [fear two], and without [fear three].”

Fill in the blanks with your audience’s exact words. Here are a few other structures that work well: “How a [wrong-type-person] [achieved result] in [short timeframe] without [feared mechanism].”

“The [counterintuitive] reason [common approach] isn’t working, and what to do instead.” “Why [common belief] is actually [opposite], and the one thing you need instead.”

Write three or four variations. You don’t have to lock in the winner right now. You’ll test them over time.

The one that converts best is almost never the one you think it will be. The Three Secrets Every Perfect Webinar teaches exactly three Secrets. Not two, not four, just three.

The Stack Slide The Stack Slide is the visual representation of your offer. It’s what you sell in the last thirty minutes of the webinar, it’s the slide that stays up through the Q&A, and it’s the screenshot the audience shares with their spouse.

Every Stack Slide has five or more elements, each one named, and each one with a clearly stated dollar value. The first element is the hero, because it’s the engine of the program, the most exciting piece, and the one most tied to the new opportunity. The rest of the elements support the hero.

Your total stack value should feel about eight to twelve times higher than the actual price. So if you’re selling a $997 program, your total stack value should be roughly $8,000 to $12,000, and if you’re selling a $2,997 program, your stack should total around $24,000 to $36,000. These are substantiated values, meaning you can prove them through market rates or actual sales history. You don’t invent numbers.

Types of elements that work well in a stack:

• Core training or system. The main course, program, or methodology.

• Done-for-you templates or assets. Swipe files, scripts, pre-built funnels.



<!-- Page 19 -->



• Live coaching or access. Q&A calls, office hours, a private community.

• Software or tools. Anything that automates part of the process.

• Physical or tangible items. A book, a workbook, a kit shipped to their door.

• Identity elements. A certification, a badge, membership in a community.

Mix and match across types. The more varied the stack feels, the more complete the offer looks. After all five elements are revealed, your total stack value goes on screen. Then comes the price reveal, which anchors against the total stack, followed by the fast action bonus, which creates real urgency.

Finally, the call to action slide goes up and stays there through the Q&A. The New Identity One last piece before we get to the exercise.

At the end of your Origin Story, which we’ll write in Chapter 4, your audience makes a commitment and claims a new identity. Not what they do, but what they become.

It’s a label they say out loud and type in the chat. Things like: “I am a funnel builder.” “I am living free.”

“I am a Rainmaker.” This is the identity that locks in the commitment, because people who adopt a new identity don’t revert.

Your audience leaves your webinar not as someone who watched a presentation, they leave as someone who became something new.

You’ll need this identity label soon, so decide on it now while you’re building the Foundation.

### The Missing Piece Story

How to Hook Your Audience in the First Ten Minutes I U S E D TO THI N K the pitch was where a webinar was won or lost, but it’s not.

The first ten minutes is where a webinar is won or lost, because if people leave in the first ten minutes, it doesn’t matter how good your pitch is, since they’re not there to hear it.

For a long time, I didn’t take those first ten minutes seriously. I’d open with some version of, “Hey, thanks for being here, my name is Russell, let me tell you a little about myself,” and then I’d launch into my origin story. I thought my origin story was doing the work.

It wasn’t. People don’t want to hear your origin story in the first ten minutes. They don’t know you yet, they don’t care about you yet, and they haven’t given you permission to take up their time.

What they want in those first ten minutes is one thing. They want to know, “Am I in the right place?”

If they decide yes, they stay, and if they decide no, they leave. The Missing Piece Story is designed to answer that question in three to five minutes so convincingly that they lean forward, stay for the rest of the presentation, and literally ask you to keep going.

The One Goal of the First Ten Minutes Every single person watching your webinar must think one thing in the first ten minutes.

“This could be MY missing piece.” Not, “good for them,” not, “who cares,” and not, “impressive stats.” What you want them thinking is, this could be mine.



<!-- Page 24 -->



“Missing piece” is the exact phrase I want you to use, and here’s why: it implies three things at the same time. It implies they’re not broken, they were just missing something. It implies they’re close to success, not far away. And it implies that what’s about to be shown is the specific thing they were missing.

That reframe is the whole game. It takes someone whose brain is saying, “Something is wrong with me, I’ve failed at this too many times to try again,” and turns that voice off. Now they’re saying, “I wasn’t failing, I was just missing one thing, and I’m about to find out what it is.”

That’s the feeling that keeps them watching. The Two Wrong Ways to Open Before I tell you what to do, let me tell you what not to do. Both of these techniques have a place later in the webinar, but neither one belongs in the first ten minutes.

Wrong way number one: the sob story. This is diving into your personal origin story, your struggles, your trials, and your emotional journey. Your audience hears that and thinks, “Good for them. What does this have to do with me?” Your origin story is coming, it’s the next chapter, but it does not belong in the first ten minutes, because the audience hasn’t earned the right to care about you yet.

Wrong way number two: sexy screenshots. This is leading with Stripe screenshots, revenue dashboards, before-and-after transformation photos, or flexing your results. This also gets a “good for them” response, because it’s about you, not about them. And worse, it triggers comparison, because the audience thinks, “Sure, you did that, but I can’t do that.”

Both wrong opens have the same fatal flaw: they’re about you. The Missing Piece Story is about someone else, told in a way that makes the audience see themselves.

The Three-Step Story The Missing Piece Story has exactly three steps, and all you need is one good case study and the willingness to tell their story well.

Step One: Failed Before. Your case study tried everything and still failed. You name specifically what they tried, you include competitors by name, and you describe the emotional low point where they wondered, “Maybe something is wrong with me.”

Step Two: The One Thing. Your case study found you, and you looked at everything they were doing and said, “You are SO CLOSE. You have the exact right X, Y, and Z. You’re just missing one thing.” Then you name the system. You don’t teach it, you just name it.

Step One, Deep Dive: Failed Before Your warmest potential buyers are the people who’ve already tried to solve this problem and failed.

They’re the most likely to buy, but they’re also the most skeptical, because they’ve been burned before.

Your case study is the version of them that tried everything and still failed. Your job is to make the audience see themselves so clearly in that story that they feel seen, not sold.

A few rules for the case study: It has to be about someone else, not you. If it’s your own story, the audience is watching you, but if it’s someone else’s story, the audience is watching themselves. That’s a huge difference.

The person has to feel relatable, not impressive. “Meet Sarah, a Harvard MBA with $500,000 in savings” is wrong, because the audience can’t see themselves in that. “Meet Sarah, a mom of two who tried to lose weight for seven years and nothing worked” is right, because the audience goes, “That’s me.”

Component Three: The Strategy (High Level) Now you teach, but you teach strategy, not tactics. Let me define that one more time, because it’s the dividing line between webinars that convert and webinars that fall flat.

Strategy is the map from 30,000 feet. The shape, the logic, the named steps, the why. Tactics are the turn-by-turn GPS. The exact click-by-click execution, the specific playbook.

In this phase, you teach the audience the map, not the GPS. You show them the route, the landmarks, and the destination, but you do not read them the street names.

The best way to do this is to teach the framework in three to five named steps.



<!-- Page 46 -->



The audience needs to see a shape to the strategy, a diagram, a simple doodle, a framework they can draw on a napkin and explain to a friend. That shape is what makes the framework sticky.

Some examples of what three-to-five named steps looks like: The Hook-Story-Offer framework: first you Hook them with curiosity, then you tell a Story that delivers the insight, then you make an Offer that’s the logical conclusion.

The 5-Phase Missing Piece Story: Welcome, Failed Before, The One Thing, Fast Forward, Mic Drop Moment.

See the pattern? Named steps. Memorable, teachable, and sticky. For each step, you do two things. One, you explain what the step IS, the mechanism, the logic, the why.

Two, you use a kinda-like bridge. For every concept past third-grade level, use an analogy. “This is kinda like…” Kinda-like bridges are how you make complex ideas intuitive.

Here’s an example. If I’m teaching the concept of funnel versus website, I don’t explain it technically. I use a kinda-like bridge.

“A website is kinda like a store at the mall with 15 different entrances. People can come in anywhere, wander around, get distracted, and leave. A funnel is kinda like a straight hallway with one entrance and one door at the end. There’s only one way in, one path through, and one place to go.”

One sentence. Now the whole audience understands the concept. Without me explaining port numbers or opt-ins or conversion rates.

Every concept in Secret One needs a kinda-like bridge. If you don’t have one, write one. If you can’t write one, the concept isn’t clear enough in your own head yet.

Let’s build this correctly the first time. What the Stack and Close Actually Does The Stack and Close is a specific architecture designed to anchor your price against the cumulative value of your entire offer, not against the last thing you mentioned.

Here’s the problem with how most people present offers. Most people reveal their offer like this:



<!-- Page 71 -->



“Here’s my program. Inside the program, you get the main training, plus these three bonuses. And today, it’s just $997.”

That sounds fine, but there’s a problem. The human brain only remembers the last thing you said, so when you reveal the price, the audience’s brain is comparing the $997 to the third bonus, the smallest, least impressive thing in the offer.

Same offer, same bonuses, different anchor, and massively different conversion. That’s what the Stack does.

### Objection Handling And The

Q&A The Second Close, Where 90% of Sales Actually Happen FOR MY F I R S T TEN OR TWE L V E Y E A R S of selling from stages, I did something that cost me more money than any other single mistake.

I would finish my pitch, reveal the price, and end the presentation. “Here’s the offer. Here’s the URL. Thanks for being here. Go buy if you want. See you later.”

I thought that was how it worked. You pitch, you show the offer, you close, and you go home. Then one day, just as an experiment, I decided to keep going after the price reveal. Ten or fifteen minutes of what I started calling after-selling, where I handled some objections, mentioned the guarantee again, and ran through a few more testimonials.

When I pulled the data, I nearly fell out of my chair. Ninety percent of my sales came from after the price reveal. Not from the pitch itself, but from the Q&A.

Nine out of ten buyers needed the post-pitch section to finally pull the trigger. For a decade, I’d been leaving 90 percent of the money on the table.

This chapter is about making sure you never do that. The Mindset Shift Most presenters view the post-pitch Q&A as a gauntlet of skepticism they have to survive, but that’s the wrong frame.

The Q&A is not an annoying afterthought. It’s not a bonus round, and it’s not the part of the presentation where you wait for hostile questions and try not to blow it.



<!-- Page 85 -->



The Q&A is the second close, and it’s the more profitable one. Here’s why it works. The people who bought during the pitch were people who were 80 to 90 percent ready to buy before the webinar started. They needed permission and a clear path, you gave them both, and they bought.

The people who haven’t bought yet are people who are 60 to 70 percent ready. They like the offer, they like you, and they have one or two specific concerns they haven’t resolved yet. They need the Q&A.

And here’s the kicker: the 60-to-70-percent-ready buyer is often your better customer. They’re more analytical, more thoughtful, and higher-value in the long run. The pitch-buyer bought fast because they were already convinced, but the Q&A buyer bought carefully because they were doing the work.

Both are great, but the second close is where the real volume happens. So from this point in the webinar forward, you’re not performing a Q&A, you’re running a second close.

The Countdown Clock Secret Before I tell you what to say in the Q&A, I want to tell you what to put on screen: a thirty-minute countdown clock.

I tested different clock durations, a 10-minute clock, a 15-minute clock, a 30-minute clock, and a 45-minute clock.

Every time I used a clock under 30 minutes, the same thing happened. Sales stopped the moment the clock hit zero.

With a 30-minute clock, sales continued the entire 30 minutes. The clock is urgency, the clock is the reason people keep buying, and the clock is what justifies you continuing to sell after the pitch.

Use a 30-minute countdown clock embedded as a video file in your final CTA slide. It starts the moment you finish your pitch and transition into Q&A, and it stays up the entire time.

Some presenters extend the Q&A to 60 or 90 minutes when engagement is high, and that’s fine. Just make sure the clock is still counting something. As long as there’s urgency on screen and sales are still coming in, you keep going.

The Pre-Built Question Technique Here’s how you fill 30 minutes. Build fifteen to twenty pre-written questions into your slides in advance. You click through them one at a time, as if someone just asked them, you answer each one, you close, and you move to the next one.

Every two to three questions, you do a full URL redirect. “If that was your concern, and you’re ready, go to [URL] right now. Grab your spot before we close this down.” Then you go to the next question.

A - Acknowledge. “That’s a great question,” or, “I get that,” or, “That’s fair,” or, “Yeah, that comes up a lot.” Validate the concern before you address it.

P - Positive Reframe. Redirect the objection into a benefit or context. “Here’s why that’s actually the perfect reason to move forward…” R - Resolve. Answer the question with substance, not platitudes. Use specifics.

C - Close. A micro-commitment or URL. “Does that answer it? Grab your spot at [URL].” Every single answer ends with the URL, every single one, with thirty or more URL mentions across the Q&A.

The “You’re Probably Thinking” Technique Here’s a technique that doubled a webinar I was running a few years ago.

After the standard Q&A, an objection handling expert jumped in and said, “Hey, I’ve been sitting here for two hours. I wrote down questions that came to my mind. Can I address them?”

For the next two hours, he said things like: “When Russell was talking about [X], you were probably thinking [objection], right? Well, here’s the thing…” And then he resolved the concern.

We more than doubled sales in those two extra hours. That’s the “You’re Probably Thinking” technique.

The technique works because you’re surfacing objections your audience hasn’t voiced yet. They’re thinking it, but they haven’t typed it. You read their mind, name the concern, and resolve it, all without them having to speak up.

Here’s how you implement it. After your pre-built questions are done, you say, “You know what, while we were going through this, I wrote down some things I bet you’re thinking about. Let me address them.”

Five or more testimonials, in picture-plus-quote format, back to back. Each one covers a different “but what about me?” scenario, spanning different ages, backgrounds, industries, timelines, obstacles, and starting points.
