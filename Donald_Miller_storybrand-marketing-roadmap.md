# StoryBrand Marketing Roadmap — Marketing Agent Reference

Operational companion to `storybrand-framework.md`. Where the framework reference covers the **what** (SB7, BrandScript, messaging principles), this reference covers the **how** — the five marketing assets that turn a clear message into a working business growth system, plus testimonial gathering and freelance-business operations.

The roadmap assumes a completed BrandScript exists. Every asset below pulls its copy directly from BrandScript elements.

---

## The Six Core Disciplines

The roadmap defines six core disciplines that, together, form a complete marketing system:

1. **Create a StoryBrand BrandScript** — the foundational message (covered in framework reference)
2. **Wireframe a website** — the conversion hub
3. **Write an email nurturing campaign** — the trust-building engine
4. **Write an effective sales letter** — the conversion document
5. **Write a lead-generating PDF** — the email list builder
6. **Gather customer testimonials** — the authority asset library

A bonus discipline — **Making money as a copywriter** — covers running this work as a business.

The order matters. Each asset depends on the one before it. Don't start the website without the BrandScript; don't start emails without a lead generator to capture the addresses; don't write a sales letter without a nurture campaign warming up the audience.

## Discipline 2: Wireframe a Website

### Core Principle

People don't read websites — they scan them. The site must be clean, clear, compelling, and visual. Wireframing means writing all the text a designer needs before the designer touches it. The brand owner controls the message; the designer renders it. Reversing that order produces beautiful sites that don't sell.

### The 10 Standard Sections

The recommended website is built from ten reusable sections. The header always comes first; the rest can be reordered, omitted, or repeated as appropriate.

#### 1. Header

The most critical section. Must pass the **grunt test** — within five seconds, a stranger should be able to answer:
1. What do you offer?
2. How will it make my life better?
3. What do I need to do to buy it?

Include a clear text headline, a supporting visual, and a direct CTA button. Everything else on the site is secondary.

#### 2. Value Proposition

Elaborates on what the customer gets. Can use icons, images, bullet points, or short sentences. Keep it brief — 3–4 specific benefits. Throwing too much at customers in this section makes them disengage.

#### 3. The Plan

Show the **process plan** here (3–4 simple steps for how to do business with you, or how to use the product after purchase). The agreement plan can appear lower on the page. Customers won't step into a fog — a visible plan removes the fog.

Two flavors:
- **Pre-purchase process plan**: how the customer buys ("Schedule a call → Get a custom plan → Enjoy [outcome]")
- **Post-purchase process plan**: how the customer uses the product after buying ("Unbox → Set up in 10 minutes → Get [result]")

#### 4. Testimonials

Authority without bragging. Two rules:
- **Keep them short** — soundbite length, not paragraphs.
- **Keep them relevant** — each testimonial should celebrate a specific victory or overcome a specific objection. Ideally both.

Include the customer's photo when possible — it makes the endorsement feel personal and verifiable.

#### 5. Logos

Visual authority. If B2B, feature client logos. If awarded, feature award logos. If covered in media, feature media outlet logos. Logos take up minimal real estate while establishing credibility quickly. They let you demonstrate competence without writing about yourself.

#### 6. The Explanation

A longer paragraph (or set of paragraphs) for customers who want to read more, plus SEO benefit. Should follow the BrandScript narrative — problem, guide, plan, success vision. Consider a "click to read more" expansion so the page stays visual for scanners while serving readers who want depth.

#### 7. The Video

Engages customers who learn by watching. Two formats work:
- A scripted brand video built from the BrandScript
- A testimonial reel with B-roll

Always overlay text on the video player inviting the click — naked YouTube embeds underperform. The video should show what life looks like after engaging the brand and address common objections.

#### 8. The Cost (of Inaction)

Pulls from the **Failure** element of the BrandScript. Spells out — visually or in text — what it costs the customer if they *don't* engage. A roofer's cost section names attic damage, mold, structural risk. A dentist's cost section names eventual root canals or worse. Keep it simple, brief, and not heavy-handed. This is seasoning, not the main course.

#### 9. The Price (or Price Choices)

If pricing is shown, place it just below the cost section — the contrast between cost-of-inaction and price-of-action increases perceived value. Tactics:
- Bundle products into packages (like value meals)
- Offer no more than three pricing tiers (more than three creates decision paralysis)
- Make the recommended option visually obvious

Pricing isn't required on every site, but when used, this position is the strongest.

#### 10. The Junk Drawer

Footer section for low-priority links — careers, press, privacy policy, contact details, internal navigation. Cluttering the top nav with these kills focus. People who need them will scroll. Move them to the junk drawer to protect the rest of the page.

### Section Layout Rules

- Lead with the header, every time.
- Repeat the direct CTA throughout — minimum three placements (header, mid-page, footer).
- Sections can be reordered to match the brand's narrative arc, but the grunt test must always pass at the top.
- After wireframing, sleep on it. Ask: can an image replace text anywhere? Can authority be shown more creatively? The first draft is rarely the best.

### Wireframing Workflow

1. Complete the BrandScript first.
2. On paper or whiteboard, sketch the 10 sections in chosen order.
3. Write the text for each section directly from BrandScript elements:
   - Header → Character + Want + hint of Problem + Direct CTA
   - Value prop → Success benefits
   - Plan → Process plan
   - Testimonials → Empathy + Authority proofs
   - Cost → Failure stakes
   - Etc.
4. Hand the wireframe to a designer with notes on visual treatment, image direction, and CTA placement.
5. Stay involved through design — the writer knows the message intent and should protect it.

## Discipline 3: Write an Email Nurturing Campaign

### Core Premise

Email is still the highest-converting digital channel. More transactions happen through email than any social platform. The reason is simple: email is personal. It's where people communicate with friends, family, and coworkers. When email marketing fails, it fails because it's done wrong — not because the channel is dead.

### The Three Email Types

Every email program is built from some combination of three categories.

#### Type 1: Live Campaign Sequences

Written in real time, sent over a defined window. Used for:

- **Product launches** — large, coordinated rollouts. Often 10–20 days of emails, 1–2 per day in the final stretch. Includes references to current events and timely market conditions to feel live.
- **Promotions** — smaller-scale campaigns for existing products or low-ticket items. Same structural patterns as launches, smaller scale.
- **Meter-mover campaigns** — emails designed to drive a specific action other than purchase: blog comments, podcast reviews, social shares.

Live sequences trade the efficiency of automation for relevance and timing.

#### Type 2: Automated Campaign Sequences (Autoresponders)

Pre-written, triggered by a customer action, delivered on a fixed schedule. Five common autoresponder types:

- **Onboarding sequence** — introduces new subscribers to the brand's worldview, voice, and value. Typically 7–30 emails. The foundation of trust-building.
- **Pre-event ("tractor beam") sequence** — runs after webinar/event signup, before the event. Sells attendance. 4–7 emails over 4–7 days.
- **Post-event ("pattern buffer") sequence** — runs for people who registered but didn't attend or didn't buy. Offers alternative consumption paths (replay, PDF, shorter video, audio). Frequently captures 20–30% of total sales for an event.
- **Post-sale ("automated retention machine," ARM) sequence** — runs after a purchase. Reminds the customer why they bought, drives product usage, reduces refunds. Should exist for every product sold.
- **Lead-nurture sequence** — runs after a lead generator download (covered in detail below).

#### Type 3: Broadcast / One-Off Emails

Single emails sent to the full list for a specific purpose. Includes the **SERMON** ("Social Equity Refill Machine of Niceness") — emails that give value with no ask attached. A free training, a useful TED Talk, a helpful resource from elsewhere. Pure deposits into the relationship account, no withdrawal. Used to maintain goodwill across the list between campaigns.

### The Standard 6-Email Nurture Sequence

A practical starter pattern, triggered after a lead generator download:

| # | Purpose | Content |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Deliver the freebie | Send the promised resource. Short. No selling. One sentence about what makes the brand different. |
| 2 | Offer more value | A second piece of free, useful information — a tip, insight, or small discount. Reinforces that opening emails is worth the time. |
| 3 | (Optional) Build trust | Continue value delivery. Story, case study, or framework that demonstrates expertise. |
| 4 | Overcome an objection | Identify the most common reason customers don't buy and address it head-on. Most prospects want to engage but use distractions and excuses to avoid the decision. |
| 5 | Deliver a paradigm shift | "You used to think X — but actually it works like Y." Reframe the customer's mental model around the problem. Powerful for distracted audiences. |
| 6 | Sales email | Ask for the sale. Clear offer. Clear next step. Three-step process plan repeated inline. Specific CTA. |

After the initial 6 emails, broadcast emails can be sprinkled in (additional value drops, additional sales asks, SERMON content). The 6-email pattern is a minimum, not a ceiling.

### Two Approaches to Sales Emails

When writing the actual sales email (#6 above, or any sales email), choose between:

- **Sell only the click.** Very short email whose only job is to drive traffic to a sales page or video. The sales page does the persuasion.
- **Sell the product directly.** A condensed version of the full sales letter — story paragraph, transition, offer, CTA. Effective only after a nurture sequence has built trust. Not a cold-traffic format.

### Practical Setup Steps

1. Pick a trigger (lead generator download, webinar signup, purchase, etc.).
2. Connect that trigger to the email platform.
3. Write email #1 (deliver the promised content; no selling).
4. Write email #2 (more value; light brand differentiator).
5. Continue through the 6-email pattern.
6. Monitor open rates and click rates. Adjust subject lines and CTAs based on data.

## Discipline 4: Write a Sales Letter — The PASTOR Framework

A sales letter is the long-form persuasion document — a sales page, a long email, a direct mail letter, a video sales letter (VSL) script. The roadmap teaches Ray Edwards's **PASTOR Framework** as the structural backbone.

### The PASTOR Framework

| Letter | Stands For | Job |
|---|---|---|
| **P** | Person, Problem, Pain | Identify who you're writing to, the specific problem, and the pain that problem causes. |
| **A** | Amplify | Spell out what happens if the problem isn't solved. Raise the stakes. |
| **S** | Story and Solution | Tell the story of someone who solved this problem with your offer (or a similar solution). |
| **T** | Transformation and Testimony | Articulate the post-purchase results. Use real testimonials to validate the claim. |
| **O** | Offer | Describe what's for sale. Lead with transformation, not deliverables. |
| **R** | Response | Ask for the order. Tell the reader exactly what to do next. |

### The 15 Building Blocks

A complete sales letter contains 15 structural elements, mapped to PASTOR:

**P — Person, Problem, Pain**
1. **Headline** — the "ad for the rest of the ad." Its only job is to get the next sentence read.
2. **Core Thesis paragraph** — the single big idea the reader should walk away with.
3. **Lead** — opening copy that connects to the reader's problem and pain.

**A & S — Amplify, Story & Solution**
4. **Body** — the main text container that holds the rest of the elements.
5. **Subheads** — smaller headlines breaking the body into sections. The "bucket brigade" that pulls scanners through.
6. **Rapport** — copy that demonstrates you understand the reader's specific pain.
7. **Bullet points** — short, curiosity-arousing statements that promise specific benefits or insights.

**T — Testimony**
8. **Credibility** — answers "Why should I listen to you?" Background, results, expertise.
9. **Testimonials** — third-party validation that the solution actually works.

**O — Offer**
10. **Implicit offer** — the transformation the customer experiences (not the deliverables).
11. **Price and value justification** — frame the value, then introduce the price favorably.
12. **Risk reversal** — money-back guarantee, free trial, or other protection that defuses fear.
13. **Bonus** — an unexpected addition that increases perceived value.

**R — Response**
14. **Explicit offer / Call to Action** — the direct ask. Tell the reader exactly what to do.
15. **Postscript (PS)** — restates the top benefit. Includes a "buy now" link. The PS is one of the most-read parts of any sales letter.

### Writing Process

1. Open the BrandScript. Every PASTOR element pulls from a BrandScript element.
   - Person → Character
   - Problem & Pain → External, Internal, Philosophical Problems
   - Amplify → Failure
   - Story → Customer transformation arc
   - Transformation → Success
   - Offer → Plan + Direct CTA
   - Response → Direct CTA
2. Write a draft headline. Iterate on it; it carries disproportionate weight.
3. Move through the 15 blocks in order. Don't skip ahead to the offer.
4. Read the entire letter aloud. Cut anything that doesn't earn its place.
5. Check that risk reversal is present and prominent — it removes the single biggest blocker (fear).

## Discipline 5: Write a Lead-Generating PDF

### Why Lead Generators Matter

A lead generator is a free, valuable asset given in exchange for an email address. It accomplishes three things at once:

1. **Provides genuine value** — solves a real, narrow problem the prospect has right now.
2. **Establishes authority** — positions the brand as a credible expert in their space.
3. **Triggers reciprocity** — the unspoken obligation that follows a free gift, predisposing the prospect toward future engagement.

The email address it captures is the asset that funds every nurture sequence and sales campaign downstream.

### The 5 Standard Formats

#### 1. The Magazine Article

Convert an existing white paper or research document into a magazine-style piece — headlines, callouts, pull quotes, images, charts. Designed to be scanned, not read linearly. Most companies have a white paper sitting in a drawer that becomes a strong lead generator with this treatment.

#### 2. The Interview

Either:
- An expert in the field providing valuable insight to the brand's customers, or
- A customer describing how they used the product to improve their life.

The focus is informational value to the reader, not biographical detail about the interviewee. Format works well as PDF transcript, audio, or video.

#### 3. The Checklist

Walks the reader through a list of things to consider, do, or check. A medical clinic might offer a "Healthy Body and Mind Checklist." Reverse-engineered checklists work especially well: each item names a pain point and points toward a service or product that resolves it. Functions as a "menu" of the brand's offerings, framed as customer-relevant problems.

#### 4. The Value List

Numbered tip lists. Among the simplest and highest-performing formats. Examples:
- "52 Tips for Giving the Best Talk of Your Life"
- "8 Things to Look Out for When Buying a New Car"
- "10 Ways of Winning at an Auction"

Every business should have at least one. The structure is forgiving and the format is universally familiar.

#### 5. Free Samples

A taste of the actual product. A day planner company offers a 7-day worksheet PDF; if the prospect wants the full year, they buy. A grocery store offers recipes that require store ingredients. A craft store offers a DIY project requiring craft supplies. The lead generator and the upsell are mechanically connected.

### Title Discipline

A weak title kills the lead generator regardless of content quality. Strong titles:
- Promise one specific outcome
- Use numbered specificity ("5 Mistakes," "10 Ways," "7 Steps")
- Hint at a curiosity gap or a contrarian frame
- Read like clickable headlines, not academic papers

Test:
- Weak: "Cooking Without Gluten"
- Strong: "The Best Gluten-Free Pizza on the Planet, Plus Ten More Mouthwatering Recipes"

The title is the entire ad for the asset. Spend disproportionate time on it.

### Production Rules

- **Make it scannable.** Drop caps, callouts, images, white space. Less text per page is better.
- **Speak into the design.** The writer should stay involved through layout — design decisions can amplify or undermine the copy intent.
- **Don't get stuck.** A downloadable PDF guide is the easiest format to start with. Avoid paralysis by analysis. Ship a v1 and iterate.

### Strong Lead Generator Examples (Illustrative Patterns)

- "5 Mistakes People Make with Their First Million Dollars" (financial advisor)
- "Building Your Dream Home: 10 Things to Get Right Before You Build" (architect)
- "Becoming a Professional Speaker" (free course from a business coach)
- "Cocktail Club: Learn to Make One New Cocktail Each Month" (liquor store; recurring engagement format)

## Discipline 6: Gather Customer Testimonials

### Why Testimonials Carry So Much Weight

A guide must establish authority — but self-promotion makes the brand sound like the hero. Testimonials solve this asymmetrically: the brand demonstrates competence using someone else's words. The reader trusts a peer's experience over the brand's self-description.

Testimonials should be cataloged systematically. The same testimonial might be deployed on a website, in a sales letter, in an email campaign, in a keynote, and in social ads. Maintain an organized library, indexed by what each testimonial proves.

### What a Good Testimonial Accomplishes

Every testimonial should do at least one of four jobs. Categorize the library by job:

#### 1. Overcomes Resistance / Objections

For specific buying objections (price, distance, time commitment, complexity), feature testimonials that directly address that objection. If the retreat center is far away, surface a testimonial that says the trip was worth it. If pricing is high, surface a testimonial that frames the value.

#### 2. Celebrates a Success

After-state testimonials. Specific outcomes: money saved, weight lost, time freed, stress reduced. Readers move toward the light — show what the customer's life looks like now and they'll project themselves into that picture.

#### 3. Demonstrates Empathy and Authority

Testimonials that mirror the SB7 guide elements. Empathy: "They understood exactly what I was going through." Authority: "They knew what they were doing and got it right the first time." Anything that pairs these two qualities is gold.

#### 4. Demonstrates Avoidance of Failure

Testimonials about what *didn't* happen. "Every contractor I'd worked with before was late and over budget. That didn't happen here — they were on time and on price every job." Pulls failure elements from the BrandScript into social proof.

### The Testimonial Interview Questions

The mistake most brands make: asking "Can you give us a testimonial?" The answer is invariably bland. Customers want to help but don't know what territory to cover. Specific questions produce specific (and usable) answers.

Standard 7-question testimonial interview, adapted to brand specifics:

1. **What problem were you dealing with when you contacted us?** (Surfaces the external problem.)
2. **How was this problem making you feel?** (Surfaces the internal problem.)
3. **How did we demonstrate that we understood your problem?** (Surfaces empathy.)
4. **Why did you trust us to solve your problem?** (Surfaces authority and decision-making.)
5. **Did you notice a difference between us and similar companies?** (Surfaces differentiation.)
6. **What surprised you about working with us?** (Surfaces unexpected value, often the best soundbite.)
7. **What does life look like now that the problem is solved?** (Surfaces the success vision.)

Customize the wording per business. Use as a written survey, a phone interview, or a video shoot. The question structure is reusable across industries.

### Operational Recommendation

Build a recurring testimonial-gathering system. Don't gather testimonials only when needed for a specific asset — gather them continuously, edit them down to soundbites, and store them in a tagged library. When a piece of marketing collateral needs a specific objection-buster or success story, it's available immediately.

## Bonus: Operating a Marketing/Copywriting Business

The roadmap closes with a module on running this work as a business — relevant whether the agent is supporting a copywriter, a coach, an in-house marketer, or a business owner doing their own marketing.

### Common Failure Modes

Six recurring problems for freelance marketers:

1. **Income below expectation** — undervaluing skills, undercharging.
2. **Feast or famine cycles** — no consistent pipeline; either overloaded or starving.
3. **The "lowball with a promise" trap** — high-status clients offering exposure or future reward in lieu of fair pay. The reward never quite materializes.
4. **Rate grinders** — clients who systematically negotiate fees down, eroding both income and confidence.
5. **Deadbeats** — clients who accept the work and disappear before paying.
6. **One-night stands** — single-engagement clients who never return, even after great work.

These problems compound — financial stress drives bad decisions, which damages work quality, which damages reputation, which deepens the financial stress.

### Pricing Insight

Most copywriters are dramatically underpriced. The lesson the founder learned by accident: when he tried to price himself *out* of unwanted work by doubling his fee, the client said yes. Doubled it again — yes again. The market was prepared to pay far more than he was charging.

The principle: **price on value to the client, not on hours or industry averages.** If the work is worth $100k in incremental revenue to the client, charge a meaningful percentage of that. Don't set fees astronomically just to sound impressive — but don't undercharge from insecurity either.

### The Seven Pillars of a Sustainable Practice

#### Pillar 1: Three Success Keys

1. **Sense of importance and urgency** — believe the work matters. Businesses depend on it; failure has real costs to real clients.
2. **Confidence** — having a framework (StoryBrand or similar) creates predictable competence. Confidence comes from method, not bravado.
3. **Willingness to act** — talk to prospects, send proposals, follow up, do the work. Thinking about a successful practice doesn't build one.

#### Pillar 2: The STAR Strategy

1. **Specialist** — own a specific framework or niche. Specialists out-earn generalists.
2. **Teacher** — teach the process while doing the work. Teaching elevates the relationship from vendor to colleague, which protects both fees and retention.
3. **Aspiration** — speak to clients' hopes and dreams. Apply storytelling skills to their future, not just their copy.
4. **Repetition** — once the system works, run it again. Most marketers abandon what works to chase novelty.

#### Pillar 3: Dimensionalize Your Value

1. **People don't buy copy — they buy results.** No one wakes up wanting a copywriter; they want sales. Lead with outcome, not deliverable.
2. **Start with pain.** Identify what keeps the client awake at night. Pain is what motivates spending.
3. **Amplify the pain.** What happens if they do nothing? The status quo is rarely neutral.
4. **Dollarize the value.** Ask the client what monetary outcome they expect. Compare your fee to that outcome. Fees become trivially justifiable.
5. **Get buy-in.** Let the client reach the value conclusion themselves. People defend conclusions they helped create.

#### Pillar 4: Marketing System

A real marketing system is:
- **Defined** — components, timing, methods, expectations are written down.
- **Calendared** — every event is on a calendar. If it's not scheduled, it's not real.
- **Perpetual** — runs continuously. Stop-and-start marketing is what creates feast-or-famine cycles.
- **Systematic** — produces a predictable number of leads or partners per period.
- **Strategic** — tied to deliberate decisions about what's sold, who it's sold to, and how communication happens.

#### Pillar 5: Build the Citadel

A citadel is a fortress that protects something valuable. The citadel mindset means:
- Build the fortress around the client — protect their interests, including from their own bad instincts.
- Care genuinely about their success. The relationship deepens when the marketer is emotionally invested in client outcomes.
- "People don't care how much you know until they know how much you care."

## Cross-Discipline Workflow: From Zero to Working Marketing System

For a brand starting from scratch, the recommended sequence:

1. **Complete the BrandScript.** (See framework reference.) Nothing downstream works without this.
2. **Create one lead generator.** Start with a value-list PDF or checklist. Title disproportionately well.
3. **Build the website wireframe.** All 10 sections sourced from the BrandScript. Pass the grunt test.
4. **Wire up email capture.** The lead generator should appear on the website and trigger the autoresponder.
5. **Write the 6-email nurture sequence.** Deliver freebie → value → objection → paradigm shift → sales.
6. **Write the sales letter.** Use PASTOR framework. Lives on a dedicated sales page; the email sequence ends with a click to it.
7. **Begin testimonial collection.** Run the 7-question interview with every successful client. Build the library.
8. **Iterate.** Watch open rates, click rates, conversion rates. Adjust subject lines, CTAs, and offers based on data.
9. **Add broadcast emails and SERMON drops** between campaigns to maintain relationship goodwill.
10. **Repeat the process** for additional audiences or product lines, each with its own BrandScript.

## Asset Production Checklist

For any client/brand, the agent should be able to confirm:

- [ ] Completed BrandScript on file
- [ ] One-liner finalized
- [ ] Website wireframe with all 10 sections specified
- [ ] Grunt test passed on the header
- [ ] At least one lead generator (with strong title) deployed
- [ ] Email capture wired to lead generator
- [ ] 6-email nurture sequence written and live
- [ ] At least one sales letter written using PASTOR
- [ ] Testimonial library with categorized entries (objection-busters, success stories, empathy/authority, failure-avoidance)
- [ ] Marketing calendar with at least the next 90 days of campaigns scheduled

If any of these is missing, that's the next asset to build.

## Glossary (Roadmap-Specific Terms)

- **Grunt test** — 5-second test of header clarity (what / why better / how to buy).
- **PASTOR** — Person/Problem/Pain → Amplify → Story/Solution → Transformation/Testimony → Offer → Response. Sales letter framework.
- **Tractor beam sequence** — pre-event autoresponder series; pulls registrants toward the event.
- **Pattern buffer sequence** — post-event autoresponder for non-attendees and non-buyers; offers alternative consumption paths.
- **ARM (Automated Retention Machine)** — post-sale autoresponder; reinforces the purchase, drives usage, reduces refunds.
- **SERMON (Social Equity Refill Machine of Niceness)** — value-only broadcast emails with no ask; deposits into the relationship account.
- **Junk drawer** — footer section for low-priority website links.
- **Cost section** — website section communicating what inaction costs the customer (BrandScript Failure element).
- **Value list** — a numbered-tip lead generator format ("10 Ways to…").
- **Citadel** — the fortress of care and protection a marketer builds around a client.
- **Dimensionalize / dollarize** — translating value into monetary terms the client can compare against fees.
- **STAR strategy** — Specialist, Teacher, Aspiration, Repetition. Practice positioning.
